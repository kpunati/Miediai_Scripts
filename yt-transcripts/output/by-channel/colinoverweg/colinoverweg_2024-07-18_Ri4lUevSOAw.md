# FPA #nexgen Advisors | Dr. Preston Cherry & Colin Overweg | Having Great Conversations with Clients!

## Summary

This FPA Michigan NexGen session features Dr. Preston Cherry discussing how financial advisers — especially those early in their careers — can master client communication by combining financial expertise with emotional intelligence and behavioral skills. Dr. Cherry walks through the importance of professional designations like the CFP, practical advice for the first three years of an adviser's career, and a detailed methodology for conducting discovery conversations. The session covers techniques for asking open-ended questions, using strategic pauses, and connecting clients' past financial experiences to their present and future goals to create genuine engagement and demonstrate value.

## Key Takeaways

- The CFP designation is the gold standard credential for financial planners and most firms will sponsor the exam cost within the first 18-24 months.
- New advisers should focus on learning their craft in the first three years — building skills, getting the CFP, and staying on parents' health insurance — rather than seeking a perfect firm immediately.
- The single best opening question in a client meeting is 'What brought you here today?' followed by a deliberate, patient pause that allows clients to self-direct the conversation.
- When clients present a specific financial question (e.g., 401(k) or tax issues), advisers should ask follow-up questions like 'Why is that important to you?' or 'What outcome would you like to experience?' to uncover deeper values and motivations.
- Dr. Cherry's three-step technique connects a client's recent past financial event, their present situation with more information, and their future aspirations to create an emotional continuum that demonstrates the value of financial planning.
- Financial documents such as tax returns and credit card statements tell stories about clients' values and priorities; reviewing them carefully is some of the most important work a junior adviser can do.
- Advisers should do their own 'honest self-audit' of their personal finances and emotions to better empathize with clients and build genuine conversational skills.

## Detailed Notes

### Professional Designations and Starting a Career [00:01:07]

Dr. Cherry positions the CFP as the gold standard analogous to a CPA or bar exam. He advises new advisers to get the CFP first, then layer on specialty designations like the AFC or CFT-I over time as skills develop. Firms generally cover the CFP exam cost in the first 18-24 months, and even large wirehouses increasingly require it.

### Practical Advice for the First Three Years [00:11:39]

Dr. Cherry outlines six priorities for advisers entering the profession: get into a decent firm, earn a fair wage, take the CFP, stay on parents' healthcare until 26, build an emergency fund, and contribute to a Roth IRA. He emphasizes that cutting teeth at a first or second employer — even imperfect ones — is a universal backstory for successful advisers.

### Opening a Client Meeting: The Discovery Question [00:20:08]

The recommended opening question is simply 'What brought you here today?' delivered with a relaxed tone and followed by a deliberate pause. Dr. Cherry demonstrates through role play how body language, eye contact, and a lower, more personal tone signal genuine care. He notes that clients frequently respond by saying they have never been asked that question before, unlocking candid conversation.

### Bridging Financial Questions to Deeper Values [00:29:37]

When clients open with a technical finance question (e.g., mega backdoor Roth or RMDs), Dr. Cherry advises answering the number first and then following with 'Why is that important to you?' or 'What outcome would you like to experience?' This surfaces underlying motivations — such as tax anxiety or unmet giving goals — and turns a transactional question into a values conversation.

### The Past-Present-Future Emotional Continuum Technique [00:43:21]

Dr. Cherry describes a three-step conversational arc: ask the client about a recent financial event they wish they had handled differently (recent past), ask what they would do with more information now (present), and then ask about their bold financial aspiration five to ten years out (future). Connecting these three moments creates an emotional through-line that lets clients feel the value of the planning process in real time.

### Document Review and Workflow Integration [00:52:53]

Dr. Cherry's process includes a dedicated 'money psychology meeting' focused solely on feelings about money, separate from the data-gathering meeting. Document requests are sent immediately after the good-fit meeting with a clear checklist. When reviewing credit card statements, he demonstrates how to raise spending patterns by framing the observation as aligning goals rather than making judgments, asking whether a line item 'jumped out' at the client.
