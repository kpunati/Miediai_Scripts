# Caleb Silver | FPA #nexgen Guest | Investing, Marketing, Future of the Profession

**Channel:** Colin Overweg

## Summary

This session from the FPA NextGen Michigan chapter features Caleb Silver, Editor-in-Chief of Investopedia, in conversation with adviser Colin Overweg. Silver shares research on how financial advisers find and consume information, emphasizing peer learning, social media, and white papers. The discussion covers content strategy for advisers, Investopedia's use of AI for operational efficiency, and Silver's personal experience receiving holistic financial planning services—including college planning and tax strategy—from his own adviser.

## Key Takeaways

- Advisers across age groups primarily learn from each other through podcasts, newsletters, social media, and industry conferences.
- Content creation functions as a modern professional resume; showing knowledge across platforms builds client trust more effectively than telling.
- The flywheel approach to content—producing once and repurposing across channels—is more sustainable than creating platform-specific material.
- Investopedia uses AI for headline optimization, content summarization, and script drafting, but not for writing or editing articles to preserve brand authenticity.
- Holistic planning that extends beyond portfolio management—such as college planning and tax efficiency—is what clients value most and what earns adviser fees.
- Current topics like Nvidia can serve as hooks to teach timeless principles such as overconcentration and diversification.

## Detailed Notes

### How Advisers Find Information [00:00:00]

Silver surveyed advisers including Preston Cherry, Doug Bonapart, and Rita Chang on information sources. The common denominator is peer learning: advisers consume each other's podcasts, newsletters, substacks, and conference talks. Younger advisers lean into social media while many still read white papers and institutional research.

### Content as the New Resume [00:00:00]

Silver argues that showing knowledge—through brief videos, charts, and social posts—is as important as telling it in client meetings. Overweg frames public content as the new professional resume; Silver agrees and describes launching a video series 'How I Explain ... to My Clients' to demonstrate adviser-style communication. Authenticity and personal voice are emphasized over copying established personalities.

### AI Tools at Investopedia [00:00:00]

Investopedia does not use AI to write or edit articles, preserving editorial trust. Approved uses include headline A/B testing, photo selection optimization, podcast topic aggregation across thousands of shows, and AI-assisted script drafting. Silver used an AI agent trained on existing articles and podcasts to generate 60 video scripts, then re-edited each one to restore his voice, saving roughly 50% of time per script.

### Holistic Planning and Earning the Adviser Fee [00:00:00]

Silver, despite his financial media background, uses a personal adviser and values the relationship for areas he does not know—tax efficiency across a complex LLC and W2 income structure, private investments, and college planning. A college planning tool his adviser introduced identified merit scholarship opportunities at alternative schools and saved $35,000 per year in college costs, illustrating the value of planning beyond asset allocation.

### Advice for Newer Advisers [00:00:00]

Silver's closing advice is to start creating content immediately and treat it like a daily habit. He encourages authenticity over imitation, noting that clients recognize and trust an adviser's genuine voice. He also highlights the value of specializing in a niche—such as tax efficiency or college planning—to differentiate a practice within the broader financial planning ecosystem.
