# Taylor Schulte & Colin Overweg | FPA #nexgen Community | Investing, Careers, Marketing and more!

## Summary

Taylor Schulte, CFP and founder of the fee-only RIA Define Financial, speaks with FPA NextGen community leader Colin Overweg about building a career and practice in financial planning. Schulte traces his path from a 2007 wirehouse role at Morgan Stanley through going fully independent in 2014, and explains how narrowing his client focus to high-net-worth pre-retirees over 50 was the inflection point that created scalable growth. He walks through a structured marketing funnel framework—top, middle, and bottom of funnel—and advocates for patience, strategy, and consistency over chasing quick results in content creation.

## Key Takeaways

- Getting any job in financial services out of college is more valuable than waiting for the perfect role; learning what you dislike is as instructive as finding what you enjoy.
- Starting an RIA means becoming a business owner, not just a financial planner—understanding the distinction before launching is critical.
- Narrowing to a specific client niche simplifies marketing, operations, and systems, and often accelerates firm growth more than any other single change.
- Marketing funnels require one top-of-funnel activity, one middle-of-funnel activity, and one bottom-of-funnel activity working together before adding more channels.
- Content marketing requires years of patience and a clear target audience before meaningful traction occurs; comparing it to compounding investment returns helps set realistic expectations.
- Standing out as a job applicant requires creativity and effort—physically walking into offices or sending personalized materials dramatically reduces competition.
- Community with other advisors—even just weekly one-on-one meetings—provides compounding career and practice benefits that peer isolation at large firms cannot replicate.

## Detailed Notes

### Taylor Schulte's Career Path: Wirehouse to Independent RIA [00:00:00]

Schulte started at Morgan Stanley in San Diego in 2007 after choosing it over Fisher Investments, without fully understanding the sales-focused nature of the role. After surviving the 2008-2009 financial crisis and growing as an advisor, he left in 2012 for an independent hybrid firm, then founded Define Financial in 2014. He credits exposure to Michael Kitces's blog and the broader fee-only fiduciary movement as pivotal influences in his decision to go fully independent.

### Career Advice for Students and New Advisors [00:00:00]

Schulte advises aspiring advisors not to be overly selective about their first role—any experience in financial services provides a useful foundation. He warns against paralysis by analysis and encourages students to get started, stay curious, and use early roles as launchpads. He also emphasizes that standing out as a job applicant through creative, high-effort outreach (walking into offices, mailing distinctive materials, sending Loom videos) dramatically differentiates candidates from the majority who apply passively.

### Business Ownership vs. Financial Planning: A Critical Distinction [00:00:00]

Schulte draws a sharp distinction between being a skilled financial planner and being a business owner, noting that the two roles eventually become incompatible at scale. Using the analogy of a popular barber who failed after opening his own shop, he cautions that entrepreneurial ambition should be paired with realistic understanding of operational demands. He also references a Harvard study suggesting that employee and business-owner net worths often converge over a 30-year career, framing the decision as about personal preference rather than purely financial outcome.

### Niche Strategy and Its Impact on Firm Growth [00:00:00]

About five years into running Define Financial, Schulte transitioned away from a broad client base and went all-in on high-net-worth individuals over 50 nearing retirement with complex tax situations. This shift required off-boarding clients who no longer fit but resulted in hockey-stick growth as systems, messaging, and processes became aligned around one target persona. He has since turned away a 30-year-old with $50 million in assets who did not fit his niche, demonstrating conviction in the strategy.

### Marketing Funnels and Content Strategy [00:00:00]

Schulte explains his marketing funnel model: a single top-of-funnel activity drives awareness, a middle-of-funnel activity (such as his Stay Wealthy Retirement Podcast) builds trust and expertise, and a bottom-of-funnel activity manages the sales process. SEO was his original top-of-funnel driver, ensuring his podcast ranks on the first page of Google for retirement podcast searches. He is currently investing in short-form video as a new top-of-funnel channel, committing to publishing one video per weekday for 60 days before reviewing analytics—emphasizing long-term consistency over short-term metrics.

### Advisor Growth Community and the Value of Peer Networks [00:00:00]

Schulte has committed to a weekly meeting with a different financial advisor for most of his career, finding peer learning more valuable than staying within the wirehouse bubble. He co-founded the Advisor Growth Community with Justin Castelli to facilitate these connections at scale, building a private online community of approximately 170 advisors focused on community first—rather than leading with content, webinars, or vendor discounts as organizations like the FPA or XYPN do.
