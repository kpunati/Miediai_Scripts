# The Psychology of Investing

**Channel:** iShares by BlackRock
**Published:** 2023-01-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=pYVzZRjy5Hg
**Duration:** 20:09

## Summary

iShares presentation on behavioral finance: common biases that derail investors. Examples include Fairfield Sentry (Bernie Madoff's feeder fund) for envy/FOMO, S&P envy (diversified portfolios always lag), lottery effect, and loss aversion. Cites Isaac Newton's £20K South Sea Company loss (~$3M today) — intelligence doesn't protect against emotion. Argues a disciplined written plan or financial advisor is the main protection.

## Key Takeaways

- How you behave as an investor often matters more than which investments you pick.
- Studies show investors prefer steady-up charts — but that pattern was historically the Fairfield Sentry feeder to Bernie Madoff's fraud. No magic bullets.
- Isaac Newton — one of history's smartest people — still lost ~£20,000 (~$3M today) to FOMO in the South Sea bubble.
- Common biases: envy/FOMO, S&P envy (diversified portfolios always feel behind), lottery effect, loss aversion, action bias.
- 1 in 3 individual stocks lost money 2017-2021; only 0.1% of diversified funds lost money.
- Loss-recovery math is non-linear: 50% loss = 100% gain to break even; 60% loss = 150% gain.
- Missing the 5 best market days over 20 years can cost ~1/3 of portfolio value.
- Disciplined plan and/or financial advisor is the main protection against emotional decisions.

## Detailed Notes

### How behavior shapes outcomes [00:00:03]

The ideal investment strategy fails if the owner keeps changing it. Investor behavior during volatility (e.g., 2020 COVID or current inflation period) is especially impactful on returns.

### The 'yellow line' trap [00:01:07]

Studies show investors prefer the steady-up chart — that pattern in the example was Fairfield Sentry, a feeder fund to Bernie Madoff's fraud. The investment that looked riskless was one of US history's biggest frauds. No magic bullets.

### Isaac Newton's bubble loss [00:02:11]

Newton was a physicist Einstein praised as among the smartest ever — but in 1720 he lost ~£20,000 (~$3M today) in the South Sea Company bubble after re-entering after taking profits. He concluded: 'I can calculate the motions of heavenly bodies but not the madness of people.'

### Discipline beats intelligence [00:03:16]

Quoting Buffett: 'We don't have to be smarter than the rest — we have to be more disciplined than the rest.' External market noise enhances natural loss aversion.

### Behavioral finance overview [00:04:20]

Common biases prevent average investors from becoming ideal ones: envy/FOMO, regret over what could have been, loss aversion (losses feel twice as bad as equivalent gains).

### Envy and FOMO [00:05:22]

Studies show bronze medalists are happier than silver medalists — silver imagines almost-gold, bronze imagines almost-no-medal. Most people regret what could have been, not just actual bad outcomes.

### S&P envy [00:06:27]

Diversified portfolios constantly feel behind: in bear markets they still lose; in bull markets they trail the market. Over 2009-2019 a diversified portfolio trailed US stocks by ~160%. Over full cycles, the diversified approach often comes out ahead — but never feels like winning.

### Diversified portfolio still works [00:07:31]

Adding up all the cycles, the diversified approach delivers competitive long-term returns even when it never feels like it's leading.

### The lottery effect [00:08:35]

Investors knowingly accept high probability of poor returns for a small chance of large returns — why people buy lottery tickets, single-stock 'next hot startup' bets, and the latest trends. Reduces overall success probability.

### Single-stock loss probabilities [00:09:38]

From 2017 to 2021, ~1 in 3 individual stocks lost money. Over the same 5-year period only 0.1% of diversified mutual funds and ETFs lost money. Avoiding big losses is the actual key to investing success.

### Recovery math is non-linear [00:10:44]

First rule of investing: don't lose money (Buffett). 10% loss → needs 11% gain. 50% loss → needs 100% gain. 60% loss → needs 150% gain. Losses compound faster than people expect.

### Loss aversion and action bias [00:11:48]

Soccer goalkeepers stop more penalty kicks by staying in the middle than by diving — but they instinctively dive because doing something feels safer than doing nothing. Same in investing during market lows.

### Don't try to time the market [00:12:51]

Missing just the 5 best market days over 20 years could cost ~1/3 of portfolio value. Time in market > timing the market.

### Sideline sitting and missed compounding [00:13:55]

People who pull out of markets during fear miss the rebound. At 10% annual returns, the difference between 10 years invested vs 5 years is not 100% more — it's 261% more (~3x). Citing Einstein on compound interest.

### Contrarian discipline [00:15:00]

Buffett: 'Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful.' 2008/9 illustrates this — investors who exited at the depth missed a historic bull market.

### Make a plan [00:16:04]

Yogi Berra: 'If you don't know where you're going, you'll end up somewhere else.' A written plan removes emotion and doubt. Consider a financial advisor as accountability.

### Advisor's real value [00:17:07]

An advisor's primary job isn't to pick the best portfolio — it's to keep you from making common mistakes (the behavioral interventions).

### No one predicts the market [00:18:09]

In a survey, only 45% of expert analysts correctly predicted whether interest rates would rise or fall — worse than a coin flip. Don't overweight news predictions.

### Recap and outro [00:19:13]

Recognize biases, be willing to be critical in good times and opportunistic in bad, invest long-term, build a plan, work with an advisor if needed. 'The more you know about behavioral biases, the better the outcome.'
