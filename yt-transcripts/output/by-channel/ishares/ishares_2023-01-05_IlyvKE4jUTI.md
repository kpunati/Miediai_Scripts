# Thinking Globally with ETFs

**Channel:** iShares by BlackRock
**Published:** 2023-01-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=IlyvKE4jUTI
**Duration:** 12:05

## Summary

An iShares presentation on home-country bias. The average US investor holds ~26% international stocks vs ~39% in the MSCI ACWI benchmark. US stocks ranked 36th of 47 in a recent global index, while rest-of-world is ~85% of GDP and ~96% of population. Surveys global innovations (BioNTech mRNA, DiDi 60M rides/day vs Uber 3M) and positions iShares international ETFs — IXUS, IEFA, IEMG, ESGD/ESGE — to address the bias.

## Key Takeaways

- Home-country bias: average US investor holds only 26% international stocks vs ~39% in the MSCI ACWI benchmark.
- US stocks ranked 36th of 47 in a recent all-world country performance index — surprising to many.
- Rest of world: 85% of GDP, 96% of population, 93% of internet users — and growing.
- US share of global venture capital fell from 95% to 50% over two decades — innovation is now global.
- DiDi (60M+ daily rides) dwarfs Uber's US 3M rides — examples of similar businesses scaling abroad.
- Adding 40% international equities to a US-only portfolio reduced one-year estimated risk from 16% to 14%.
- iShares international ETFs: IXUS (broad), IEFA (developed ex-NA), IEMG (emerging), ESGD/ESGE (sustainable variants).

## Detailed Notes

### Three opening questions [00:00:02]

Frames the talk around three questions: why investing succeeds long-term, what are you invested in, and where are you invested. Sets up the focus on geographic diversification as the often-overlooked dimension.

### Recent US outperformance and the 36th-place quiz [00:01:06]

US stocks have done well in the past decade, but in the all-world country index covering 47 countries, US stocks rank 36th in performance in a recent period. The point isn't that the US will underperform — it's that owning both US and international has complementary effects.

### Home-country bias by the numbers [00:02:10]

A study of ~20,000 portfolios shows the average US investor holds only 26% international stocks. The MSCI ACWI benchmark for geographical diversification is ~39% international. The average advisor portfolio went from 70% US 4 years ago to 74% US at end of 2021 — bias is increasing.

### The rest-of-world growth story [00:03:14]

Rest of world: 85% of global GDP, 96% of population, 93% of current internet users. Projected: 96% of internet users outside US by 2025 and 90% of GDP outside US — concentrated US portfolios may miss meaningful growth.

### Innovation history beyond US borders [00:04:17]

Henry Ford's Model T (1908) revolutionized mass production; Ferrari and Fiat carry the torch globally. DiDi reports 60M+ daily rides vs Uber's 3M in the US — similar businesses at very different scales.

### Modern non-US innovations [00:05:20]

BioNTech (Germany) pioneered the first mRNA vaccines and has 8 cancer treatments in human trials. Bell Telephone (1876) → Samsung foldable phones today. Meta/Twitter/Apple have international analogues in WeChat, TikTok, Samsung. US share of global VC fell from 95% to 50% over the past two decades.

### 700M Chinese still offline [00:06:22]

Nearly half of China's population (700M+) has never made a mobile payment, tweeted, or streamed. The activities that drove US equity returns may now be coming to the rest of the world more rapidly.

### Deglobalization and US-foreign-revenue stagnation [00:07:25]

iShares' view: deglobalization may continue, making it harder to capture international exposure through US-only holdings. US companies' foreign revenue growth has slowed from 11% a decade ago to 3% today as local emerging-market companies take share.

### Concentration risk in the FANG-M cohort [00:08:27]

Holding the S&P 500 already concentrates exposure in a small group of mega-cap tech. Adding international equities can actually LOWER overall risk: a 40% international allocation reduces one-year estimated risk from 16% to 14%.

### iShares international ETF lineup [00:09:30]

IXUS for broad international exposure (developed + emerging). IEFA for developed Europe/Australasia/Far East. IEMG for emerging markets. ESGD and ESGE for sustainable variants of developed/emerging exposure.

### Wrap-up [00:10:33]

Light analogy: just as America's Got Talent has 47+ international versions, talent and opportunity exist globally — investors should challenge home-country bias as part of building a diversified portfolio.

### Outro [00:11:39]

Standard investment-risk disclaimer and prospectus pointer.
