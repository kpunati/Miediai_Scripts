# Sustainability   A new standard for investing

## Summary

An iShares presentation arguing sustainable (ESG) investing supplements traditional financial analysis. Volkswagen's 2015 emissions scandal, Vale's 2019 Brumadinho dam disaster (3B gallons, ~300 deaths, stock -20%), and Equifax's 2017 data breach (150M people) illustrate ESG risks traditional financials missed. Sustainable ETFs grew at 80% CAGR since 2015; 92% of S&P 500 firms publish sustainability reports vs 20% in 2011. iShares ESG ETFs: ESGU, EAGG, ICLN, SDG.

## Key Takeaways

- Sustainable investing supplements traditional financial analysis with environmental, social, and governance (ESG) insights.
- Volkswagen 2015 emissions scandal: governance failures (insider-dominated supervisory board, sales-pressure culture) preceded the financial blow-up.
- Vale's 2019 Brumadinho dam disaster: ~3B gallons of mine waste, ~300 deaths, stock -20% in a day — but MSCI had already rated Vale at the 2nd-lowest ESG tier.
- Equifax 2017 breach (150M people): MSCI downgraded its ESG rating over a year earlier — ESG analysis caught what traditional analysis missed.
- Climate risk: billion-dollar weather events totaled $700B+ over 5 years with frequency more than doubling.
- ESG growth: S&P 500 sustainability disclosure rose from 20% (2011) to 92% (2021); sustainable ETFs grew at 80% CAGR since 2015.
- iShares ESG ETFs: ESGU (large/mid-cap US), EAGG (investment-grade bonds), ICLN (clean energy), SDG (UN SDG-aligned companies).

## Detailed Notes

### Defining sustainable investing [00:00:05]

Sustainable investing combines traditional investment approaches with environmental, social, and governance (ESG) insights. The pitch is that ESG provides an additional risk lens financial analysis alone can miss.

### Volkswagen as the opening case study [00:01:10]

2015 emissions scandal: VW admitted cheating on US Clean Air Act tests. Governance red flags preceded the event — insider-dominated supervisory board, too many significant shareholders on the board, high-pressure sales culture. Traditional financials missed it; ESG analysis surfaces these signals.

### ESG framework: E, S, G [00:02:12]

Environmental: climate risk, resource use, pollution, clean-tech innovation. Social: human-capital management, product safety, data privacy, worker health and safety. Governance: accounting practices, ownership/control, board independence, corporate ethics.

### Where ESG insight changes the picture [00:03:16]

Companies exposed to extreme weather can face operational disruptions. Poor product quality risks recalls, litigation, and customer loss. Conversely: clean-tech and water-efficiency products may benefit from favorable policy and demand trends.

### Vale dam disaster as risk case study [00:04:20]

Vale's January 2019 Brumadinho dam collapse: ~3 billion gallons of mine waste released, ~300 killed. Financial analysts were broadly positive on Vale; MSCI had it at the second-lowest possible ESG rating due to prior dam collapse (2015) and weak oversight. Stock fell over 20% on the day.

### Climate risk at the macro scale [00:05:23]

Over 5 years, billion-dollar weather and climate disasters totaled over $700 billion in costs. Annual frequency of such events more than doubled. Companies that prioritize climate risk may outperform long-term.

### Equifax as a social-risk case study [00:06:28]

September 2017 Equifax breach affected 150M people. Financial analysts had Equifax as a buy; MSCI had downgraded Equifax's ESG rating to the lowest possible tier in August 2016 — over a year earlier — due to weak data controls.

### Human-capital management as a social factor [00:07:32]

About 2M American workers leave jobs annually due to unfairness/discrimination, with estimated replacement costs of $64B. Strong human-capital management aids talent retention and reduces turnover costs.

### Governance and gender diversity [00:08:35]

Volkswagen illustrates how weak governance impacts companies. Separately, companies with gender-diverse executive teams show higher profitability than those with poor gender diversity.

### ESG opportunities beyond risk [00:09:40]

Consumer demand for sustainability is rising, affecting business outcomes regardless of personal view. Clean energy is a specific thematic opportunity: an estimated $32 trillion in new investment needed over the next decade to implement global renewable energy targets.

### Why ESG is becoming a standard [00:10:42]

S&P 500 companies publishing a sustainability report rose from 20% (2011) to 92% (2021). 73% of institutional investors have implemented ESG strategies. 71% of US population expresses moderate or higher interest in sustainable investing.

### Asset-growth trajectory [00:11:46]

Sustainable ETFs grew at 80% compound annual growth rate from 2015 through end of 2021. Sustainable mutual funds grew at 22% CAGR. BlackRock projects $1.5T in new sustainable index AUM between 2020 and the end of the decade.

### Core ESG ETFs as building blocks [00:12:50]

Replace traditional market-cap-weighted core exposures with ESG analogues. ESGU = large/mid-cap US stocks with favorable ESG. EAGG = investment-grade bonds from issuers with favorable ESG. Risk/return characteristics close to parent indexes.

### Thematic sustainable ETFs [00:13:54]

ICLN = global clean energy companies producing and distributing renewable energy and equipment — renewables expected to be 3/4 of $12T new power tech investment through 2040. SDG = global companies addressing UN Sustainable Development Goals (17 goals: nutrition, sanitation, education, climate, etc.).

### Conclusion and outro [00:14:56]

Sustainable investing is about pursuing better outcomes by adding ESG analysis to traditional financials. iShares positions itself as a platform of choice. Standard prospectus disclaimer.
