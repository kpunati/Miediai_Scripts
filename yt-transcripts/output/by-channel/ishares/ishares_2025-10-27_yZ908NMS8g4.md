## Summary

Kevin Lee defines active ETFs as a fast-growing category projected by BlackRock to triple global AUM to $4.2 trillion by 2030. Contrasts active ETFs with index ETFs (which seek to mirror an index): active-ETF managers use proprietary research and real-time decisions. Surveys three active-ETF categories: alpha-seeking, outcome (e.g., income maximization), and exposure (hard-to-index assets). Cites BALI (iShares US Large Cap Premium Income ETF) as an example active ETF.

## Key Takeaways

- Active ETFs are a fast-growing ETF category, projected to triple to $4.2T in global AUM by 2030.
- Index ETFs mirror a benchmark; active ETFs use proprietary research and real-time decisions.
- Three active-ETF categories: alpha-seeking, outcome (e.g., income), and exposure (hard-to-index assets).
- BALI (iShares US Large Cap Premium Income ETF) is an example of an active ETF in the outcome category.

## Detailed Notes

### Active ETFs and their growth [00:00:01]

Active ETFs combine the ETF wrapper with active management. BlackRock projects global active ETF AUM will triple to $4.2 trillion by 2030. Index fund managers mirror a benchmark; active managers can be proactive.

### Three categories of active ETFs [00:00:37]

Alpha-seeking active ETFs target outperforming an index. Outcome-focused ones target a specific result (e.g., maximizing income). Exposure-focused ones provide access to assets that are hard to capture via traditional indexing. BALI (iShares US Large Cap Premium Income ETF) is cited as an example.
