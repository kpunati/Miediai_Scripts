# 3 Social Security Timing Mistakes Every Retiree Makes

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-07-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=IJI3fCCdZ1Q
**Duration:** 15:10

## Summary

Nick Davis outlines three common Social Security timing errors and how to avoid them. The first is evaluating benefits in isolation rather than integrating them with a retiree's full financial picture—including 401k RMDs, order of withdrawals, and tax bracket management. The second is misunderstanding how benefits are calculated from 35-year earnings records, indexing, bend points, and age adjustments, which can lead to underestimating or overestimating the benefit. The third is failing to recognize how spousal and survivor benefits interact, especially the income drop at a spouse's death, and the advantage of maximizing the higher earner's benefit.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security decisions must be evaluated in the context of the full financial picture—other income sources, tax brackets, and account withdrawal order—not as a standalone cumulative math problem.
- Delaying Social Security allows retirees to draw down pre-tax accounts at lower tax rates first, potentially saving thousands in lifetime taxes versus the reverse sequence.
- The Social Security benefit calculation uses the highest 35 working years; fewer than 35 years or reduced late-career earnings can meaningfully lower the benefit.
- Benefits are estimated based on prior-year earnings; retirees reducing work before claiming may receive a lower benefit than their SS statement projects.
- Spousal benefits are up to 50% of the higher earner's benefit while both are alive; survivor benefits are up to 100%, creating a significant income cliff at the first death.
- Maximizing the higher earner's benefit through delay is often the most valuable strategy for households because the larger benefit persists for life and becomes the survivor benefit.

## Detailed Notes

### Sign 1: Ignoring the Full Financial Story [00:00:00]

Evaluating Social Security only on cumulative lifetime totals ignores how benefit timing interacts with taxable income from 401k withdrawals, RMDs, and capital gains. Because Social Security income can push retirees into higher tax brackets, coordinating benefit timing with account withdrawal order can materially reduce lifetime taxes. A retiree with a $2 million pre-tax 401k generating approximately $90,000 in eventual RMDs faces very different calculus than a retiree without that liability.

### Coordinating Withdrawal Order with Social Security Timing [00:04:13]

Order of withdrawals—drawing from taxable accounts, then traditional IRAs and 401ks, then Roth IRAs—interacts with Social Security income to determine total tax cost. A case study illustrates living off long-term capital gains at a zero or near-zero rate for three years while deferring Social Security, taking advantage of the current provision that allows long-term capital gains to be taxed at 0% for taxpayers in the 12% or lower ordinary income bracket.

### Sign 2: Misunderstanding Benefit Calculations [00:06:18]

Social Security benefits are calculated from the highest 35 years of indexed earnings, divided into monthly equivalents and processed through three bend points. Fewer than 35 contributing years result in zero-dollar placeholders that lower the average. The SS statement estimates benefits based on last year's earnings, so reducing work income in pre-retirement years will produce a lower actual benefit than the statement shows. The 2024 wage base cap of $168,600 means higher earners receive no additional benefit credit above that threshold.

### Sign 3: Misunderstanding Spousal and Survivor Benefits [00:11:33]

Spousal benefits provide up to 50% of the living spouse's benefit; survivor benefits provide up to 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit. When a higher-earning spouse dies, the surviving spouse loses their own lower benefit and retains only the survivor benefit, creating a step-down in household income. The illustration shows the advantage of having the higher earner delay to age 70: Couple A receives roughly $83,000 at age 80 versus $68,000 for Couple B with identical starting benefits, and the survivor differential widens further.
