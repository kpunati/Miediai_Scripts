# Should I Pay Off My Mortgage Before Retiring

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=TSW0cXl3CIw
**Duration:** 17:42

## Summary

Nick Davis and co-hosts of the Money Master podcast explore the common pre-retirement question of whether to pay off a mortgage before retiring. The discussion covers both the mathematical and emotional dimensions: the case for payoff rests on reduced fixed expenses, peace of mind, and a guaranteed return equal to the mortgage rate; the case against centers on opportunity cost, maintaining liquidity, and flexibility. The hosts highlight that the decision is highly individual, depending on interest rates, cash flow adequacy, retirement timeline, and spousal alignment.

## Key Takeaways

- Whether to pay off a mortgage before retirement depends on individual cash flow, interest rate, liquidity needs, and emotional preferences — there is no universal answer.
- Paying off a low-rate mortgage (e.g., 2-3%) may sacrifice investment growth if portfolio returns exceed that rate; higher-rate mortgages change the calculus.
- The emotional benefit of being debt-free is a legitimate financial planning consideration even when the math does not clearly favor payoff.
- Middle-ground options such as accelerating payments or bimonthly payments can satisfy both spouses when they have different preferences.
- Home equity conversion mortgages (reverse mortgages) are non-recourse loans that can provide access to home equity in retirement more safely than HELOCs.
- If the mortgage will be paid off within retirement through cash flow alone, there is generally no urgency to accelerate payoff before retirement.
- Retirees who reach an 'upper guardrail' spending level can direct surplus to mortgage paydown as a financially disciplined strategy.

## Detailed Notes

### Why This Question Comes Up Before Retirement [00:00:00]

Many people feel they should enter retirement debt-free, though this belief is sometimes cultural rather than mathematically driven. Advisors note that eliminating a mortgage lowers fixed monthly expenses and removes liability, reducing the income needed in retirement. However, the right answer is highly case-by-case based on debt level relative to savings and proximity to payoff.

### The Case For Paying Off the Mortgage [00:05:11]

Payoff reduces fixed expenses, provides emotional security and peace of mind, and acts as a guaranteed risk-free return equal to the mortgage interest rate. Authors like Morgan Housel and commentators like Dave Ramsey advocate for payoff even when the math is not clear. For couples with misaligned views, payoff may satisfy the more debt-averse spouse.

### The Case Against Paying Off the Mortgage [00:08:22]

If the mortgage rate is low (e.g., 2.5-3%) and the portfolio targets 6-7%, keeping the mortgage preserves investment growth opportunity cost. Paying off also reduces liquidity, converting liquid investments into illiquid home equity. HELOCs carry recourse risk, making them less ideal as emergency access vehicles. Reverse mortgages (home equity conversion mortgages) are non-recourse and considered a safer, though complex, access tool.

### Middle-Ground Approaches and Reflection Questions [00:11:13]

Accelerating payments (extra monthly amounts or bimonthly payments) trims years off the loan without a large lump-sum outlay. Refinancing or recasting can lower the monthly payment. The hosts offer four reflection questions: Does debt-free status bring peace or stress from illiquidity? Does income comfortably cover the payment? Are you and your spouse aligned? Does your plan remain flexible under either scenario?
