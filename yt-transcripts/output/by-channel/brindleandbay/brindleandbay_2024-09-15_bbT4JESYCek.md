# The 4 Levels Of Wealth In Retirement - Where Are You?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=bbT4JESYCek
**Duration:** 16:43

## Summary

Nick Davis presents a four-stage framework for retirement wealth: Survival (primarily dependent on Social Security with minimal savings), Stretching (significant savings but lifestyle replacement is under pressure), Stability (sufficient resources to maintain a comfortable standard of living), and Abundance (wealth grows faster than it is spent, shifting focus to legacy and tax strategy). Each stage is defined by observable financial and behavioral characteristics, and the presenter provides concrete action steps for improving from the lower stages. The Stability stage is identified by the 4% rule benchmark, while Abundance is characterized by a shift from preservation to legacy thinking and intensified focus on tax planning including QCDs.

## Key Takeaways

- The Survival stage—relying primarily on Social Security with minimal personal savings—is the trajectory for a large share of Americans over 55, underscoring the urgency of early planning.
- The Stretching stage is characterized by replacing less than 70% of current salary and carrying consumer debts that limit additional saving, creating financial stress even with meaningful assets.
- The Stability stage is defined by the 4% rule: if 4% of retirement savings plus other stable income covers first-year lifestyle needs, the retiree is in the stable category regardless of absolute dollar amount.
- Abundance-stage retirees face new problems—RMD management, QCD strategy, gifting logistics—that require a fundamental shift from preservation to legacy thinking.
- Two common risks for Stability-stage retirees are under-preparing for healthcare costs and failing to optimize the tax efficiency of withdrawals.
- Moving from Survival toward Stability can begin with simple steps: reducing work stress to extend working years, auditing spending, paying down debt, and maximizing Social Security by delaying claims.

## Detailed Notes

### Stage 1: Survival [00:00:00]

Survival-stage retirees rely primarily on Social Security and have minimal personal savings. A Western Southern report cited average account savings of $256,200 for the 55–66 age group. These retirees face limited discretionary spending flexibility and may exhaust savings within the first 10 years. Recommended actions include reducing work stress to work longer, auditing spending and reducing debts, and maximizing Social Security through delayed claiming.

### Stage 2: Stretching [00:03:08]

Stretching-stage retirees have significant savings and assets but are on pace to replace less than 70% of current salary or face a dramatic lifestyle reduction in retirement. Consumer debts reduce saving capacity, and tension between current lifestyle aspirations (boat, second home, new car) and long-term affordability creates persistent stress. Action steps include documenting all income sources, debts, and investments; projecting the true cost of maintaining the desired lifestyle including inflation; and making deliberate trade-off decisions.

### Stage 3: Stability [00:08:17]

Stability is the destination for the majority of successful retirees. Its benchmark is that 4% of total retirement savings plus stable income sources covers first-year lifestyle needs—regardless of whether that is $500,000, $1M, or $3M. Stability-stage retirees typically had low debt, funded emergency savings, and began tracking their budget at least five years before retirement. Key risks are inadequate healthcare planning and suboptimal tax withdrawal sequencing.

### Stage 4: Abundance [00:13:33]

Abundance-stage retirees see their wealth grow faster than they spend it, and their focus shifts from income adequacy to legacy and tax optimization. New concerns include managing large RMDs, executing qualified charitable distributions, gifting strategies to children and grandchildren, and managing an escalating tax bill. The shift to abundance is not purely about a dollar amount—some families reach it with modest savings plus a large pension.
