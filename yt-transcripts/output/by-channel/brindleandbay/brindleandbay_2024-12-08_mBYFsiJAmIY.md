# This is boring, but it will fix your broken retirement…

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=mBYFsiJAmIY
**Duration:** 13:22

## Summary

Nick Davis outlines three underappreciated reasons retirement plans fail: procrastination fueled by an all-or-nothing mindset, inaccurate estimation of spending needs across a multi-decade retirement, and casual treatment of healthcare costs. He provides concrete action steps for each: stopping self-recrimination and listing assets and debts, splitting savings between an emergency fund and retirement accounts, maximizing employer match and catch-up contributions, tracking actual spending for three months, running a retirement lifestyle trial, and researching Medicare options including the Medigap versus Medicare Advantage decision and long-term care planning.

## Key Takeaways

- An all-or-nothing mentality leads to total inaction or panic investing, both of which produce worse outcomes than steady incremental progress.
- A 2023 study found older adults averaged only a 31% score on retirement income literacy, highlighting widespread knowledge gaps on Social Security, healthcare, and taxes.
- Most retirees discover they spend 20-40% more than they estimated once they track actual expenses, making the three-month tracking exercise a critical planning tool.
- Retirement spending categories should be split into current versus retired columns to capture what increases (healthcare, hobbies) and what decreases (commuting, work meals).
- Medicare does not cover long-term care, dental fully, or all prescriptions; failing to budget for these gaps is one of the most common planning oversights.
- An HSA, if available, serves as a tax-advantaged dedicated reserve specifically for medical costs in retirement.
- Running a one-month lifestyle trial on projected retirement income reveals which luxuries feel essential versus optional before the transition is permanent.

## Detailed Notes

### Reason 1: Thinking It Is Too Late to Plan [00:00:00]

The all-or-nothing mentality causes some people to do nothing while others rush into high-risk investments or get-rich-quick schemes to compensate for lost time. The practical fix is to stop fixating on missed opportunities, list all accounts, debts, and monthly expenses, split extra cash between a three-month emergency fund and retirement contributions, and automate investments using low-cost ETFs or target date funds.

### Reason 2: Poor Estimation of Retirement Needs [00:04:11]

A 2023 retirement income literacy study of 3,800 people found an average score of only 31% across topics including Social Security, healthcare, and taxes. Unplanned expenses—helping adult children, grandchildren, aging parents, and paying others for tasks once self-performed—consistently exceed retirees' budgets. The three-step fix is tracking actual spending for three months, creating a two-column current-versus-retired expense comparison, and running a one-month retirement income trial.

### Reason 3: Underestimating Healthcare Costs [00:09:23]

Medicare Advantage plans may feature zero extra premium but carry higher co-pays and out-of-network costs for emergencies and specialized procedures compared to original Medicare with a Medigap supplement. Medicare does not cover long-term care, requiring a separate plan for extended custodial needs. The recommended steps include comparing total out-of-pocket costs across plan types, building healthcare as a separate budget line item, and maximizing an HSA if available.
