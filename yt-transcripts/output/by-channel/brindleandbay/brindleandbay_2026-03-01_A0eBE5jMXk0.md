# I've Helped 1,000 Retirees, Here's How I'd Invest $2M Once I Retire

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-03-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=A0eBE5jMXk0

## Summary

This video argues that at the $2 million retirement threshold, financial structure matters more than investment returns because this is the level where penalty systems — IRMAA, taxable Social Security, and compounding RMDs — begin to have major impact. The host proposes a three-tier visibility framework: capping tax-deferred accounts at roughly $600k, aggressively converting the remainder to Roth during the low-income window before age 70, and holding quality ETFs in a taxable account for stealth income that does not trigger penalty thresholds. The central claim is that structure beats balance — two identical $2 million retirees can end up with dramatically different after-penalty income depending solely on how assets are allocated across account types.

## Key Takeaways

- At $2 million, retirees enter the zone where IRMAA, taxable Social Security, and RMD-driven bracket creep begin compounding against them.
- The goal in retirement is to manage income visibility — what the IRS and Medicare see — not just to maximize raw returns.
- Tier 1 (tax-deferred): limit traditional IRA/401k to roughly $600k so RMDs at 73 are in the $22,000-$25,000 range, staying below penalty thresholds.
- Tier 2 (Roth): aggressively convert pre-tax money to Roth during the low-income window (post-retirement, pre-Social Security, pre-RMD) when brackets are most compressible.
- Tier 3 (taxable): hold quality, dividend-growing ETFs for stealth income where capital gains timing is self-controlled.
- Two retirees with identical $2M balances structured differently can differ by $8,000+ per year in IRMAA surcharges alone.
- A $2 million IRA with $600k at 73 in the tax-deferred tier, $800k-$1M in Roth, and $400k in taxable ETFs maximizes the ability to fund any lifestyle without triggering cascading penalties.

## Detailed Notes

### Why $2 Million Is a Fault Line [00:01:05]

Below $2 million, most retirees avoid the most severe penalty thresholds. Above $2 million, they are firmly in the crosshairs of IRMAA, taxable Social Security, and large RMDs. Right at $2 million, small structural mistakes can cascade into cascading penalties across multiple systems simultaneously.

### Three Visibility Tiers [00:03:27]

The three tiers are: (1) tax-deferred control — limit IRA/401k size to cap RMDs around $22,000-$25,000/year by age 73; (2) liquidity control via Roth — money that is invisible to all penalty systems and accessible without creating visible income; (3) stealth income from a taxable account holding low-turnover ETFs, where capital gains timing is self-controlled. Together these three tiers allow a retiree to fund any lifestyle while minimizing what the penalty systems can see.

### The Roth Conversion Window [00:07:15]

The gap between retirement and age 70 (when Social Security starts) and 73 (when RMDs begin) is the optimal window for Roth conversions because marginal rates are at their lowest — no paycheck, no Social Security, no RMDs stacking on top. Front-loading conversions at 12%-22% now avoids 24%+ later, and the window closes once Social Security and RMDs layer in.

### Structure Beats Balance [00:12:12]

The video concludes by illustrating that two retirees with identical $2 million account balances can have very different retirement outcomes based purely on account structure. One might pay $8,000/year in IRMAA surcharges with 85% of Social Security taxed; the other pays standard Medicare premiums and keeps most Social Security income protected — purely because their money is held differently across account types.
