# You're Being Too Cheap. Watch This Video To Spend More in Retirement

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-05-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=L2xgQEQlVx4
**Duration:** 15:20

## Summary

This video argues that many financially prepared retirees fail to spend at their true capacity due to psychological barriers—chiefly a scarcity mindset formed from decades of saving and a poor or unclear withdrawal plan. Through a client case study (Mike and Susan) and references to research on the consumption gap, the advisor illustrates that the problem is emotional rather than mathematical. A practical framework is offered: visualizing multiple retirement scenarios, adopting a dynamic guardrails-based withdrawal strategy, prioritizing active years, identifying spending permission triggers, and aligning spending with personal values.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirees with sound finances often live unnecessarily frugally because they have not psychologically shifted from accumulation to distribution mode.
- A clear and dynamic withdrawal plan—not a static or market-reactive one—is a prerequisite for spending confidence in retirement.
- Research shows higher-wealth retirees systematically underspend because flatline financial plans fail to reflect declining spending in later retirement years.
- Retirees spend in phases resembling a spending smile: more active spending early, less in the middle, with potential uptick for healthcare at the end.
- Identifying personal spending permission triggers in advance allows retirees to consciously choose to spend rather than reflexively saying no.
- Connecting retirement spending to personal values (family, health, freedom) helps retirees spend purposefully rather than out of guilt or fear.

## Detailed Notes

### The Underspending Problem [00:00:00]

Many retirees with adequate savings live more frugally than necessary due to a scarcity mindset and lack of trust in their withdrawal plan. The advisor identifies two causes: decades of saving mode are hard to reverse, and vague plans that react to annual market performance do not give retirees confidence to spend. This results in missed experiences during the most active years of retirement.

### Behavioral Roots and the Consumption Gap [00:02:59]

A client couple (Mike and Susan) with a clear retirement income plan still fell far short of their spending capacity a few months into retirement, avoiding even a long-planned road trip. The advisor traces this to a psychological barrier of decades of saving and references an academic paper on the consumption gap showing higher-wealth retirees systematically underspend because financial plans use rigid flatline spending assumptions that do not reflect actual retirement spending patterns.

### The Spending Smile and Dynamic Withdrawal [00:09:09]

Retirement spending follows a smile curve: higher in early active years, lower in the middle, and potentially rising again for healthcare. Flatline rules like the 4% rule ignore this, causing overcaution early when spending capacity is highest. Risk-based guardrails are recommended because they signal when it is safe to spend more, not just when to cut back.

### A Framework for Spending More [00:10:49]

The advisor outlines five steps: visualize multiple retirement scenarios (earlier retirement, part-time work); replace rigid withdrawal rules with a dynamic guardrails approach; prioritize active years and bucket-list experiences; identify personal spending permission triggers in advance; and align spending to core personal values like family, health, or freedom. Connecting financial decisions to values reduces guilt and enables purposeful, abundant spending.
