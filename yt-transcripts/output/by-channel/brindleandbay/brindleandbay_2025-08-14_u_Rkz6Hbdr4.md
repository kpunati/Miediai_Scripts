# I am 58 with $1,450,000. Can I Retire Now AND Spend $10k A Month?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=u_Rkz6Hbdr4
**Duration:** 17:32

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP at Brindle and Bay, presents a detailed case study of Tim and Sue (pseudonyms), a 58-year-old couple with $1.45 million in savings who want to retire immediately and spend $10,000 a month. The video begins with back-of-napkin math using a 4.5% withdrawal rate alongside Social Security projections to show the numbers are 'close but not confident.' It then moves to financial planning software to expose two major gaps: an 8% initial withdrawal rate driven by the gap before Social Security, and a probability of success of only 45%. The advisor explains how separating expense categories, adjusting for the spending smile, and modeling various levers — Social Security timing, part-time work, spending reductions — can move the plan toward the target 80% confidence level. Healthcare from ages 58-65 and long-term care risk are identified as critical unresolved items requiring individual solutions.

## Key Takeaways

- A simple 4.5% withdrawal rate calculation can make a plan look viable on paper while masking an 8% actual early-retirement withdrawal rate before Social Security begins.
- The advisor targets 80% probability of success, not 100%, and plans to use a guardrail strategy rather than the probability dial to manage withdrawals post-retirement.
- Separating vacation, housing, and healthcare from the base spending figure and applying a spending-smile reduction can improve plan accuracy without reducing the desired lifestyle.
- Social Security timing had minimal impact on this couple's plan, but the advisor notes it is highly situation-dependent and can matter significantly in other cases.
- The healthcare gap from ages 58 to 65 and long-term care risk are critical unaddressed risks that must be solved individually through part-time work, assets, or insurance.
- Part-time work during early retirement is a powerful and flexible lever for improving plan viability without permanent lifestyle sacrifice.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Introduction and the Retirement Dilemma [00:00:00]

Tim and Sue are a 58-year-old couple with just over $1.4 million across 401ks, IRAs, and investment accounts. They want to retire and spend $10,000 per month but have received wildly different answers from online calculators. Tim remains in a high-pressure sales job while Sue has already stepped away. The emotional and financial stakes are clearly framed.

### Back-of-Napkin Math and Its Limits [00:03:43]

A 4.5% withdrawal rate on $1.45 million yields roughly $65,000. With Tim's projected $3,900/month Social Security at 67 and Sue's benefit, combined income approaches the $120,000 gross goal, or about $135,000 after-tax gross-up. The video highlights what this simple calculation omits: early Social Security claiming penalties, the 7-year healthcare gap before Medicare, tax efficiency across account types, and long-term care.

### Software Modeling: Finding the Real Withdrawal Rate and Confidence Level [00:06:24]

Financial planning software reveals an 8% withdrawal rate in the years before Social Security begins, well above the sustainable range. The probability of success dial shows 45%, far below the advisor's 80% target. The video explains that the probability dial is useful for setting initial targets but should not drive ongoing retirement decisions, which is where the guardrail strategy takes over.

### Adjustments to Improve Plan Viability [00:10:26]

The advisor separates healthcare and vacation costs from the $10,000 base figure, stages vacations ($6,000 early, $3,000 later, stopping in mid-70s), and reduces base monthly spending to $7,500 before healthcare. A spending smile adjustment of roughly 1% per year is applied only to lifestyle spending. Social Security timing scenarios (claiming at 62, 67, or 70) are tested and found to have minimal impact for this couple. Options explored to reach 80% confidence include spending cuts, part-time encore career income, and longer working years.

### Unresolved Risks and Next Steps [00:15:44]

Two major plan gaps remain: bridging health insurance costs from age 58 to Medicare at 65 (potentially through part-time work, an inheritance, or other assets) and planning for long-term care (self-insuring, traditional LTC insurance, or home equity). The advisor frames these as personalized conversations that must be resolved to make the plan realistic and actionable.
