# How SMART 401(k) Millionaires Pay Their Roth Conversion Tax

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-03-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NagZQJAj1fs
**Duration:** 14:40

## Summary

Nick Davis walks through the critical but often-overlooked decision of how to pay the tax bill generated by a Roth conversion. The central argument is that paying conversion taxes from outside retirement accounts (taxable brokerage, savings, money market) preserves the full converted amount inside the Roth, substantially increasing the tax-free compounding benefit. The video layers in two additional controls—bracket filling and IRMAA awareness—as part of a four-step system for executing conversions efficiently across the early retirement window.

## Key Takeaways

- Paying Roth conversion taxes from the conversion itself shrinks the Roth balance and permanently reduces the tax-free compounding advantage.
- Most brokerages default to withholding taxes from the conversion unless the investor opts out, making this mistake common.
- Bracket filling—converting only up to the top of the current tax bracket—keeps the conversion cost predictable and avoids spillover into higher rates.
- IRMAA surcharges are cliffs, not gradual; exceeding the threshold by even one dollar triggers a full year of higher Medicare premiums.
- The optimal conversion window is the low-income years between retirement and the onset of Social Security and RMDs.
- Retirees who coordinate all four elements—timing, bracket filling, IRMAA awareness, and outside tax payment—can preserve significantly more wealth over a full retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Payment Source Changes the Math [00:01:41]

Davis illustrates that converting $50,000 while paying $12,000 in tax from the conversion leaves only $38,000 in the Roth, versus $50,000 if the tax is paid from a taxable account. The same conversion amount produces materially different outcomes depending solely on the funding source of the tax payment.

### The Cost of Withholding from the Conversion [00:03:28]

Most custodians default to a 22% withholding unless the investor actively opts out, causing thousands of dollars to bypass the Roth permanently. Davis estimates that $20,000 growing at 6% for 20 years becomes roughly $70,000, illustrating the long-term cost of this common mistake.

### How Smart Converters Pay the Tax [00:05:39]

The recommended approach uses after-tax dollars from taxable accounts or savings to cover the tax bill, ensuring the entire pre-tax conversion amount lands in the Roth intact. This converts after-tax dollars into a shield that protects pre-tax wealth from shrinkage during its transfer to the tax-free bucket.

### Bracket Filling and IRMAA Awareness [00:08:31]

Converting only up to the top of the current bracket (e.g., 22%) prevents spillover into higher brackets and keeps the annual tax cost predictable. IRMAA adds a second constraint: conversion size must also respect Medicare premium cliffs at $109,000 (individual) and $218,000 (married) MAGI, since exceeding them triggers full-year surcharge increases.

### The Four-Part Conversion Framework [00:11:55]

Davis synthesizes the strategy into four coordinated steps: (1) convert during the low-income window before Social Security and RMDs, (2) fill brackets deliberately without spillover, (3) stay below IRMAA cliffs, and (4) pay the tax from outside sources. Consistent execution over several years is what separates retirees who maximize the strategy from those who get only partial benefit.
