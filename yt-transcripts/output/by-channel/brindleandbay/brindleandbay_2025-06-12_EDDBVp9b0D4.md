# How Do I Handle Drawdowns in Retirement?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-06-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=EDDBVp9b0D4
**Duration:** 19:54

## Summary

Nick Davis and Tori from Brindle and Bay address the common retiree concern of how to handle market drawdowns without panic-selling or disrupting income. They compare four main withdrawal methodologies — cash buffer, bucket strategy, annuity, and risk-based guardrails — arguing that the math across methods is roughly equivalent and the real differentiator is emotional support for staying invested. The episode concludes with practical steps for retirees when a drawdown occurs: pause, check the plan, stay invested, and avoid strategy changes during a crisis.

## Key Takeaways

- All major withdrawal methodologies (cash buffer, bucket, annuity, guardrails) can work mathematically; the primary difference is the emotional framework they provide.
- Market drawdowns of 10-14% occur regularly and 30% bear markets occur every 5-6 years — acknowledging this in advance reduces panic when they happen.
- Moving to cash during a drawdown is the single worst decision a retiree can make, as it locks in losses and misses the recovery.
- Risk-based guardrails allow for small, early spending adjustments rather than large reactive cuts, preserving the portfolio while maintaining steady monthly income.
- Retirees who retired in favorable sequences (e.g., 2009) versus unfavorable ones (e.g., 1968 with stagflation) face very different outcomes, highlighting sequence-of-returns risk.
- Practical steps during a downturn: pause and review, stick with the existing plan, stay invested, and do not change strategy mid-crisis.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Market Drawdowns Concern Retirees [00:00:38]

Newly retired individuals often become more risk-averse because they are no longer adding to savings and depend on the portfolio for income. The hosts note that regular drawdowns (10-14% in the S&P 500) and periodic bear markets (30% every 5-6 years) are inevitable and must be planned for rather than ignored. Living longer with inflation also means bonds-only portfolios are no longer adequate.

### Withdrawal Methodologies Compared [00:03:40]

Four main strategies are discussed: (1) 1-2 year cash buffer with predominantly equity portfolio; (2) three-bucket strategy with near-term cash/CDs, mid-term moderate portfolio, and long-term equities; (3) annuity strategy for guaranteed income; and (4) risk-based guardrail strategy with dynamic spending adjustments. The hosts argue all converge to a total-return portfolio with a systematic withdrawal rate. The challenge with cash buffers is knowing when and how to refill them.

### Risk-Based Guardrails Explained [00:07:44]

Traditional guardrails and risk-based guardrails share a name but differ significantly in mechanics. Risk-based guardrails set upper and lower portfolio value thresholds: when the portfolio hits the upper guardrail, spending increases; when it hits the lower guardrail, spending temporarily decreases to allow recovery. The goal is small, early adjustments rather than large reactive cuts. This approach acknowledges the unpredictability of sequence-of-returns and provides flexibility compared to the static 4% rule.

### Practical Steps During a Market Downturn [00:15:11]

When markets drop: step one is to pause and review rather than react emotionally; step two is to stick with the pre-established withdrawal plan and remain invested; step three is to not change strategy mid-crisis. The advisors distinguish between proactive check-ins (e.g., seeing if a guardrail has been hit) versus panic-driven changes to allocation. They also warn against consuming fear-based media and treating market timing as a viable strategy.
