# Warren Buffett is Sitting on Cash—Should You Be?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-10-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AB4OJYIUtvU
**Duration:** 15:07

## Summary

This episode directly addresses the viral headline that Warren Buffett moved Berkshire Hathaway to a record cash position and whether retirees should follow suit. The hosts explain the Buffett indicator—total US market cap relative to GDP—as a long-term valuation gauge, noting it was near 197% in mid-2025, but emphasize that even Buffett himself keeps the majority of Berkshire's assets in equities. Case studies of Apple, Walmart, Microsoft, Procter & Gamble, and JPMorgan Chase during the 2008 and 2020 downturns illustrate how great companies adapt and thrive in adversity. The practical conclusion is that retirees with multi-decade horizons should stay invested, rebalance rather than retreat, and check in annually with their plan.

## Key Takeaways

- Buffett's elevated cash position reflects Berkshire-specific capital allocation, not a personal signal to exit equities—his own shareholder letter confirms most money stays in equities.
- The Buffett indicator near 200% signals elevated long-term valuations but is not a market-timing tool and does not imply an imminent crash.
- Retirees face multi-decade time horizons and need equity exposure to hedge inflation; dividends tend to grow while bond yields do not.
- Apple, Walmart, Microsoft, P&G, and JPMorgan Chase all emerged from prior recessions stronger by innovating or acquiring during downturns.
- When valuations are high, the right response is to stay the course, rebalance to the target allocation, and not retreat to cash.
- Financial plans built for retirees already assume market volatility and drawdowns; reacting to individual headlines undermines that built-in resilience.
- Checking in with one's financial plan at least annually—rather than reacting to each headline—is the recommended practical step.

## Detailed Notes

### Context: Buffett's Cash Position and the Buffett Indicator [00:00:00]

Berkshire Hathaway's cash pile reached a record $347 billion, generating widespread headlines. The Buffett indicator—total US market capitalization divided by US GDP—stood near 196.85% as of late May 2025, close to Buffett's own stated warning threshold of 200. However, the hosts emphasize this is a long-term valuation tool, not a short-term market-timing signal.

### Buffett's Own Stance on Equities [00:03:47]

Buffett's 2024 shareholder letter explicitly states the great majority of Berkshire's money remains in equities and that preference will not change. At a 1999 shareholder meeting, he stated Berkshire has no idea whether the market will go up in the short term. The hosts note that Buffett himself has always focused on owning great businesses, not timing the market.

### Case Studies: Great Companies Thriving in Downturns [00:08:31]

Apple launched the App Store during the 2008 crisis and diversified its supply chain to Vietnam and India during US-China tensions. Walmart gained market share in both 2008 and 2020 by leaning into low prices and scaling curbside pickup rapidly. Microsoft invested in cloud computing post-2008, positioning Azure and Teams for dominance during remote work adoption in COVID. Procter & Gamble ramped up hygiene and cleaning product production during COVID shortages. JPMorgan Chase entered 2008 with a strong balance sheet and acquired Bear Stearns and Washington Mutual at bargain prices, emerging even stronger.

### Practical Steps for Retirees [00:12:37]

The hosts advise retirees to view high valuations as a signal to rebalance rather than exit, keep a diversified low-correlation portfolio, and prioritize time in the market over market timing. An annual plan check-in is recommended as the primary vehicle for calibrating any adjustments, rather than reacting to individual headlines.
