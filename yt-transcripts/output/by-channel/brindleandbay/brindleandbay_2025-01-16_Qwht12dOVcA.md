# When you feel like you can’t retire soon enough

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Qwht12dOVcA
**Duration:** 15:05

## Summary

This Money Master Podcast episode tackles the emotional difficulty of feeling behind peers on retirement timing, arguing that social media comparisons are distorted because people share highlights and rarely the full financial or personal picture. The advisors note that retirement ages in practice span the late 50s to early 70s, driven by factors such as job satisfaction, health, income, and healthcare needs. Rather than benchmarking against peers, the hosts recommend anchoring to personal goals, building and testing a sustainable financial plan, and remaining flexible because retirement dates nearly always shift. The episode closes with advice to reframe comparison anxiety as a prompt to clarify one's own goals and identify financial knowledge gaps.

## Key Takeaways

- Social media retirement posts reflect highlights, not reality, and overrepresent early retirees who are vocal about their success.
- Social Security data suggests an average retirement age of 62, but in practice advisors see a wide range from late 50s to early 70s.
- Many people delay retirement to age 65 specifically to maintain employer health insurance before Medicare eligibility.
- Retirement dates are almost always flexible; most people set a target and then adjust it due to bonuses, health, or changed goals.
- Sustainable retirement requires understanding income sources, inflation, healthcare costs, and having a guardrails-based withdrawal plan.
- Working longer has genuine benefits: income, community, purpose, and engagement—especially if the job is enjoyable.
- When feeling behind peers, reframe the comparison as a prompt to clarify personal retirement goals and address the most pressing financial knowledge gaps.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Comparisons Feel Distorted: Social Media and Recency [00:00:00]

Social media amplifies retirement announcements and exotic vacations while filtering out the messier financial realities. The hosts note that vocal posters tend to be outgoing or high-achieving personalities who retired early, creating a skewed sample. Listening only to these voices makes average workers feel perpetually behind despite being on a normal trajectory.

### Actual Retirement Age Range and the Factors That Drive It [00:01:04]

Social Security data shows an average retirement age of 62, but the advisors observe a wide distribution in their practice from the late 50s to beyond 70. Key factors include job satisfaction, health changes, income levels (making it hard to quit high-earning years), caring for family members, and healthcare coverage needs. Many people delay to age 65 specifically to avoid buying individual insurance before Medicare kicks in.

### Why Retirement Dates Shift and How to Work With That [00:07:19]

Almost every retirement date changes—delayed by bonuses, accelerated by health events, or modified to a partial or phased retirement. The hosts recommend setting a target date as a planning anchor while building in flexibility. Phased transitions to part-time work or consulting are common and allow people to reduce hours while maintaining engagement, community, and supplemental income.

### Practical Questions for Retirement Readiness [00:09:25]

Key readiness questions include: Can I sustain retirement financially, not just reach it? Do I understand my income sources, withdrawal strategy, and inflation adjustments? Have I planned for healthcare costs? Am I clear on what I want to do in retirement? The hosts also note that some people retire and then feel purposeless, so clarity about post-retirement identity and goals is as important as financial readiness.
