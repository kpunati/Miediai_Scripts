# If You Have $2.5M at 62 — Why Aren’t You Retired Yet?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=2Y_C_Zi5fgE
**Duration:** 17:13

## Summary

Nick Davis examines why high-net-worth individuals who are objectively financially ready still delay retirement. He identifies three interconnected barriers: identity loss (careers provide purpose, status, and self-definition), moving goalposts (once one financial milestone is met, a new concern replaces it), and a trust deficit (decades in control-oriented careers make it difficult to rely on probabilistic plans). Davis presents a framework for overcoming these barriers through identity exploration, purpose building, and gradual trust accumulation — arguing that psychological preparation often matters more than adding more money.

## Key Takeaways

- For high-net-worth individuals, the barrier to retirement is typically psychological, not financial — adding more money does not resolve the hesitation.
- Career identity runs deepest in high achievers, making the retirement transition particularly difficult for executives, business owners, and professionals like surgeons.
- The 'one more year' pattern is a displacement activity — a way to avoid confronting the identity work of stepping away from a decades-long career.
- Financial goalpost-moving is a symptom of unresolved purpose and identity concerns, not genuine financial inadequacy.
- The trust deficit — difficulty accepting that a probabilistic plan will work without direct control — requires lived experience, not more spreadsheets, to resolve.
- Practical trust-building steps include gradual work reduction, practice spending from retirement accounts, and trial retirement periods.

## Detailed Notes

### The Misconception That Hesitation Is Financial [00:01:03]

Davis argues that for people with $2.5 million at 62, the belief that the problem is the portfolio number is itself the first misconception. Focusing on accumulating more becomes a displacement activity that feels responsible and measurable but avoids the uncomfortable psychological work of redefining identity. If the issue were financial, adding $500,000 would create confidence — but it never does, because the goalpost simply shifts again.

### Identity Loss as the True Barrier [00:03:52]

Successful professionals invest 20-40 years building an identity around their career — title, responsibilities, achievements, and daily routine. Retirement represents not just the end of work but the loss of that identity. Davis recounts speaking with a surgeon for whom this concern was especially acute given the contributions of his career. He notes that retirement can feel like abandoning one's core self, not entering a new chapter.

### Moving Goalposts and the Displacement Pattern [00:06:33]

Each time a financial milestone is met, a new worry replaces it — market volatility, inflation, healthcare, the political environment. The specific concern changes every year, but the level of hesitation stays constant, revealing that the underlying issue is not financial. Davis has observed this in people with $5-7 million, confirming that no portfolio size resolves the pattern without addressing its psychological root.

### The Trust Deficit [00:09:21]

High performers in control-oriented careers are accustomed to steering outcomes directly. Retirement requires trusting a probabilistic plan and accepting uncertainty about markets, health, and longevity — a fundamentally different psychological mode. Davis argues the solution is not a larger portfolio but rather gradual trust-building through lived experience: easing work hours, practicing withdrawal behavior, and trial retirement periods that make the plan feel real.

### The Combined Framework: Math and Mindset [00:13:58]

Davis recommends first validating the financial plan through proper projections to eliminate mathematical excuses, then addressing identity and purpose outside the career, and finally building trust through small real-world experiments. He emphasizes that advisers who understand both the financial and emotional sides of retirement are essential, and that most people discover they were financially ready years before they were emotionally ready.
