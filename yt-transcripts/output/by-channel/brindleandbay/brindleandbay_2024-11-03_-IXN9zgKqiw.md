# I am 62 With $1.9M In My 401(k). Should I Do A Conversion?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=-IXN9zgKqiw
**Duration:** 13:49

## Summary

Nick Davis uses a detailed case study of Greg (62) and Norma (57), a Colorado couple with $1.9M in 401(k)s and $450K in taxable accounts, to demonstrate Roth conversion analysis. He runs multiple withdrawal and conversion scenarios through tax planning software, comparing outcomes on total lifetime taxes, average tax rate, and portfolio net income. The optimal strategy—converting to just below the first IRMAA bracket—saves an estimated $537,457 in taxes versus the default withdrawal sequence, though the break-even is not until around 2051.

## Key Takeaways

- The single most important filter when evaluating Roth conversions is marital status, because the married filing jointly bracket delays the point at which single-filer rates become punishing.
- Converting 100% of a large 401(k) in one year maximizes immediate tax cost but is not the optimal strategy for most couples.
- Filling tax brackets up to just below the IRMAA Medicare surcharge threshold is often the most tax-efficient Roth conversion approach.
- The Roth conversion break-even timeline can extend into the late 80s, making legacy intent and views on future tax rates critical decision factors.
- Withdrawal sequencing (taxable first, then tax-deferred, then tax-free) is distinct from and interacts with the Roth conversion question.
- Emotional factors—willingness to pay larger taxes earlier—often override the pure math in clients' decisions about conversions.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Overview and Key Questions [00:00:00]

Greg (62) and Norma (57) are the case couple, with Greg retiring immediately and Norma working part-time until 62. Their $1.9M in 401(k)s plus $450K taxable account form the planning base. The video frames three key questions: convert all, convert partially, or convert nothing.

### Disqualifying Question: Marital Status [00:01:02]

Nick introduces marital status as the primary filter: single filers hit higher tax brackets earlier, amplifying the benefit of Roth conversions. The case study benefits apply to both couples and singles aiming to leave a legacy.

### Income Timeline and RMDs [00:02:05]

Norma's part-time income is small and Social Security is deferred for several years, creating a window of low income ideal for conversions. Required minimum distributions will eventually force large taxable withdrawals, underscoring the value of converting earlier.

### Analyzing Withdrawal and Conversion Strategies [00:04:12]

Tax planning software shows multiple scenarios: pro-rata withdrawal, taxable-first sequencing, and various Roth conversion amounts filling the 10%, 12%, 22%, and IRMAA brackets. The default strategy produces an average tax rate of 15.51%.

### Optimal Strategy and Tradeoffs [00:07:21]

The best strategy—converting to just below the IRMAA bracket 1—results in total taxes of approximately $476K versus $1M with no conversion. However, paying larger taxes in early retirement years (blue line) versus slowly rising taxes later (yellow line) is a behavioral hurdle for many clients.

### Decision Framework for Clients [00:10:31]

Nick offers three clarifying questions: willingness to pay larger taxes earlier, beliefs about future tax rate changes, and legacy importance. He notes that Roth inherited by a beneficiary can grow tax-free for 10 additional years before required withdrawal.
