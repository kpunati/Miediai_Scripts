# If You Want To Retire Before 65, But Don't Feel Ready - Watch This

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-06-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=g3iGnO4vZD0
**Duration:** 17:55

## Summary

This video from Nick Davis, CFP at Brindle and Bay, presents an eight-step retirement readiness checklist aimed at people approaching retirement who feel underprepared. The checklist moves from foundational steps—financial inventory, emergency fund, debt elimination—through income and expense planning, healthcare and Medicare preparation, and estate document setup, and concludes with investment strategy and tax-efficient withdrawals. Davis argues that true retirement readiness is less about hitting a savings number and more about having a coordinated plan for converting assets into income while managing taxes and healthcare costs.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement readiness is not just about the size of a nest egg but about having a coordinated plan for income, taxes, healthcare, and estate matters.
- An emergency fund of six months of living expenses in a high-yield savings or money market account is important even in retirement to avoid unplanned IRA withdrawals and tax consequences.
- High-interest consumer debt should be eliminated before retirement, while mortgage and low-rate car financing may be acceptable.
- Healthcare costs often increase after age 75, and proactive Medicare planning—choosing the right coverage type—can provide significant financial protection.
- Estate documents including a will, trust, power of attorney, and healthcare directives should be in place and reviewed every five years or after major life changes.
- Roth conversions, managing taxable income to avoid IRMAA surcharges, and coordinated withdrawals across account types can significantly reduce lifetime taxes in retirement.
- The shift from accumulation to distribution mindset is a critical psychological turning point for retirement confidence.

## Detailed Notes

### Steps 1–3: Inventory, Emergency Fund, and Debt [00:02:50]

Step one is a full financial inventory covering all assets, debts, and income sources—including 401(k)s, savings, social security estimates, pensions, and real estate. Step two is maintaining six months of liquid emergency savings in a high-yield savings or money market account to avoid forced, unplanned IRA withdrawals. Step three is eliminating high-interest consumer debt before retirement; mortgage and low-rate financing can be acceptable in retirement.

### Steps 4–5: Retirement Budget and Healthcare Planning [00:07:28]

Step four involves building a personalized retirement budget based on actual spending, timeline of income sources (Social Security, RMDs, pensions), and lifestyle goals—not generic 70% income replacement rules. Step five addresses healthcare costs, which tend to increase after age 75 per BLS consumer data. Within six months of age 65, a Medicare specialist should be consulted to select appropriate coverage; Medigap or Medicare Advantage decisions carry significant long-term financial implications.

### Steps 6–8: Estate Planning, Investment Strategy, and Tax-Efficient Withdrawals [00:09:46]

Step six requires at minimum a will, preferably a trust, a durable power of attorney, and healthcare directives, reviewed every five years. Step seven argues against moving entirely to bonds at retirement—portfolios still need growth for inflation and healthcare costs over a 30-year horizon. Step eight covers coordinating withdrawals across taxable, tax-deferred, and Roth accounts; Roth conversions during lower-income years, managing IRMAA thresholds, and strategic RMD planning can save thousands in taxes. Required minimum distributions start at age 73 and can create significant tax bills without advance planning.
