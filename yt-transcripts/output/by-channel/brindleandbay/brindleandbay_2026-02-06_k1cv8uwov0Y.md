# Why Financial Headlines Keep You Anxious (and Broke)

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=k1cv8uwov0Y
**Duration:** 15:07

## Summary

This Money Master Podcast episode addresses the psychological harm that financial media headlines inflict on investors, particularly those nearing retirement. The hosts reference researcher Joe Wiggins's eight-stage model of how investors predictably cycle through fear, shallow analysis, poor decisions, and eventual forgetfulness in response to any given headline. Investment author Nick Murray's view that a behavioral investment counselor is indispensable to help clients override these instincts is presented as the central thesis. The practical prescription is to anchor behavior to a written long-term plan, conduct periodic lifeboat drills so clients know how they will respond to downturns, and consult an adviser before acting on any headline.

## Key Takeaways

- Financial headlines are designed to hook readers with fear and urgency, not to provide personalized guidance relevant to any individual's plan.
- Recency bias causes investors to project the severity of recent downturns—such as 2008—onto future market events, distorting decision-making.
- Joe Wiggins's eight-stage headline life cycle shows that most headline-driven fears are forgotten within months, yet cause lasting portfolio damage if acted upon.
- Nick Murray argues that staying the course requires more than willpower; an empathetic behavioral investment counselor is necessary to override emotional reactions.
- Advisors recommend conducting lifeboat drills with clients during good markets so that a mental and financial plan is already in place when volatility strikes.
- Asking 'will this headline still matter in six months?' is a practical filter before making any portfolio change.
- A long-term, guardrails-based financial plan is the primary insulator against reactive headline-driven behavior.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Headlines Trigger Emotional Reactions [00:00:51]

The hosts argue that financial headlines are engineered to capture attention broadly rather than speak to any individual's situation. Investors fear missing a critical signal, which makes them emotionally reactive. Recency bias amplifies this: people who lived through 2008 initially misread the 2020 COVID drop as a similarly prolonged crisis, though markets recovered within the same year.

### Joe Wiggins's Eight-Stage Headline Life Cycle [00:06:02]

Wiggins identifies eight predictable stages: a story grabs attention; investors overestimate its long-term importance; they develop shallow expertise and opinions; they assess personal exposure; they incorrectly predict market reaction; they make a bad decision or stay frozen; they move on to the next headline; and they forget the issue within months. The tariff headlines running at the time of the episode are cited as a live example of this cycle in motion.

### The Role of a Behavioral Investment Counselor [00:09:10]

Nick Murray's commentary builds on Wiggins by arguing that no amount of individual willpower is sufficient—professional empathetic guidance is required to keep investors from self-destructing. The hosts compare this to having a fitness trainer versus trying to exercise alone. The firm's role, they say, is to preach staying the course and to help clients zoom out when headlines create panic.

### Practical Steps to Insulate from Headlines [00:11:02]

Key actions include anchoring to a written long-term financial plan with a guardrails-based withdrawal strategy, conducting 'lifeboat drills' during calm markets so clients already know what to do when downturns hit, and using an adviser as a sounding board before reacting. The hosts suggest asking whether any given headline-driven concern will even be discussed six months from now.
