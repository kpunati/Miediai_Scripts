# Can They Retire Early Without Regret? | Real Case Study.

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=1SA9MwiC5Yc
**Duration:** 22:42

## Summary

This podcast episode uses a case study of Mike and Julie — a couple in their early 60s with $1.2 million in savings, no pension, and a small mortgage — to illustrate the retirement planning process at Brindle and Bay. CFP Tori Tenhagen walks through the emotional and mathematical dimensions of the couple's retirement readiness: fears about market timing, healthcare costs before Medicare, running out of money, and the finality of leaving work. The planning solutions included a guardrail-based income strategy, scenario comparisons of retiring now vs. working two more years, Social Security timing analysis, Roth conversion testing, and stress-testing against historical bear markets including the Great Depression. The episode concludes with the couple gaining confidence to retire now with travel included.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement readiness has both a math component and an emotional component; financial plans must address both to produce lasting confidence.
- Separating needs from wants in budgeting allows planners to show clients what is truly flexible in their spending plan.
- Guardrail income strategies give retirees a dynamic framework showing when and how to adjust spending, rather than a static pass/fail probability number.
- A 100% probability of success signals overly conservative spending — an 80% target is more appropriate because it means 80% of scenarios require no adjustment at all.
- Healthcare costs between early retirement and Medicare eligibility at 65 are a concrete planning gap that can be funded but often deters people from retiring earlier than they otherwise could.
- Historical stress testing against events like the Great Depression reduces the psychological impact of scary financial headlines by showing clients their plan could have survived extreme scenarios.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Profile and Concerns [00:00:00]

Mike is 62 and Julie is 60. They have approximately $1.2 million in savings, no pension, a small mortgage, and Social Security is several years away. Their primary fears include retiring too early, not having enough saved, depleting their portfolio and burdening their children, healthcare costs before Medicare, and the psychological difficulty of shifting from saving to spending. They did not want to return to work after retiring and did not want to feel financially constrained in retirement.

### Planning Process: Scenarios and Guardrails [00:05:54]

The planning process began by separating baseline needs from discretionary wants, then building a guardrail-based income strategy showing projected cash flows across retirement. Scenarios compared retiring immediately versus working two additional years, tested Social Security claiming at 62 versus 67, ran Roth conversion modeling, and explicitly budgeted for the healthcare insurance gap before Medicare age. The scenario comparison helped the couple see that the numerical difference from working two more years did not outweigh the value of those two additional years of freedom.

### Probability of Success and Guardrail Philosophy [00:12:13]

The team discussed the limitations of standard Monte Carlo probability-of-success figures. A 95% success rate may feel reassuring but provides no intuitive sense of what retirement will feel like. Crucially, a 100% success probability signals that the retiree is nearly certain to significantly underspend, leaving resources unused. The firm targets approximately 80% success — strong enough that four in five scenarios require no adjustments, while keeping spending at a level that actually uses the accumulated wealth.

### Outcome and Emotional Resolution [00:14:46]

Mike and Julie decided to retire immediately, with travel included in their spending plan and a monthly income structured to feel like a steady paycheck. Seeing their full plan, including worst-case scenarios modeled against historical events like the Great Depression, shifted their mindset from scarcity and survival to confident flexibility. They later emailed asking to add $500/month to their budget — a sign of growing trust in the plan's durability rather than ongoing anxiety.
