# I Have Reviewed 25 Withdrawal Strategies. Here Is The BEST One...

## Summary

This video surveys the major retirement withdrawal strategies that retirees and advisors consider, including fixed-dollar withdrawals, the bucket strategy, and the 4% rule, walking through the strengths and weaknesses of each. The advisor concludes that risk-based guardrails are superior because they synthesize complex individual financial information into three simple questions about spending capacity, staying on track, and warning signals. Unlike static-percentage methods, guardrails adapt dynamically using Monte Carlo simulations updated monthly and can accommodate irregular spending needs such as an RV payment or long-term care.

## Key Takeaways

- Fixed-dollar withdrawals lose purchasing power over time and require a separate corrective process if the withdrawal rate climbs too high.
- The bucket strategy provides psychological peace of mind and built-in risk management by time-horizon, but can be operationally messy for rebalancing and RMD purposes.
- The 4% rule is best used as a rough readiness benchmark, not a withdrawal plan; it is overly conservative for most retirements and inflexible for actual spending patterns.
- Risk-based guardrails communicate three key outputs: current safe spending amount, whether the plan is still on track, and how to recognize when it is not.
- Traditional guardrails share only a name with risk-based guardrails and have a critical flaw: they cannot account for future earmarked spending such as long-term care.
- Statistics show retirees frequently die with more money than they started with, suggesting most plans cause unnecessary underspending.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Withdrawal Strategy Selection Matters [00:00:00]

A withdrawal strategy is the framework for determining how much to safely spend each year from retirement savings, replacing the steady paycheck from employment. The advisor frames the challenge as balancing avoiding running out of money against unnecessarily sacrificing lifestyle, with the additional complexity of account ordering for tax efficiency.

### Fixed-Dollar Withdrawals and the 4% Rule [00:02:06]

Fixed-dollar withdrawals start at 3–5% of the portfolio and are adjusted every five years; the main weaknesses are loss of purchasing power and tendency to drift to dangerously high effective withdrawal rates without triggering correction. The 4% rule improves on this by adjusting for inflation annually but requires a roughly 60/40 stock-bond allocation, is overly conservative for most retirements, and was never intended as a rigid spending guideline by its originator.

### The Bucket Strategy [00:04:13]

The bucket strategy separates savings into time-segmented buckets (near-term spending in conservative assets, later spending in growth assets). Its main benefits are psychological comfort during market volatility and natural risk alignment to time horizon. Its primary weakness is operational complexity around rebalancing and RMD distributions, and the risk of overloading the cash bucket with too many low-yielding assets.

### Risk-Based Guardrails: The Recommended Approach [00:09:28]

Risk-based guardrails answer three questions at all times: how much can I spend now, am I on track, and how will I know if I am not. They use Monte Carlo simulations updated every 30 days incorporating the retiree's age, spending plans, and current capital market assumptions. They place visual upper and lower guardrails around the portfolio, signaling when it is safe to spend more or when a pre-planned reduction is needed. This approach handles irregular spending goals and distinguishes itself from traditional guardrails, which lack holistic future-spending awareness.
