# I Am 59.5 With $1.1M: Can I Retire Now Without Regret?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-09-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=osp9vCmoiBs
**Duration:** 17:25

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP, walks through the retirement readiness analysis for Mark and Linda, a couple considering retiring at 59.5 with $1.1 million in combined assets. The video illustrates how a risk-based guardrails system, spending categorization (needs vs. wants), phased retirement, and strategic use of home equity can address an apparent income shortfall. Davis stress-tests the plan against historical periods including 1968 stagflation, finding that in most scenarios the couple can maintain or increase spending. Three levers — trimming overstated long-term travel costs, phased retirement, and a reverse mortgage to cover long-term care — meaningfully improve the plan's viability.

## Key Takeaways

- At 59.5 with $1.1M, retiring immediately is close but not yet fully viable without making adjustments to spending assumptions and income strategy.
- Separating needs ($90k/yr) from wants ($110k/yr) and time-limiting discretionary travel goals can significantly improve projected retirement sustainability.
- A risk-based guardrails system with an upper rail set 36% above and a lower rail 35% below current value modestly adjusts spending rather than making drastic cuts.
- Phased retirement — reducing hours before fully stopping — can bridge healthcare costs before Medicare eligibility at 65.
- Using a home equity conversion mortgage (reverse mortgage) to cover long-term care earmarks raises projected average monthly income by approximately $700 per month over a lifetime.
- Historical stress testing, including 1968 stagflation, showed the guardrails plan maintained income above the long-term ledger in most scenarios.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Situation and Retirement Anxiety [00:00:35]

Mark (59.5, accountant) and Linda (58, nonprofit part-time) have $1.1M in 401ks, IRAs, and personal investments. Their peer group has already retired and spends freely, creating pressure. Linda fears being 'the couple that always says no' due to financial constraints, and Mark is concerned about the permanence of leaving his career.

### Three Key Planning Questions [00:05:07]

Davis identifies three questions: (1) What do they need vs. want to spend — $90k needed, $110k wanted; (2) Are they willing to phase retirement by cutting hours before fully stopping; (3) Are there major events such as inheritance, geography changes, or legacy goals — they answered no to all. These answers frame the quantitative analysis that follows.

### Guardrails System and Initial Income Projection [00:09:12]

The risk-based guardrails system accounts for all financial factors including future Social Security and flags their accounts at $1.238M with a spending capacity of $8,210 per month. The upper guardrail triggers a spending increase and the lower guardrail triggers a $300/month reduction. Davis notes most historical periods showed income staying above the long-term plan.

### Three Plan Levers and Final Outcome [00:14:26]

Three adjustments improve the plan: time-limiting travel spending to 15 years instead of 30 (potentially eliminating $150k in overplanning), phased retirement to cover healthcare costs before Medicare, and using a reverse mortgage to fund long-term care earmarks. The reverse mortgage alone raises average monthly income from $7,200 to $7,900. Together these levers allow Mark and Linda to retire on time with confidence.
