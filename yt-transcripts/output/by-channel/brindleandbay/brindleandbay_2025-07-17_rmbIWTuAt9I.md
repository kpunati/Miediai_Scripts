# Withdrawals That Work: The Risk-Based Guardrails Q&A Session!

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®

## Summary

Nick Davis of Brindle and Bay Financial Advisers explains risk-based guardrails as a withdrawal strategy that sets dynamic upper and lower spending boundaries for retirees based on ongoing Monte Carlo or historical testing. Unlike the 4% rule, which was designed as a theoretical academic limit rather than a practical strategy, risk-based guardrails retest the plan monthly and trigger only modest, incremental spending adjustments when guardrails are reached. Davis emphasizes that retirees can preview how often and by how much spending adjustments might occur before committing to the strategy, giving them informed control over the trade-off between current spending and longevity risk.

## Key Takeaways

- Risk-based guardrails set spending boundaries using probability-of-success testing rather than fixed percentage rules, making them more responsive to actual market conditions.
- The 4% rule was never intended as a withdrawal strategy but as an academic stress test; applying it literally without inflation adjustment is a common professional error.
- Monthly retesting means the plan continuously reflects updated capital market assumptions, inflation (CPI), and even actuarial life expectancy data.
- When a lower guardrail is hit, the strategy recommends only a 10% step back toward the target probability, keeping adjustments modest and temporary rather than drastic.
- Retirees should explore the historical frequency and magnitude of potential spending cuts before adopting the strategy, so they can choose guardrail widths aligned with their tolerance.
- The strategy accommodates windfalls, large one-time expenses, irregular income, early retirement, and inheritance goals through dedicated variable expense and cash flow inputs.
- The 'spending hatchet' feature smooths income across the period before Social Security begins so retirees can spend at a steady level rather than drawing heavily from the portfolio early.

## Detailed Notes

### What Risk-Based Guardrails Are [00:01:02]

Risk-based guardrails place an upper and lower boundary around a retiree's portfolio. Monthly spending stays constant until a guardrail is breached. The upper guardrail signals an opportunity to increase spending; the lower guardrail triggers a modest, temporary reduction. This is distinct from traditional guardrails, which use fixed percentage bands rather than probability-of-success testing.

### How the 4% Rule Differs and Where It Falls Short [00:03:01]

The 4% rule was designed as a worst-case academic limit for 30-year periods with a 60/40 portfolio, not as a practical spending rule. The correct interpretation requires adding inflation each year, not simply withdrawing 4% annually. Davis notes that even financial professionals sometimes misquote it.

### Setting Guardrails and Testing Methodology [00:05:14]

The system targets 80% probability of success as the steady spending baseline. The upper rail is set at 100% success and the lower rail at a chosen lower threshold. Regime-based Monte Carlo is used rather than traditional Monte Carlo; it segments the projection period and incorporates current capital market assumptions (including near-term equity outlook and current inflation) rather than applying historical averages uniformly across all years.

### Triggering Adjustments and the Mechanics of Cuts [00:10:47]

Adjustments are automatic and occur only when a guardrail is hit, not on a fixed annual schedule. When a lower guardrail is breached, spending is nudged only 10% of the way back toward the 80% target, keeping reductions modest. The system monitors whether conditions recover (like 2009) or worsen further before applying additional cuts. Historical testing for a conservatively set plan shows spending reductions would have been required only every 10 to 13 years.

### Handling Special Situations [00:22:36]

Large one-time expenses are modeled as variable expenses in the software so the plan accounts for them without changing the guardrail structure. Inheritances and windfalls are discounted conservatively (e.g., expected $800,000 modeled as $600,000). The spending hatchet feature smooths the transition before Social Security begins. The strategy can incorporate irregular income by using a conservative floor figure as stable income.
