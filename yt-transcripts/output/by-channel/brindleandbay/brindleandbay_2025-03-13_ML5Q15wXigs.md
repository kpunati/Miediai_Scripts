# Some People Should Not Hire an Advisor...Yet

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-03-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ML5Q15wXigs
**Duration:** 21:20

## Summary

This episode of the Money Master podcast features Brindle and Bay advisors Nick Davis and Tori discussing the optimal timing for hiring a financial planner. The conversation covers scenarios where a simple financial situation, DIY enthusiasm, or lack of complexity makes professional advice unnecessary. It also explores major life transitions—divorce, inheritance, and approaching retirement—as indicators that professional guidance becomes more valuable. The hosts emphasize that advisors serve as emotional buffers against panic selling and help clients apply financial knowledge rather than just learn it.

## Key Takeaways

- A financial advisor may not be necessary for people with simple finances, strong DIY skills, and no major tax or estate complexities.
- Overconfidence and unawareness of one's own financial blind spots are red flags that may indicate a need for professional guidance.
- Major life transitions such as inheritance, divorce, or nearing retirement are key signals to seek an advisor.
- Advisors provide the most value as emotional buffers during market downturns, preventing costly panic-driven decisions.
- Application of financial knowledge—not just learning it—is often where clients need the most help, making an ongoing advisor relationship valuable.
- DIY investors often seek advisors eventually to ensure their spouse has a trustworthy sounding board if they are no longer around.

## Detailed Notes

### When You Might Not Need an Advisor [00:04:09]

If your financial plan is simple—standard 401k, no stock options or deferred compensation complexity—and you have DIY skills, you may not need an advisor yet. People in accumulation phase who can research basic allocation and tax-bucket questions independently can often get close to the right answer without professional help.

### Overconfidence and Blind Spots as Risk Factors [00:07:17]

The hosts note that everyone makes financial mistakes, and the best planning clients are those who are aware of past or potential mistakes. Overconfidence—illustrated by the analogy of a novice rodeo cowboy—is identified as a key red flag. People who are unaware they have made mistakes, particularly around nuanced issues like net investment income tax or capital gains brackets, are most at risk.

### When to Hire an Advisor [00:13:39]

Key triggers for hiring a professional include major life transitions (inheritance, loss of a loved one, divorce), approaching retirement, needing an emotional sounding board, or wanting a trusted relationship for a spouse. Advisors serve as emotional buffers to prevent panic selling during downturns—described as the single most damaging financial mistake.

### The Value of Application and Ongoing Guidance [00:15:45]

The hosts argue that the gap between learning financial concepts and correctly applying them is where advisors earn their keep. ChatGPT and Google can provide information, but navigating cause-and-effect decisions—Social Security claiming affecting Roth conversion capacity, or pension timing affecting ACA eligibility—requires experienced judgment. Advisors help clients prioritize and build a one-page roadmap to serve as a north star.
