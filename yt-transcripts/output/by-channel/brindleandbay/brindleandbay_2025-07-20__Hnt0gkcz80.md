# My Honest Advice To Retirees on ROTHs... Don't Fall For This!

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-07-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=_Hnt0gkcz80
**Duration:** 13:13

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP, systematically debunks three common 'lies' about Roth conversions: that they always save taxes, that doing them now before tax rates rise is universally sound advice, and that the cash flow demands are manageable for most retirees. He argues that conversion benefits depend heavily on individual circumstances including current tax bracket, age, time horizon, and whether outside cash is available to pay the tax bill. The video concludes with practical guidance on when conversions do make sense.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth conversions do not automatically reduce taxes; whether they benefit you depends on your current bracket, future income expectations, and available cash.
- Paying conversion taxes from the IRA itself rather than outside cash significantly erodes the benefit and can trigger early withdrawal penalties for those under 59.5.
- The 'do it now before taxes go up' argument ignores that a rate increase doesn't impact everyone equally and that break-even timelines may extend decades.
- People in their peak earning years converting at top marginal rates often destroy more value than they create; income gaps after retirement or between jobs are better windows.
- Age and time horizon are among the most commonly oversimplified variables in Roth conversion advice from financial media and influencers.
- Conversions make the most sense when you are young with decades of compounding ahead, in a temporarily low-income year, and have outside cash to cover the tax bill.

## Detailed Notes

### Lie 1: Roth Conversions Always Save Taxes [00:00:00]

The host frames the core misconception: many people believe a traditional-to-Roth conversion is a guaranteed tax win. In reality, prepaying taxes assumes future rates will be higher, which is not guaranteed. Using retirement funds to cover the tax bill compounds the cost, potentially leaving only 50-70 cents of value per dollar converted.

### Lie 2: Do It Now Before Taxes Go Up [00:03:11]

Fear of future tax rate increases is a common driver of conversions, but it overlooks individual timing. People in peak earning years converting at 24-32% may be paying more than they would on future RMDs. The host notes that low-income windows such as between jobs or early in retirement before Social Security begins are far more strategically appropriate.

### Cash Flow Risks and Real Numeric Example [00:06:19]

A $50,000 conversion at 22% triggers $11,000 in taxes immediately due. If funded from the IRA and the person is under 59.5, an additional 10% early withdrawal penalty adds $1,100. Only $38,900 reaches the Roth. Without outside cash, total conversion costs can reach 30-50% of the converted amount, undermining the long-term tax benefit.

### When Conversions Do Make Sense [00:10:35]

Conversions are most beneficial for those in their 30s with low tax brackets and decades of compounding ahead, those experiencing income gaps, and those who have outside cash to cover taxes. The host recommends calculating the true cost including taxes and penalties, comparing current versus expected future brackets, and modeling the break-even point before proceeding.
