# I Retired With $3M at 56, I Wish I Waited. Here's Why...

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=TkpA4cNwq_8
**Duration:** 18:32

## Summary

Using a composite client case study of 'Dave and Susan,' Nick Davis illustrates how retiring at 56 with $3 million without a sequencing plan can create lasting tax damage. The couple made four decisions in their first 18 months that compounded into a tax problem still unresolved by the time they sought advice at age 58. Davis walks through each mistake, quantifies the cost, and explains the recovery strategy built around the remaining 15-year conversion window before RMDs.

## Key Takeaways

- Retiring mid-year without accounting for salary already on the books can make the first year of retirement the highest-tax year ever.
- Tapping the wrong accounts first can eliminate the cheapest Roth conversion window, which may never reappear.
- Defaulting to COBRA without comparing marketplace options can cost tens of thousands more than necessary.
- Delaying financial decisions in early retirement is not free; the cost of missed Roth conversion space compounds every year.
- A surviving spouse inherits the full traditional IRA burden at single-filer tax rates, making early conversion planning a joint-life issue.
- A recovery plan starting at 58 with 15 years of conversion window remaining can still produce dramatically better outcomes than inaction.
- Large portfolio balances without a sequencing plan create a false sense of security that often hides a growing tax liability.

## Detailed Notes

### Dave and Susan's Starting Position [00:01:54]

Dave accumulated $3 million by age 56: $2.4 million in tax-deferred accounts, $350,000 in brokerage, and $250,000 cash. His wife Susan, age 54, had $200,000 in IRA and $50,000 in Roth. The couple had no debt, a paid-off home, and planned to spend $150,000 per year. On paper, a 5% withdrawal rate supported their goals.

### The Four Costly Mistakes [00:03:30]

First, they funded year-one spending entirely from Dave's traditional IRA, stacking it on top of partial-year salary to push income above $220,000 and into the 32% bracket. Second, Susan claimed Social Security spousal disability benefits at 55, adding taxable income during prime conversion years. Third, they defaulted to COBRA rather than evaluating ACA options, spending $43,000 that could have been a fraction of that cost. Fourth, they did zero Roth conversions in the two cheapest tax years of their retirement.

### The Real Cost at Age 58 [00:08:03]

Two full years of 12% bracket conversion space were unused. COBRA drained $43,000 from cash reserves. $300,000 was pulled from the IRA at the highest effective rates of their retirement. The traditional IRA balance still sat above $2 million, meaning the future tax problem had not shrunk at all.

### Six Diagnostic Questions [00:10:28]

Davis walked the couple through six questions covering: why year-one spending came entirely from the IRA when taxable and cash accounts could have funded 2-3 years; whether the salary stacking effect was understood; whether ACA subsidies were compared to COBRA; how much Roth conversion space was available and unused; what the traditional IRA balance looks like at 73 with compounding; and what the survivor scenario means for RMDs at single-filer rates.

### The Recovery Plan [00:16:22]

The recovery plan front-loaded conversions over the remaining 15 years before RMDs, shifted spending to taxable and Roth accounts to keep brackets open, and explored whether Social Security could be suspended to create more conversion room. Davis frames the lesson as: the regret is not retiring at 56 but retiring without a plan for what came next, and that distinction still has a solution.
