# OPTIMAL ORDER Of Retirement Withdrawals For High Savers

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=OhEjhLc9MH8
**Duration:** 14:30

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP of Brindle and Bay, challenges the conventional retirement withdrawal sequence — taxable first, then traditional IRA/401k, then Roth — for retirees with large savings. He explains that high savers face compounding tax problems from RMDs, inherited IRAs, and bracket creep that generic advice does not address. The video demonstrates tax-bracket blending, capital gains netting, and Roth allocation strategies using real planning software screenshots, referencing a Schwab study that quantifies savings from personalization.

## Key Takeaways

- Conventional withdrawal ordering (taxable, then tax-deferred, then Roth) is suboptimal for high savers with significant pre-tax account balances.
- RMDs at ages 73-75 can force unwanted taxable income for high savers, making proactive Roth conversion and income advancement strategies valuable.
- Blending withdrawals across taxable and pre-tax accounts each year allows retirees to stay in lower tax brackets and pay capital gains rates instead of ordinary income rates.
- Taxable brokerage accounts allow income creation through capital-gains netting, potentially generating spending money with little or no reportable gain.
- A personalized strategy identified in the Schwab study saved roughly $1,000 per year in taxes vs. conventional ordering for a $1 million account holder.
- Roth accounts offer three strategic uses: tax-bracket blending, aggressive growth allocation (no RMDs), and backdoor/mega backdoor conversions in low-income years.

## Detailed Notes

### Defining High Savers and Their Unique Tax Challenge [00:00:00]

Davis distinguishes high savers — those who started early, saved 15-20% or more of income, or are high-income professionals — from median savers. High savers often face RMD problems because their large pre-tax balances will generate forced income exceeding their lifestyle needs, pushing them into higher tax brackets in later retirement.

### Conventional Withdrawal Order and Its Limits [00:02:07]

The conventional Schwab-documented withdrawal sequence is: taxable brokerage first, then traditional IRA/401k, then Roth. Davis cites the same Schwab study to argue that a personalized approach — blending account types annually based on current tax bracket — outperforms this default by roughly $1,000 per year for a $1 million portfolio.

### Tax-Bracket Blending With Taxable Accounts [00:06:20]

By pulling from both an IRA (at ordinary income rates, e.g., 22%) and a taxable brokerage account (at long-term capital gains rates, e.g., 15%) in the same year, retirees can smooth taxable income and reduce the average rate paid. The video shows a planning tool illustrating how much capital gains can be harvested before triggering Medicare surcharges (NIIT) at a marginal dollar amount.

### Capital Gains Netting Strategy [00:08:27]

Some high savers can generate retirement spending from taxable accounts with zero or minimal reported capital gains by netting embedded losses against gains before selling. For example, a $50,000 loss position netted against a $50,000 gain position produces $0 reportable income, allowing tax-free access to capital without an IRA distribution.

### Strategic Uses of Roth Accounts [00:09:29]

Roth accounts contribute to an optimal strategy in three ways: blending tax brackets (pulling Roth instead of IRA to stay below a higher bracket), allocating most aggressive growth investments there since there are no RMDs, and executing Roth conversions or backdoor Roth contributions in low-income years between retirement and Social Security.
