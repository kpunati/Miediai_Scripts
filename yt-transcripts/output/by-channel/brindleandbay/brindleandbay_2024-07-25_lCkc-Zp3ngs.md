# Which is Better: $5,000 Monthly or $1M for Retirement?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-07-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lCkc-Zp3ngs
**Duration:** 13:39

## Summary

In this podcast episode, Nick Davis and co-hosts walk through the pension lump-sum-vs-monthly-payment decision, critiquing a widely-circulated article that frames the choice as a simple break-even calculation. Davis argues the article asks the wrong question because it ignores inflation: the fixed $5,000 monthly payment typically carries no inflation adjustment, so its real purchasing power erodes continuously. A $1 million lump sum invested in a diversified portfolio with at least 60% equities can provide inflation-adjusted withdrawals that surpass the fixed payment after 6-10 years while also preserving legacy wealth for heirs.

## Key Takeaways

- Break-even analysis alone is an insufficient basis for the lump sum versus monthly payment decision because it ignores the time value of money and inflation.
- Fixed monthly pension payments typically carry no inflation adjustment, meaning purchasing power declines each year — especially acutely during high-inflation periods.
- A $1 million portfolio withdrawing 4-5% annually with a 3% inflation adjustment can equal or exceed $5,000 per month within 6-10 years.
- The lump sum option preserves legacy value; when the retiree (and possibly spouse) dies, the remaining portfolio passes to heirs, whereas a fixed pension payment typically terminates.
- Flexibility is a key advantage of the lump sum: withdrawals can be adjusted up or down based on actual inflation, market conditions, or personal circumstances.

## Detailed Notes

### Framing the Question [00:00:00]

Nick Davis introduces the classic retirement question of whether to take a $1 million pension lump sum or $5,000 per month for life, referencing an article he critiques for asking the wrong question. The hosts note this is one of the most frequently asked questions they receive from clients facing a pension decision.

### Why the Break-Even Framing Misleads [00:01:17]

The article claims $5,000 per month accumulates to $1 million after 16 years, making it seem like an obvious winner. Davis counters that this ignores inflation: since most fixed pensions carry no inflation adjustment, the real value of $5,000 monthly erodes each year, while the lump sum invested with a proper withdrawal strategy can provide inflation-adjusted income that grows over time.

### Lump Sum Math: Safe Withdrawal Rates and Inflation [00:03:34]

Davis works through the numbers: at a 4.5% withdrawal rate, a $1 million portfolio initially provides $3,750 per month, but with a 3% annual inflation raise it reaches $5,000 per month after roughly 10 years. At a 5% withdrawal rate, the starting draw is about $4,167 per month and reaches $5,000 after only about 6.5 years. Studies supporting these withdrawal rates typically assume at least 60% equity allocation.

### Legacy and Flexibility Advantages [00:09:31]

A fixed monthly pension ends at the retiree's (or possibly the surviving spouse's) death, leaving nothing to heirs. The lump sum portfolio, if managed prudently, can appreciate and be passed on as an inheritance. Davis also highlights that flexible withdrawals allow retirees to respond to actual inflation, better or worse market returns, and changing personal needs, which a locked-in monthly payment cannot accommodate.
