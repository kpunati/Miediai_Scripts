# If You’ve Saved $2M, Here’s Your Retirement Reality

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=KvSL49PDvyI
**Duration:** 19:51

## Summary

Nick Davis provides a detailed financial walkthrough of what $2 million in retirement actually looks like, arguing that this amount is simultaneously more than enough for most couples and large enough to create serious tax complexity. The income picture is strong — roughly $130,000-$150,000 per year with a real withdrawal rate of 1.5-2.5% once Social Security covers baseline spending. However, the tax picture is problematic for the majority of savers who hold most of their $2 million in traditional pre-tax accounts: forced RMDs stack on Social Security to push effective tax rates to 20-26%, IRMAA surcharges add tens of thousands in Medicare premiums, and the surviving spouse faces even worse outcomes in compressed single-filer brackets. A Roth conversion strategy executed during the retirement window — ideally 10 years at the 12% bracket — is presented as the primary solution.

## Key Takeaways

- $2 million is genuinely enough for most retirees, with a real portfolio withdrawal rate of just 1.5-2.5% once Social Security covers baseline spending needs.
- Most savers hold $2 million almost entirely in traditional pre-tax accounts, creating forced taxable income via RMDs that stacks on Social Security and triggers IRMAA.
- Lifetime taxes on an unstructured $2 million balance can reach $650,000-$1.1 million, while a structured conversion plan may save $200,000-$500,000.
- The conversion window between retirement and RMD onset (before age 73-75) offers the lowest-cost opportunity to shift funds from traditional to Roth accounts.
- The 2026 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act brackets provide the widest 12% conversion space in recent history — over $96,000 for married couples — though this may change.
- The survivor scenario is the most motivating factor for conversions: the surviving spouse pays higher effective rates on the same RMD income due to compressed single-filer brackets and halved IRMAA thresholds.

## Detailed Notes

### The Income Reality of $2 Million [00:02:20]

At a 4% withdrawal rate, $2 million produces $80,000 per year. Adding $50,000-$70,000 in Social Security for a couple generates $130,000-$150,000 in total annual income. Because Social Security covers a significant portion of spending, the actual portfolio withdrawal rate needed is just 1.5-2.5%, which is historically sustainable for 35+ years. Retirees at this level often end up with more money at 75 than at 65 due to low real withdrawal rates.

### The Tax Problem: RMDs and Cascading Effects [00:05:19]

When the $2 million is primarily in traditional accounts, RMDs begin at age 73-75 and the government controls withdrawal amounts. The first-year RMD on a $2 million balance is approximately $73,000, rising to $88,000 by 78 and $113,000 by 83. Stacked on $55,000-$60,000 in Social Security, total income climbs from $133,000 at 73 to $168,000-$183,000 by 83, pushing filers from the 22% bracket to 24% and causing 85% of Social Security to become permanently taxable.

### IRMAA Surcharges and Medicare Cost [00:08:08]

IRMAA is a cliff-based surcharge on Medicare premiums triggered when income crosses specific thresholds. Crossing the first tier by even $1 triggers the full premium increase for that level. At the income levels created by $2 million in RMDs plus Social Security, single filers and many married filers will hit IRMAA territory. Surcharges start around $2,000 per year (both spouses) at 75, can reach $4,000-$6,000 by 80, and can total $40,000-$100,000 over 20 years.

### The Conversion Window and Roth Strategy [00:11:07]

The retirement window before age 73-75 (no salary, no RMDs, potentially no Social Security) offers the cheapest Roth conversion opportunity. A 10-year plan converting $60,000-$80,000 annually at the 12% bracket can move $600,000-$800,000 to Roth, reducing the first-year RMD from $73,000 to approximately $44,000. The conversion cost of $72,000-$96,000 in taxes is typically recovered through lifetime tax savings of $150,000-$350,000. The 2026 TCJA brackets (12% up to $96,000+ for married couples) are unusually favorable but may not persist.

### The Survivor Scenario [00:15:16]

When one spouse dies, the survivor files as a single filer with brackets roughly half as wide, keeping only one Social Security benefit but continuing to take RMDs on the same combined traditional balance. This means the same RMD income that fit comfortably in joint 22% brackets now spills into 24% or higher in single brackets. IRMAA thresholds for single filers are also about half of joint thresholds, compounding the tax burden. Every dollar converted to Roth during the joint years provides the survivor tax-free flexibility and reduces compressed single-bracket exposure.
