# 5 Things To Stop Doing After 60 To Enjoy Retirement

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lettCLaNewY
**Duration:** 17:13

## Summary

Nick Davis presents five behavioral and financial mistakes people make after age 60 that undermine retirement enjoyment. The video covers the importance of ongoing plan reviews rather than treating financial planning as a one-time task, the need to redefine lifestyle expectations rather than replicating peak-earning years, the value of addressing retirement lifestyle decisions proactively, the risks of neglecting estate planning and beneficiary designations, and a practical three-step approach to healthcare cost planning. Throughout, Davis emphasizes personalization and early action.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement plans require ongoing monitoring — ideally reviewed every 3-6 months — covering spending, allocation rebalancing, RMDs, tax opportunities, and life changes.
- Replicating a peak-earning-year lifestyle in retirement often leads to financial stress; digging into what genuinely brings fulfillment frequently reveals a less expensive lifestyle than assumed.
- Delaying lifestyle and housing decisions out of anxiety about new retirement income causes decisions to pile up and worsens stress; gradual retirement transitions are a valid solution.
- Estate plan errors — particularly outdated beneficiary designations — can force accelerated taxation on inherited accounts, creating large and avoidable tax bills.
- Healthcare costs should be viewed as an ongoing monthly expense rather than a lump-sum liability; separating predictable premiums from variable out-of-pocket costs makes planning tractable.
- Medicare does not cover long-term custodial care, making separate planning for that potential expense essential.

## Detailed Notes

### Mistake 1: Treating the Plan as One-and-Done [00:01:03]

Davis compares a well-maintained retirement plan to a golf-course lawn — it needs initial effort and ongoing upkeep, not a set-it-and-forget-it approach. Reviews should cover spending pace, tax planning windows, allocation rebalancing, RMD accuracy, and life changes. Advisers typically review every 3-6 months; self-managed retirees might review annually, more frequently in early retirement.

### Mistake 2: Reliving Peak Earning Years [00:03:09]

Many retirees try to sustain or elevate their working-years lifestyle without questioning what actually brings them happiness. Davis shares an example of a couple living in a 3,500 sq ft home they barely used, who discovered they only needed a smaller home plus a seasonal rental condo — at lower total cost. He recommends a 'more of / less of' list exercise as a starting point for aligning spending with genuine priorities.

### Mistake 3: Delaying Lifestyle Decisions [00:07:24]

Anxious near-retirees often postpone housing, travel, health, and social decisions until they compound into a crisis. Davis describes the 'spending smile' concept — higher spending in early retirement, less in the middle years, rising again for healthcare — as a useful framework for making peace with front-loaded spending. Gradual transitions such as part-time work, test withdrawals, or trial periods off help people acclimate.

### Mistake 4: Avoiding Estate Planning [00:10:34]

Davis recounts a case where a 60-year-old's 403(b) flowed to a trust that did not qualify as a see-through trust after her unexpected death, requiring full taxation in under five years rather than over decades — a large and preventable tax error. He notes that outdated beneficiary designations and inadequate recordkeeping are the most common estate planning failures, leading to probate, family conflict, and unfavorable tax treatment.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Healthcare Costs [00:13:43]

Davis recommends framing healthcare as a predictable monthly premium plus a variable out-of-pocket allowance rather than as a scary lump sum. Premiums cover roughly 73-81% of retiree healthcare costs per an industry source, providing a planning anchor. He recommends visiting medicare.gov, building a separate cash reserve for annual out-of-pocket expenses, and separately planning for long-term custodial care which Medicare does not cover.
