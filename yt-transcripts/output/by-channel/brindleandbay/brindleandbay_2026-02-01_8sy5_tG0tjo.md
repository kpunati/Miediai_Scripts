# The PERFECT Retirement Spending Plan To Pay Less Taxes If You Have $1M+ (Step by Step)

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=8sy5_tG0tjo
**Duration:** 19:29

## Summary

Nick Davis lays out a four-step retirement withdrawal sequence designed to reduce lifetime taxes for retirees with $1 million or more in savings. The framework centers on the concept of income visibility to the IRS: different account types make different amounts visible as taxable income, and controlling visibility year by year — rather than spending less — is the primary lever for reducing lifetime taxes. Davis illustrates the sequence with a case study of Mike, a 62-year-old with $1.2 million across a traditional IRA, taxable brokerage, and Roth IRA, showing how order-of-withdrawal decisions affect ACA subsidies, Roth conversion opportunities, IRMAA exposure, and RMD size.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement taxes are based on income visibility to the IRS, not on portfolio size; controlling which account dollars come from each year is the core tax management tool.
- Before age 65, taxable brokerage accounts should fund most spending to keep MAGI low enough to preserve ACA premium subsidies, which can be worth $15,000-$20,000 per year.
- Small traditional IRA withdrawals up to the standard deduction can be made during pre-65 years without creating taxable income, avoiding both income tax and ACA subsidy loss.
- The lowest-income years between retirement and RMD start (age 73-75) form a Roth conversion window where conversions at 12% or 22% prevent future forced withdrawals at 24-32%.
- After age 65, blending taxable brokerage gains, traditional IRA income, and Roth withdrawals manages IRMAA thresholds while keeping each income source in its most tax-efficient bracket.
- Roth withdrawals are invisible to Medicare premium calculations, making them the preferred marginal source when approaching IRMAA thresholds.
- Unplanned withdrawal behavior — pulling from the largest account because it is convenient — is the single most controllable cause of preventable lifetime tax overpayment in retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Withdrawal Sequence Matters [00:00:00]

Davis frames the entire video around income visibility: retirement taxes are assessed on income that becomes visible each year, not on total portfolio value. Poor sequencing layers income from multiple sources simultaneously, pushing brackets higher, triggering IRMAA, and causing more Social Security to be taxed. He illustrates the 8% versus 28% lifetime tax gap as a realistic outcome of deliberate versus accidental sequencing on the same $1 million balance.

### Step 1: Pre-65 Brokerage-First Strategy [00:04:24]

From retirement until Medicare eligibility at 65, taxable brokerage accounts should be the primary income source to minimize MAGI and preserve ACA health insurance subsidies. In the Mike example, pulling $50,000 from taxable brokerage (of which only $15,000 is a long-term gain) plus $20,000 from a traditional IRA to fill the standard deduction keeps MAGI around $35,000, preserving a $15,000 ACA subsidy. The same $70,000 pulled entirely from the traditional IRA eliminates the subsidy and costs $18,000 more.

### Step 2: Roth Conversion Window [00:09:07]

The period from retirement until RMDs begin at 73-75 is often the lowest-income window in a retiree's life: no salary, Social Security not yet started, no forced distributions. Davis argues this is the highest-leverage period for Roth conversions — converting at 12% or 22% now avoids forced conversions at 24-32% later when RMDs, Social Security, and other income stack together. The right conversion amount fills the current bracket without spilling into the next and stays aware of IRMAA thresholds.

### Steps 3 and 4: Post-65 Blending and RMD Management [00:12:22]

After age 65, healthcare shifts to Medicare and the income mix expands to blend taxable brokerage gains (potentially at 0% or 15%), traditional IRA income (for bracket filling), and Roth withdrawals (invisible to IRMAA). Social Security deferral to age 70 extends the Roth conversion window and delivers an 8% annual delayed retirement credit plus a larger inflation-adjusted lifetime benefit. Once RMDs begin, the goal is to withdraw only the required minimum from pre-tax accounts, supplementing with qualified dividends and Roth to avoid stacking income into higher brackets.
