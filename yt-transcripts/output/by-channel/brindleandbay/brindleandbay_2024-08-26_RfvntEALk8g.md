# FULL GUIDE: How To Retire In The Next Five Years (In 30 Minutes)

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP and founder of Brindle & Bay, presents a structured five-stage retirement planning system called the Money Master Plan aimed at people within five years of retirement. Stage one replaces the 4% rule with a dynamic guardrail withdrawal system that adjusts spending based on portfolio balance thresholds. Stage two applies a screen-and-rank momentum formula to IRA accounts to reduce volatility. Stage three addresses tax planning, including the Social Security phantom tax that can push effective rates above the nominal bracket. Stage four covers healthcare costs across pre-Medicare, Medicare, and long-term custodial phases. Stage five introduces an estate directory service to ensure heirs and loved ones can act quickly during incapacitation.

## Key Takeaways

- Modern guardrails replace the static 4% withdrawal rule by setting upper and lower spending thresholds tied to portfolio balance, allowing retirees to spend more while managing longevity risk.
- Reducing portfolio volatility during the withdrawal phase can be more impactful than maximizing average returns, as shown by a hypothetical comparison producing a $1.2 million difference in terminal portfolio value.
- The Social Security phantom tax can push a household in the 12% bracket to a 50% effective rate on incremental IRA withdrawals by simultaneously increasing taxable Social Security income and triggering capital gains taxes.
- Withdrawal order across account types (traditional IRA, non-qualified, Roth) can extend portfolio longevity by over six years according to academic research.
- Medicare does not cover custodial long-term care costs, making this the most commonly overlooked component of retirement healthcare planning.
- An estate directory service storing key contacts and documents online with trusted-person access is presented as the missing piece from most conventional estate plans.

## Detailed Notes

### Stage 1: Dynamic Guardrail Income Strategy [00:05:12]

The guardrail system sets upper and lower spending boundaries based on portfolio balance. In the illustrated example, a family with $2.2 million can withdraw $1,430 per month; if the portfolio grows to $2.8 million they may increase to $2,252, and if it falls to $1.2 million they reduce to $1,958. The system continuously updates for the client's age and current market assumptions, unlike the static 4% rule.

### Stage 2: Screen and Rank Investment Formula [00:10:25]

IRA accounts are managed using a momentum and volatility scoring system across a globally diversified set of asset classes. Higher momentum and lower volatility scores lead to overweighting; weights are adjusted on a planned schedule. The goal is not to beat the market but to reduce volatility, which dramatically improves outcomes when withdrawals are occurring simultaneously with market fluctuations.

### Stage 3: Tax Planning and the Social Security Phantom Tax [00:15:41]

Phantom taxes arise when incremental IRA withdrawals increase the taxable portion of Social Security benefits and push long-term capital gains above the zero-rate threshold. A hypothetical couple in the 12% bracket paid roughly $2,500 in taxes on a $5,000 IRA withdrawal. Annual income projections done each February allow the adviser to adjust which accounts income is drawn from to avoid crossing these thresholds. Optimizing withdrawal order across account types can reduce lifetime taxes by over $280,000.

### Stage 4: Healthcare Planning Across Three Phases [00:23:05]

Pre-Medicare costs are calculated by inflating current annual premiums at roughly 5% per year until Medicare eligibility. During Medicare years, out-of-pocket costs are estimated using T. Rowe Price data showing 90% of Americans spend under $5,000 annually. Long-term custodial care—assistance with daily living activities—is not covered by Medicare and must be separately earmarked, making this the most commonly overlooked healthcare cost.

### Stage 5: Estate Directory and Legacy Planning [00:26:12]

The estate directory is an online repository of key contacts, account information, and estate documents accessible by a trusted person. Davis describes a client whose spouse had a stroke and whose family found the process significantly easier because everything was organized and accessible. This service is presented as the missing component from most conventional estate plans, providing practical value beyond legal documents alone.
