# Will my Social Security Benefits be taxed?

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-05-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cCMfKEY2USs
**Duration:** 17:58

## Summary

This episode of the Moneymaster Podcast explains whether and how Social Security benefits are taxed, using the concept of provisional income (MAGI plus half of Social Security) to determine what portion of benefits is subject to tax. Three tiers are described: no tax below certain thresholds, 50% of benefits taxable in a middle band, and up to 85% taxable above higher thresholds. The hosts discuss the phantom tax effect, where IRA withdrawals or capital gains pull more Social Security into the taxable column, and highlight Roth accounts as a tool to avoid this. Legislative proposals to eliminate Social Security taxation are addressed with a conservative planning stance.

## Key Takeaways

- Provisional income equals MAGI plus half of Social Security, and determines how much of benefits are taxed across three tiers.
- Up to 85% of Social Security benefits can be taxed, but no more—even for high-income retirees.
- IRA withdrawals and long-term capital gains create a phantom tax by pushing more Social Security benefits into the taxable tier.
- Roth account withdrawals do not increase provisional income, making them a valuable tool for managing Social Security taxation.
- The taxation thresholds have not been indexed for inflation, meaning more retirees are affected over time.
- Advisors recommend planning conservatively—assuming current Social Security tax rules remain—regardless of legislative proposals.

## Detailed Notes

### How Social Security Is Taxed: The Provisional Income Framework [00:02:11]

Social Security benefits have been subject to federal income tax since 1984. Provisional income equals MAGI plus half of Social Security benefits. Three tiers apply: no tax below $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married); 50% of benefits taxable between $25,000–$34,000 (single) or $32,000–$44,000 (married); up to 85% taxable above those upper limits. These thresholds have not been adjusted for inflation in over a decade.

### Phantom Tax: How Additional Income Layers Social Security Taxes [00:05:22]

Taking extra money from an IRA or realizing long-term capital gains can push provisional income higher, causing more Social Security to become taxable. This creates a phantom tax effect where someone nominally in the 12% bracket effectively pays a higher marginal rate. Required minimum distributions from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are a major source of this forced income in later retirement years.

### Strategies to Minimize Social Security Taxation [00:09:10]

Roth account withdrawals do not count toward provisional income, making them useful for funding expenses without triggering additional Social Security tax. Tax projections at the start of each year help clients plan withdrawals strategically. Delaying Social Security to create a low-income window for Roth conversions and tax gain harvesting can reduce lifetime taxes significantly.

### Legislative Context: Possibility of Tax-Free Social Security [00:11:03]

There is political discussion about eliminating federal taxation of Social Security benefits, which would benefit the roughly 50% of recipients currently subject to it. Critics raise concerns about funding impacts, potential Medicare cost increases, and accelerated depletion of the Social Security trust fund. The advisors recommend planning under current law rather than counting on favorable legislative changes.
