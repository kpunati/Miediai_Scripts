# VANGUARD UPDATE: The NEW BEST Way To Convert To Roth

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=PbeJUc_YDCU
**Duration:** 14:49

## Summary

Nick Davis reviews Vanguard's July 2025 research paper titled 'A Better Approach to Roth Conversions,' which challenges the long-standing rule of thumb to only convert when future tax rates will be higher. The paper introduces the break-even tax rate (BETR)—the future tax rate at which a retiree would be indifferent between converting and not converting—and demonstrates that where conversion taxes are paid from dramatically affects this threshold. Paying from outside the IRA rather than from within it can lower the BETR well below the current marginal rate, making conversions valuable even in situations where conventional advice would say to skip them.

## Key Takeaways

- The traditional Roth conversion rule—convert only if your future tax rate will be higher—is incomplete because it ignores where the conversion taxes are paid from.
- The break-even tax rate (BETR) is the future tax rate at which conversion is financially neutral; actual benefits accrue when the expected future rate exceeds the BETR.
- Paying conversion taxes from outside the IRA (taxable accounts or cash) dramatically lowers the BETR compared to paying from within the IRA.
- For a 35% bracket taxpayer, the BETR drops from 35% (pay from IRA) to roughly 23% (tax-inefficient taxable account) to ~14% (cash), depending on the tax efficiency of the source.
- IRA non-deductible basis (tracked on Form 8606) can further reduce the BETR by up to 40%, strengthening the case for conversion.
- Future backdoor Roth access is an additional benefit that pushes the BETR lower for high earners, per Vanguard's research.
- The real goal is tax diversification across taxable, traditional retirement, and Roth accounts—not necessarily converting everything.

## Detailed Notes

### Problem with Traditional Conversion Advice [00:02:01]

The standard advice to convert Roth only when the current rate is lower than the future rate omits a crucial variable: the source of tax payment. This incomplete framework has led many retirees to forgo conversions that would have improved their after-tax wealth.

### Vanguard's Break-Even Tax Rate Framework [00:03:56]

Vanguard's 2025 white paper introduces the BETR as the tipping point future tax rate. When the expected future rate exceeds the BETR, conversion adds value; when it falls below, conversion does not. The BETR accounts for the source of tax payment and the length of the investment horizon—factors the simple rate-comparison ignores entirely.

### Impact of Tax Payment Source [00:06:55]

Using Vanguard's examples for a 35% bracket taxpayer: paying from the IRA yields a BETR of 35% (no benefit); paying from a tax-efficient taxable portfolio drops it to roughly 30%; from a tax-inefficient portfolio it falls to about 23.5%; and from cash, it falls all the way to approximately 14%. The same conversion produces vastly different outcomes solely based on the funding source.

### Case Study: Jill [00:09:22]

Jill is in the 35% bracket today and expects to be in the 24% bracket at retirement 20 years away. Conventional advice says do not convert. But paying conversion tax from a taxable account yields a BETR of 23.3%, meaning her expected 24% future rate is actually slightly above the threshold and the conversion marginally favors her—a result opposite to traditional advice.

### Additional BETR-Lowering Factors and Takeaways [00:11:40]

Non-deductible IRA basis (reported on Form 8606) reduces the taxable portion of conversions and can lower the BETR by up to 40%. Future backdoor Roth contributions also become cleaner when traditional IRA balances are reduced, adding further benefit for high earners. The overall lesson is strategic, per-conversion evaluation rather than blanket rules, with tax diversification as the ultimate goal.
