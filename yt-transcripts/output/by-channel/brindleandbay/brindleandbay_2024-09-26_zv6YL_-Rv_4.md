# How to face the phases of retirement.

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=zv6YL_-Rv_4
**Duration:** 16:54

## Summary

In this podcast episode from Brindle and Bay Wealth Management, advisors Nick Davis, Connie Davis, and Tori Tenhagen discuss four distinct phases they observe clients moving through in retirement: the honeymoon phase (emotional freedom and activity surge), the settling-in phase (finding structure and purpose), the healthcare phase (managing medical needs and revisiting long-term care funding), and the solo years (navigating life after a spouse's death). Each phase carries different financial planning priorities and emotional challenges. The conversation draws on personal observations of clients to highlight both the rewarding and difficult dimensions of each stage.

## Key Takeaways

- The traditional 'go-go, slow-go, no-go' framework is an overarching model; within it are four more granular experiential phases that each demand different planning attention.
- During the honeymoon phase, one spouse often retires first, leading to budget surprises and the need for more frequent financial planning check-ins.
- The settling-in phase requires retirees to proactively build daily structure, community, and purpose to avoid restlessness and dissatisfaction.
- The healthcare phase surfaces the true cost of long-term care and may require revising goals if a serious illness is diagnosed unexpectedly.
- Financial plan flexibility — avoiding being locked into illiquid products — is essential so that resources can be redirected when health needs change.
- Building social connections and community before entering the solo years is a critical, underappreciated component of retirement preparedness.
- Making good new friendships in early retirement is easier than during peak career years, more closely resembling the social openness of college.

## Detailed Notes

### Honeymoon Phase: Freedom and Adjustment [00:03:06]

The honeymoon phase is characterized by emotional highs, the absence of work obligations, and exploration of new activities and identities. Many clients see health improvements — reduced medications, weight loss, and lower stress — once work pressure is removed. Advisors note increased meeting frequency during this phase as income patterns shift and spending surprises emerge, especially when one spouse retires before the other.

### Settling-In Phase: Structure and Purpose [00:06:13]

After the initial excitement fades, retirees enter a settling-in phase where finding daily structure and renewed purpose becomes critical. Without intentional planning, some retirees report drifting — staying in bed late and feeling unproductive. This is when community involvement, volunteering, grandchildren, or new hobbies become important both emotionally and financially, as goal priorities are often revised and goals initially thought important turn out not to matter as much.

### Healthcare Phase: Medical Realities and Financial Flexibility [00:09:21]

In later retirement, doctor's appointments and medical coordination increase, and some retirees receive difficult diagnoses that require revising their financial plan. The advisors emphasize maintaining financial flexibility — avoiding products that lock up assets — so that funds can be redirected toward unexpected long-term care needs. This phase also surfaces end-of-life questions about charitable giving and legacy planning.

### Solo Years: Emotional and Practical Preparation [00:11:28]

The solo years begin when one spouse dies, creating both logistical challenges (loss of pension income, revised Social Security, required account consolidation) and deep emotional challenges (loneliness, isolation). The advisors stress that proactive relationship-building — maintaining friendships, church community, or activity groups like cycling or pickleball — dramatically reduces the hardship of this phase. Financial planning in advance prevents rash decisions made under grief and emotional stress.
