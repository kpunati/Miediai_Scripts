# STOP Trusting These Retirement Numbers, They're BETTER Than You Think

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-04-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=4IMQozwklLI
**Duration:** 13:54

## Summary

Nick Davis of Brindle and Bay challenges the widespread reliance on Monte Carlo success scores as the primary measure of retirement readiness. He shows that high success percentages can mask dangerous blind spots: software setup errors, omitted real-world shocks, emotionally unsustainable scenarios, and the absence of contingency planning. The video proposes three practical alternatives: building spending flexibility into needs/wants/wishes categories, embracing dynamic planning with regular reviews, and understanding Monte Carlo as a diagnostic tool rather than a verdict.

## Key Takeaways

- Monte Carlo success scores run from 0 to 100% but do not translate the way school grades do—a 100% score can indicate dangerous over-saving, not just good planning.
- Software setup errors can silently inflate success scores, such as when the model assumes the retiree will re-save withdrawn amounts rather than spend them.
- The simulations omit real-world risks including major tax law changes, health diagnoses, and adverse early-retirement market sequences.
- Dynamic, adaptive planning—regularly reviewing and adjusting withdrawals—provides more real-world security than optimizing a static probability number.
- Categorizing expenses into needs, wants, and wishes reveals how much spending flexibility most retirees actually have, reducing anxiety around the success dial.
- Risk-based guardrail strategies avoid the success dial entirely and adjust spending dynamically based on actual portfolio performance relative to plan targets.

## Detailed Notes

### The Problem with Monte Carlo Success Scores [00:00:00]

Nick Davis opens by noting that traditional Monte Carlo models measuring retirement risk are often misleading. Retirees tie their emotional well-being to the percentage dial, but the score does not mean what they think. A 100% success score can imply grossly underspending retirement assets, significant exposure to uncaptured risks, or a simple software configuration error where the model assumes the retiree re-saves withdrawals.

### Missing Context and Real-World Factors [00:02:57]

The success score omits factors like tax law changes, major health events requiring custodial care, a bad first decade in the market, or a single $150,000 medical expense. Behavioral factors—selling during a 30–40% drawdown—are entirely absent from the math. Simulations show the middle path but not the worst 10-year scenario, which is what investors actually need to prepare for.

### Dynamic vs. One-and-Done Planning [00:04:27]

Many retirees treat their plan as complete once a high success score is achieved and then never revisit it. Every withdrawal strategy—4% rule, bucket strategy, or guardrail approach—requires ongoing review and adjustment as conditions change. Without systematic check-ins, retirees can miss early warning signs that their withdrawal rate is no longer sustainable.

### Practical Alternatives: Flexibility, Dynamic Planning, and Wise Interpretation [00:10:03]

Davis offers three practical steps: (1) Categorize spending into needs, wants, and wishes to reveal built-in flexibility. (2) Use dynamic planning with a guardrail strategy that adjusts withdrawals based on portfolio performance, and avoid the set-it-and-forget-it mindset. (3) Treat Monte Carlo as a diagnostic comparison tool, not a retirement verdict, and audit the assumptions carefully—especially around re-saving assumptions and Social Security inputs.
