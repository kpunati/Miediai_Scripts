# One Trick That Makes RMDs a Non-Issue for Retirees

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=fBcHDE0stZE
**Duration:** 16:54

## Summary

Nick Davis, CFP at Brindle and Bay, explains the 'tax bomb' that builds over decades of 401(k)/IRA contributions and detonates at age 73 when the IRS mandates required minimum distributions. The video identifies a strategic control window between ages 60 and 73 — often a period of low income — during which voluntary withdrawals and partial Roth conversions can shrink the future RMD base, keep the household in lower tax brackets, and prevent Medicare IRMAA surcharges. Davis presents a framework covering the optimal conversion and withdrawal cadence, bracket-filling techniques, Social Security timing coordination, and the value of annual tax projections.

## Key Takeaways

- At age 73, RMDs stack on top of Social Security and investment income, potentially pushing retirees into the 32–37% brackets and triggering Medicare IRMAA charges for the first time.
- The control window between ages 60 and 73 is the single most valuable planning period, when income is often at its lowest and the household can move IRA dollars at 12–24% tax rates.
- Every dollar voluntarily withdrawn or converted during the control window permanently reduces the base used to calculate future RMDs.
- Systematic annual Roth conversions filling — but not crossing — each tax bracket outperform a single large conversion by keeping tax rates low and preserving flexibility.
- Delaying Social Security can extend the length and effectiveness of the low-income control window.
- Annual tax projections are essential to avoid accidental bracket jumps or early IRMAA triggers that waste the best planning years.

## Detailed Notes

### The Age-73 Tax Bomb [00:01:01]

Decades of tax-deferred compounding in 401(k) and traditional IRA accounts can leave a household with $1–2 million subject to forced distributions starting at age 73. RMDs of roughly 3.8–4% annually stack on Social Security (up to 85% taxable) and investment income, pushing effective tax rates to 32–37% and triggering Medicare IRMAA surcharges of $3,000–$6,000 per person per year. Most retirees fail to anticipate this because the future tax obligation feels invisible during accumulation.

### The Strategic Control Window (Ages 60–73) [00:04:49]

Early retirement often produces years of dramatically reduced income before Social Security and RMDs begin. During this window, voluntary IRA withdrawals or Roth conversions can be taxed at 12–24% rather than the 32–37% that will apply later when income stacks. Each dollar moved out of the traditional IRA permanently shrinks the future RMD base; Davis cites client examples where this cut RMDs by 40–70% and saved $100,000–$300,000 in lifetime taxes.

### Bracket-Filling Withdrawal and Conversion Strategy [00:07:58]

The core technique is to calculate the remaining capacity in the current tax bracket each year and withdraw or convert up to — but not over — that threshold. This requires coordinating with Social Security provisional income calculations and watching for IRMAA thresholds. Gradual annual conversions over the full window typically move more total dollars at lower rates than a single large conversion, and they preserve the flexibility to adjust if circumstances change.

### Practical Framework and Monitoring [00:13:52]

Davis summarizes a four-step framework: identify the control window, calculate annual withdrawal or conversion capacity by bracket, coordinate Social Security timing to maximize window length, and run annual tax projections to prevent mistakes. Monitoring projected RMD balances on a ledger each year helps track whether the strategy is on pace. A tax-focused professional is recommended to spot timing advantages that DIY planning typically misses.
