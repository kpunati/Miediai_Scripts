# Investing During An Election Year

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=4Po3BGpFEQw
**Duration:** 14:48

## Summary

In this podcast episode from Brindle and Bay, Nick Davis, Connie, and Tori address the common anxiety retirees feel about how election outcomes will affect their portfolios. They present the core argument that market returns have historically been roughly equal regardless of which party holds office and that election cycles produce normal short-term volatility that should not alter a long-term plan. The discussion covers practical mindset questions—where one gets financial information, what one would do if moving to cash, and whether short-term risk aversion conflicts with long-term goals—and uses a real client example to illustrate how a guardrail system provides a better framework for retirement income than reacting to market events.

## Key Takeaways

- Historical market returns have been roughly equal under both Democratic and Republican administrations, making political party a poor basis for portfolio decisions.
- Every market crisis—tech bubble, financial crisis, COVID—felt unprecedented at the time; the instinct that 'this time is different' is a reliable indicator not to act on it.
- Missing the best market days due to market timing compounds losses permanently; being wrong about timing has outsized long-term consequences.
- Evaluating news sources critically—understanding the financial incentives and biases behind information—is essential to sound investment decision-making.
- If you cannot articulate when you would buy back in after moving to cash, you should not move to cash in the first place.
- Risk tolerance (psychological comfort) may differ from risk capacity (what the plan requires); advisers should address both rather than defaulting to reducing risk at a client's request.
- Books like 'A Random Walk Down Wall Street' and 'Stocks for the Long Run' provide a more objective, long-term perspective than news articles or social media.

## Detailed Notes

### The Core Argument: Stay the Course [00:01:03]

Nick Davis states his answer to election-year investing is simply to stay the course. Historical data shows that market returns have been roughly equivalent under both political parties, and the feeling that 'this time is different' has accompanied every market crisis throughout history without justifying a change in strategy.

### Mindset and Information Quality [00:03:11]

The hosts encourage investors to examine their own psychological motivations for asking election-related investment questions and to evaluate their information sources critically. News articles are motivated by clicks and analysts are frequently wrong in market predictions; books like 'A Random Walk Down Wall Street' and 'Stocks for the Long Run' are recommended as more objective, long-horizon resources.

### The Market Timing Trap [00:09:27]

The hosts ask a diagnostic question: if you sell and move to cash, when would you buy back? Inability to answer this question reveals that the move is emotionally driven. Studies show that moving to cash consistently costs investors more than staying invested, and missing the best market days compounds losses permanently. A client story about someone who moved to cash against advice before a prior election illustrates the real cost.

### Risk Tolerance vs. Risk Capacity and Guardrail Approach [00:11:31]

Using a real client example—someone in retirement living off pension and Social Security who asked about reducing allocation—the hosts distinguish between emotional risk tolerance and objective risk capacity. If the portfolio won't need to be drawn down substantially for six or more years, reducing allocation primarily reflects anxiety rather than financial necessity. A guardrail-based income distribution system is referenced as a framework that provides predefined rules to avoid emotional decisions during market volatility.
