# Why You Should Convert Late (Even If It Delays Your Retirement)

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-03-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=oIDPg7SRxZg
**Duration:** 16:08

## Summary

This video challenges the conventional wisdom that retirees should convert to Roth IRA as soon as they stop working, arguing that many retirees carry pensions, rental income, dividends, or part-time income into their early retirement years that keep tax brackets elevated. Nick Davis contends that patient retirees who wait for a genuine income drop can convert at 12% rather than 22-24%, and must also account for Medicare IRMAA surcharges triggered by the IRS two-year look-back rule. The core message is that a personalized income map—tracking every income source year by year—is the only reliable basis for timing conversions.

## Key Takeaways

- The conventional 'gap year' conversion window assumes work stoppage equals income stoppage, which is false for retirees with pensions, rentals, or consulting income.
- Roth conversions stack on top of existing income, so a $50,000 conversion atop $80,000 of other income may face a 22-24% rate rather than 12%.
- The Medicare two-year look-back means a large conversion at 63 can raise Part B and Part D premiums at 65 via IRMAA surcharges, adding hundreds to thousands of dollars annually.
- Before converting, project future RMDs at ages 73-75 to determine whether conversions today actually lower lifetime taxes or merely prepay them.
- Conversion timing should be driven by a year-by-year income map, not generic rules of thumb; when no genuine bracket gap exists, the right answer is to wait.
- Small, controlled conversions sized to fill a specific bracket reliably outperform large one-time conversions made at the wrong time.

## Detailed Notes

### Why the Standard Gap-Year Advice Breaks Down [00:00:00]

Davis explains that pensions, taxable dividends, rental income, and part-time consulting all stack in early retirement years, preventing income from actually dropping when a paycheck stops. Retirees who assume their situation matches the textbook example and convert aggressively can end up paying 22-24% on money they expected to convert at 12%.

### How Stacking Makes Conversions Expensive [00:00:00]

A $50,000 conversion on top of $80,000 in other income does not receive 12% treatment; it is taxed at 22-24% depending on filing status. Davis emphasizes that the point of converting is to pay less tax, not the same or more, and that wrong-year conversions simply prepay tax at the wrong rate. He also notes that Social Security taxability and IRMAA further complicate the effective rate calculation.

### The IRMAA Hidden Cost [00:00:00]

Medicare premiums are based on income from two years prior; a large conversion at 63 appears in premiums at 65 as IRMAA surcharges on Part B and Part D. For married couples both spouses receive separate calculations, potentially doubling the surcharge. Davis argues that waiting for lower-income years can eliminate IRMAA exposure entirely, saving thousands per year over multiple years.

### RMD Projection and the Case for Waiting [00:00:00]

Davis recommends projecting required minimum distributions at ages 73-75 before converting anything, because many households stay in the same or lower brackets through their 70s as spending slows. If the projected RMD bracket is not materially higher than the current conversion rate, early aggressive conversion provides no advantage and merely accelerates the tax bill.

### Building a Timed Conversion Strategy [00:00:00]

The recommended framework is to map all income sources year by year, identify the years where taxable income genuinely drops, and convert to the top of the 12% or 22% bracket without spilling over. Social Security timing is a key coordination point because once benefits start they stack on all other income, typically closing the conversion window or narrowing it significantly.
