# I Have $4M in my 401(k). Here's My Tax Bill (and How I Avoid It)

**Channel:** Nick Davis, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Qu1CrVFnsiE
**Duration:** 21:12

## Summary

Nick Davis of Brindle and Bay presents a detailed breakdown of the tax exposure facing a retiree with $4 million entirely in a tax-deferred 401k. He explains how RMDs, IRMAA Medicare surcharges, and the taxation of Social Security stack to create a forced income stream of $200,000–$220,000 annually before any elective spending, resulting in a potential lifetime tax bill of $1.1–$1.5 million. The video then walks through four complementary strategies to restructure that exposure: converting to a three-tier account structure (traditional, Roth, and taxable brokerage), executing Roth conversions during the low-income window before RMDs begin, applying capital gains harvesting and sequencing from the brokerage tier, and using qualified charitable distributions to satisfy RMDs tax-free for charitably inclined retirees.

## Key Takeaways

- The IRS has an implicit claim on every pre-tax dollar in a 401k; the real balance available to a retiree is meaningfully less than the account statement shows.
- RMDs at age 73 on a $4 million balance force approximately $146,000 in income regardless of spending needs, which can cascade into higher tax brackets and IRMAA surcharges.
- A three-tier account structure (traditional, Roth, taxable brokerage) restores income flexibility that a single-bucket 401k permanently eliminates.
- The window between retirement and the onset of RMDs and Social Security is the most valuable tax-planning period because it typically offers the lowest taxable income in decades.
- Roth conversions during that window, ideally $200,000–$300,000 per year over 8–10 years, can reduce traditional balances enough to shrink future RMDs to manageable levels.
- QCDs let charitably inclined retirees satisfy RMD requirements without the income showing up on their tax return, directly lowering adjusted gross income and potentially dropping them into a lower IRMAA tier.

## Detailed Notes

### The Hidden Tax Liability in a $4M 401k [00:02:07]

Every dollar in a traditional 401k is pre-tax; none of it belongs fully to the account holder until taxes are paid. At $4 million, the IRS's RMD mechanism forces approximately $146,000 out in year one at age 73, and that percentage grows each subsequent year. Combined with Social Security income, a married couple could face $200,000–$220,000 in taxable income before making any voluntary withdrawal.

### How Taxes Stack: Brackets, IRMAA, and Social Security [00:04:45]

At $220,000 in combined income, a retiree enters the 24% federal bracket and potentially the 32% bracket depending on additional income. IRMAA Medicare surcharges add an extra $5,000–$10,000 or more per year. Up to 85% of Social Security income becomes taxable once income crosses relatively low thresholds. Over 25 years, an effective rate of 22–28% on forced income translates to $1.1–$1.5 million in lifetime taxes.

### The Three-Tier Account Structure [00:07:54]

Wealthy retirees manage income by spreading assets across three tax tiers: traditional pre-tax accounts (IRS-visible), Roth (tax-free, invisible to the IRS, no RMDs), and taxable brokerage (taxed at long-term capital gains rates). The ideal restructured split for a $4 million balance would be $600,000–$800,000 traditional, $1 million–$1.5 million Roth, and $800,000–$1.2 million in taxable brokerage, allowing year-by-year income sequencing decisions.

### Roth Conversion Window and Capital Gains Harvesting [00:10:34]

The years between retirement and the start of RMDs and Social Security represent the lowest-income window in decades. Converting $200,000–$300,000 per year to Roth at the 22–24% bracket is typically cheaper than paying higher forced rates later. Capital gains harvesting from the brokerage account can generate $100,000 or more in annual income while paying under $15,000 in taxes when income is kept below IRMAA thresholds and provisional income triggers.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions [00:16:29]

Once RMDs begin at age 73, a QCD allows up to $111,000 per person per year to flow directly from an IRA to a qualified charity. The distribution satisfies the RMD requirement but never appears on the tax return, reducing adjusted gross income directly—unlike a charitable deduction that only helps itemizers. This can eliminate an entire RMD from taxable income and potentially lower the retiree into a lower IRMAA tier.
