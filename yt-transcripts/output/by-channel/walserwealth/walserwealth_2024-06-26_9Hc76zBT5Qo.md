# 3 Financial Tips For Your 30s

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2024-06-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=9Hc76zBT5Qo
**Duration:** 1:31

## Summary

Shannon Stophel, a financial adviser at Walser Wealth, shares three essential financial tips for people navigating their 30s. She emphasizes the importance of opening and maximizing Roth IRA contributions for tax-free compounding growth, paying off high-interest debt using the Avalanche method, and establishing a sustainable budget. The advice aims to help younger adults build strong financial foundations and avoid lifestyle inflation.

## Key Takeaways

- Open and max out a Roth IRA if eligible, taking full advantage of the $7,000 annual contribution limit (or $11,000 for those 50+) to build tax-free retirement savings.
- Use the Avalanche debt repayment method: make minimum payments on all debt while directing extra money toward the highest-interest debt to minimize total interest paid.
- Create a realistic budget that dedicates at least 25% of income to savings and retirement, allowing you to live comfortably on 75% or less.
- Avoid lifestyle inflation and the temptation to purchase luxury items that would compromise your ability to save for retirement.
- Recognize that perfect budgeting systems are less important than starting early and maintaining consistent savings discipline.

## Detailed Notes

### Roth IRA Strategy for Tax-Free Growth [00:00:00]

Stophel recommends opening a Roth IRA in your 30s to maximize tax-free compounding growth. Emphasizes that individuals under 50 can contribute $7,000 annually in 2024, while those 50 and over qualify for $11,000 catch-up contributions. The key advantage is that contributions are made with after-tax dollars but grow tax-free.

### High-Interest Debt Elimination Using Avalanche Method [00:00:00]

Stophel presents the Avalanche method as an effective debt payoff strategy. The approach involves maintaining minimum payments on all debts while directing extra money toward the debt with the highest interest rate, thereby saving money in the long run and prioritizing the most expensive debt first.

### Practical Budgeting and Lifestyle Discipline [00:00:00]

Stophel advises against overcomplicating budgeting and instead recommends a simple rule: save at least 25% for future needs and retirement while living on 75% or less of income. She includes a bonus tip about avoiding keeping up with peers through unnecessary luxury purchases, noting that discretionary spending should not interfere with retirement account contributions.
