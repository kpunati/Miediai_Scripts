# Global Energy Crisis, Private Credit Bubble & A.I.

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2026-04-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=X4ZUBL9A6Sc
**Duration:** 43:49

## Summary

Rebecca Walser joins Paul Barron to analyze a Middle East conflict (Iranian war context, Strait of Hormuz blockage) that has triggered a global energy crisis, driving oil toward $86-89/barrel while December futures price in $75/barrel—creating a disconnect with the Fed's rate watch tool showing near-zero probability of cuts through mid-2027. Walser argues the confluence of stagflation risk, a $1.3-1.8 trillion private credit bubble, AI displacement of SaaS businesses, and stablecoin regulatory progress represents a convergence of multiple systemic risks not seen simultaneously before. She positions gold, stablecoin adoption, and selective digital assets as navigational tools while warning of significant destructive volatility in legacy equity sectors over the next 48-60 months.

## Key Takeaways

- The gap between December oil futures ($75/barrel) and current spot prices ($86-89) implies markets expect rapid conflict resolution that the Fed's own rate projections do not reflect.
- Payroll weakness combined with energy-driven inflation is creating early-stage stagflation, which historically requires accommodative policy but is difficult to execute with elevated inflation.
- Private credit at an estimated $1.3-1.8 trillion is similar in scale to pre-GFC MBS exposure; redemption restrictions at Apollo and Blackstone signal stress that could trigger broader financial contagion.
- AI is eroding the SaaS business model that has underpinned private credit growth, creating a potential chain reaction from software revenue erosion through credit losses to bank credit line draws.
- The Clarity Act's compromise on passive vs. active stablecoin yields is viewed as a temporary banking lobby concession; Walser argues user demand will ultimately force full yield on stablecoin holdings.
- Central banks have been liquidating precious metals to raise liquidity for energy costs, contributing to a three-part selloff in gold alongside profit-taking and fear-driven exits.
- Walser frames the current period as a 'convergence of all things'—energy, currency transition, AI, and credit restructuring—analogous to the dot-com and GFC shocks occurring simultaneously.

## Detailed Notes

### Fed Policy and the Energy-Stagflation Dilemma [00:03:32]

Powell's FOMC comments were interpreted by Walser as a deliberate political signal to the Trump administration, noting repeated references to tariffs and ambiguity about his post-DOJ inquiry tenure. With five of the last ten payroll readings negative and energy prices elevated, Walser characterizes the environment as early-stage stagflation. The incoming chair Worsh is assessed as hawkish overall, making near-term rate cuts unlikely despite Trump's stated preference for 300 basis points of cuts to ease short-end government refinancing.

### Oil Market Divergence: Spot vs. Futures [00:05:48]

WTI crude is trading around $86-89/barrel while December futures price in $75/barrel, a sharp backwardation Walser attributes either to insider knowledge of conflict resolution or to market manipulation. She notes Qatar Energy's main LNG plant, supplying 17% of global LNG, sustained damage requiring three to five years of repairs, and that Europe is particularly exposed due to decommissioned nuclear capacity and prior Russian sanctions cutting supply. Energy costs are triply bifurcated—cheapest in the US, mid-range in Europe, and highest in Asia.

### Precious Metals and Bitcoin During Energy Crisis [00:14:01]

Gold and silver sold off sharply despite typically benefiting from inflationary shocks. Walser attributes this to three concurrent forces: central banks liquidating metals to raise energy-crisis liquidity, profit-taking from January 2026 record highs, and fear-driven exits. Bitcoin, by contrast, rose approximately 7.7% since the Iranian conflict began (February 28th), benefiting from its speed and fungibility as a capital flight vehicle. Walser views Bitcoin's decoupling from both equities and metals as a maturing sign of its independent asset class status.

### Private Credit Contagion and AI Disruption of SaaS [00:25:10]

Barron argues AI is destroying the SaaS revenue model that has fed private credit markets, which Walser estimates at $1.3-1.8 trillion in the US—comparable to pre-GFC MBS exposure. Apollo limited redemptions to 5% against 11% requests; Blackstone's BCR fund saw its largest-ever single-quarter distribution of 8%. She warns that private credit funds drawing on bank credit lines during redemption events links non-public BDCs to the broader banking system, creating contagion risk. Financial derivatives are characterized as roughly 5x pre-GFC levels, amplifying systemic fragility.

### Stablecoin Regulation and Banking System Disruption [00:27:34]

Walser describes the Clarity Act's passive-vs-active yield compromise as a temporary banking lobby win that will not survive user demand for yield on stablecoin balances. She predicts stablecoins will become the dominant payment processing system within 18 months as mass dissemination reaches a tipping point, rendering traditional banks progressively irrelevant. Short-term capital flight into DeFi from centralized exchanges is expected but characterized as transitory, driven by strong-dollar dynamics rather than structural shift away from stablecoin.
