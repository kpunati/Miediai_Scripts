# W.H.O.'s Sneaky Plan To Take Away Your Freedom Is Already Working

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2024-07-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=r6eDXL2PBnA
**Duration:** 41:11

## Summary

In this episode of Crashes and Taxes, Rebecca Walser analyzes the June 2024 amendments to the WHO's International Health Regulations, explaining why she considers them a greater threat to sovereignty than the failed pandemic treaty. She walks through specific provisions requiring nations to adopt WHO guidelines, combat 'misinformation and disinformation,' fund WHO activities, and authenticate health documents. Walser frames these developments within a broader 'convergence of all things' thesis connecting global governance trends to financial, geopolitical, and biblical narratives.

## Key Takeaways

- The global pandemic treaty failed to pass, but IHR amendments passed by consensus at the 77th World Health Assembly in June 2024 with no member state objection.
- Amended IHR now require nations to develop 'core capacities' to counter misinformation/disinformation as defined by the WHO, and a monitoring/scoring system is being developed.
- Member nations must adjust domestic legislation to comply with new IHR, which Walser argues constitutes a meaningful erosion of national sovereignty.
- The WHO can now declare a pandemic emergency based on conditions that are only 'likely' to materialize, including events causing social, economic, or political disruption.
- Amended IHR commit the WHO to develop technical guidance for authenticating health documents (vaccine passports equivalent) in both digital and non-digital formats.
- Nations are required to maintain or increase domestic funding for WHO implementation, including between pandemics.
- Walser cites the FDA's court-ordered retraction of ivermectin social media posts as evidence that prior censorship targeted accurate information.

## Detailed Notes

### Background: Pandemic Treaty vs. IHR Amendments [00:05:18]

Walser explains that the Biden Administration wrote 13 amendments to the IHR, which had not been revised since 2005. Initial media coverage emphasized the failure of the full pandemic treaty, leading many observers to believe the sovereignty concerns had been avoided. She uses a Reuters article on the UK's refusal to sign the pandemic treaty as the pivot point.

### What the IHR Amendments Actually Passed [00:14:50]

The 77th World Health Assembly closed without adopting the pandemic agreement but passed wide-ranging IHR amendments by consensus. The new IHR significantly enhance WHO's standing and authority, including requirements for member states to address misinformation and disinformation as a core national capacity. Walser highlights that no country objected.

### Misinformation, Censorship, and Free Speech Provisions [00:15:55]

The amended IHR direct governments to engage stakeholders and take measures to counter misinformation and disinformation, with a monitoring system and scoring to be developed by the WHO. Walser argues this codifies the 2020-era censorship of experts like those behind the Great Barrington Declaration, whose positions were later vindicated by peer-reviewed studies.

### Sovereignty, Domestic Legislation, and Funding Obligations [00:25:24]

Article 4 bis requires nations to adjust domestic legislative and administrative arrangements to comply with the IHR. Nations must maintain or increase domestic funding for WHO implementation continuously between pandemics. Walser notes the WHO classifies China as a developing country, meaning US funding could flow there under these provisions.

### Expanded Pandemic Declaration Powers and Health Documents [00:32:48]

The amended definition of pandemic emergency includes events that are only 'likely' to spread and may involve political or economic disruption, not just health system overload. The IHR also commit the WHO to develop authentication standards for health documents (vaccine passports) in digital and non-digital form to prevent forgeries. Walser closes by warning that the bird flu situation, combined with gain-of-function research concerns, may trigger these new powers before the 2024 election.
