# A Deep Dive Into NVIDIA

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2025-06-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=48NJLHUANAg
**Duration:** 19:35

## Summary

Rebecca Walser of Walser Wealth Management appears on Market Overtime to analyze Nvidia's fiscal Q1 2026 earnings report, covering headline metrics (adjusted EPS of 81 cents, revenue of $44 billion), the $8 billion H20 chip write-off from US export controls, and the declining but still significant China revenue exposure (12.1% of total). Walser views Nvidia as dominant in the AI chip market with no near-term competitor able to replicate its GPU infrastructure moat, but cautions that macroeconomic global headwinds, China's IP circumvention capabilities, and investor overconfidence represent meaningful risks. The interview also discusses autonomous vehicle adoption as an underappreciated AI growth vector, the Mag 7's market concentration risk, and why Walser recommends diversifying into utilities and energy to benefit from AI's energy demand surge.

## Key Takeaways

- Nvidia beat earnings estimates despite the H20 export ban headwind, demonstrating revenue resilience through hyperscaler and Mag 7 demand.
- China's share of Nvidia revenue is declining (12.1% vs. 14-15% in prior quarters) as export controls tighten, but the company is pursuing Middle East deals and other markets to offset this.
- The Blackwell GPU line and the forthcoming Ultra Blackwell (which increases token throughput by 25x) represent the next major growth driver for Nvidia.
- Walser argues Nvidia has no credible near-term competitor — AMD cannot replicate Nvidia's infrastructure scale, and China's alternatives require massive investment and time.
- China's AI development ambitions (evidenced by DeepSeek) represent a strategic competitive threat that export controls may actually accelerate by forcing China to develop domestic alternatives.
- The entire Mag 7 concentration risk in US equity markets is problematic, but AI adoption will eventually lift utilities, energy, and other sectors beyond technology.
- Investors should avoid treating Nvidia as an infallible company; even strong companies face macro headwinds and competitive pressures that could disrupt the current growth trajectory.

## Detailed Notes

### Q1 FY2026 Earnings Overview and H20 Write-Off [00:00:06]

Nvidia reported adjusted EPS of 81 cents (down from 90+ cents due to the H20 write-off) on $44 billion in revenue. The H20 chip ban is an $8 billion write-off taken in two tranches; Walser views this as outside the company's control and sees investors correctly interpreting the underlying business as strong. China represented 12.1% of revenue, down from 14-15% in prior quarters.

### Hyperscaler Demand and Blackwell GPU Ramp [00:03:21]

Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, AWS) remain the dominant buyers of Nvidia chips, with data center revenue up more than 70% year-over-year. Nvidia's goal is 72,000 Blackwell GPUs produced weekly; the Ultra Blackwell, coming later in 2025, increases token throughput by 25x. Walser identifies autonomous vehicles (320 partnerships) as a significant underappreciated growth vector.

### China Risk and Export Controls [00:10:37]

Jensen Huang stated that US export controls effectively close China as a market and may accelerate China's domestic AI development, a view Walser largely agrees with. Walser characterizes China as a strategic IP threat that will pursue circumvention through any available means, including shell corporations and reverse engineering. She expresses support for Nvidia's position that controlled engagement with China would be preferable to an outright ban.

### Competitive Landscape and Macro Risks [00:07:46]

Walser argues Nvidia is 'one of one' with no near-term competitor able to match its GPU infrastructure investment; AMD cannot scale to Nvidia's level. However, global macroeconomic headwinds — de-dollarization trends, BRICS block expansion, China's Belt and Road initiative — could dampen overall economic activity and create headwinds for even dominant tech companies. She recommends investors also consider utilities and energy to benefit from AI's electricity demand growth.
