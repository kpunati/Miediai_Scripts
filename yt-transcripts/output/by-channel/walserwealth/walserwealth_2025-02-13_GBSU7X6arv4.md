# "We Are Approaching A Debt Bubble..."

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser

## Summary

This Fox Business 'Mornings with Maria' segment aired on February 12, 2025, and features a panel of financial commentators led by Rebecca Walser discussing the expanding role of DOGE in cutting federal spending, with Elon Musk and President Trump defending the initiative. The segment also covers a hot Q4 earnings season, automaker responses to new steel and aluminum tariffs, and breaks live with a hotter-than-expected January 2025 CPI report showing 3% year-over-year inflation. A significant portion is devoted to the bird flu-driven egg supply crisis, which pushed wholesale egg prices to all-time highs and contributed to the elevated CPI reading.

## Key Takeaways

- DOGE's expanded executive order requires agencies to hire only one employee for every four departures and evaluate which agencies can be eliminated or merged.
- Q4 2024 earnings growth came in near 13%, far exceeding pre-season estimates of 8%, with broad beat rates across most sectors.
- GM indicated it can absorb 30-50% of tariff impacts through existing supply contracts, while Ford's leadership characterized the situation as causing chaos.
- January CPI printed hotter than expected at 3.0% year-over-year, driven by shelter (contributing 30% of the increase), energy, gasoline, and a 53% spike in egg prices.
- Rebecca Walser warned the U.S. is approaching a debt bubble, noting that interest on the national debt now exceeds defense spending and is the largest budget line item after Social Security.
- Palantir was highlighted as expensive at 200x earnings for 20% growth compared to Nvidia at 30x earnings for 75% growth, though defense tech spending expectations support Palantir's valuation.
- Economists flagged that consumer pull-forward buying ahead of anticipated tariff-related price increases may be distorting near-term inflation readings.

## Detailed Notes

### DOGE Executive Order and Federal Spending Debate [00:00:00]

President Trump signed an executive order giving DOGE more power to cut costs, requiring agencies to limit hiring to one hire per four departures and evaluate eliminating non-statutory agency functions. Panelists praised the initiative's transparency goals while Democrats characterized it as a constitutional crisis. Rebecca Walser specifically called for public, C-SPAN-style live disclosure of spending findings.

### Q4 Earnings Season and Stock Market Outlook [00:16:40]

Ryan Payne reported Q4 earnings growth came in near 13% versus an 8% pre-season estimate, with a 6.7% beat rate over analyst consensus. Adam Johnson compared Nvidia and Palantir on a price-to-growth basis, arguing Nvidia offers far better value at 30x earnings for 75% growth versus Palantir at 200x for 20% growth. Payne and Johnson were bullish on deploying capital into equities, while Walser remained cautious, citing overvaluation concerns.

### Tariffs: GM vs. Ford Divergence [00:19:50]

Following Trump's executive order imposing 25% tariffs on steel and aluminum effective March 4, GM CEO Mary Barra stated the company could mitigate 30-50% of the tariff impact using existing long-term supply contracts, without additional capital deployment. Ford's Jim Farley described the situation as causing chaos. Panelists noted companies would eventually shift manufacturing to the U.S. to avoid tariffs, though political uncertainty around policy continuity post-Trump remains a concern.

### Live CPI Release: Hotter Than Expected [00:44:59]

The January 2025 CPI printed 0.5% month-over-month (vs. 0.3% expected) and 3.0% year-over-year (vs. 2.9% expected). Core CPI came in at 0.4% month-over-month and 3.3% year-over-year, both above estimates. Shelter rose 0.4% month-over-month and accounted for 30% of the overall increase. Economist Jake Oubina cautioned that tariff-related pull-forward consumer buying may be contributing to sticky near-term inflation, while Sam Stovall noted defensive sectors like energy, healthcare, and consumer staples have the lowest correlation with inflation increases.

### Egg Price Crisis and Bird Flu Impact [00:49:10]

A particularly deadly bird flu strain caused farmers to lose more than 46 million chickens since mid-October 2024, pushing the U.S. laying population to its lowest level since 2015. Wholesale egg prices hit an all-time high of $8.11 per dozen, up from $2.21 in October 2024, a 267% increase. Major retailers including Costco, Trader Joe's, Whole Foods, Kroger, and Safeway implemented purchase limits. Retail prices in New York City were reported at $10-$16 per dozen depending on egg type.
