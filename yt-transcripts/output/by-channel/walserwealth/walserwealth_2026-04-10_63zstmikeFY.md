# Divided Politics, CA Controversies & ICE Arrest

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2026-04-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=63zstmikeFY
**Duration:** 20:53

## Summary

Rebecca Walser joins Newsmax's National Report alongside host John Burnett to weigh in on three politically charged topics: Al Sharpton's opposition to celebrating America's 250th anniversary, California's expanding legal framework around conversion therapy and immigrant legal aid, and crimes committed by undocumented immigrants already subject to deportation orders. Walser frames each issue through a lens of fiscal conservatism and national unity, arguing that divisive identity politics and costly state programs undermine public safety and economic stability. The segment also covers California AB 2600, which would extend state-funded legal counsel to undocumented adults in deportation proceedings, and cases where immigrants with prior deportation orders went on to commit violent crimes.

## Key Takeaways

- Walser argues that political figures like Al Sharpton use racial division for financial gain rather than promoting national unity around America's 250th anniversary.
- The US Supreme Court's 8-1 ruling in Charles vs. Salazar found that therapist speech is constitutionally protected, complicating state bans on conversion therapy.
- California's AB 2600 would expand state-funded legal aid to undocumented adults in deportation proceedings starting in 2027, drawing criticism as fiscally irresponsible given the state's existing budget deficit.
- Walser and Burnett argue that releasing criminal undocumented immigrants back into communities represents a public safety failure, citing specific crimes committed by individuals already ordered deported.
- New York City's experience, including a reported $7 billion budget gap tied to immigrant-related costs, is presented as a cautionary lesson for California.

## Detailed Notes

### Political Division and America's 250th Anniversary [00:00:00]

The segment opens with commentary on Al Sharpton's call to not celebrate America's 250th birthday. Walser argues the historical timeline of slavery's abolition is often simplified for political purposes, noting the 13th Amendment as the actual abolition mechanism. She emphasizes that hundreds of thousands of Union soldiers died to end slavery, and questions why prominent figures continue to drive national division rather than unity.

### California's Conversion Therapy Legislation [00:05:41]

After the Supreme Court's 8-1 ruling in Charles vs. Salazar protecting therapist speech, California pivoted to imposing civil liability on therapists who provide conversion therapy, potentially for discussions from age 4 through 40. Walser frames this as a chilling effect on free speech and warns that such liability frameworks could be turned back on school programs promoting gender ideology, which receive public funding.

### California AB 2600: State-Funded Legal Aid for Immigrants [00:09:28]

AB 2600, introduced by Assemblywoman Mia Bonta, expands state-funded legal counsel to undocumented adults facing deportation starting in 2027, building on an existing law (AB 1261) that covered minors. Critics argue the bill uses taxpayer money to fight federal immigration enforcement and lacks provisions preventing funding for individuals with violent criminal records. Walser criticizes the measure as economically indefensible given California's existing budget deficit.

### Criminal Immigrants and ICE Enforcement [00:16:22]

The segment closes with discussion of crimes committed by undocumented immigrants who had already been ordered deported. Walser cites the kidnapping of a 4-year-old by an individual deported three times and argues that continuing to release such individuals endangers communities. Participants argue that Democrats focus disproportionately on immigrant rights while ignoring public safety implications, and call for stricter enforcement of existing deportation orders.
