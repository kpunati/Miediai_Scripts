# 3 Problems With New Taxes On Farmers

**Channel:** Rebecca Walser
**Published:** 2024-11-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=TIQjus6CFLs
**Duration:** 6:02

## Summary

In a discussion on the Cow Guy Close podcast, Rebecca Walser critiques emerging climate policies targeting agriculture, particularly proposed taxes on meat and inheritance taxes on farms announced at COP29 and in the UK. She presents three arguments against these policies: China's absence from climate agreements undermines their effectiveness, the scientific basis for food supply disruption is not proven, and she claims the actual goal is population reduction rather than environmental sustainability.

## Key Takeaways

- Without China's participation in climate agreements, taxing farmers and food production is ineffective since the world's largest polluter and manufacturer remains outside the framework.
- Climate policies lack scientific proof demonstrating they will achieve stated environmental goals, constituting 'junk science' according to critics.
- The inheritance tax on UK farms (20%) represents direct economic punishment of agricultural producers without demonstrated climate benefit.
- Historical parallels to Margaret Thatcher's approach to miners suggest governments may be targeting sector disruption rather than sustainable reform.
- Agricultural disruption through petrochemical phase-out could reduce global food supplies, which is presented as intentional rather than unintended consequence.

## Detailed Notes

### Climate Policy Proposals Against Agriculture [00:00:00]

The TAP Coalition advocated at COP29 for meat taxes. UK recently proposed 20% inheritance tax on farms, reversing traditional exemptions. A UK labor leader controversially suggested treating farmers like Margaret Thatcher treated miners in the 1970s, which resulted in riots and economic disruption.

### Problem One: China's Non-Participation [00:00:00]

Walser argues global climate policies fail without China, the largest polluter and manufacturer, on board. Punishing rest of world while China remains unbound undermines policy rationale. Claims this creates 'Titanic situation' where developed nations suffer while China continues.

### Problem Two: Lack of Scientific Proof [00:00:00]

Walser criticizes 'trust the science' messaging without showing actual scientific data. Claims policies require complete disruption of global food supplies without proven climate benefit. Describes this as 'gaslighting' by scientific communities refusing to provide supporting evidence.

### Problem Three: Population Reduction Agenda [00:00:00]

Walser alleges climate groups publicly state earth's ideal population is 2 billion or less, requiring reduction from current 8 billion. Claims all health directives, WHO policies, UN food policies, and climate measures are actually geared toward 'population destruction' rather than sustainability.

### Lack of Rationality in Policy Design [00:00:00]

Walser compares climate policy to totalitarianism and communism, arguing proponents proceed 'like sheep' without scientific proof or actual plans. Notes moral inconsistency: Margaret Thatcher's mining confrontation addressed real economic disruption, while proposed climate policies would cause disruption for unproven benefit.

### Virtue Signaling vs. Practical Solutions [00:00:00]

Shellady notes policies designed to 'look good' and 'feel good' without homework or evidence. Walser counters that such policies appear virtuous but lack substantive backing, becoming 'virtue signals' that make promoters feel moral without actual results.

### Agricultural Disruption and Population Dynamics [00:00:00]

Walser traces population growth to introduction of petrochemical-based commercial industrial agriculture. Claims current policies 'reverse engineer' this process to destroy food supplies and reduce population. Calls for transparency about actual intentions behind food policy disruption.
