# Dimensional (DFA) vs. Vanguard

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-03-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=JfknibBat2A
**Duration:** 16:07

## Summary

Ben Felix traces the parallel histories of Vanguard and Dimensional Fund Advisors back to the 1964 Wells Fargo quantitative research group and explains how academic finance—particularly the Fama-French factor models—provides the theoretical basis for Dimensional's factor tilts toward small-cap, value, and profitable stocks. He then reviews matched live fund comparisons going back 25 or more years and finds that most Dimensional funds have outperformed comparable Vanguard index funds, with stronger results in international and emerging markets than in the US in recent history. Felix acknowledges the real pain of multi-year factor underperformance and concludes that Dimensional is worth considering only if one has already decided to hire a financial adviser.

## Key Takeaways

- Vanguard and Dimensional share intellectual roots in the 1964 Wells Fargo quantitative research group that pioneered index investing.
- Dimensional is not technically an index fund; it uses flexible trading around factor criteria rather than rigid index replication, aiming to capture size, value, and profitability premiums.
- The Fama-French five-factor model explains roughly 95% of differences in returns between diversified portfolios, making factor exposure the primary driver of long-run performance differences.
- Most matched Dimensional funds have outperformed comparable Vanguard funds over their full histories, but factor tilts can produce multi-year underperformance as seen in US small-cap value over the prior decade.
- International and emerging markets factor premiums have remained stronger in recent decades than US factor premiums, so Dimensional's relative advantage has been more evident outside the US.
- Paying an adviser fee solely for access to Dimensional funds is not justified; but if hiring an adviser anyway, preferring one that uses Dimensional signals evidence-based commitment.

## Detailed Notes

### Shared Origins: Wells Fargo to Vanguard and Dimensional [00:01:03]

Mac McQuown assembled a group at Wells Fargo in 1964 that created the first index fund—deployed for the Samsonite pension in 1971—and eventually gave the research freely to John Bogle, enabling Vanguard's 1976 retail index fund launch. David Booth, a member of the same group, founded Dimensional in 1981 to offer a small-cap fund to institutional investors, starting with a more flexible product that resembles but is not technically an index fund.

### Factor Premiums and Fama-French Models [00:04:11]

Academic finance identified size, value, and profitability premiums beyond the market risk premium. Fama and French's 1992 paper introduced the three-factor model and their 2015 and 2017 papers extended it to five factors (market, size, value, profitability, investment) covering international markets. This model explains about 95% of return differences between diversified portfolios, meaning most apparent 'alpha' from active managers is really uncompensated factor exposure.

### Live Fund Performance Comparison [00:09:23]

Felix reviews matched Dimensional vs. Vanguard fund pairs with at least 25 years of history. Results are mixed in the US: the DFA US Micro Cap and US Small Cap portfolios lag the Vanguard 500 but beat Vanguard's small-cap index funds. The DFA US Small Cap Value, Large Cap International, International Value, International Small Cap Value, and Emerging Markets funds all outperform comparable Vanguard funds. Recent US underperformance reflects the large-cap growth dominance of the past decade.

### Risk of Factor Investing and Conclusion [00:12:30]

Felix explicitly warns that Dimensional funds will diverge from the market when factor premiums underperform, citing the current 10-year US small-cap value lag of 4.5 points annualized and a similar 6.3-point lag from 1993 to 2000. Despite those painful stretches, the DFA US Small Cap Value fund has beaten the Vanguard 500 over its full lifetime. Dimensional ETFs became available to US retail investors starting in 2020; in Canada most access remains through advisers.
