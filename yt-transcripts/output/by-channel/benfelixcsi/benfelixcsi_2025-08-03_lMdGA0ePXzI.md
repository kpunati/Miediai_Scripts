# Comparing U.S. Equity ETFs: VTI vs. DFUS

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-08-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lMdGA0ePXzI
**Duration:** 14:42

## Summary

Ben Felix examines DFUS and VTI in detail to explain why non-indexed total market funds can offer structural advantages over index-tracking funds. The core finding, drawn from a 2025 academic paper, is that total market indexes suffer a roughly 60 basis point annual drag because their rebalancing trades are adversely timed relative to companies' own stock issuance and buyback behavior. DFUS avoids this by not tracking an index, delaying IPO purchases, and excluding categories of small cap stocks with historically poor expected returns. Felix also addresses counterarguments including the REIT exclusion and the fund's pre-ETF underperformance history, concluding that while the evidence is compelling, tracking error relative to familiar indexes remains a real psychological and practical barrier for many investors.

## Key Takeaways

- Index funds are great investments but are not perfect; they incur hidden costs from mechanically following rebalancing rules tied to share issuance and buyback timing.
- The 2025 paper on index rebalancing estimates a 60 basis point annual drag on total market indexes from adverse selection on market composition changes.
- DFUS avoids most of this cost by not tracking an index, delaying IPO buys, and excluding low-expected-return small cap growth stocks.
- The REIT exclusion in DFUS explains some but not all of the post-2021 performance difference; after adjusting for REIT exposure the excess return remains approximately 60 basis points.
- DFUS's pre-ETF predecessor fund DTMEX underperformed VTI but was materially different in strategy and had a significantly higher expense ratio for most of that period.
- Investors who cannot tolerate tracking error relative to well-known indexes may be better served by standard index funds despite the theoretical structural advantage of DFUS.

## Detailed Notes

### Index Rebalancing and Adverse Selection [00:03:00]

Total market indexes rebalance quarterly, buying newly issued shares and IPOs and selling during buybacks. A 2025 paper finds this timing aligns with companies believing their stock is overpriced when issuing and underpriced when buying back, creating an implicit performance drag estimated at 60 basis points annually. Lazy indexes that delay incorporating these changes improve returns by 40-60 basis points per year.

### DFUS Design Choices and Exclusions [00:06:17]

DFUS avoids adverse selection by not tracking any index and delaying IPO purchases. It explicitly excludes small cap growth stocks with weak profitability, aggressive investment, or high securities lending fees—categories known for poor returns and sometimes called 'small crap growth.' It also excludes REITs, which is a design choice by Dimensional rather than evidence-based, allowing investors to add REIT exposure separately via a dedicated ETF.

### Market Coverage and REIT Adjustment [00:07:14]

Despite holding roughly 1,100 fewer stocks than VTI, DFUS's excluded stocks represent only about 3.6% of Russell 3000 market cap weight, of which approximately 2.6 percentage points is REITs. After constructing a model portfolio that adds VNQ at REIT-weight, DFUS plus VNQ still shows substantial excess returns over VTI, attributable to trading flexibility and adverse selection avoidance rather than the REIT exclusion alone.

### Pre-ETF History and Tracking Error Considerations [00:10:21]

DTMEX, the predecessor mutual fund, had a different strategy and much higher expense ratios than DFUS, making pre-ETF performance comparisons unreliable. Felix acknowledges that the post-2021 outperformance history is short and that DFUS could underperform VTI in some future periods. He cautions that investors who cannot psychologically tolerate tracking error relative to mainstream indexes probably should not use a non-indexed strategy regardless of its theoretical merits.
