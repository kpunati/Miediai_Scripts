# The Problem With Private Credit

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2024-07-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=DQAr1DeDIro
**Duration:** 8:58

## Summary

Ben Felix provides a comprehensive critique of private credit funds as a retail investment product. While these non-bank lending vehicles are promoted for high yields and stable returns, Felix reveals that subjective valuations, opaque fee structures, and illiquidity mask substantial equity-like risks. Research shows that apparent outperformance evaporates when assets are properly priced by markets, and investors bear risks equivalent to public small-cap stocks while paying high fees that absorb all excess returns generated by skilled managers.

## Key Takeaways

- Private credit funds are non-publicly traded loans from private credit funds and business development companies, structured to appear bond-like but with embedded equity components like warrants.
- Private credit valuations are subjective and not marked-to-market daily, causing reported returns to appear smoother and less risky than comparable public assets.
- When BDCs (which invest 70%+ in private credit) are properly benchmarked against equity and debt indices, they show no statistically significant excess returns after fees, suggesting returns equal public small-cap and leverage loan benchmarks.
- Fee structures averaging 3-4% total (1.5% base plus 15-20% performance fees) capture all excess returns from manager skill, leaving investors with returns matching the underlying risk without compensation.
- Private credit funds frequently gate redemptions and halt distributions during market stress, as demonstrated by Canadian fund failures when loan defaults spiked during rising interest rate periods.
- Retail investors lack tools to evaluate private credit risks and often get blindsided by illiquidity and losses, making high-fee promotion of these products as 'safe and stable' problematic.

## Detailed Notes

### Private Credit Market Overview and Fee Structure [00:00:00]

Private credit funds consist of non-publicly traded loans to private firms typically unable to obtain bank financing. Key growth drivers include investor demand for floating-rate yields during low-rate periods and banks' tightened capital requirements pushing SMEs to alternative lending. Typical fee structure is 1.5% management fee plus 15-20% performance fee, totaling approximately 3-4% annually. Loans contain equity features like warrants and higher risk premiums, making them equity-like despite bond classification.

### Valuation Opacity and Reported Performance Bias [00:02:06]

Private loans lack daily mark-to-market pricing and secondary markets, so valuations are subjective. Different funds assign materially different values to identical loans. When marked down, adjustments are muted compared to public assets. This creates illusion of smooth returns and lower risk; smooth reported returns and generous valuations make private credit appear more attractive than actual risk profile warrants.

### Academic Research: BDCs as Private Credit Proxies [00:03:10]

2023 Financial Analyst Journal study of Business Development Companies (publicly traded direct lenders required to invest 70%+ in non-public US equity and debt) found outperformance of 2.74 percentage points per year based on reported net asset values. However, BDCs frequently trade at discounts to reported NAV during market stress, suggesting valuations are inflated. When evaluated at market prices rather than reported NAV, BDCs underperform liquid benchmarks of small-cap value stocks and leverage loans, indicating apparent outperformance is a valuation illusion.

### Risk-Adjusted Returns Analysis and Fee Impact [00:05:16]

2024 working paper applying cash flow-based methods to private debt fund portfolios found insignificant abnormal returns when properly benchmarked against equity and debt indices. However, removing equity benchmarks makes private credit appear attractive, confirming that private credit's returns are equivalent to public small-cap and leverage loan exposure. Gross of fees, funds have significant excess returns, but managers capture all benefits through 3-4% fee structures, leaving net investor returns indistinguishable from what they could earn on cheaper, liquid public alternatives.

### Illiquidity Risks and Recent Fund Failures [00:07:22]

Private credit funds are structurally illiquid and prone to gate redemptions when investor demand is highest. Recent examples in Canada: Romson Investment Corp, a large private lender, cut distributions multiple times and froze redemptions dealing with loan defaults. Ninepoint Partners LP stopped cash distributions and skipped redemption windows due to insufficient cash. These failures stem from concentration in high-risk borrowers who struggle when interest rates rise, illustrating that perceived stability is illusory.

### Investment Recommendation and Conclusion [00:08:24]

For most retail investors, private credit allocation results in high fees, confusion about actual risk exposure, and illiquidity without compensating excess risk-adjusted returns. While managers demonstrate skill in loan selection and monitoring, they extract all excess returns as fees. Investors are better served by transparent, liquid public market alternatives offering similar risk profiles at lower cost. Only suitable for sophisticated investors fully aware of risks and comfortable with illiquidity.
