# Was I Wrong About Covered Calls?

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-10-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=xzDFbv_JSks
**Duration:** 16:00

## Summary

Ben Felix revisits covered call ETFs after observing heavy promotion of these products at a retail investor conference. The video presents performance data for multiple covered call funds versus their underlying equity benchmarks — both on a total return basis (with distributions reinvested) and through a withdrawal analysis — consistently showing that covered call strategies underperform. Felix argues this is not a coincidence but the expected result of a structure that caps upside returns without meaningfully reducing downside risk, and that investors seeking income are no better served by covered calls than by holding equities and selling shares.

## Key Takeaways

- Covered call funds cap upside returns when options are exercised but leave downside risk nearly unchanged, resulting in an asymmetric reduction in expected total returns.
- Distribution yields from covered call ETFs are misleading; when distributions are reinvested, these funds still significantly underperform their underlying equity benchmarks.
- In a withdrawal analysis with identical monthly spending, investors in underlying equity funds had on average 26% more capital remaining after 10 years than those in covered call funds.
- The implied cost of covered call strategies — modeled as the break-even fee versus simply owning the underlying — ranges from 1.5% to 2.7% per year depending on the fund.
- Enhanced (leveraged) covered call funds outperform plain covered call funds when markets are positive but still underperform simply leveraging the underlying equity exposure.
- A significant portion of content promoting covered call ETFs is sponsored by the companies selling those ETFs, creating a conflict of interest investors should factor into their research.

## Detailed Notes

### How Covered Calls Work and Why the Structure Is Flawed [00:01:02]

Selling a call option caps an investor's upside above the strike price while leaving full downside exposure. A fund distributing option premiums creates the appearance of income, but the premium is insufficient to offset the lost upside when markets rise. The strategy also removes mean-reversion benefits that make stocks less risky over long horizons.

### Live Fund Performance: Total Return Comparison [00:04:27]

With distributions reinvested, the Global X S&P 500 covered call ETF underperformed the iShares Core S&P 500 ETF by 3.15 annualized percentage points since 2014; the Global X S&P/TSX 60 covered call ETF trailed by 3.81 points since 2011; and the BMO covered call Canadian banks ETF trailed the BMO equal weight banks ETF by 2.86 points since 2011. Across a 20-fund Canadian sample, average underperformance was 3.25% annualized.

### Withdrawal Analysis: Income Investors Also Lose [00:05:29]

Felix constructs a head-to-head comparison where both a covered call investor and an equity investor spend the exact same monthly dollar amount — the covered call investor from distributions, the equity investor from dividends plus share sales. After 10 years, the equity investor had on average 26% more capital remaining. The implied break-even fee equivalent to the covered call drag ranged from 1.5% to 2.7% per year. Alternatively, holding 19%–36% cash alongside equities would produce similar wealth outcomes as the covered call funds.

### Enhanced Covered Call Funds and Conflicts of Interest [00:11:14]

Leveraged versions of covered call ETFs outperform plain covered call funds in rising markets but still underperform simply applying leverage to the underlying equity without writing calls. Felix also notes that much promotional content for covered call ETFs is sponsored by ETF issuers, a disclosed but still relevant conflict of interest; PWL Capital itself has no financial incentive related to whether clients use covered call products.
