# I (almost) got scammed so you don't have to

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-07-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=gJLZRnfstpM
**Duration:** 15:10

## Summary

Ben Felix uses his experience of being impersonated in multiple scam formats to educate viewers on how investment fraud operates and how to recognize it. Drawing on academic research on fraud susceptibility and behavioral finance concepts, he describes three scam variants—Telegram/WhatsApp pump-and-dump groups, email impersonation campaigns targeting his YouTube audience, and YouTube comment section pig-butchering schemes. Felix decodes a scammer's PDF pitch, explaining concepts like financial bullshit, guaranteed-return claims, and fake insurance provisions. He concludes with actionable verification steps and the Canadian regulatory innovation of the trusted contact person.

## Key Takeaways

- Investment group scams on Telegram and WhatsApp use impersonated identities and pump-and-dump mechanics to profit at followers' expense by inflating illiquid stocks.
- Email impersonation scams target engaged YouTube viewers and leverage source credibility by mimicking trusted creators.
- YouTube comment pig-butchering scams use fake financial advisors with AI-generated, contextually relevant comments, fake credential websites, and staged profit illusions before eventually stealing deposited funds.
- Scammers deliberately exploit documented psychological biases including optimism bias, present bias, social proof, halo effect, and regret aversion.
- Guaranteed returns of 5-10% per month are implausible by any financial market standard; the best-performing hedge fund in history averaged roughly 40% annually after fees.
- Legitimate financial advisors will not contact clients through WhatsApp, Telegram, or personal Gmail accounts; verifying advisor registration through official regulatory sites is essential but not always sufficient.
- Canada's trusted contact person requirement for licensed advisors provides a regulatory safeguard against financial exploitation of clients.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Scammers Impersonate Ben Felix and the Psychology They Exploit [00:00:00]

Scammers target credible financial educators to exploit source credibility, using the existing trust relationship with an audience to bypass skepticism. Beyond credibility, scammers leverage social proof through staged comments and fake testimonials, optimism bias, present bias toward immediate gains, regret aversion about missing opportunities, and representativeness—drawing conclusions from cherry-picked data points. Academic research from 2014 shows that education about the specific scam being pitched significantly reduces susceptibility.

### Investment Group Scams on Telegram and WhatsApp [00:03:31]

Scammers create groups using the name and photo of credible figures; Felix joined one with 90 members, found an admin impersonating him, and was directed to buy a specific small illiquid stock. The mechanics are a classic pump-and-dump: group members buy on recommendation, the scammer sells their holdings at the inflated price, and the stock declines. Felix notes the scammer was polished, used a local phone number, and offered the firm's official website as verification.

### Email Impersonation and YouTube Comment Pig-Butchering Scams [00:05:58]

Email impersonation targets commenters on Felix's videos, posing as a grateful outreach to share investment opportunities. Pig-butchering scams in YouTube comments use AI-tailored opening statements, fake financial advisor identities with professionally designed but fraudulent websites, and names that often do appear in real securities registration databases—limiting the effectiveness of the standard registration check. The full scam sequence is: build trust, show fake profits in a fraudulent trading account, encourage increasing deposits, then disappear when withdrawal is attempted.

### Anatomy of the Scammer's PDF: Red Flags to Recognize [00:09:06]

Felix reviewed a scammer pitch PDF containing financial bullshit—impressive-sounding but meaningless assertions—accompanied by stock charts going up. The PDF promised guaranteed monthly returns of 5-10%, implicitly 80%+ annualized, compared to the Medallion Fund's ~40% post-fee record. Claims of an insurance mechanism covering any shortfall in promised monthly returns are fabricated. The document lists a range of unrelated services reminiscent of a fraudulent everything-bundled pitch.

### Verification Steps and the Trusted Contact Person [00:13:48]

Checking registration on regulatory websites (areytheyregistered.ca in Canada) is important but insufficient alone since scammers often use real registered names. A more reliable test is whether the advisor contacts you through verified firm channels, not personal messaging apps or Gmail. In Canada, licensed portfolio managers are required to ask clients to designate a trusted contact person—someone who can be contacted if the advisor suspects financial exploitation—a valuable safeguard for vulnerable clients.
