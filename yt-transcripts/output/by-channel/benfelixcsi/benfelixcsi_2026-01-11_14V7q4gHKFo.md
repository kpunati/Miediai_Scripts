# The Rise of ETF Slop

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2026-01-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=14V7q4gHKFo
**Duration:** 18:50

## Summary

Ben Felix coins the term 'ETF slop' to describe a wave of complex, high-fee ETFs that exploit investor behavioral biases without improving long-term outcomes. He examines four categories — thematic ETFs, buffer ETFs, covered call ETFs, and single-stock ETFs — and shows through academic research and live data that each category systematically underperforms simple, low-cost alternatives. The video frames this proliferation as a marketing triumph that reverses the investor-friendly gains achieved by the index fund revolution, echoing warnings John Bogle made in 2015.

## Key Takeaways

- There are now more ETFs than individual US stocks and more actively managed ETFs than index ETFs, with over 1,000 US launches in 2025 alone.
- Thematic ETFs launch after their themes have risen and go on to underperform broad markets by 6% per year on average in the five years post-launch.
- Buffer ETFs cater to loss aversion but a 2025 paper shows simple stock-plus-cash combinations outperform them even in drawdowns.
- Covered call ETFs market high distribution yields but mechanically trail their underlying equities in total return, making them inferior even for income-seeking investors.
- Leveraged single-stock ETFs underperform a frictionless leverage benchmark by over 9 percentage points per year on average, with a non-trivial probability of total loss.
- Complexity in investment products is consistently associated with poor performance; simple low-cost products remain the best tools for most investors.

## Detailed Notes

### The Rise of ETF Slop: Context and Definition [00:00:00]

Felix introduces 'ETF slop' as an analogy to low-quality AI-generated digital content, describing the proliferation of complex, high-fee ETFs engineered to attract assets rather than improve investor outcomes. The ETF structure is neutral, but it is now being used to package strategies that exploit attentional bias, optimism bias, and loss aversion. John Bogle flagged this risk as early as 2015.

### Thematic ETFs [00:04:47]

Thematic ETFs launch after themes have delivered strong returns, when investor excitement is highest, and then underperform by 6% per year on average in the five years following launch according to a 2021 study. Morningstar's 2025 report shows only about 10% of global thematic funds outperform at the 10-year horizon, with Canadian funds showing a 100% failure or closure rate at 10 years. Cannabis-themed funds in Canada collapsed from 60% of the Canadian thematic market to 1.4%.

### Buffer and Covered Call ETFs [00:08:10]

Buffer ETFs offer capped upside with partial downside protection using options, catering to loss aversion. A 2025 paper shows they have inconsistent downside protection and that simple stock-plus-cash mixtures outperform them even in drawdowns. Covered call ETFs sell call options to generate high distribution yields, but mechanically cap upside; Felix's own tests and the literature confirm they trail the underlying equities in total return and leave income-seeking investors worse off than a combination of dividends and portfolio sales.

### Single-Stock and Leveraged ETFs [00:14:00]

Single-stock ETFs combine leverage and/or covered calls on individual stocks, with many expense ratios above 1% and hidden financing costs baked into swap contracts. A 2025 paper finds long leveraged single-stock ETFs underperform by 0.79% per month on average; inverse funds underperform by 1.01% per month. Simulations going back to 1974 show that over 60% of hypothetical 3x leveraged single-stock funds underperform the unleveraged total market at the one-year horizon, and 56% have negative absolute returns. The SEC issued a formal warning to investors about these products.
