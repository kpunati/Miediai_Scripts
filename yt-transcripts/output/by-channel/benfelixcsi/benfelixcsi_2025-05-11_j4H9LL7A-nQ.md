# Renting vs. Buying a Home: What People Get Wrong

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-05-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=j4H9LL7A-nQ
**Duration:** 18:52

## Summary

Ben Felix systematically dismantles four widely held beliefs about home ownership using Canadian and global academic data. He demonstrates that salience bias causes people to overestimate housing returns by ignoring ongoing maintenance, depreciation, and opportunity costs, and that when all costs are properly accounted for, total housing costs closely approximate rent. Model-based comparisons show that disciplined renters who invest the cash flow difference between renting and owning in stocks can accumulate comparable or greater wealth than homeowners over long horizons, though behavioral factors like forced savings and tax treatment make owning advantageous for many people.

## Key Takeaways

- Real inflation-adjusted housing returns globally average just over 1% per year since 1870, far below equities.
- Annual maintenance, depreciation, and renovation costs of 2–3% of home value dramatically reduce the apparent investment return of homes.
- When ongoing costs plus a 3% opportunity cost are included, rent and ownership costs converge to approximately 5% of home value per year.
- A 50-year model shows a cash-buying homeowner's counterfactual stock-investing renter accumulates $2.2 million more wealth.
- Paying off a mortgage counterintuitively raises housing costs because the opportunity cost of home equity exceeds the cost of mortgage debt.
- Owning a home can make sense due to tax-free capital gains, hedging against local housing costs, behavioral commitment benefits, and availability of certain property types.
- Successful financial renting requires extreme discipline — saving and investing the full cash flow cost difference — which most people will not sustain.

## Detailed Notes

### Homes as Investments: Salience Bias and True Returns [00:00:32]

Felix argues that homeowners systematically overestimate investment returns by focusing only on purchase and sale prices. Using a real Vancouver example — $51,900 in 1974 to $1.65M in 2024 — the 7.16% compound return looks impressive but trails the MSCI World (10.2%) and TSX (9.44%). Removing 2% annual maintenance costs drops the return to approximately 5.16%, and global data shows real housing returns average just over 1% since 1870.

### Total Cost of Ownership and Rent-Own Parity [00:04:34]

Academic research and Statistics Canada data support annual costs of 2–3% of home value for maintenance, depreciation, and renovations. Adding a 3% opportunity cost (stocks vs. home equity) implies a total ownership cost of roughly 5% of home value per year. Felix shows this model closely predicts Canadian rents: the implied monthly ownership cost of the average $512,300 apartment is $2,134 vs. an average rent of $2,119.

### Mortgage Debt and the Wealth Comparison [00:09:01]

In a cash-purchase scenario, the renter invests the full home price in stocks and ends up $2.2M ahead after 50 years. When the owner uses a 20% down payment mortgage, the gap narrows to $467,000 in favor of the renter, because the mortgage's high cash flow costs allow the renter to save and invest the difference. Mortgage debt helps the owner build equity but does not overcome the higher expected returns of a stock portfolio.

### When Owning Makes Sense [00:14:54]

Valid arguments for ownership include: Canadian primary residence capital gains exemption (especially valuable for taxable investors who have maxed registered accounts); the hedge property against future housing cost increases in a specific location; unavailability of certain property types for rent; and the behavioral benefit of forced savings through mortgage payments. For disciplined, high-income investors with maxed registered accounts, ownership becomes more attractive due to the tax disadvantage of taxable investing.
