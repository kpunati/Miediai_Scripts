# 20 Years of Renting vs. Buying a Home in Canada (2005 to 2024)

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-06-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lBG-g1CKfgs
**Duration:** 21:11

## Summary

Ben Felix, CIO of PWL Capital, assembles 20 years of data on apartment prices, rents, mortgage rates, property taxes, and maintenance costs for 12 Canadian cities to rigorously compare the wealth outcomes of renting and investing versus buying and building equity from January 2005 through December 2024. The model assumes a disciplined renter invests any cash flow saved versus the cost of ownership in a low-cost stock index portfolio (30% Canadian, 70% global, 0.25% fee). Key findings include renters outperforming in 7 of 12 cities over the full period and in 71% of 5-year sub-periods, with an average renter-to-owner wealth ratio of 1.17. The analysis includes sensitivity testing and a detailed walkthrough of assumptions around maintenance costs, mortgage financing, and transaction costs.

## Key Takeaways

- The narrative that homeownership has been clearly superior in Canada over the last 20 years is not supported by the data when renting is paired with disciplined investing.
- Renters came out ahead in 7 of 12 cities over the full 20-year period and in 71% of all 5-year sub-periods studied.
- The stock market's 8.62% annualized return over the period meaningfully outpaced the 5.11% average annualized price appreciation of Canadian apartments.
- Outcomes vary significantly by city and time period, with Calgary, Edmonton, Vancouver, Kitchener-Waterloo, and Victoria all favoring ownership over the full period.
- Forced savings through mortgage payments is a genuine behavioral advantage for homeowners; a renter who spends instead of invests the cost difference will likely fare worse.
- High investment fees (1% instead of 0.25%) or a failure to invest 100% of the cash flow difference materially erode the renter's wealth advantage, suggesting the edge is conditional on investor discipline.

## Detailed Notes

### Methodology and Model Setup [00:03:49]

The model places a hypothetical investor in January 2005 with enough cash for a 20% down payment and closing costs, choosing between buying an apartment or renting and investing in stocks. The renter's portfolio holds 30% Canadian equity and 70% global equity index at 0.25% annual fees, similar to BEQT. The monthly cash flow difference between owning and renting is invested or withdrawn from the renter's portfolio. Rent data come from CMHC; home prices from the MLS HPI apartment benchmark. The model applies 1% annual depreciation and has the owner spend one-third of rent on maintenance and repairs.

### Full-Period Results by City [00:09:16]

Over the full 20-year period, renters outperformed in Toronto (1.05), Montreal (1.48), Ottawa (1.97), Winnipeg (above 1), Quebec City (1.14), Hamilton (1.12), and Halifax (1.26). Owners outperformed in Vancouver (0.71), Kitchener-Waterloo (0.71), Victoria (0.85), Calgary (0.37), and Edmonton (0.39). The average renter-to-owner wealth ratio was 1.17. Ottawa stood out with a 1.97 ratio due to modest real estate price growth and low rents relative to ownership costs.

### 5-Year Sub-Period Analysis [00:12:16]

Examining four non-overlapping 5-year windows reveals more nuance. The 2005–2009 period generally favored ownership. The 2010–2014 period favored renting almost everywhere due to low real estate returns averaging 2.43% annualized. The 2020–2024 period favored renting almost universally because real estate prices fell in many cities while the stock market soared. Across all 48 sub-periods, renting wins 71% of the time.

### Sensitivity Analysis and Behavioral Caveats [00:19:45]

Raising investment fees from 0.25% to 1% (common for active funds) drops the average renter-to-owner wealth ratio from 1.17 to approximately 1.02. If the renter only invests 80% of the cash flow cost difference rather than 100%, the wealth ratio drops further. These sensitivities suggest that while the data favor disciplined renters, the behavioral challenge of maintaining discipline through market volatility may make homeownership the practical superior choice for many Canadians.
