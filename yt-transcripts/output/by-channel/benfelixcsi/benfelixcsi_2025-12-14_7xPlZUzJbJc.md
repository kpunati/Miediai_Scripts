# The "AI Bubble"

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-12-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=7xPlZUzJbJc
**Duration:** 17:57

## Summary

Ben Felix investigates the claim that the US stock market is in an AI bubble by examining two features: high market concentration (top 7 stocks at 36% of the S&P 500, the highest since 1927) and elevated valuations approaching 1999 dot-com levels. Drawing on historical cases—Nortel/Canada, the US dot-com period, and Japan's 1989 crash—he shows that concentration alone has a weak and statistically insignificant relationship with future returns, while high valuations have a somewhat stronger but still imperfect predictive relationship. The core recommendation is global diversification and maintaining exposure to value and small-cap value stocks, which have historically outperformed during market downturns driven by concentrated growth stocks.

## Key Takeaways

- US market concentration at 36% of the S&P 500 in just 7 stocks is historically unprecedented since 1927, but high concentration does not reliably predict poor future returns.
- Market valuations (CAPE ratio) have a stronger relationship with future 10-year returns than concentration, with higher starting valuations associated with lower subsequent returns.
- The Canadian market recovered from extreme Nortel-driven concentration (36%) faster than the less-concentrated US market recovered from its dot-com bust.
- Value stocks and small-cap value stocks in Canada, the US, and Japan all delivered positive returns during periods when their overall markets suffered, illustrating the benefits of factor diversification.
- Japan's stock market has not recovered in real terms from its 1989 crash, underscoring the tail risk of extreme valuations without global diversification.
- AI-related stocks drove 75% of S&P 500 returns and 90% of capital spending growth since ChatGPT's launch, per JP Morgan, supporting concerns about both concentration and valuation.

## Detailed Notes

### Current US Market Features: Concentration and Valuations [00:00:00]

Seven stocks make up 36% of the S&P 500 and 32% of the total US market, the highest concentration since 1927. US valuations are approaching 1999 dot-com peak levels. AI-related stocks have driven 75% of S&P 500 returns, 80% of earnings growth, and 90% of capital spending growth since ChatGPT launched in November 2022, according to a September 2025 JP Morgan report.

### Historical Precedent: Nortel and the Canadian Market [00:05:13]

Nortel Networks peaked at over 36% of the Canadian TSE 300 index during the dot-com era and had a Shiller PE of 60.6. When it collapsed, the TSE 300 dropped 43% from September 2000 to September 2002. Despite this, the market recovered by July 2005 and outperformed the US market over the subsequent decade. Canadian value stocks did not crash with the overall market and delivered strong returns.

### The US Dot-Com Lost Decade and Japan's Unrecovered Market [00:08:46]

The US market, measured in Canadian dollars, remained flat from March 2000 to July 2013—a lost decade compounded by the 2008 financial crisis. The Japanese market crashed at end of 1989 and has still not recovered in real inflation-adjusted terms. In both cases, global diversification and exposure to value/small-cap value stocks would have provided meaningful protection.

### Empirical Evidence on Concentration and Valuations [00:10:10]

Analysis of US returns from 1926 to 2024 shows only a weak, statistically insignificant negative correlation between top-7 concentration and 10-year future returns. Among the 10 largest non-US markets over the last decade, the relationship between concentration and returns is essentially nonexistent—Switzerland at 60% concentration still delivered positive returns. Valuations measured by the CAPE ratio show a clearer monotonic relationship with future returns across developed markets back to 1982.

### Investment Implications: Diversification and Discipline [00:16:40]

The main practical lessons are global diversification (across countries and factors such as value and small-cap) and behavioral discipline to hold through poor periods. High valuations moderate return expectations for the US market but do not necessarily predict an imminent crash. Felix explicitly does not recommend exiting US stocks, noting high valuations persisted and US returns remained strong from 2021 onward.
