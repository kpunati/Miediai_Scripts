# The Problem With Index Funds

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-04-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=mqIHa6URUPk
**Duration:** 18:51

## Summary

Ben Felix challenges a core assumption underlying the arithmetic of active management — that index funds effectively hold the market without trading — by documenting three quantifiable hidden costs index funds incur when they must trade. Drawing on multiple peer-reviewed papers, the video explains how adverse selection from firm-driven equity issuances and buybacks, short-term price pressure from predictable index reconstitution, and mean reversion in the valuations of added versus deleted stocks can collectively cost index investors 30–80 basis points per year, an order of magnitude greater than index fund expense ratios. Felix presents Dimensional Fund Advisors' non-indexed total market fund DFUS as a live example of an implementation that exploits this flexibility and has outperformed VTI by 56 basis points since its 2021 ETF launch.

## Key Takeaways

- Index funds must trade in response to market composition changes, which breaks the ideal model of the arithmetic of active management.
- Firms issue stock when they believe their prices are high and buy back when they believe prices are low, putting index fund rebalancing directly at odds with informed corporate behavior.
- The long-short portfolio mimicking index rebalancing trades (buy issuances, sell buybacks) delivers a negative 4% annual return.
- Predictable index reconstitution creates temporary price pressure that reverses after index funds trade, costing S&P 500 index funds an estimated 0.21–0.28% annually.
- A fund like DFUS that delays or avoids trading on index changes can potentially recapture 30–80 basis points per year without abandoning broad diversification or low costs.
- These findings do not invalidate low-cost index investing; they point to an enhanced implementation that preserves index fund benefits while reducing hidden trading costs.

## Detailed Notes

### The Arithmetic of Active Management and Its Limits [00:00:00]

Felix revisits Bill Sharpe's 1991 paper, noting that footnote 4 acknowledges passive managers may trade with active managers at a price disadvantage when they do buy or sell. The 2018 paper 'Sharpening the Arithmetic of Active Management' formalizes this, showing that the market portfolio is never static, leaving room for informed active managers to add value against uninformed index funds, even though high fees eliminate that benefit for investors in active funds.

### Adverse Selection Costs [00:05:35]

The 2025 paper on index rebalancing and stock market composition shows index funds incur adverse selection costs by mechanically buying stock when firms issue (high prices) and selling when firms buy back (low prices). The long-short portfolio replicating these trades earns negative 4% annually with negative factor loadings on value and profitability. Counterfactual index funds with one-year rebalancing delays beat quarterly rebalancing by 0.32% annualized over 1981–2023, rising to 0.81% with a four-year delay.

### Price Impact and Index Reconstitution [00:10:49]

Predictable index additions and deletions cause temporary price pressure in the direction of the expected trade that reverses once index funds execute. Research since the 1980s documents this for S&P 500 additions, with the 2011 paper estimating a lower-bound cost of 0.21–0.28% annually (peaking at 0.65–0.82% in 2000). A 2024 Dimensional paper finds average excess returns of 4% in the 20 days before reconstitution followed by a negative 5.7% reversal the next month. The effect has declined as index fund market share rose but remains significant for non-migrating stocks.

### Mean Reversion and the Dimensional Solution [00:14:45]

Stocks added to the S&P 500 tend to be 92% more expensive than the market while deleted stocks are 55% cheaper, generating a mean-reversion headwind for index funds. DFUS, Dimensional's non-indexed total market ETF, avoids IPOs immediately after listing and implements flexible rebalancing without rigid index tracking. With an expense ratio of 9 basis points versus VTI's 3 basis points, DFUS has outperformed VTI by 56 basis points annualized net of fees since June 14, 2021 through March 2025, within the predicted 30–80 basis point range.
