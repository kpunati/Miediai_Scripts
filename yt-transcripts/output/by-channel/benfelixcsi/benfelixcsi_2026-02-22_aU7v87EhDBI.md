# Renting vs. Buying a Home: The Reckoning

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2026-02-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=aU7v87EhDBI
**Duration:** 14:40

## Summary

Ben Felix updates his annual Canadian rent-vs-own study with full-year 2025 data across 12 cities, finding that the average hypothetical renter has now accumulated 14% more wealth than an otherwise-identical homeowner over the 20-year period since January 2005. Canada's real estate market is in its second-worst inflation-adjusted decline on record, driven by rising mortgage rates, immigration reform, foreign ownership restrictions, and anti-money-laundering policies. The analysis uses a matched counterfactual model that tracks renter and owner net worth over time using actual historical price, rent, and cost data. Felix acknowledges owning his own home and frames the video not as an attack on homeownership but as a challenge to the dogmatic belief that owning is always financially superior.

## Key Takeaways

- Canada's inflation-adjusted home prices have fallen 28% from their peak as of December 2025, the second-worst decline since 1975.
- Hypothetical renters who invested in diversified global equities now outpace homeowners by 14% on average across 12 Canadian cities since 2005.
- Real estate pricing reflects discounted expected future rents; when rates rose and immigration tightened, the elevated price-to-rent relationship corrected.
- Homeownership provides a hedge against being priced out of a specific area, but that protection cuts both ways when values fall.
- Renters who save the cash-flow cost difference between renting and owning and invest it in equities have a strong financial case; those who do not save the difference have no chance financially.
- The PWL Capital calculator allows Canadians to run personalized rent-vs-buy projections with province-specific tax treatment.
- Either renting or owning can be the better financial choice depending on the specific rent, price, and personal circumstances involved.

## Detailed Notes

### Canada's Real Estate Correction and Its Causes [00:01:45]

Felix attributes the price decline to a confluence of policy responses to affordability problems: rising mortgage rates from pandemic-era lows, a major overhaul of Canada's immigration system, foreign ownership taxes and the federal ban on certain foreign purchases, and federal initiatives to increase housing supply. He notes that rents and prices have fallen materially in many cities, calling it a likely healthy correction.

### Asset Pricing Framework for Housing [00:02:31]

Drawing on stock-valuation principles, Felix explains that house prices represent discounted future rents—higher expected future rents and lower discount rates push prices up, just as higher expected earnings and lower discount rates push stock prices up. At the recent peak, both rents and prices had become some of the most expensive in the world, creating the conditions for a correction once policy responses took hold.

### Methodology of the Rent-vs-Own Model [00:06:52]

The model starts both a hypothetical renter and owner in January 2005 with equal liquid wealth (enough for a 20% down payment). The owner finances with a 25-year mortgage at prevailing rates; the renter invests the down payment and any monthly cash-flow surplus over the owner's costs into a 30/70 Canadian-global equity portfolio with a 0.25% fee (similar to VEQT). Rent increases annually in line with historical city-specific data. Both scenarios are tracked to year-end 2025.

### 2025 Results and City-Level Findings [00:10:00]

The geometric mean renter-to-owner wealth ratio moved from 0.99 at end-2024 to 1.14 at end-2025 as real estate continued falling while global stock markets remained strong. No cities that previously favored owners flipped to renter-favored, but cities like Kitchener-Waterloo and Victoria saw dramatic narrowing, ending 2025 at roughly 0.99.
