# Covered Calls: A Devil's Bargain

**Channel:** Ben Felix
**Published:** 2025-09-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ygVObRx9X68
**Duration:** 16:13

## Summary

Ben Felix presents a rigorous analytical takedown of covered call ETF strategies, explaining from first principles why selling call options on held equities reduces expected returns in direct proportion to the income generated. The video covers the mechanics of how covered calls reduce equity beta asymmetrically — capping upside while leaving most downside intact — and why this eliminates the mean-reverting property of equities that benefits long-term investors. Performance data from multiple Canadian and US covered call ETFs with 10+ year histories consistently confirms the theoretical prediction: all trail their underlying equity benchmarks. The video concludes that covered call yields are not income in any sustainable sense and that marketing them as such is irresponsible.

## Key Takeaways

- Covered call distribution yields are inversely related to expected total returns: targeting higher income mechanically reduces equity exposure and expected returns.
- Selling a call option creates a liability that empirically more than offsets the premium income received, making the trade a net negative in expected value terms.
- Covered calls eliminate mean reversion in equity returns by capping upside, which is particularly costly when equities recover after downturns.
- Every covered call ETF examined with 10+ year histories has underperformed its underlying equity index, with underperformance worsening at higher targeted distribution yields.
- A portfolio of cash plus the underlying equity achieves a similar beta reduction as a covered call but without the asymmetric downside and with better risk-adjusted performance.
- The volatility risk premium that could theoretically help covered calls has been insufficient to offset reduced equity exposure since approximately 2011.
- These strategies are particularly dangerous for retirement income seekers who mistake high distribution yields for sustainable income, potentially depleting capital faster than anticipated.

## Detailed Notes

### Mechanics: Why Yields Are Inversely Related to Expected Returns [00:00:00]

Selling a call option receives a premium but creates a liability: the obligation to sell the underlying stock below market value if it rises above the strike price. Higher targeted distribution yields require lower strike prices, which means higher short delta, less equity exposure, and lower expected returns. This relationship is mathematical and unavoidable.

### Asymmetric Risk Profile and Mean Reversion [00:04:32]

Covered calls reduce equity beta asymmetrically: most downside is retained while upside is capped at the strike price. This eliminates the mean-reverting tendency of equities, which is an important feature for long-term investors — stocks tend to recover after large declines, but covered call strategies miss that recovery. This asymmetry compounds over time into increasing underperformance.

### Cash-Plus-Equity Comparison [00:09:02]

Jeff Ptak of Morningstar found that for 22 single-stock covered call ETFs, a cash-plus-underlying-stock portfolio outperformed most funds, had lower volatility than all, and had higher Sharpe ratios than most. A 60/40 equity-cash blend approximates the beta reduction of a covered call without the asymmetric return damage.

### Live Fund Performance Evidence [00:09:02]

BMO Covered Call Utilities ETF: -2.6% annualized vs. underlying since 2011, trailing in 70%+ of 3-year periods. BMO Covered Call Canadian Banks ETF: -2.71% annualized, trailing in <1% of rolling 3-year periods. Global X S&P TSX 60 Covered Call ETF: -3.65% annualized, trailing in 92% of 3-year periods. Higher yield strategies perform even worse.

### High-Yield and Single-Stock Extremes [00:11:44]

Hamilton ETFs' yield maximizer targets 12%+ by selling at-the-money calls, underperforming the S&P 500 by 4.48% annualized. JEPI underperformed the S&P 500 by 5.92% since May 2020 despite gaining a large retail following. TSLY, with a ~48.59% yield, underperformed Tesla by more than 20% annualized since November 2022, illustrating the extreme end of the yield-for-returns tradeoff.

### Cost and Suitability Conclusions [00:14:16]

Covered call ETFs charge significantly higher fees (0.63% average MER, 0.16% TER) than their underlying equity ETFs (0.25% MER, 0% TER). The volatility risk premium that could partially offset underperformance has been insufficient since 2011. These products are marketed on distribution yield rather than expected returns, which the presenter characterizes as indifference to the truth.
