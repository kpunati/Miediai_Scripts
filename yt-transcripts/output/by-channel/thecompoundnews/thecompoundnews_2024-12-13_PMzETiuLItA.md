# Is Rick Rieder a Bond King? | TCAF 169

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=PMzETiuLItA
**Duration:** 65:15

## Summary

In this episode of The Compound and Friends, Rick Rieder, BlackRock's CIO of Global Fixed Income, joins hosts Michael Batnick and Josh Brown for an in-depth conversation spanning his career, macro views, and investment strategy. Rieder traces his path from EF Hutton in 1987 through Lehman Brothers' rise and fall, the founding of R3 Capital, and ultimately joining BlackRock in 2009 with a team of 42. The discussion covers his thesis that the US service-driven economy is broadly recession-resistant, critiques of Fed policy under Powell, the structural case for private credit, and the rationale behind BINC, BlackRock's actively managed fixed income ETF targeting 6-6.5% yield.

## Key Takeaways

- Rieder argues the US service-oriented economy, where 70% of consumption is services, is structurally resistant to traditional recessions absent a systemic shock.
- Negative interest rates are viewed as a failed experiment that destroys bank and pension system health without stimulating velocity.
- The Fed under Powell is criticized for being too slow to raise rates after the pandemic fiscal stimulus and too slow to subsequently lower them.
- Underweighting long-duration Treasuries is preferred given a flat yield curve, high issuance volumes, and the lack of a reliable equity-hedge function.
- Private credit offers attractive IRRs of 10-14% today, but returns will compress as the asset class grows and banking deregulation increases competition.
- BINC is designed as a high-grade, short-duration active ETF targeting stable 6-6.5% yield, democratizing access to institutional-grade fixed income management.
- Market concentration has intensified: the top mega-cap tech stocks now equal the bottom 450 S&P 500 companies by market cap, up from 282 in 2018.

## Detailed Notes

### Rick Rieder's Career Path [00:18:57]

Rieder started at EF Hutton in 1987 after being drawn to trading over his planned banking analyst role. After Black Monday that same year, EF Hutton was absorbed into Lehman Brothers where Rieder rose from corporate bond trader to head of credit businesses and member of the management committee. Following Lehman's collapse, he founded R3 Capital Partners (named for Reading, Writing, and Arithmetic, not his initials, reflecting a commitment to urban education funding) and then joined BlackRock in 2009 with a team of 42 people.

### Macro View: US Economy and Recession Resistance [00:14:46]

Rieder presents a 'satellite economy' thesis: with 70% of US consumption driven by services, and the service sector having entered recession only twice in 75 years (pandemic and financial crisis), he does not believe the US enters normal cyclical recessions. He also notes that large cap tech companies are net cash holders, meaning rate hikes actually boost their income rather than constricting them, while older wealthier consumers who are net savers also benefit from higher rates.

### Federal Reserve Policy Assessment [00:40:59]

Rieder credits the Fed for its response during the pandemic but criticizes it for being too slow to raise rates as fiscal stimulus flooded the economy, and then too slow to lower them. He argues the Fed was the sole engine of economic management for too long because fiscal policy was gridlocked, and that the new era of large fiscal action requires the Fed to recalibrate more actively. He also notes that keeping rates above neutral created stimulus for the wealthy and asset owners, contrary to the intended restrictive effect.

### Fixed Income Strategy: Duration and Treasuries [00:46:18]

Rieder is underweight long-duration Treasuries, preferring the front to belly of the yield curve. He identifies heavy auction supply (nearly 600 billion dollars per week) and the potential for bond vigilante action as tail risks. His preferred hedge against equity volatility is to simply hold less of the risky asset or use equity options rather than relying on long bonds that have shown positive correlation with equities in inflationary environments.

### Private Credit Outlook [00:49:27]

Rieder sees private credit as having a runway two to three times larger than private equity relative to public market penetration. He views current 10-14% IRRs for secured lending as attractive but expects returns to compress over time as the space matures and banking regulation loosens. He is cautious about rising payment-in-kind structures as a signal of deal quality deterioration, though notes that the direct lending model allows for workouts rather than defaults, which he views as a stabilizing feature.

### BINC: BlackRock's Active Fixed Income ETF [00:53:42]

BINC (iShares Flexible Income Active ETF) launched in May 2023 targeting a 6-6.5% yield with a high BBB average rating and roughly 2.5-year duration. By late 2024 it had reached approximately 6.7 billion in AUM, including 2.2 billion in inflows in a single quarter, named a top new ETF by Morningstar. Rieder views it as a vehicle for democratizing institutional-grade fixed income management, allowing retail investors to access globally diversified, yield-focused credit that previously required hedge fund or separately managed account minimums.
