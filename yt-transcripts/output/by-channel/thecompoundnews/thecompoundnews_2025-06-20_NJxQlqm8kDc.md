# Why Defensive Stocks Have Disappeared | TCAF Chartpalooza Special

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-06-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NJxQlqm8kDc
**Duration:** 59:20

## Summary

This Chartpalooza special episode brings together Josh Brown, Michael Batnick, Todd Sohn of Strategus, and Matt Cerminaro (Chart Kid) for a chart-driven deep dive into current U.S. equity market conditions. The panel reviews the Fed's June 2025 decision to hold rates while downgrading growth and upgrading inflation forecasts, then examines historical precedents for market behavior after bear-market recoveries. A central theme is the structural disappearance of traditional defensive sectors, now at a 35-year low as a share of S&P 500 market cap, alongside bullish breadth-thrust data and concerning outflows from small-cap ETFs.

## Key Takeaways

- The Fed held rates in June 2025 while projecting lower GDP growth, higher unemployment, and higher inflation — a stagflationary signal the panel views as a policy error.
- Historically, after a 20% drawdown and a rally back to within 5% of all-time highs, it takes an average of 3.6 months to make a new high, suggesting near-term consolidation is normal.
- Defensive sector weights (staples, energy, healthcare, utilities) in the S&P 500 have dropped below 21% — a 35-year low — forcing investors to rethink what 'playing defense' means.
- The MAG7's share of S&P 500 market cap has only recovered about half its 2025 decline even as the index approaches all-time highs, signaling broadening market participation.
- A breadth thrust signal (58%+ of S&P stocks hitting 4-week highs) occurred in May 2025; historically this has produced a 19% average return over the following 12 months with a 100% win rate.
- Small-cap ETF outflows are at multi-year extremes, but structural shifts (private equity absorbing small companies) may limit the contrarian opportunity in public small-cap equities.
- The proliferation of leveraged 2x single-stock ETFs into micro-cap and speculative names (quantum stocks, meme stocks) signals frothy retail sentiment in specific pockets of the market.

## Detailed Notes

### Fed June 2025 Decision and Macro Backdrop [00:03:10]

The Fed held rates in June, maintaining language about solid economic activity and low unemployment while noting elevated inflation. Updated projections showed GDP falling to 1.4%, unemployment rising to 4.5%, and headline PCE rising to 3% — changes the panel characterized as uniformly in the wrong direction. The panel broadly agreed that rates should be cut, pointing to a frozen housing market and risk of construction-sector layoffs as key downside risks.

### Market Recovery and Historical Precedents for New Highs [00:12:44]

Matt Cerminaro presented data showing that after a 20% drawdown followed by a rally to within 5% of all-time highs, markets have taken an average of 3.6 months to reach a new high. The panel attributed this to overhead supply — investors who held through the drawdown and use the near-recovery as an opportunity to reduce risk. The February 2007 instance was noted as a cautionary example where a near-recovery preceded additional severe losses.

### International Equities and Sector Leadership Shift [00:17:46]

Todd Sohn highlighted that the MSCI developed international market index hit an all-time high for the first time since 2007, driven by more cyclical sector composition — industrials up roughly 5%, financials flat, tech rising. Within U.S. markets, industrials (especially defense and AI infrastructure) are leading the best-stocks list, a reversal from Q1 when utilities dominated amid trade-war fears.

### Valuation Comparison: Today vs. 2021 Peak [00:19:56]

Cerminaro's chart broke the S&P 500 into forward P/E deciles and compared current valuations to the 2021 peak. The most expensive cohort (top decile) now trades at 63x forward earnings versus 104x in 2021 — a roughly 40% discount. The panel noted pandemic-era accounting charges may have artificially elevated 2021 P/Es, but broadly agreed current valuations are less extreme than the prior peak.

### Disappearance of Traditional Defensive Sectors [00:26:42]

Todd Sohn's chart showed the combined weight of defensive sectors (staples, energy, healthcare, utilities) in the S&P 500 has fallen below 21%, the lowest in his 35 years of data, driven largely by healthcare's GLP-1-related decline and the overall growth of tech and financials. The panel debated whether traditional defensives still serve their historical role, concluding that investors must rethink the definition — with Visa, Mastercard, Netflix, and utilities (now partly AI plays) all cited as potential modern equivalents. Josh Brown argued the old defensive/cyclical paradigm has been disrupted by the shift from active stock-pickers to ETF portfolios.

### MAG7 Concentration Declining; Broadening Participation [00:33:06]

Cerminaro's chart showed the MAG7's weight in the S&P 500 has recovered only about half its 2025 decline even as the broader index is approximately 2% from all-time highs. A separate chart decomposed S&P returns by market-cap decile, showing the largest-cap half of the index contributing about 192 basis points of year-to-date return while the smaller half detracted. The panel identified the 8th–9th decile (Oracle, Netflix, Uber, CrowdStrike, IBM) as the sweet spot of market leadership, and discussed an upcoming ETF concept targeting the S&P 200-500 to capture this tier.

### Leveraged Single-Stock ETFs: Proliferation into Speculative Names [00:42:54]

Cerminaro's scatter plot of leveraged single-stock ETFs illustrated that the original 2x products focused on MAG7 names, but recent launches cover $3–5 billion market-cap companies including quantum computing stocks (IonQ, Rigetti), meme stocks (GameStop), and other speculative names. The panel viewed this as a short-term buy signal but intermediate-term sell signal for the underlying stocks if and when a 2x ETF's AUM exceeds the company's own market cap.

### Breadth Thrust Signal and Small-Cap Outflows [00:47:31]

Cerminaro presented data showing every instance where 58%+ of S&P 500 stocks hit a new 4-week high has preceded a positive 12-month return with a 100% win rate and 19% average return, with this signal triggering in mid-May 2025 off the April low. The panel contrasted this bullish signal with Todd Sohn's chart of rolling 3-month small-cap ETF flows, which have reached outflow extremes not seen since late 2006. The panel acknowledged the apparent contrarian opportunity but tempered enthusiasm noting that private equity and venture capital now absorb much of the capital that previously flowed into public small-cap equities.
