# The World According to Joe Lonsdale | TCAF 185

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-31
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vd5tWUlqvME
**Duration:** 62:40

## Summary

In this 62-minute episode of The Compound and Friends (TCAF 185), Joe Lonsdale — co-founder of Palantir, 8VC, and Opto — and Jacob Miller, co-founder of Opto Investments, discuss private market dynamics, the five-layer AI investment framework, and how Opto aims to give RIA clients access to top-tier private funds without broker-dealer conflicts. The conversation covers macro uncertainty around tariffs, the IPO window, AI's disinflationary potential, the Bitcoin strategic reserve controversy, and DOGE's government efficiency efforts. Both guests express bullishness on the US AI advantage over Europe and China over a multi-year horizon while acknowledging near-term market volatility.

## Key Takeaways

- Private markets are constipated but not broken — the bigger structural issue is the FTC blocking acquisitions rather than any lack of capital.
- Lonsdale frames AI investing across five layers (chips, data centers, model companies, AI infrastructure, AI services) and argues the best risk/reward sits in layers four and five, not in the widely-known model companies.
- The democratization of private markets carries real danger: most retail-accessible products are sold rather than chosen, and the best funds rarely need to pay platforms to raise capital.
- Opto's business model aligns incentives by charging carry above a hurdle instead of taking fees from fund managers, and builds custom fund-of-funds vehicles with a single sub-doc and K-1.
- Lonsdale criticized the Bitcoin strategic reserve announcement not on principle but because visible front-running options trades suggested insiders were tipped off in advance.
- DOGE's most defensible wins include shuttering USAID programs tied to ideological grift and discovering government computers issuing payments from no actual account.
- Both guests separate China tariffs (viewed as justified given currency manipulation and IP theft) from broader tariff policy, which they see as more uncertain and potentially painful for large-cap equities with heavy overseas exposure.

## Detailed Notes

### IPO Window and Private Market Liquidity [00:00:00]

Lonsdale says public markets are 'constipated' due to tariff uncertainty and lingering macro noise but expects the IPO window to reopen within a few years. He notes that the bigger constraint has been FTC merger blocking, which was a greater drag on VC return of capital than public market conditions. Jacob Miller argues companies stay private longer because they no longer need the IPO check — liquidity for employees can be handled via secondary rounds.

### Five Layers of AI Investment [00:00:00]

Lonsdale outlines five investable AI layers: chips (layer 1), data centers (layer 2), model companies such as OpenAI and Anthropic (layer 3), AI deployment infrastructure like Llama and Palantir (layer 4), and AI-native services companies (layer 5). He argues layers four and five offer superior risk-adjusted returns because model companies are already highly valued, drawing a parallel to SaaS where Salesforce-style application companies captured more value than cloud infrastructure.

### Private Markets Democratization Risks [00:00:00]

Lonsdale and Miller warn that most products reaching retail investors through wealth channels are sold rather than curated — wholesalers incentivized by placement fees push mediocre funds. Lonsdale argues top-quartile funds rarely need to pay to raise capital, so platforms that charge GPs 2–10% to access advisers attract second- and third-tier managers. The 'betification' of private markets via vehicles like the PRIV ETF creates illusory daily liquidity over fundamentally illiquid assets.

### Opto Investments Business Model [00:00:00]

Opto builds custom fund-of-funds for RIAs and family offices using technology to consolidate sub-docs and K-1s into a single vehicle. Rather than charging GPs for distribution, Opto takes 5% carry above a hurdle (approximately 10%), aligning its economics with end-client returns. The platform aggregates commitments across firms, enabling smaller checks to qualify for institutional discounts. Lonsdale emphasizes that the best deals in his network — such as Anduril — come from relationships, not placement fees.

### Bitcoin Strategic Reserve and Crypto Governance [00:00:00]

Lonsdale says he supports holding already-seized Bitcoin in a government account but objected publicly to the manner in which specific coin names were included in a presidential tweet, given that 50-to-1 options bets appeared in those markets ahead of the announcement. He was harshly negative on Melania Coin, describing approximately $5 billion flowing in before it went near zero, calling it a transfer of wealth from uninformed retail participants.

### DOGE and Government Waste [00:00:00]

Lonsdale praises DOGE's discovery of government computers in HHS, DoD, and Treasury issuing payments with no backing account, and the audit of Social Security recipients listed as over 120 years old. He frames the broader DOGE effort as long-overdue cleanup that both parties should support in principle, even if politically difficult. He also defends Elon Musk personally, describing him as genuinely mission-driven and willing to endure personal and financial costs.

### Macro Outlook: Tariffs, American Exceptionalism, and AI [00:00:00]

Both guests separate China tariffs (justified by currency manipulation and adversarial behavior) from broader tariff policy, which Miller says will be most painful for large-cap multinationals with heavy overseas exposure. Lonsdale argues US dominance in AI, venture capital dynamism, and entrepreneurial culture make American exceptionalism a durable thesis over a 5–10 year horizon, even if near-term market volatility continues. He expects meaningful productivity surges to become visible toward the end of the current administration.
