# The Nothing Ever Happens Market | Animal Spirits 418

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-06-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=io627rzOgcc
**Duration:** 61:24

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 418 features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson exploring the concept of 'market amnesia'—investors' growing tendency to shrug off geopolitical crises—anchored in a MIT paper showing geopolitical shocks move markets only modestly. The hosts debate whether AI-driven white-collar layoffs represent a structural shift or cyclical correction from pandemic-era overhiring, using Amazon CEO Andy Jassy's memo as a lens. A substantial segment examines the stablecoin regulatory landscape under the Genius Act, arguing that dollar-backed stablecoins could meaningfully grow Treasury demand and challenge card network fee structures. The episode also covers the rising cost of car ownership, baby boomer housing lock-in, and private market valuation concerns.

## Key Takeaways

- Investors have increasingly learned to ignore geopolitical headlines because corporate earnings—especially from large-cap tech—are not materially affected by most geopolitical events.
- White-collar workforce reductions are partly a normalization from 2022 peak overhiring, not purely an AI displacement story, though AI-driven job losses are likely to accelerate over the long term.
- The Genius Act's requirement of dollar-for-dollar reserves for stablecoins could structurally boost demand for US Treasuries and enable large retailers like Amazon and Walmart to build their own payment rails to avoid credit card processing fees.
- Private equity and venture capital show extreme return dispersion—bottom-quartile PE returns can be as low as 1.5% while top-quartile approaches 21%—making manager selection critical; private credit has the narrowest dispersion among alternatives.
- The average American car now costs roughly 30% more to own and operate than a decade ago, with new vehicles averaging nearly $50,000 and depreciation running almost $4,700 per year.
- One in three baby boomers say they will never sell their homes, constraining housing inventory, while two-thirds of boomers have lived in their home for 16 or more years.
- Market concentration is a global phenomenon—the top 10 UK stocks represent 50% of that market, versus 26% for the top five in the S&P 500, highlighting that US concentration concerns are often overstated in relative terms.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Amnesia and Geopolitical Events [00:01:03]

Batnick argues the defining feature of this decade is investor amnesia: markets have absorbed the pandemic, meme stocks, 9% inflation, rapid Fed rate hikes, SVB collapse, the carry trade blow-up, Liberation Day tariffs, and US-Iran tensions without sustained drawdowns. He cites a Mike Bird piece in The Economist referencing an MIT paper co-authored by Larry Summers showing geopolitical shock days produce volatility less than three times normal, and that several historic large single-day drops had no identifiable news catalyst. Carlson attributes resilience to tech earnings dominance—if energy were still 15% of the index, each crisis would bite harder.

### Labor Market: AI Displacement vs. Cyclical Normalization [00:07:38]

A Wall Street Journal analysis shows US public companies cut white-collar headcount by 3.5% over three years, with middle managers disproportionately affected. Batnick argues this is partly a reversion from the record-hot 2022 job market rather than purely AI-driven displacement. Carlson is less sanguine, pointing to rising unemployment among college grads (6.6% for ages 20-24) and Andy Jassy's public acknowledgment that Amazon plans to reduce its corporate workforce as generative AI and agents drive efficiency. The hosts agree the labor market data will be 'bizarre' for the next decade as AI effects combine with 10,000 daily baby boomer retirements.

### Radiologists as AI Case Study [00:25:02]

Geoffrey Hinton's 2016 prediction that AI would make radiologists obsolete within five years proved wrong—radiologist demand is at record highs and AI serves as a productivity-enhancing second set of eyes at institutions like Mayo Clinic. Hosts outline three AI labor outcomes: displacement (customer service call centers employing 2.8-3.4 million people), augmentation (radiology), and new jobs not yet imaginable. They express concern that public discourse fixates on displacement while ignoring the latter two categories.

### Rising Cost of Car Ownership [00:18:34]

New car average prices are approaching $50,000, total ownership costs are up roughly 30% over a decade, and light vehicle sales have fallen about 1.7 million units per year since 2016. AAA calculates new vehicles depreciate around $4,680 annually. Rising repair costs stem from embedded sensors and electronics requiring specialist labor. Surging used-car demand has pushed average used-car prices above $25,000. Hosts speculate more urban households may abandon car ownership as ride-share and e-bike alternatives improve.

### Stablecoins and the Genius Act [00:31:32]

The Genius Act mandates $1 in dollar-denominated reserves (likely T-bills or Treasuries) for every $1 of stablecoins issued, providing regulatory legitimacy and potentially expanding Treasury demand. Crypto analyst Sam Lee argues this opens the door for large corporations to build stablecoin-based payment rails that undercut Visa/Mastercard's 2-3% processing fees, with Amazon and Walmart cited as logical candidates. Tether currently earns roughly $6 billion annually on its reserve portfolio. Circle's IPO saw extreme daily volatility (up 168% on day one), signaling high speculative interest in the sector.

### Real Estate: Boomer Lock-In and Home Improvement Costs [00:41:09]

Redfin data shows one in three baby boomers say they will never sell their homes; another 30% say they won't sell within the next decade. Two-thirds of boomers have lived in their current home for 16-plus years. Carlson shares a personal anecdote about the cost and hassle of selling a rental property after a tenant departure, illustrating the poor 'return on hassle' of direct real estate investment. He also notes how home renovation decisions cascade—new flooring necessitates repainting, which necessitates new banisters—with costs far exceeding initial estimates.

### Private Markets: Valuations, Dispersion, and Access [00:43:46]

JP Morgan's Guide to Alternatives shows private equity return dispersion is extreme (1.5% to 21% between bottom and top quartiles) while private credit has the narrowest dispersion among alternatives, making it the easiest product for advisors to recommend. Large-cap and middle-market private equity buyout multiples now match public market multiples, eroding the valuation discount that historically justified illiquidity premiums; only sub-$100 million companies still trade at a meaningful discount (8.8x EV/EBITDA). BlackRock manages roughly 10x the assets of Blackstone yet has a smaller market cap, illustrating how alternative managers command disproportionate valuations due to their fee structures.

### Market Concentration and Foreign Ownership Trends [00:14:43]

Foreign ownership of US equities has risen from about 5-6% in the mid-1990s to nearly 20%, reflecting global catchup on home country bias correction. Meanwhile, S&P 500 pure growth exposure has risen from 35% in 2005 to 50% in 2025, while pure value has shrunk from 25% to 9%. Hosts note market concentration is more severe internationally—top 10 UK stocks are 50% of that index, Samsung alone is 20% of South Korea's ETF—suggesting US concentration concerns are relative rather than absolute.
