# How to Earn Stock Market Returns With Half the Risk | TCAF 210

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qXyfgEh8Aeg
**Duration:** 70:08

## Summary

Steve Romick, co-portfolio manager of FPA Crescent at First Pacific Advisors, joins The Compound and Friends for a wide-ranging conversation on his three-decade career running a flexible, go-anywhere value fund. Romick explains how disciplined price-consciousness helped FPA Crescent survive and profit through the dot-com bust while losing the majority of its assets under management to redemptions, only to recoup performance over the subsequent five years. The fund has since delivered S&P 500-equivalent annualized returns with approximately a third less volatility and roughly half the peak drawdown, largely by holding significant cash and short positions, owning international and smaller-cap companies, and avoiding sectors where valuations cannot be justified. Current market conditions concern Romick: the percentage of S&P companies trading above 10x sales now exceeds the dot-com peak, high yield spreads offer historically poor compensation, and the fund has been reducing net equity exposure as the market rallies.

## Key Takeaways

- Price matters above all else — businesses can change the world and still produce poor investment returns if purchased at excessive valuations, as Microsoft's 2000-2009 decade illustrates.
- FPA Crescent's go-anywhere mandate — able to own stocks, bonds, cash, and shorts across market caps and geographies — has allowed the fund to match S&P returns with materially lower volatility and drawdown over 30 years.
- The share of S&P 500 companies trading above 10x sales (35%) now exceeds the 25% figure at the dot-com peak, a metric Romick watches as a valuation warning signal.
- High yield bonds are currently unattractive: spreads near 300 basis points are roughly 200 basis points tighter than the 20-year average, yields are below average, and covenants are unusually issuer-friendly.
- Intellectual integrity — admitting mistakes and exiting positions without public defensiveness — is a key survival trait for long-term investors, contrasted with value investors who 'rode Sears to zero.'
- The secondary productivity benefits of AI have not yet been broadly felt, but Romick expects some companies will capture durable margin improvements while others will see gains competed away.
- The trend toward passive investing and pod-shop trading structures may create more opportunity for disciplined active managers who can wait for and respond quickly to dislocations.

## Detailed Notes

### Steve Romick's Background and FPA Crescent Origin [00:08:02]

Romick started at a hedge fund in the mid-1980s and identified a gap in the market for a public go-anywhere fund not constrained to a single asset class, market cap, or geography. He launched FPA Crescent in 1996, joining First Pacific Advisors, which manages approximately $30 billion across multiple strategies. He describes the allocation as a byproduct of security selection rather than a top-down asset class decision, with cash building when few attractive opportunities exist.

### Dot-com Bubble: Surviving by Not Owning Internet Stocks [00:15:34]

In the late 1990s, FPA Crescent underperformed the market by roughly 40 percentage points over two years by avoiding internet and technology stocks such as Microsoft, Sun Microsystems, Cisco, Amazon, and eBay. Romick owned mundane businesses with insider buying — companies like Pinkertons and IHOP. The fund lost the majority of its AUM to redemptions but made money in 2000 when markets fell, and outperformed over the full 1998–2002 period. The experience reinforced the principle that returns are driven by what you don't own as much as what you do own.

### Price Matters: Valuation as the Primary Risk Control [00:19:51]

The central lesson of the dot-com era is encapsulated in the phrase 'price matters': even transformative businesses like Cisco can deliver 20-year returns of zero if purchased at 120x earnings. Romick quoted Sun Microsystems CEO Scott McNealy's famous post-bubble admission that at 10x revenue, the assumptions required to justify the price were mathematically absurd. The fund evolved from Graham-style balance sheet protection to business quality assessment, but price discipline remained constant.

### Current Portfolio and Market Valuation Concerns [00:33:09]

As of the episode, FPA Crescent holds approximately 40% of its equity exposure in non-US companies, has minimal high yield bond exposure (roughly 1%), and has been gradually reducing net equity exposure as markets have rallied. Romick notes that 35% of S&P 500 market cap now trades above 10x sales, exceeding the 25% figure at the dot-com peak. High yield spreads at ~300 basis points over Treasuries are about 200 basis points below the 20-year average, with covenants unusually favorable to issuers. The fund did add exposure briefly during Liberation Day tariff volatility but found the window very short.

### FPA Crescent Track Record and Fund Objective [00:49:46]

Since inception, FPA Crescent has delivered annualized total returns roughly equivalent to the S&P 500 (approximately 10%) with a standard deviation of 10.89% versus the S&P's 14.96%, and a largest drawdown of approximately half the S&P's 55.4% peak-to-trough loss during the financial crisis. Romick emphasizes that lower volatility and smaller drawdowns are a byproduct of disciplined security selection and capital protection, not an explicit volatility target. He positions this as delivering equity-like returns while avoiding permanent impairment of capital.

### Hot Button Topics: Fed Independence, Crypto, AI, and Earnings Reporting [01:01:23]

Romick declines to predict Fed policy moves, noting that Fed chairs from Greenspan to Yellen have made high-profile calls that proved wrong. He is agnostic on crypto, stating he cannot ascribe a fundamental value to Bitcoin. On AI, he distinguishes between applied AI (which will durably benefit some industries such as biotech lab productivity) and benefits that will be competed away (such as lower-cost labor displacement in call centers). He supports a shift to semi-annual earnings reporting, arguing businesses do not change meaningfully quarter to quarter and that longer reporting periods favor long-term owners over short-term renters.
