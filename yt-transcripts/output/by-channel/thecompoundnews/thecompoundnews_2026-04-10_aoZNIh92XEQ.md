# Stocks Took the Stairs Down and the Elevator Up | TCAF 237

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=aoZNIh92XEQ
**Duration:** 70:05

## Summary

Episode 237 of The Compound and Friends brings together Michael Batnick, Sam Ro (Ticker newsletter), and Steve Sosnick (Interactive Brokers chief strategist) to analyze the stock market's surprisingly muted response to Middle East conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruptions. The conversation highlights the dramatic divergence between collapsing software valuations and surging semiconductor stocks as AI disruption fears reshape the market structure. The hosts also examine what a wave of mega-IPOs (SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic) could mean for equity supply-demand dynamics, and close with a detailed look at earnings season expectations and the Disney layoff story as a lesson in avoiding overblown macro narratives.

## Key Takeaways

- The market's resilience during the Middle East conflict reflects post-COVID investor conditioning to buy dips, FOMO dynamics, and persistent institutional career risk tied to benchmark-beating.
- Software stocks are suffering a secular re-rating — not because earnings have fallen, but because markets are discounting a multi-year AI disruption to terminal values, while semiconductor stocks hit relative all-time highs simultaneously.
- Interactive Brokers customer data showed the most-bought asset during the crisis was VOO, signaling a shift toward long-term buy-and-hold behavior rather than speculative dip-buying.
- Potential IPOs from SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic could significantly disrupt the favorable equity supply-demand balance that has underpinned the bull market, as index funds would need to sell existing holdings to fund purchases.
- Rate cut expectations collapsed by roughly 60 basis points during the conflict period, yet stocks were essentially flat — suggesting the equity market was pricing in a resolution and earnings resilience rather than persistent macro headwinds.
- Emerging market indices have been quietly reinvented: over 32% of IEMG is now technology, with TSMC alone representing 11%, shifting EM from a commodity/energy proxy to an AI infrastructure proxy.
- Disney's reported 1,000-person layoff is statistically trivial against 150,000-200,000 employees and 1,300 active open roles — illustrating how individual layoff headlines often mislead about broader macro health.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Resilience and the FOMO Dynamic [00:08:19]

Steve Sosnick notes that the Strait of Hormuz closure was historically modeled as a 10% S&P correction with oil at $150-200/barrel and VIX at 45-50; instead oil peaked around $110-115 and VIX topped at 35. Both retail FOMO and institutional career risk (benchmark-hugging) drove investors to interpret the conflict as another buyable dip rather than a structural break.

### Software vs. Semiconductor Divergence [00:22:03]

The hosts analyze an unprecedented two-day period where the S&P rose 3.1% while IGV (software ETF) fell 5.2% — a combination that had never previously occurred in the dataset going back to 2001. Software names like Palantir, Palo Alto, CrowdStrike, and ServiceNow each dropped 5-8% in individual sessions despite reporting record-high earnings, as markets price in AI-driven terminal value destruction. Semiconductor hardware names went vertical on the same AI chip demand story, with SMH within 1% of all-time highs.

### IPO Pipeline and Equity Supply Risk [00:34:57]

The discussion of OpenAI's $120 billion private raise at an $830 billion valuation and SpaceX's pending IPO (potentially the largest ever) raises a structural concern: if SpaceX enters the QQQ index and 30% is allocated to retail, roughly $20-25 billion in net new equity supply would need to be absorbed. Index funds tracking the Nasdaq-100 would need to sell existing holdings proportionally, potentially disrupting the favorable supply-demand balance that has supported equity prices.

### Earnings Season: Expectations, Beats, and Guidance [00:38:33]

Sam Ro explains that 70-80% of S&P 500 companies routinely beat consensus estimates by approximately 5%, making headline earnings beats informationally worthless on their own. Steve Sosnick adds that companies may use Middle East uncertainty as a convenient excuse for vague guidance, raising the bar for the beats to drive stock prices. Delta Air Lines' earnings and a United executive's comment projecting $10 billion in incremental fuel costs highlight the real but manageable headwinds companies face.

### Rate Cuts, International Equities, and Macro Outlook [00:54:21]

Fed funds futures repriced from two cuts plus a partial third to just a 25% chance of a single cut — about 60 basis points of easing removed — without materially damaging stocks, reflecting strong earnings expectations. International equities are drawing interest both for value characteristics and policy reforms in China, Korea, and Japan aimed at improving shareholder returns. The hosts note that GDP was revised down 0.5% alongside a 0.4% PCE rise — a mild stagflationary signal — while the labor market remains in a 'no hire, no fire' equilibrium.
