# How Individual Investors Outsmarted the Pros | TCAF 195

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-06-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=InXHy9LsT0o
**Duration:** 72:23

## Summary

Episode 195 of The Compound and Friends was recorded live in Chicago on June 3, 2025, featuring a two-part panel. The first segment has Michael Batnick, Josh Brown, Callie Cox, and Ben Carlson debating the biggest market surprises of the year: consumer and corporate resilience through tariff shocks, the uninterrupted AI capital expenditure narrative, unexpected international equity outperformance, and Bitcoin's rally past $110,000. The second segment features Morningstar CEO Kunal Kapoor discussing how AI, the rise of active ETFs, private market access, and Morningstar's expanding data and ratings franchise are reshaping the investment landscape for individual investors.

## Key Takeaways

- Consumer spending and corporate profit margins have remained resilient through tariff shocks, COVID-era supply disruptions, and multiple market corrections, suggesting a potentially secular shift in household risk appetite.
- The AI investment theme survived a deep-seek-driven sell-off and a 20% S&P drawdown, with mega-cap tech companies doubling down on GPU and infrastructure spending during Q1 2025 earnings calls.
- International developed-market equities posted their best relative quarter versus U.S. stocks in 25 years, driven partly by dollar weakness, European fiscal expansion, and a rotation away from U.S. tech concentration.
- Money market assets remain near record levels at nearly $7 trillion, suggesting that a large reservoir of sidelined capital has not yet rotated into risk assets despite strong equity returns.
- Morningstar is extending its ratings methodology to semi-liquid and interval funds as private credit and private equity converge with public markets, aiming to create a common language for retail investors accessing private assets.
- Active ETFs are gaining momentum as large asset managers launch lower-cost clones of existing mutual fund strategies, with costs converging toward 75 basis points.
- Labor market data—specifically continuing jobless claims trending higher—is identified as the single most important variable to watch for a potential recession signal in the back half of 2025.

## Detailed Notes

### Biggest Market Surprises of 2025 [00:03:28]

Panelists identify the near-bear-market drawdown followed by a full V-shaped recovery as the year's defining sequence. Ben Carlson highlights the secular resilience of households, arguing that pandemic-era experiences may have permanently raised risk tolerance across a generation of new investors. Josh Brown adds that the AI spending narrative emerged intact from the April sell-off, with major tech companies reinforcing capex commitments on earnings calls.

### Millennial vs. Gen Z Investor Psychology [00:07:27]

Callie Cox distinguishes between millennials, who entered markets during the Great Financial Crisis and have consistently held above-average cash levels, and Gen Z investors who have only experienced brief drawdowns followed by sharp recoveries. The panel notes that Robinhood's user and deposit growth continued even through the 2022 bear market, suggesting Gen Z investors did not reduce risk exposure during corrections.

### Recession Risk and Consumer Spending Indicators [00:24:25]

The panel reviews recession probability estimates that peaked around 70% earlier in 2025 but declined to 31% by early June. Mastercard spending data through mid-May showed no deceleration versus prior-quarter trends. Callie Cox flags rising continuing jobless claims as the most credible leading indicator to watch, while Josh Brown argues that $20 trillion in total cash holdings provides a substantial buffer against recession dynamics.

### International Stocks, Bitcoin, and Dollar Weakness [00:17:15]

The panel is surprised by the magnitude of developed international outperformance, with EFA up 18% year-to-date versus roughly 2% for the S&P 500. Dollar weakness is identified as a structural tailwind, alongside German fiscal expansion and a relative valuation shift away from U.S. tech. Bitcoin's recovery from a $74,000 April low to above $110,000 is cited as another unexpected outcome, with Josh Brown admitting a personal position above 10% of portfolio.

### Morningstar's Business and Private Market Expansion [00:41:54]

Kunal Kapoor reviews Morningstar's 20-year public company history, noting 10x revenue and operating income growth, 15% annualized stock compounding, and a total return of 1,615% since its $18.50 IPO. He describes the evolution from a mutual fund ratings firm into the fourth-largest credit rating agency globally and the owner of Pitchbook, the leading private market data provider.

### AI's Role in Financial Services and Morningstar's Mo [01:03:04]

Kapoor sees AI as transforming the advisor workflow primarily by automating compliance and paperwork, freeing advisors for client-facing work. Morningstar's AI tool 'Mo,' built on ChatGPT, allows users to query the full Morningstar database conversationally. Kapoor is candid that consumer AI finance apps have not yet hit the anticipated breakthrough moment, but expects a killer B2C AI advisor app to emerge in the near term.

### Active ETFs and Public-Private Convergence [01:00:36]

Active ETFs are reframed as a low-cost distribution channel rather than a cannibalization threat to mutual funds, with costs converging toward 75 basis points. Morningstar is launching Medalist ratings for semi-liquid and interval funds to help investors compare liquid and illiquid alternatives on a common basis. Kapoor expects private credit to continue its integration into retail portfolios, but cautions that daily-liquidity wrappers are inappropriate for illiquid underlying assets.
