# Why Is the Stock Market Going Up?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=SXuhaiuXYus
**Duration:** 38:29

## Summary

Ask The Compound episode 222 brings together Ben Carlson, Duncan Hill, and Ritholtz CFO Bill Sweet to address why the US stock market continues rising despite war, debt concerns, and geopolitical uncertainty. The answer centers on accelerating corporate earnings growth — particularly in tech — with the S&P 500 PE ratio actually falling since early 2025 as earnings outpaced prices. The episode also covers the theoretical risk of a US value-added tax for Roth account holders, the nuances of foreign tax credit optimization when holding international funds like VXUS, 529 front-loading for college savings, tax-efficient retirement vehicles for small business owners, and sophisticated strategies for unwinding a highly concentrated position in Applied Materials with a massive embedded capital gain.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500's continued rise is driven by fundamental earnings growth — particularly in AI-exposed tech and communication services — not detachment from reality.
- The forward PE ratio for the S&P 500 has declined since early 2025 despite prices rising ~30%, signaling earnings are accelerating faster than stock prices.
- A potential US value-added tax would not necessarily favor traditional over Roth accounts, since both would be subject to consumption taxes on spending.
- Holding VXUS in a taxable brokerage account to claim foreign tax credits offers marginal benefit and is generally not worth the complexity for most investors.
- Front-loading 529 plans maximizes tax-free compounding and is recommended even if future college plans are uncertain, given rollover flexibility to Roth IRAs.
- Small business owners with few employees should consider SEP IRAs or Solo 401ks to significantly increase tax-deferred savings beyond the $7,000 Roth IRA limit.
- For highly concentrated single-stock positions with large embedded gains, strategies include realizing gains early in the tax year, gifting shares, donor advised funds, exchange funds, and prepaid variable forward contracts.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Is the Stock Market Rising? Fundamentals, Not Euphoria [00:03:35]

Ben Carlson argues the market is tracking fundamentals, not detaching from reality. Q1 2026 S&P 500 earnings growth was 25% year-over-year overall, with tech at 45% and communication services (dominated by Meta and Google) at 54%. Consumer discretionary also showed 35% earnings growth. Revenue growth was in the double digits across most sectors, and this marks the sixth consecutive quarter of double-digit earnings growth per FactSet.

### Historical Context: S&P 500 Returns in Perspective [00:08:31]

Analyzing rolling 12-month returns since 1975, the current 31% return ranks at the 88th percentile — strong but not unprecedented. The best 12-month return ever was 61% (early 1980s after back-to-back Fed-induced recessions); the worst was -43% during the financial crisis. Returns of 40% or more have occurred 22 times and 50% or more seven times, confirming this run is exceptional but not unique.

### VAT and the Roth 'Double Tax' Concern [00:11:56]

A viewer raises the concern that a US value-added tax would effectively double-tax Roth IRA holders — taxed on income going in and taxed again via consumption on the way out. Bill Sweet counters that a traditional IRA would fare no better, facing income tax on distributions plus any VAT. He views the VAT as politically unlikely given the difficulty of passing a national sales tax, and notes its distortionary effects were partly previewed through recent tariffs which raised consumer prices.

### Foreign Tax Credits: VXUS in Taxable vs. Retirement Accounts [00:19:50]

Holding VXUS in a taxable account allows claiming foreign tax withholding as a US tax credit, which is not available inside a Roth or traditional IRA. However, only about 60% of non-US dividends are qualified (vs. nearly all US dividends), meaning 40% of the higher VXUS yield faces ordinary income tax rates. The foreign tax credit averages roughly 7% of taxes paid to foreign governments. Bill Sweet concludes the complexity outweighs the benefit for most investors and recommends simply splitting 50/50 across accounts and moving on.

### 529 Plans: Front-Loading and Flexibility [00:23:59]

Ben and Bill endorse front-loading 529 college savings plans because early contributions compound tax-free for longer and early contributors have seen distributions where ~90% of the amount was earnings. College inflation has slowed to below broad CPI in recent years, but costs remain high. 529 plans now allow rollovers of up to $35,000 to a Roth IRA, addressing flexibility concerns. Maintaining a taxable brokerage account alongside a 529 adds further optionality if college plans change.

### Small Business Retirement Accounts [00:27:47]

For a small business owner with fewer than five employees, the recommended progression is: Roth IRA ($7,000/year), then SEP IRA or Solo 401k (up to ~$72,000/year as a percentage of business income), then HSA ($4,400/year with high-deductible health plan). A new 538 account (named after a Trump initiative, launching July) is also noted for children. The hosts lament the fragmented complexity of the US retirement account system and advocate for a universal savings account framework.

### Concentrated Stock: Applied Materials Exit Strategies [00:31:21]

A father-in-law holds Applied Materials shares bought at $4 now worth approximately $440 with ~$700,000 in embedded capital gains. Strategies discussed: (1) realizing gains early in January/February to allow the rest of the year for tax-loss harvesting offsets; (2) gifting shares to children or grandchildren in lower tax brackets; (3) donating via a donor advised fund to avoid realizing gains; (4) an exchange fund with a 7-year lock-up for instant diversification; (5) a prepaid variable forward contract to defer the sale. The panel emphasizes not letting the tax tail wag the dog — if you've won the game, stop playing a single stock.
