# This Is the Dumb Money | Animal Spirits 423

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-07-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=K8YT2guvg5M
**Duration:** 63:10

## Summary

In episode 423 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson explore the thesis that widespread expectation of an AI bubble blowoff top is itself creating a floor under stocks like Nvidia, preventing investors from selling even after large gains. They discuss the collapse of once-reliable market indicators, the alarming corporate crypto buying spree, structural challenges in the housing market, and the risks and realities of private markets being marketed to retail investors. The episode blends data-driven market commentary with listener emails, travel anecdotes, and entertainment recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- The psychological anticipation of an AI bubble blowoff top may be acting as a self-fulfilling floor for stocks like Nvidia, discouraging profit-taking.
- Classic indicators like the CAPE ratio and inverted yield curve have failed as reliable predictors in recent cycles, highlighting the limits of rules-based market analysis.
- A surge of companies raising billions to buy Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies is being called 'the dumb money,' with both hosts viewing it as a late-cycle warning sign.
- The housing market is structurally locked up — demand is strong but supply is trapped by high rates — with investor purchases at a record 30% share of all home buys.
- The middle class's primary wealth vehicle is housing, and being boxed out of that market without stock market participation could widen wealth inequality.
- Private market funds being pushed into 401ks carry significant risks around liquidity, mark manipulation, and fee extraction that retail investors may not fully understand.
- S&P 500 earnings growth is the primary driver behind market gains, a fundamental that the hosts argue cuts through the noise of macro headlines.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Bubble Floor and Nvidia [00:02:51]

Hosts discuss the theory that investor psychology around an anticipated AI blowoff top is keeping a floor under tech stocks. A Data Trek chart overlaying today's NASDAQ against the late-1990s dot-com run suggests the bull market could have significant runway. Both hosts bought Nvidia for the first time during the April correction when it was down 35% and have since seen roughly 70% gains; they debate whether to take profits or hold for a potential bubble peak.

### Broken Market Indicators and Earnings as the Real Driver [00:12:49]

Sam Ro's piece on the CAPE ratio debate from 10 years ago is cited to illustrate how even Robert Shiller expressed uncertainty about his own metric. The inverted yield curve, which Cam Harvey noted had an 8-for-8 recession-predicting track record, failed to predict a recession this cycle. Hosts argue that S&P earnings growth alongside stock prices — both near all-time highs — is the simple, unglamorous explanation for why the market is up.

### Investor Behavior: Housing vs. Stocks [00:17:21]

Ben Carlson presents Fed data showing the top 10% own 87% of stocks but only 44% of housing, meaning housing remains the primary wealth vehicle for the middle class. A listener email describes using a down payment fund to invest in stocks during the April correction instead of buying in overpriced South Florida real estate, and hosts debate whether this is a structural shift in wealth building or a risky gamble.

### The Trade War and the Resilient Economy [00:24:03]

Carson Group's Sunny Vargis argues the trade war is effectively over as deals covering 80-85% of US trade have been struck with Japan, China, the EU, Canada, and Mexico. Hosts coin the term 'the resilient 20s' to describe an economy that has absorbed the pandemic, 9% inflation, and rates moving from zero to 5% without tipping into recession.

### Summer Crypto Frenzy — The Dumb Money [00:29:02]

A Wall Street Journal article reports 98 companies have announced over $43 billion in crypto buying since June 1st, with diverse businesses from hotel operators to toy makers rushing in. Hosts are sharply critical, viewing it as a clear late-cycle signal similar to the SPAC boom. They also note that Bitcoin early ideological adopters may be selling at current prices, potentially undermining the religion-like conviction that has historically supported the asset.

### Housing Market: Record Prices and Investor Share [00:34:30]

Existing single-family home prices hit a new record high in June. Investor purchases (flippers and landlords) now account for 30% of all home purchases — the highest share on record — with small investors of 3-9 properties remaining the largest cohort. The hosts note that three years of mortgage rates stuck between 6.5% and 7.5% have locked existing owners in place, suppressing supply and sustaining prices against all expectations.

### Private Markets: Wall Street Fighting Back Against Passive [00:40:11]

Referencing a Jason Zweig piece, the hosts debate the push to put illiquid private market assets into 401ks. They acknowledge real risks — liquidity mismatches, opaque marks, and high fees — while conceding that broader access could eventually lead to more transparency and lower costs. The dynamic is framed as Wall Street's attempt to recapture fee revenue lost to the rise of passive index investing.
