# Is Financial Education Working? | Animal Spirits 413

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-05-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=h_mymc9hKkc
**Duration:** 61:59

## Summary

Episode 413 of Animal Spirits features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson discussing evidence that retail investors have become better-behaved and more disciplined, pointing to record inflows into Vanguard's VOO ETF and retail buying during April's market dip. The hosts analyze where the buying power comes from, pointing to $6.9 trillion in money market funds as dry powder. They also cover the Moody's US credit downgrade (characterizing it as a non-event), tariff impacts on retailers like Walmart, and housing market dysfunction driven partly by baby boomer demographics. Lighter topics include AI tool adoption, restaurant takeout culture, Social Security claiming trends, and personal travel and entertainment recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- Retail investor behavior has markedly improved over the past decade due to financial education from blogs, podcasts, and platforms like Vanguard and Robinhood, with record ETF inflows as evidence.
- During April's market dip, individual investors bought a record net $4.1 billion in US stocks in a single morning, with money market outflows of $125 billion likely a key source of dry powder.
- The Moody's US credit downgrade is dismissed as a non-event given the US's status as the global reserve currency with the ability to print its own currency.
- Tariffs remain structurally high even after partial rollbacks and Walmart's thin 2.8% profit margins mean retailers cannot absorb tariff-related cost increases.
- The median home buyer age has risen to 56, partly reflecting baby boomer wealth concentration — boomers hold $17 trillion in home equity, roughly half the national total.
- Consumer sentiment surveys (particularly Michigan's) are increasingly viewed as unreliable because stated expectations diverge sharply from actual spending behavior.
- Social Security early claiming jumped 13% year-over-year, driven partly by fear-mongering about the program's future solvency rather than rational present-value calculations.

## Detailed Notes

### Retail Investors Are Getting Smarter [00:00:04]

Batnick argues that the old institutional hierarchy — endowments over pensions over retail — has flattened. Financial education via blogs, podcasts, and platforms like Vanguard and Robinhood has instilled long-term discipline. The Great Financial Crisis served as a catalyst that shocked many into holding or, conversely, scared others into cash permanently, but the current cohort demonstrates notably improved behavior.

### Record Retail Buying and Money Market Dry Powder [00:00:04]

VOO is on track for nearly a trillion dollars in assets at $660 billion, with $46 billion in inflows above any other ETF — 400% more. During April's dip, individuals bought a net $4.1 billion by 12:30 p.m. on one day, a record. Hosts hypothesize that the $6.9 trillion in money market funds, which saw $125 billion in outflows in April, is the primary source of this buying power.

### Bond Yields and the Moody's Downgrade [00:00:04]

The 30-year Treasury hit 5% and Moody's downgraded the US, but hosts view both as non-events. They argue the US as global reserve currency cannot rationally be rated below AAA, and that rising bond yields have repeatedly been misread as either recession signals or deficit alarms. TIPS now offer ~2.73% real yields, the highest in 23 years, which Carlson notes benefits baby boomers heading into retirement.

### Tariffs, Walmart, and Political Portfolio Risks [00:00:04]

Walmart's CFO noted tariffs are too large for any retailer or supplier to fully absorb, with price increases expected to start in late May. Walmart's 2.8% profit margin leaves no buffer. Despite Trump's public pressure on Walmart to absorb costs, the hosts note this mirrors rhetoric they have criticized from both parties. The broader lesson is that actual policies — not political rhetoric — move markets, and investors should not let political views drive portfolio decisions.

### Housing Market Dysfunction and Demographics [00:00:04]

The median home buyer age has risen to 56 (from 31 in 1981), and 40% of homes carry no mortgage, largely reflecting locked-in baby boomer wealth. Boomers hold $17 trillion in home equity — about half the US total — while the median home buyer born year has barely moved between 2007 and 2024. Hosts acknowledge both a structural affordability problem and a demographic distortion driven by the largest generational cohort.

### Consumer Sentiment, Social Security, and Miscellaneous Macro [00:00:04]

Michigan consumer sentiment reached its second-lowest level on record with one-year inflation expectations jumping to 7.3%, but hosts argue the surveys are unreliable because stated sentiment does not match actual spending behavior. Social Security early claiming rose 13% year-over-year, attributed to fear-mongering about program solvency rather than rational present-value analysis. Current macro snapshot — 2.3% inflation, ~4.2% unemployment, 4.5% 10-year — looks broadly normal despite fiscal concerns.
