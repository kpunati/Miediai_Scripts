# Is AI a Mistake? | Animal Spirits 452

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=SmhNN5X1ylo
**Duration:** 73:06

## Summary

In Animal Spirits episode 452, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examine the intense debate over AI's economic impact, concluding that while AI disruption is real and accelerating, near-term catastrophism about white-collar job destruction is likely overstated due to the complexity of actual job functions and enterprise switching costs. The hosts also cover a wave of AI-driven multiple compression in SaaS stocks, arguing the market is mis-diagnosing the risk for companies like S&P Global while correctly questioning moats at pure-play SaaS vendors. Additional segments cover Japan's equity market surge, Gen Z's shift from housing to brokerage investing, the rise of prediction markets as an alternative information source, and a critique of The Millionaire Next Door's frugality-centric worldview.

## Key Takeaways

- AI disruption is genuine and accelerating, but extrapolating from software engineering to all white-collar jobs ignores the breadth and relational complexity of most roles.
- Enterprise software buyers optimize for career risk, not unit cost, which gives incumbents like Salesforce and ServiceNow significant staying power despite AI coding tools.
- The market cannot simultaneously be correct that AI CapEx will not deliver ROI and that AI will destroy SaaS businesses — both cannot be true at once.
- Japanese equities are outperforming the S&P 500 over both 1-year and 5-year horizons as Japan exits financial repression and rising bond yields signal genuine economic normalization.
- Prediction markets are gaining institutional credibility, with research from NBER and London Business School showing crowd forecasters match or beat professional forecasters on economic indicators and earnings estimates.
- Gen Z is redirecting housing savings into brokerage accounts, with the share of 26-year-olds holding investment accounts rising from 8% to 40% between 2015 and 2025.
- The frugality thesis of The Millionaire Next Door is challenged: high income, not frugality, is the dominant driver of wealth accumulation for most households.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Impact Debate: Middle-Ground Position [00:02:23]

Batnick and Carlson position themselves as middle-grounders between the 'AI is vaporware' and 'AI will automate all white-collar jobs in 18 months' camps. They cite a listener email from a machine-learning engineer at a large payments company who describes task-completion time horizons doubling every seven months and expresses genuine fear about job security. Dario Amodei's Dwarkesh podcast appearance is referenced as evidence that AI insiders believe people are not prepared for what is coming, though his 10-20% GDP growth projection is questioned given that 70% of the economy is consumer spending.

### SaaS Valuation and Multiple Compression [00:07:01]

The hosts discuss why SaaS stocks like Salesforce and ServiceNow have been re-rated lower even without revenue collapses: markets are pricing out the premium multiple that assumed durable moats. A cited analysis distinguishes SaaS companies by whether they have proprietary data, regulatory lock-in, or embedded transactions; those lacking all three (e.g., FactSet) are seen as most vulnerable, while regulated data providers like S&P Global's ratings business are viewed as structurally protected. The symmetry of AI as a competitive weapon is highlighted — incumbents can deploy AI features to existing large user bases overnight, arguably advantaging them over startups.

### Stock Market Dynamics and International Rotation [00:30:59]

Amid AI-driven volatility in individual names, overall S&P 500 earnings season shows roughly 9% revenue growth, the highest since 2022. US versus rest-of-world equities are at their worst divergence start in decades, with Japan specifically highlighted: MSCI Japan is up over 40% in 12 months and is now outperforming the S&P over 5 years, driven by the end of financial repression and rising inflation expectations. The hosts also note the shift in S&P 500 company capital allocation away from buybacks toward CapEx since ChatGPT's launch in late 2022.

### Prediction Markets: Growth, Research, and Needed Guardrails [00:54:08]

DraftKings is highlighted as a cautionary tale — revenue up 43% yet the stock is in a 60% drawdown as prediction markets take wallet share. NBER research finds Kalshi bettors as accurate as professional forecasters on economic indicators, and London Business School and Yale find Polymarket bettors more accurate on corporate earnings than sell-side analysts. Interactive Brokers founder Thomas Peterffy calls prediction markets potentially the biggest development in financial markets in 100 years. Batnick advocates for guardrails including banning prop bets on individual athlete performance and closing markets ahead of events where outcomes could be leaked.

### Personal Finance: Millionaire Next Door Critique and Wealth Psychology [01:03:44]

A podcast critique of The Millionaire Next Door is discussed, arguing the book's survey was biased toward already-frugal respondents and overstates the role of frugality versus income in wealth accumulation. Both hosts agree that high income is the primary driver of savings and that excessive frugality as a life philosophy should be graduated from over time; the book's positive influence is acknowledged for young earners with limited income but deemed less applicable as earnings grow.
