# The Blue Chips of Junk | TCAF 175

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3OLs7I8eP0U
**Duration:** 97:43

## Summary

Bob Elliott of Unlimited Funds joins Michael Batnick and Josh Brown for a wide-ranging macro discussion at the start of 2025. Elliott argues that equity markets are pricing in consensus-best-case scenarios across earnings growth, credit spreads, and economic conditions, leaving little margin of safety when those expectations inevitably disappoint. The conversation covers Netflix as a paradigm-breaking compounder, the structural degradation of junk bond markets as lower-quality credits shift to private lending, an actionable case for TIPS at 2.5% real yields, the role of central bank gold buying, and the structural problems plaguing both hedge fund fee extraction and private equity liquidity.

## Key Takeaways

- Market expectations entering 2025 are at consensus-high levels across earnings growth, credit spreads, and economic conditions, meaning even modest disappointment could trigger a repricing cycle.
- Netflix exemplifies a class of paradigm-breaking compounders that traditional valuation metrics like CAPE ratios cannot adequately capture, as their network effects turn competitive entrants into market validators.
- The publicly traded junk bond market (HYG) now represents the 'blue chips of junk' because weaker credits have migrated to private credit markets, making tight public spreads an unreliable recession warning signal.
- TIPS offering 2.5% real yields are as attractive as they have been since before the Global Financial Crisis, providing a guaranteed real return benchmark against which equity risk premiums look increasingly thin.
- Central bank gold buying, especially by China amid capital controls and currency weakness, has created a structural bid for gold that is largely independent of US real yields.
- Private equity and venture capital distribution rates have collapsed to roughly 5%, trapping institutional capital and driving fund managers to aggressively target wealth management clients as a new liquidity source.
- Hedge fund fees have consumed over half of gross performance industry-wide over two decades, with allocator career-risk incentives sustaining flows to underperforming brand-name managers.

## Detailed Notes

### Cold open: OpenAI Operator and AI agentic capabilities [00:00:00]

The pre-show discussion covers OpenAI's newly released Operator product, which enables AI agents to complete real-world transactions such as restaurant reservations, grocery orders via Instacart, and event ticket purchases. The hosts debate trust barriers around giving AI bots credit card access and Apple's lagging position in conversational AI relative to competitors.

### Sponsor introduction and guest background [00:08:27]

The show formally opens with a sponsor segment for KraneShares Man Buyout Beta Index ETF (BUYO), described as replicating PE buyout fund characteristics using publicly traded small- and mid-cap companies. Bob Elliott is introduced as Co-Founder, CEO, and CIO of Unlimited Funds and former head of research at Bridgewater.

### DEI, ESG, and debanking under Trump administration [00:10:34]

Elliott and the hosts discuss Trump's public pressure on banks to stop 'debanking' conservative groups, the rapid corporate retreat from DEI language, and Elliott's prediction that ESG investment mandates will be the next target. Elliott notes that corporations' swift compliance reflects fiduciary and political calculations rather than conviction, and distinguishes rhetoric from actual policy impact.

### Market consensus and the difficulty of beating elevated expectations [00:18:59]

Elliott argues that 2025 begins with consensus expectations already at maximum optimism — strong earnings growth priced in for the broader S&P 493, tight credit spreads, elevated equity multiples, and pro-business rhetoric fully reflected in asset prices. He contrasts this with 2023 and 2024, when growth surprised well above depressed expectations, providing rocket fuel for equity returns that cannot repeat at current starting levels.

### Netflix and paradigm-breaking growth stocks [00:32:40]

Brown uses Netflix's record Q4 subscriber and revenue beat to illustrate why macro and value-based frameworks have consistently underpriced a class of compounders. Netflix's competitive moat was validated rather than eroded by rivals entering streaming, making traditional CAPE-based valuation frameworks inadequate. Elliott agrees that high market-share-capturing innovators are poorly captured by static valuation models designed for a 1950s industrial economy.

### The Blue Chips of Junk — private credit and HYG quality migration [00:42:12]

Elliott explains that the explosion of private credit / non-bank lending has pushed the weakest borrowers out of the public junk bond market into private markets, leaving HYG populated with higher-quality high-yield issuers. This means tight public junk spreads may not provide the early warning signal they historically did, and that even off-market lower-quality credits are currently trading at historically tight spreads.

### Labor market deterioration signals [00:48:31]

Elliott and Brown discuss a chart from Warren Pies showing the percentage of industries with year-over-year job gains turning negative, a level historically associated with recession. Elliott characterizes the current slowdown as a gradual late-cycle normalization driven by companies simply not hiring rather than active layoffs, and flags declining housing permits and slowing manufacturing investment as additional leading indicators turning negative.

### Trade policy uncertainty and tariff pricing [00:54:52]

A trade policy uncertainty index, based on automated media text searches, has spiked to levels only seen during Trump's first term. Elliott notes that current equity market pricing implies essentially zero tariff impact on earnings, as tariff-winner and tariff-loser stocks show near-identical earnings growth expectations, contrasting with emerging market currency markets that are pricing meaningful tariff risk.

### Bonds, TIPS, term premium, and debt spiral myths [00:59:00]

Elliott argues that TIPS at roughly 2.5% real yield are the most attractive they have been in 25 years and offer a risk-free real return that compresses the equity risk premium. He dismisses bond vigilante debt-spiral narratives by pointing to cross-market evidence: a strong dollar and rising stock prices accompanying rising bond yields is a pro-growth signal, not a fiscal distress signal. He notes Japan's far higher debt-to-GDP ratio without default as further evidence the narrative is politically rather than analytically driven.

### Gold allocation and central bank structural bid [01:10:34]

Elliott attributes gold's strength despite positive real US yields to central bank buying globally, particularly from China where capital controls and a weakening economy make gold one of the few accessible stores of value. He recommends a roughly 10% allocation to gold as a portfolio diversifier and draws on 50-year historical data showing gold and bonds providing similar portfolio outcomes, with gold particularly effective in the 50% of equity drawdowns where bonds fail to diversify.

### Hedge fund fees, private equity liquidity crisis, and Unlimited Funds pitch [01:22:03]

Elliott cites data showing hedge funds have taken over half of gross performance in fees over two decades, and highlights Baupost Group's decade of underperformance (roughly 4% annualized vs. S&P at 15%) as an example of how allocator career-risk incentives sustain flows to brand-name managers. He flags collapsing private equity and VC distribution rates (near 5%) as trapping institutional capital and driving the aggressive push into wealth management channels. Unlimited Funds is described as providing ETF-wrapped, low-cost access to hedge fund strategies by replicating manager positioning from regulatory filings.
