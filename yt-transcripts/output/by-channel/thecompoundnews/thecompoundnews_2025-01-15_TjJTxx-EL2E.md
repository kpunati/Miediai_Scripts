# A Key Market Signal | Animal Spirits 395

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=TjJTxx-EL2E
**Duration:** 61:01

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 395 opens with a discussion of the devastating Los Angeles wildfires, examining how homes serve as the primary financial asset for middle-class Americans and how California's regulatory environment contributed to an insurance market failure. The hosts then pivot to financial markets, explaining the post-election stock market roundtrip as a result of rising bond yields reflecting a stronger-than-expected economy, and discussing Howard Marks' bubble watch memo questioning the durability of Magnificent Seven dominance. The episode also covers the dramatic collapse of quantum computing stocks following Jensen Huang's 20-year timeline comment, social isolation trends from Derek Thompson's Atlantic cover story, and the performance of thematic versus target-date funds.

## Key Takeaways

- For middle-class Americans, the home is often their largest or only financial asset, making wildfire losses far more financially devastating than stock market losses.
- California's Prop 103 (1988) capped insurer premium increases behind government approval, leading major insurers to exit the state and leaving many homeowners without coverage.
- Stocks round-tripped their post-election gains as rising bond yields reflected a stronger economy, with the hosts framing this as a healthy correction rather than a worrying signal.
- Jensen Huang's comment that practical quantum computing is roughly 20 years away caused quantum computing stocks to drop 55-60% in a single session.
- Target-date fund investors significantly outperformed thematic fund investors over three years, illustrating the gap between product performance and investor returns driven by behavior.
- Derek Thompson's research shows adults spend 99 more minutes at home per day than in 2003, restaurant off-premise traffic reached 74%, and friend socialization is down 30% over 20 years.
- Howard Marks argues that today's Magnificent Seven companies may not maintain dominance for decades, citing how few of the top 20 S&P 500 companies from 2000 remain in that group today.

## Detailed Notes

### LA Wildfires and Home as Financial Asset [00:00:04]

Batnick and Carlson discuss the Los Angeles wildfires as potentially the costliest wildfire disaster in US history. They highlight that unlike stocks — where 10% of people own 90% of the market — real estate wealth is more evenly distributed, making the loss of a home catastrophically different for middle-class owners. The hosts note that California's Prop 103 created a perverse incentive structure where insurers could not raise premiums to reflect actual risk, causing many to exit the state.

### Market Pullback and Rising Bond Yields [00:00:04]

The stock market round-tripped its post-election gains, which the hosts attribute to rising bond yields driven by stronger-than-expected economic growth. Torsten Slok's chart shows global 10-year government bond yields rising in tandem, not just US Treasuries. Batnick frames the correction as healthy, noting that back-to-back 20% years in equities made the market prone to any selling catalyst.

### Howard Marks Bubble Watch and Magnificent Seven Durability [00:00:04]

Howard Marks' Oaktree memo compares the top 20 S&P 500 companies in 2000 versus 2024, showing significant turnover — only Microsoft from today's Magnificent Seven was in the top 20 in 2000. Marks argues investors treat today's leading companies as permanent leaders, which history suggests is unlikely. The hosts debate which Magnificent Seven member is most at risk of dropping out, with Carlson suggesting Meta and Batnick suggesting Apple.

### Quantum Computing Stock Collapse [00:00:04]

A listener email highlighted a 90-cent entry into quantum computing stocks that had risen near $10-20, only for Jensen Huang's comment estimating practical quantum computing at roughly 20 years away to cause 55-60% single-session drops. The hosts discuss the challenge of managing speculative positions, noting that catching 100-baggers requires tolerating extreme drawdowns and is better approached with a diversified VC-style portfolio.

### The Anti-Social Century and Social Isolation Trends [00:00:04]

Derek Thompson's Atlantic cover story documents a broad retreat from social activity: off-premise restaurant orders are now 74% of traffic (up from 61% pre-COVID), adults spend 99 more minutes at home daily versus 2003, and the share of 12th graders going out with friends twice a week fell from roughly 80% in 1980 to around 50-60% now. Batnick argues this trend increases the premium on housing quality and space, and that social media has been net-harmful for humanity despite connectivity benefits.

### Thematic Funds vs. Target-Date Funds and Investor Behavior [00:00:04]

Citing Jeffrey Ptak of Morningstar, the average dollar in thematic funds lost 7% per year over three years ending November 2024, versus a 6.9% gain for Target Date 2050 funds. Even excluding ARK, thematic fund investors lost nearly 6% annually, illustrating how chasing exciting themes versus staying in boring diversified vehicles dramatically damages investor outcomes.
