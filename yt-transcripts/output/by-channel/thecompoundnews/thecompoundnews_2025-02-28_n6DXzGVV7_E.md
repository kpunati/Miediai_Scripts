# Staying Bullish on Stocks Through Economic Pain | TCAF 180

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-02-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=n6DXzGVV7_E
**Duration:** 80:19

## Summary

In TCAF episode 180, Josh Brown and Michael Batnick interview Jim Lebenthal, Chief Equity Strategist at Cerity Partners, who argues for staying bullish on equities despite near-term economic headwinds from tariffs, DOGE-driven austerity, and slowing immigration. Lebenthal contends that the current market rotation away from mega-cap tech into financials, healthcare, and industrials is healthy and unusual in occurring without a broad market washout. He discusses specific stock picks—Cisco, AbbVie, and Delta—and frames the tariff situation as a temporary pain that will ultimately resolve in a sharp bull rally when tariffs are rolled back.

## Key Takeaways

- Jim Lebenthal maintains a bullish equity stance, arguing that strong corporate profits and full employment make a near-term recession unlikely despite policy uncertainty.
- The rotation away from Mag-7 and Nvidia into financials, REITs, and healthcare is viewed as healthy market broadening, not a precursor to a washout.
- Nvidia's post-earnings selloff is attributed to elevated expectations and leveraged ETF outflows rather than fundamental deterioration; the PEG ratio suggests the stock remains undervalued.
- Apple's $500 billion U.S. investment pledge is partly a tariff-avoidance strategy, with analysts estimating tariff savings that cover a large portion of the incremental spend.
- Lebenthal's thesis is that tariff-driven earnings downgrades will eventually be reversed when tariffs are reduced, triggering a significant rally beyond prior highs—a pattern he describes as 'the thing bears hate most.'
- Citigroup, Cisco Systems, AbbVie, and Delta Air Lines are highlighted as undervalued names with improving fundamentals and management execution.
- Consumer spending concentration among the top 10% of households (now over 50% of spending, up from ~35%) means the stock market and housing wealth are the primary variables to watch for economic resilience.

## Detailed Notes

### Cold Open and CNBC Career Background [00:00:00]

The episode opens with Jim Lebenthal recounting his time as a Navy nuclear submarine officer before transitioning to finance. He discusses his 12-year tenure as a CNBC Halftime Report contributor alongside Josh Brown, noting the importance of gratitude and staying bullish through periods like 2022 and the 2023 bank panic.

### Nvidia Earnings Reaction and Valuation [00:13:43]

Josh Brown reviews Nvidia's quarterly results, which showed 77% gap operating income growth and 93% data center revenue growth year-over-year but failed to deliver an upside shock, causing a roughly 6% stock decline. Lebenthal argues the PEG ratio near 0.7 with ~50% forward earnings growth makes the stock a bargain, while Brown notes zero-DTE options and leveraged ETF activity (NVDL outflows) amplify volatility disconnected from fundamentals.

### Market Broadening and Mag-7 Rotation [00:24:14]

The hosts observe that despite Nasdaq weakness, the broader market is holding up through rotation into financials, healthcare, and REITs—described as an unusual broadening without a full washout. Berkshire Hathaway crossing $500 billion in market cap and reporting $50 billion in annual earnings is cited as an example of large-cap strength outside Mag-7. AAII bearish sentiment at 61% while only 3% off highs is highlighted as an extreme contrarian signal.

### Apple's U.S. Investment and Tariff Strategy [00:28:24]

Apple's $500 billion four-year U.S. investment commitment is analyzed as primarily a tariff-avoidance strategy. Gene Munster's estimate suggests U.S. revenue at risk from a 20% tariff totals roughly $24 billion, meaning tariff savings could fund about 60% of the incremental announced investment. Critics note Apple routinely makes similar grand announcements every few years without fully executing on the stated dollar amounts.

### Tariffs, Reshoring, and GDP Outlook [00:45:06]

Lebenthal frames reshoring as a structural policy trend regardless of political views, with autarky aspirations being economically costly but directionally real. Steve Cohen's public call for GDP to slow to 1.5% in H2 2025 is discussed alongside treasury secretary Bessent's acknowledgment of near-term economic pain. Lebenthal agrees on short-term pain but believes the tariff cycle will follow a familiar pattern: downgrades followed by relief-driven rally overshooting prior highs.

### Consumer Spending and Labor Market [01:01:00]

The top 10% of households now account for over 50% of U.S. consumer spending, up from roughly 35% historically, making equity and housing wealth the dominant economic variables. Federal workforce reductions of potentially 500,000 workers are placed in context of a 175,000-per-month job creation pace, suggesting absorption over three months. Labor force contraction due to Baby Boomer retirements is cited as a structural support for unemployment remaining low.

### Stock Picks: Cisco, AbbVie, Delta Air Lines, Citigroup [01:03:08]

Cisco is favored for hardware-software diversification, the Splunk cybersecurity acquisition, and a likely re-rating from 16-17x toward market-average 21-22x earnings as its AI and cloud narratives gain traction—analogous to Oracle's multiple expansion. AbbVie replaced Humira revenue with Skyrizi and Rinvoq, trades at ~16x earnings with a 3.2% dividend yield. Delta is highlighted for operational excellence, fleet segmentation, and deleveraging that could support a re-rating to 10x earnings implying $75-85 per share. Citigroup's Jane Fraser-led restructuring and 40% share count reduction buying back below tangible book value remain a core holding.
