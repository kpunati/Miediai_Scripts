# It Feels Like 1999 | Animal Spirits 433

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ymvQIk6FFLY
**Duration:** 73:06

## Summary

In episode 433 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examine growing consensus that markets are entering a bubble melt-up phase reminiscent of 1999, drawing on Paul Tudor Jones's CNBC appearance and Derek Thompson's analysis. They assess the evidence for and against bubble conditions — including record hyperscaler capex projections, a surge in unprofitable stock performance, and retail trading froth — while debating how severe a crash would need to be to definitively confirm a bubble. The episode also covers structural cracks appearing in private credit through BDC underperformance, modest housing inventory normalization, and the growing wealth inequality narrative as top-1% holdings approach bottom-90% levels.

## Key Takeaways

- Paul Tudor Jones told CNBC the market 'feels like 1999' and could be more explosive, reversing prior recession warnings after the S&P crossed above its 200-day moving average.
- The hosts argue a true bubble burst would require the NASDAQ 100 to fall at least 50%, meaning smaller corrections prove nothing about whether a bubble existed.
- Hyperscaler AI capex of a projected $1.2 trillion from 2025-2027 makes bearish positioning feel nearly impossible even for those who believe the cycle will end badly.
- Junk and unprofitable tech stocks are outperforming dramatically since April lows, with nonprofitable tech up 105%, a classic late-cycle signal the hosts acknowledge but refuse to trade against.
- Private credit stress is emerging via BDC share price declines and two auto-sector bankruptcies, though the hosts disagree on systemic risk implications.
- Housing inventory is normalizing in roughly 40% of the 50 largest metros compared to 2019 levels, but the hosts expect any rate decline to 5% would immediately reignite demand.
- Wealth concentration data — top 1% holdings nearly equal to the bottom 90% combined — is prompting more inequality discussion, which the hosts expect to intensify as AI accelerates the trend.

## Detailed Notes

### Bubble or Melt-Up? The 1999 Analogy [00:00:04]

Paul Tudor Jones appeared on CNBC calling the market environment 'potentially more explosive than 1999' and attributing his prior bearish stance to the S&P being below its 200-day moving average. The hosts note broad consensus has formed around a blowoff-top thesis — everyone is bullish short-term but expects a bad ending — and argue this very consensus makes the prediction worthless since no one will be able to time the exit. They set a threshold: only a 50%+ NASDAQ decline would qualify as definitive bubble confirmation.

### AI Capex, Hyperscalers, and Market Concentration [00:00:04]

JP Morgan estimates five major AI hyperscalers will spend a combined $1.2 trillion from 2025-2027, and a Bridgewater-sourced chart showed nearly 40% of US real GDP growth last quarter was driven by tech capex. OpenAI announced a chip partnership with AMD, with Sam Altman describing it as incremental to existing Nvidia plans. The hosts observe the circular demand dynamic: Oracle buys from OpenAI, OpenAI buys from Nvidia, Nvidia buys from Oracle. Despite acknowledging concentration risk, they see no bearish entry point given the spending momentum.

### Retail Froth and Junk Stock Rally [00:00:04]

Bespoke data showed 19 stocks up 400% since Liberation Day lows, most being money-losing companies. Goldman's retail stock index registered 19 straight days of gains. Defiance launched multiple 3x leveraged ETFs. Nonprofitable tech stocks are up 105% since April lows. The hosts treat these as late-cycle signals but note the absence of the mass-quitting-jobs-to-day-trade phenomenon that characterized 1999, leaving the bubble verdict ambiguous.

### Private Credit and BDC Stress [00:00:04]

Two auto-sector companies (including First Brands) filed for bankruptcy with significant off-balance-sheet private credit exposure, raising canary-in-the-coalmine questions. BDC share prices — including Blue Owl and HSBD — are underperforming sharply. Man Group data shows private credit grew at 17% CAGR from 2016-2022 and is forecast to grow 11% from 2024-2028, reaching $3 trillion. Batnick bought ARCC as a contrarian position; Carlson remains agnostic on systemic risk.

### Housing, Wealth Inequality, and Personal Finance [00:00:04]

Inventory in 40% of the 50 largest metros now exceeds 2019 pre-pandemic levels, up from 26% a year ago, and completed new homes on market are at a 16-year high. The hosts expect normalization to reverse immediately if mortgage rates fall to 5%. On wealth inequality, Charter data showed the top 1% holds 31% of wealth versus 33% for the bottom 90%. Buy-now-pay-later use for groceries rose from 14% to 25% year-over-year, though the hosts compare this to credit card usage and are skeptical of alarm.
