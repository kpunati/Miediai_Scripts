# The Bears Capitulate | Animal Spirits 390

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=SzDAaVJWSKE
**Duration:** 67:57

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 390 features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson reviewing an exceptional 2024 stock market year marked by the S&P 500 returning 29% with minimal volatility and frequent new highs. The hosts reflect on the long-running CAPE ratio debate, arguing that the ratio's historical comparisons are misleading given the changing composition and profitability of index constituents. Bitcoin crossing $100,000 and the explosive growth of leveraged single-stock ETFs are highlighted as notable market developments, while housing undersupply and post-election business optimism provide broader economic context.

## Key Takeaways

- 2024 was one of the least volatile bull market years in recent memory, with 57 new all-time highs and only three down days exceeding 2%.
- The CAPE ratio's predictive value has been undermined by the structural shift toward highly profitable mega-cap tech companies that now dominate index earnings.
- Leveraged single-stock ETFs have exploded 12-fold in net assets over the past year, with Nvidia, MicroStrategy, and Tesla ETFs comprising 85% of the category.
- Bitcoin's rise to $100,000 and US spot ETFs becoming the largest Bitcoin holders validate the supply-demand thesis even though original crypto ideological arguments have not materialized.
- The US faces a significant housing supply deficit, needing at least 2 million new homes annually but adding only 1.4 million, a legacy of post-2008 construction collapse.
- Post-election consumer and small business optimism surged, with the hosts arguing that sentiment can be self-fulfilling for spending and investment behavior.
- Three durable inflation hedges identified: a good job with rising wages, a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage on a home, and long-term equity ownership.

## Detailed Notes

### 2024 Market Performance and Bear Capitulation [00:12:42]

The S&P 500 finished 2024 up approximately 29% following a 20% gain in 2023. The worst drawdown was only 8%, there were 57 new all-time highs, and just three days with declines exceeding 2%. Hosts note this follows two consecutive strong years and express concern that another 30% gain driven purely by multiple expansion — rather than earnings growth — would set up a dangerous correction. Both hosts assign roughly 85-90% probability to a 10%+ correction occurring at some point in 2025.

### CAPE Ratio Limitations and Market Valuation [00:14:49]

Batnick recounts his years-long pushback against Robert Shiller's cyclically adjusted P/E ratio, arguing that comparing current valuations to a century-old historical average ignores the structural shift toward highly profitable mega-cap technology companies. He notes that in 2018 Research Affiliates cited and challenged his view; he now claims vindication. Data from a Kelvin Kench analyst shows the top 25 S&P companies represent 49% of both market cap and net income, suggesting current concentration is justified by earnings rather than speculation alone.

### Leveraged Single-Stock ETFs and Capital Flows [00:22:11]

A chart from Jeffrey Ptak shows net assets in single-stock leveraged long ETFs have risen 12 times in the past 12 months. Nvidia, MicroStrategy, and Tesla ETFs account for roughly 85% of all assets in the category. Monthly net inflows into US equities hit $140 billion post-election according to the Financial Times. Foreign private purchases of US stocks also reached an all-time high in September, suggesting international investors are driving some of the demand surge.

### Bitcoin at $100,000 [00:39:53]

Bitcoin crossed $100,000, recovering from $15,000 in November 2022 during the crypto winter. US spot Bitcoin ETFs, not yet one year old, now hold over 1.1 million Bitcoin — more than any single entity including the presumed Satoshi Nakamoto holdings of approximately 1.1 million coins. Batnick frames his own Bitcoin thesis as purely supply-demand driven rather than ideological. Contributor Jack Raines argues the original decentralized-currency narrative is effectively dead, with the asset now centralized through major exchanges and ETFs, better described as digital gold.

### Global Wealth, Housing Supply, and Inflation [00:28:27]

UBS Global Wealth Report data shows 8% of US adults are millionaires (22 million people, 38% of global millionaires), while 75% of the global adult population holds under $100,000 in wealth. On housing, the US currently builds about 1.4 million homes per year for a population of 335 million, versus 2.4 million homes in 1972 for 200 million people; experts suggest 2 million annually is needed to address the shortfall. Hosts argue that inflation cannot meaningfully be reversed — businesses routinely raise prices to grow revenue — and that the best individual hedges are employment, fixed-rate mortgage homeownership, and equity ownership.
