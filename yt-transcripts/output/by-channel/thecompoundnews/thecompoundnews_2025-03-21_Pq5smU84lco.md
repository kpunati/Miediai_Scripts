# "How Not to Invest" by Barry Ritholtz

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Pq5smU84lco
**Duration:** 36:52

## Summary

Josh Brown interviews Barry Ritholtz at the launch of his book 'How Not to Invest,' which catalogs common investment mistakes organized around bad ideas, problematic numbers, and behavioral failures. Ritholtz frames the book around the insight — borrowed from Charlie Ellis and Charlie Munger — that the path to investment success is eliminating errors rather than finding alpha. He discusses economic innumeracy as a pervasive problem, using examples such as the misleading 'dollar lost 96% of purchasing power' narrative and the misuse of non-farm payroll data. The conversation ends with practical advice: invest with a purpose, understand the limits of stock-picking skill, and avoid panic-driven portfolio exits.

## Key Takeaways

- Ritholtz's book argues that the primary driver of poor investment outcomes is avoidable behavioral mistakes, not lack of information about what to buy.
- Charlie Ellis's 'Winning the Loser's Game' analogy — amateur tennis players lose by making unforced errors, not by failing to hit winners — directly inspired the book's thesis.
- Economic innumeracy, such as misunderstanding cumulative inflation versus real returns or treating monthly non-farm payroll noise as signal, leads investors to make systematically poor decisions.
- Henry Bessembinder's research shows that less than 2% of all stocks account for all equity market returns, making concentrated individual stock-picking a statistically near-impossible task.
- Fund managers are skilled stock buyers but poor sellers, with random selling outperforming deliberate selling decisions by hundreds of basis points in academic simulations.
- 31% of investors who exit equities in a panic never return, representing a severe and irreversible financial cost.
- Having a clearly defined financial purpose — retirement, college funding, philanthropy — is the first and most important determinant of appropriate risk-taking.

## Detailed Notes

### Book Origin and Structure [00:01:16]

Ritholtz explains the book grew from a pattern of client and reader emails asking about investment mistakes, which he organized on 3x5 index cards in his home office. He notes the book's physical size is partly illusory: the last 150 pages are footnotes and chapters are 1.5 to 3 pages each, structured for rapid reading. The book is divided into three sections: bad ideas people believe, numbers that trip people up, and how those ideas manifest in bad behavior.

### Intellectual Foundations: Ellis and Munger [00:06:34]

Ritholtz dedicated the book to Charlie Ellis and Charlie Munger, whose works converge on the insight that investment success comes from error-reduction rather than superior stock selection. Ellis argued that amateur investors, like amateur tennis players, lose through unforced errors rather than failure to hit aces. Munger's formulation — 'it's not that we're smarter, we're just less stupid' — reinforces the same principle. Ritholtz also cites neurologist-investor Bill Bernstein's warning that failure to control the limbic system will lead to poverty.

### Behavioral Pitfalls and the Dunning-Krueger Effect [00:12:58]

Ritholtz recounts the 2020-2021 pandemic trading boom, when retail investors conflated a rising market with personal skill and began launching funds. He identifies the limbic system's dopamine-driven feedback loop as the neurological root of overconfidence after winning trades. He uses his own experience of a successful but subsequently reversed out-of-the-money NASDAQ call purchase in October 2022 as a personal example of the overconfidence trap.

### Economic Innumeracy Examples [00:21:30]

Ritholtz highlights three commonly misunderstood numbers. First, the claim that the dollar has lost 96% of its value over a century ignores that salaries and invested wealth have risen correspondingly — a $1,000 investment in 1925 would be worth approximately $32 million today at 8-10% annual returns. Second, monthly non-farm payroll figures are noise: roughly 3.9 million people enter and exit the workforce each month, and the reported 100,000-200,000 net change is a rounding error within a 165-million-person workforce. Third, Henry Bessembinder's research shows less than 2% of stocks globally and 1.5% in the US account for all stock market returns.

### Worst Investing Mistakes and Practical Guidance [00:29:56]

Ritholtz identifies not having a financial plan as the top mistake, arguing money is a tool that must be invested toward a defined purpose — retirement, education funding, philanthropy — which then determines risk tolerance and time horizon. The second major mistake is Dunning-Krueger-driven overconfidence in stock-picking and market-timing ability, compounded by the finding that 31% of panic-sellers never return to equities. Ritholtz and Brown close by endorsing broadly diversified, low-cost index portfolios as the practical antidote to these behavioral failures.
