# The 3 Biggest Investing Lessons of the Year | WDWL

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-12-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=tHgA_GNdtes
**Duration:** 35:40

## Summary

Nick Colas and Jessica Rabe of DataTrek Research join Josh Brown to distill three big investing lessons from a volatile 2025 that nonetheless produced strong equity gains. The first lesson is that policymakers from the Fed to the White House consistently chose to protect economic growth over ideological consistency, as seen in Trump's tariff capitulation and the Fed's December rate cut despite a government shutdown that obscured economic data. The second lesson contextualizes elevated S&P 500 valuations by pointing to record-high corporate net margins achieved without stimulus, arguing that high P/E ratios are fundamentally justified when profitability is structurally higher. The third lesson argues that generative AI's enormous capital requirements are shifting corporate strategy back from Christensen's low-end disruption model toward Chandler's scale-and-scope model, favoring well-capitalized incumbents like Alphabet over capital-light startups.

## Key Takeaways

- Policymakers in 2025 repeatedly erred on the side of economic stability over dogma, providing a useful framework for investors to lean bullish when policy uncertainty peaks.
- S&P 500 forward P/E of 22 times is near a 25-year high but is justified by S&P 500 net margins of 13.1%, which match the stimulus-era peak without any artificial support.
- Earnings revisions for 2025 and 2026 broke the historical pattern of downward drift, rising since May-July as companies beat estimates by unusually wide margins for multiple consecutive quarters.
- Real 10-year Treasury yields at 2% are identical to the 2003-2007 period despite debt-to-GDP doubling to 125%, indicating no meaningful market concern about US sovereign credit.
- Seasonal data shows the S&P 500 has peaked for the year in December more than half the time since 1980, supporting a year-end rally thesis alongside the catalyst of a Fed rate cut.
- Generative AI's capital intensity means the Christensen disruption playbook is giving way to a Chandler scale-and-scope model; vertically integrated companies like Alphabet have structural advantages over capital-dependent startups like OpenAI.
- Companies responded to the 2025 trade shock by aggressively improving efficiency without cutting headcount, driving broad-based margin improvement across six or seven of eleven S&P 500 sectors.

## Detailed Notes

### 2025 Market Overview: Volatility and Resilience [00:02:15]

Nick Colas recaps 2025 as a master class in market behavior: a calm start, pandemic-level VIX above 50 in April from trade policy shock, a vicious rotation into non-US assets with questions about dollar and Treasury safety, then a strong recovery driven by policy changes and generative AI investment. Despite all of this, the S&P 500 gained 15-16% and trades at 22 times forward earnings near a 25-year valuation peak. The longest-ever US government shutdown also caused lapses in economic data series tracked since 1948.

### Lesson 1: Policymakers Choose Growth Over Dogma [00:06:06]

Jessica Rabe argues that 2025 demonstrated policymakers will err on the side of economic stability, illustrated by Trump's tariff capitulation and the Fed's December rate cut. DataTrek used non-traditional labor market indicators, specifically Google Trends search volumes for 'find job' and 'new job' reaching 20-year highs, to predict a December cut even when fed funds futures assigned it less than 50% probability two weeks prior. Unemployment rising to 4.4% provided the data backdrop, and NY Fed President John Williams's speech subsequently pushed futures to 87% odds for a December cut.

### Lesson 2: Valuations Require Margin Context [00:11:51]

Nick Colas shows S&P 500 forward P/E multiples from 2000 to the present, ranging from 22 at the dotcom peak to 9 during the financial crisis and back to 21-23 today against a 25-year average of 16. He argues the chart is misleading without context: S&P 500 net margins of 13.1% in Q3 2025 match the stimulus-era Q2 2021 peak despite no fiscal or monetary support, justifying higher valuations via higher return on capital. Earnings estimate revisions also broke the historical downward drift pattern, rising since May-July as companies beat by 10% or more each quarter, driven by corporate efficiency efforts in response to trade uncertainty.

### Treasury Markets and Real Rates [00:23:23]

Nick presents real 10-year Treasury yields averaging 2%, identical to the 2003-2007 period, despite US debt-to-GDP rising from 70% to 125%. The absence of any risk premium spike in real rates is presented as evidence that the bond market does not currently price any meaningful sovereign credit concern. The chart shows QE was itself triggered by a spike in real rates to 3% during the November 2008 financial crisis; at 2% today, there is no market signal of a treasury safety problem.

### Lesson 3: Corporate Strategy Shifts Back to Scale and Scope [00:26:16]

The third lesson draws on Alfred Chandler's scale-and-scope framework (economies of scale, economies of scope, management structure) as the 1990 counterpoint to Clayton Christensen's disruption model, which dominated corporate thinking since the late 1990s. Generative AI's enormous capital requirements are shifting the balance back: vertically integrated incumbents like Alphabet, which own data centers, chips, software, and distribution, hold structural advantages over capital-dependent startups like OpenAI. Jessica illustrates the disruption arc using Amazon's ascent from books to broader e-commerce, Japanese automakers moving from small cars to luxury segments, and smartphones disrupting PCs in developing markets. The thesis is that the AI era will be a hybrid of both models, roughly 50/50 Chandler and Christensen.
