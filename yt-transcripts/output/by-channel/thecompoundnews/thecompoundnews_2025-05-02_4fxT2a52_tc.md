# Every Recession Begins With a Slowdown | TCAF 190

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-05-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=4fxT2a52_tc
**Duration:** 84:36

## Summary

Neil Dutta of Renaissance Macro Research joins Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to make the case that the US economy was already weakening before tariffs — with sluggish hiring, falling quits rates, and decelerating wage growth — and that the tariff shock has accelerated a slowdown that markets have not fully priced. The episode explores the striking divergence between large-cap tech (Microsoft, Meta, Amazon) reporting explosive earnings and sectors like trucking, airlines, and small business facing severe headwinds. Dutta argues the Fed should be cutting but is constrained by inflation optics, and that consumer confidence deterioration is hard to reverse once it sets in. The title phrase — 'every recession begins with a slowdown' — captures Dutta's core warning that current conditions rhyme with early-cycle recessionary setups.

## Key Takeaways

- The US economy was already slowing before Liberation Day tariffs, with private sector wage growth under 3% and labor market metrics like quits rates and hiring rates at multi-year lows.
- Large-cap tech (Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, Alphabet) is effectively acting as a defensive sector, with cloud and advertising revenue resilient even as trucking, airlines, and small business deteriorate sharply.
- Dutta believes the Fed should be cutting rates but Powell is constrained by inflation optics from tariffs, creating a policy error risk similar to the 2018 hiking cycle.
- Consumer confidence has dropped to levels not seen even during the Great Recession, with 44% of Americans expecting to be worse off financially in a year — a condition hard to reverse quickly.
- Retail investors net bought a record $40 billion in April, buying both the April 3rd crash and the April 9th rally, while Wall Street strategists aggressively cut year-end S&P targets.
- Dutta warns that even a mild recession may produce a prolonged, soggy recovery because fiscal space is limited and the confidence shock resembles the 2001-2003 period.
- Section 232 national security tariffs on pharma and semiconductors are still coming, meaning the trade war is not over even if reciprocal tariff negotiations de-escalate.

## Detailed Notes

### Stock Market vs. Real Economy Divergence [00:00:00]

Dutta frames the equity rebound as a disconnect: the macro outlook has not improved, but peak trade policy uncertainty may have passed. The rally is concentrated in large-cap tech — Microsoft, Meta, Google — while trucking, energy, and small caps remain depressed. Josh Brown argues mega-cap tech platforms may now function as 'defensive utilities' given consumers will cancel most services before cutting Netflix or cloud subscriptions.

### Pre-Existing Economic Weakness and Labor Market [00:00:00]

Dutta argues the economy was deeply imbalanced entering 2025: frozen housing market, low quits and hiring rates, declining job openings, and state/local governments set to spend less as pandemic relief funds dried up. Private sector wages and salaries grew less than 3% year-over-year through March — below the Fed funds rate — signaling household budget stress that predates tariff impacts. Continuing claims at 2 million signal rising unemployment difficulty rather than a surge in layoffs.

### Federal Reserve Policy and Tariff Inflation [00:00:00]

Dutta believes the Fed should be cutting rates to match the slowing economy, and that absent the trade war they likely would have telegraphed a March cut. Powell is constrained by inflation optics — tariffs are a supply shock, not demand-driven inflation — but Dutta argues tariffs destroy demand over time, reducing prices for non-tariffed goods and ultimately warranting cuts. Treasury Secretary Bessent has publicly stated the 2-year Treasury is signaling rates are too tight.

### Consumer Confidence and Recession Risk [00:00:00]

Every major consumer confidence metric is deteriorating sharply; 44% of Americans expect to be worse off financially in a year, unprecedented even versus the Great Recession. Consumption has been growing at roughly twice the pace of disposable income over the last year (~3% vs. ~1.5%), a pace that is unsustainable and points to a rising savings rate ahead. Dutta forecasts GDP near zero for 2025 and believes Wall Street's consensus for 9-12% earnings growth is incompatible with that nominal growth environment.

### AI Capex and Single-Cylinder Economy [00:00:00]

Dutta and Brown discuss how the market is hanging on reaffirmation of AI capital spending from Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta — Azure grew 33%, AWS 17%, and Google Cloud 28% year-over-year — while ignoring misses from McDonald's, UPS, and materials companies. Dutta warns that AI investment will eventually hit a saturation point, meaning the economy may be firing on one cylinder just as that cylinder risks overcapacity, creating a soggy recovery even after Fed rate cuts.

### Retail vs. Institutional Investor Divergence [00:00:00]

Wall Street strategists aggressively cut year-end S&P targets (Deutsche Bank from 7,000 to 6,150; Oppenheimer from 7,100 to 5,950), while retail investors bought a record $40 billion in April. Dutta and Brown conclude that retail capitulation would come from the duration of a bear market rather than its depth, and that first-time dip buyers are conditioned by years of 'buy the dip' success.
