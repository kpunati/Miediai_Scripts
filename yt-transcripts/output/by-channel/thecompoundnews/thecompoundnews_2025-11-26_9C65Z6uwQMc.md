# Wait, Are We in a Recession??? | Animal Spirits 440

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=9C65Z6uwQMc
**Duration:** 64:56

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 440, recorded on the Tuesday before Thanksgiving 2025, opens with Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson dissecting an unusual intraday market reversal in which the S&P 500 opened sharply higher on blowout Nvidia earnings before closing well into negative territory — a pattern historically associated with market tops. The hosts debate whether the AI-driven rally has run its course, weigh labor market deterioration data against resilient consumer spending, and work through macro themes including cumulative inflation, Bitcoin's disappointing price action despite a pro-crypto White House, and the structural shift of private credit into the retail wealth channel. Ben's ongoing observations from a Disney vacation serve as a real-time anecdote on consumer spending resilience and the K-shaped economy.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500's intraday gap-up/reversal on Nvidia earnings day was statistically rare and historically has appeared near market tops, though subsequent days showed no follow-through selling.
- High yield credit spreads (JNK) remain near cycle lows, which the hosts cite as the key signal that broad financial stress has not materialized despite equity volatility.
- US private sector job growth outside healthcare and leisure/hospitality has been negative for five consecutive months, raising genuine recession questions that the hosts view as ambiguous rather than conclusive.
- Cumulative goods and services inflation of roughly 25% above 2020 levels is causing persistent consumer pain even as the annual inflation rate has normalized, partly because no recessionary deflationary episode occurred to reset expectations.
- Bitcoin dropped roughly 30-35% despite the most favorable regulatory and political environment in its history (pro-crypto administration, spot ETFs), which Carlson calls surprising and a challenge to the digital-gold/debasement-trade narrative.
- Private credit expanding into the retail wealth management channel will likely compress fees and may dilute gross returns, but Batnick argues the net effect for retail investors could roughly offset because institutions have historically paid excessively high fees.
- Disney's packed parks and the ability to pay for line-skipping passes offer a live illustration of the K-shaped economy: most attendees are not wealthy, yet economic stress is not visibly dampening discretionary consumer spending.

## Detailed Notes

### Nvidia Earnings Reversal and Market Behavior [00:02:01]

Nvidia reported blowout earnings, initially popped 6% after-hours, but sold off sharply on the open and dragged the broader market lower. The hosts note that failing to rally on great news is a classic late-cycle signal; however, Friday and Monday showed strong breadth recovery with 444 S&P 500 stocks rising Friday — the best since late May.

### AI Bubble Comparisons and Current Market Valuation [00:11:52]

The S&P 500 P/E multiple has remained at roughly 24.5x all year, meaning all 2025 gains came from earnings growth rather than multiple expansion. Hosts argue this dynamic is healthier than 1990s-style euphoria, citing Bespoke data showing the Nasdaq's rolling 5-year return is far below dot-com peak levels. GMO's Ben Inker and Damodaran are cited as notable bears, while Carlson floats a contrarian bull case of another 20% S&P gain in 2026.

### Labor Market, Recession Risk, and Inflation Burden [00:15:44]

A tweet citing September jobs data claims private-sector employment ex-healthcare and leisure has declined five straight months — unprecedented outside recessions in 35 years. The hosts treat this as a concern but note the labor market is normalizing from an historically hot 2021 baseline. On inflation, they estimate the US ran above 3% average only from April 2021 to May 2023, but the absence of a recession means no deflationary correction occurred, leaving cumulative prices roughly 25% above 2020 levels.

### Google vs. AI Competition and Rotation [00:24:40]

Mark Benioff's tweet praising Gemini over ChatGPT added more market cap to Google than Salesforce's entire value. Hosts observe Google's chart has diverged sharply higher from the Nasdaq 100 since July, and argue that AI investment winners rotate (OpenAI, then Nvidia, then Oracle, now Google) — reinforcing a picks-and-shovels Nasdaq 100 approach over single-name AI bets.

### Bitcoin and Crypto Outlook [00:28:31]

Bitcoin fell roughly 30-35% from its high (~$126k) despite a pro-crypto US administration and spot ETFs. Carlson notes this undermines the debasement-trade and digital-gold narratives since gold is up nearly 60% on the same thesis. He sold roughly a third of his position near $121k and has started buying back around $82k; Batnick similarly began re-entering the position.

### Housing Market and Consumer Resilience [00:35:17]

Logan from HousingWire argues that survey-based data on home buyer demographics may be severely flawed (6,000 responses from 170,000 sent). Separately, The Economist data cited shows US mortgage debt has fallen to a 25-year low vs. GDP and a 60-year low vs. total housing value, with ~40% of homeowners mortgage-free; TransUnion's CEO confirms consumers remain resilient with manageable delinquency levels.

### Private Credit: Retail Expansion and Institutional Concerns [00:39:04]

Institutional investors (e.g., Teacher Retirement System of Texas, a $221 billion pension) are alarmed that the flood of retail money into private equity could erode their returns. Hosts counter that (1) private credit sitting outside the banking system is structurally safer than 2008 bank-balance-sheet leverage, and (2) fee compression from retail competition may offset lower gross returns on a net-of-fee basis for all investors.

### Disney as K-Shaped Economy Anecdote [00:46:23]

Ben reports Disney parks are as busy as ever during the Thanksgiving holiday week, with people across income levels attending. Lightning Lane passes that allow paying customers to skip queues illustrate the K-shaped dynamic in microcosm: those who can pay move faster, but the broader crowd is still engaged and spending, consistent with the hosts' view that economic pessimism overstates how many households are struggling.
