# Why Retail is Outperforming | Animal Spirits 432

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Mk3rSegO3Fc
**Duration:** 52:27

## Summary

In episode 432 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson argue that the AI boom — not politics — has been the singular driver of stock market performance since late 2022, with AI-linked stocks accounting for the vast majority of S&P 500 returns and earnings growth. They debate whether current AI capital spending constitutes a bubble, concluding the spending itself looks bubble-like even if valuations of the underlying companies may be supported by strong fundamentals. The conversation broadens to cover rising wealth inequality, improved retail investor behavior through index fund adoption, private equity's slow-distribution problem, and data showing US disposable incomes far outpacing peer nations like the UK.

## Key Takeaways

- AI-related stocks have driven roughly 75% of S&P 500 returns, 80% of earnings growth, and 90% of capex growth since ChatGPT launched in November 2022.
- Both hosts believe current hyperscaler AI spending levels exhibit bubble-like characteristics, though the strong fundamental earnings of the companies involved complicate a clean bubble call.
- Emerging markets have been the best-performing asset class in 2025, surprising given the US-centric AI narrative; Chinese internet stocks are a significant contributor.
- Retail investor behavior has structurally improved: the most popular ETF is now VOO (Vanguard S&P 500), reflecting a broad shift toward low-cost index investing over speculation.
- The top 10% owning 87% of US stocks versus the bottom 90% owning most housing means rising stock prices disproportionately benefit wealthy households and structurally entrenches wealth inequality.
- Private equity faces a slow-moving distribution crisis: at current payout rates it would take approximately nine years to return capital to investors, complicating new fund raising.
- American disposable incomes are substantially higher than in the UK and most peer nations, even after accounting for healthcare and education costs.

## Detailed Notes

### AI as the Only Thing That Matters [00:04:32]

Batnick contends that regardless of which political party held power, the AI boom since November 2022 has been the dominant factor for markets. Data cited: AI-linked stocks account for 75% of S&P 500 returns, 80% of earnings growth, and 90% of capital spending growth. Strong earnings growth in the 2010s (~11% per year) and 2020s (~9%) are cited as the real explanation for the bull market rather than Fed policy alone.

### AI Bubble Debate [00:06:34]

Batnick calls current AI spending a bubble by historical standards, drawing parallels to the railway and dot-com bubbles; both hosts acknowledge the difficulty of timing such a call when underlying earnings are genuinely strong. Historical analogs include Charles Darwin losing 60% of his net worth in the railway bubble. The hosts also discuss two possible outcomes: the bubble pops and markets crash, or AI succeeds and causes significant labor displacement — neither is clearly desirable.

### Market Concentration, Quantum Speculation, and Investor Behavior [00:10:12]

Jurrien Timmer's analysis shows current top-heavy market concentration could persist for years, as it did in the 1950s-60s, rather than reversing quickly as in 2000. Four quantum computing stocks with minimal revenue traded at 371 times combined 2025 revenue estimates after gaining ~2,750% on average. Despite speculative pockets, Bank of America data shows retail, institutional, and hedge fund clients were all net sellers the prior week, suggesting no broad euphoria.

### Retail Investor Behavior and Index Fund Adoption [00:21:30]

Jason Zweig's retrospective on Jonathan Clements highlights how conventional wisdom in the 1990s treated index funds as inferior and high-cost brokers as necessary — beliefs now widely discredited. The hosts credit improved investor behavior to greater access to information and the shift toward low-cost index ETFs, with VOO leading all ETF inflows. Robinhood is viewed more positively in hindsight for bringing new investors into the market.

### Economy, Inequality, and Housing [00:26:20]

Final Q2 GDP came in at 3.8% with healthy consumption; the Atlanta Fed GDPNow for the upcoming quarter sits near 3.9%. Consumer sentiment tracks closely with stock market performance for households earning over $100,000, while lower-income households are more tied to the frozen housing market. The hosts note that 87% of equities are owned by the top 10%, meaning stock market gains primarily benefit wealthier households and wealth inequality is unlikely to be structurally resolved.

### Real Estate and Mortgage Market [00:30:47]

A listener corrects earlier data: banks preferentially hold adjustable-rate mortgages on balance sheet because ARMs carry near-zero interest rate duration risk, while fixed-rate loans are sold to agencies. The hosts discuss the host's personal experience taking a 6.5% jumbo ARM with a 2% closing cost credit, then rate-modifying down to 5.375% after rates fell — effectively functioning as a bond trader on his own mortgage.

### Private Equity Distribution Slowdown [00:41:54]

Bloomberg analysis indicates PE firms are seeking $3.3 trillion in new commitments while holding ~$1.2 trillion in dry powder, with at-current-payout-rate timelines of approximately nine years to return capital from more than 12,000 held companies. The hosts do not foresee a crisis, noting firms have contractual flexibility to extend fund terms and are incentivized to keep assets alive rather than force distressed sales.

### US Wealth vs. Other Countries [00:44:04]

A Telegraph article finds roughly 40% of US households have after-tax disposable earnings above £70,000, versus only 10% in the UK. The top 10% US disposable income is £153,000 versus £71,000 in the UK. The average earner in Mississippi — the poorest US state — is described as substantially better off than the average British worker.
