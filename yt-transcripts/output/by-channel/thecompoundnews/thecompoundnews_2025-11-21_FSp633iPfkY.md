# The Case for a Year-End Melt-Up | TCAF 218

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FSp633iPfkY
**Duration:** 59:46

## Summary

This episode of The Compound and Friends brings in Warren Pies of 314 Research to analyze Nvidia's exceptional earnings beat, including a 21% sequential jump in net income and strong Q4 guidance, framing it as a sentiment-clearing event rather than a fundamental surprise. Pies presents evidence for a year-end market melt-up driven by favorable seasonality, resuming corporate buybacks, and sentiment that has finally corrected from extreme optimism. The discussion pivots to the K-shaped economy, with housing affordability — the median home now requiring the 60th percentile of income versus the 40th pre-pandemic — as the defining pressure on lower-income households. Pies argues the Fed will respond to this lower-K pressure with continued rate cuts, which in turn supports the ongoing equity bull market dominated by large-cap tech.

## Key Takeaways

- Nvidia's blowout quarter, with net income up 65% YoY and strong Blackwell guidance, resolved a sentiment-driven correction rather than a fundamental one.
- Market breadth is historically narrow, with fewer than 24% of S&P 500 stocks beating the index over three years, exceeding even the tech bubble era.
- Corporate buybacks resume after the earnings blackout period just as seasonals turn favorable, setting up a year-end rally driven by the same mega-cap leaders.
- The K-shaped economy is best illustrated by housing: the median home now requires the 60th income percentile, pricing out the middle class and generating political pressure for Fed easing.
- Policy proposals like 50-year and portable mortgages are marginal at best; structural supply constraints and fiscal deficits running at 7-8% of GDP are the root causes.
- Small caps are not the buy despite easing expectations and higher earnings growth forecasts — those estimates have repeatedly disappointed and early-cycle conditions are absent.
- Margin expansion of 250 basis points expected through 2027 historically correlates with multiple expansion, not contraction, validating continued bullish positioning in large-cap tech.

## Detailed Notes

### Nvidia Earnings and AI Sentiment Reset [00:02:12]

Nvidia entered earnings in one of its largest pre-report drawdowns, with sentiment stretched after a prolonged AI-driven rally. Jensen Huang delivered another beat-and-raise quarter — net income $32B (up 21% sequentially, 65% YoY), data center up 66%, and Q4 revenue guidance of $65B (14% sequential growth). The CFO addressed GPU useful-life concerns by noting CUDA-enabled A100s shipped six years ago are still running at full utilization.

### Market Breadth and Narrowness [00:12:12]

Only 23-24% of S&P 500 stocks are beating the index on a trailing three-year basis, a level that surpasses the peak narrowness of the dot-com bubble. More S&P constituents are trailing the index by 20% than are beating it by any margin. Warren Pies explains this persists because the mega-cap leadership never cedes the baton during broadening episodes — equal weight gets a brief bounce but the same tech/comms giants reassert dominance each cycle.

### The K-Shaped Economy and Housing Crisis [00:27:39]

Warren Pies uses housing affordability as the defining chart of the K-shaped economy: the income percentile needed to buy the median US home rose from the 40th to the 60th percentile post-pandemic, pricing out the middle class. He argues this pressure — compounded by insurance and healthcare costs — forces policy makers toward an easing bias regardless of asset-market conditions. Supply-side solutions (zoning reform, YIMBY policies) are also needed but face structural political barriers.

### Fed Policy, Small Caps, and Investment Implications [00:33:12]

Pies is skeptical that rate cuts will deliver the typical early-cycle boost to small caps: estimates of 30% small-cap earnings growth for 2024 proved flat, and there is no recession washout to trigger a genuine early-cycle rotation. The Fed's easing is driven by lower-K pain and will support the bull market, but flows and window-dressing favor the existing large-cap tech leaders into year-end, with beaten-down software names like Salesforce and Adobe more likely to sell off further before rebounding in January.

### Year-End Rally Setup and Profit Margins [00:45:42]

314 Research's proprietary sentiment index fell from extreme optimism (above 70) to near 44.8, approaching excessive pessimism territory just as buyback seasonality peaks in December. Analysts forecast 250 basis points of S&P 500 margin expansion through 2027; historically, margin expansion years almost never produce multiple contraction. Combined with Walmart's margin beats and AI-driven headcount discipline, the hosts see continued upside for high-quality large caps.
