# Old Man Yells at the Cloud | TCAF 167

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-11-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=GVGPCcEQpmI
**Duration:** 104:41

## Summary

In TCAF episode 167, Cliff Asness of AQR Capital Management presents his thesis that markets have become measurably less efficient over his three-decade career — not in the daily noise sense, but in their susceptibility to prolonged bubbles like the dot-com era and the 2019-2021 growth stock mania. He attributes this deterioration to three forces: the growth of passive cap-weighted indexing weakening price discovery, a multi-year period of ultra-low real interest rates that distorted investor behavior, and social media plus zero-commission trading that can turn the wisdom of crowds into a coordinated mob. The conversation also covers why value investing has underperformed since the Global Financial Crisis, the quants' exhaustive but inconclusive search for a structural explanation, and the deceptive presentation of risk in private equity, which Asness labels 'volatility laundering.' Throughout, Asness defends the continued long-run validity of factor-based value strategies while conceding that bouts of irrationality may now be deeper and longer than they used to be.

## Key Takeaways

- Asness argues the stock market is prone to more severe and longer-lasting mispricings than it was 30 years ago, driven by indexing, low rates, and social media — not a permanent collapse of value investing.
- Speed of information transmission (milliseconds vs. minutes) is essentially irrelevant for multi-month holding-period strategies; market efficiency is about accuracy, not latency.
- Despite exhaustive internal research, AQR found no evidence that value spreads predict future earnings growth any better today than historically, undermining the 'intangibles disrupted value' narrative.
- Jack Bogle himself acknowledged passive indexing cannot approach 100% of the market without breaking price discovery, though he admitted his 75% threshold figure was intuitive, not model-derived.
- Private equity volatility laundering — marking illiquid assets at smooth, internally generated valuations — artificially depresses reported risk, and investors are now paying for the psychological comfort of not seeing daily marks.
- The AMC meme-stock episode illustrated how online communities can misread short sellers as adversaries and amplify tribal irrationality, consistent with Asness's third efficiency hypothesis.
- Asness believes value strategies should deliver higher long-run returns in a less-efficient market, but investors must accept that the painful drawdown episodes will also be deeper and more prolonged.

## Detailed Notes

### Cold Open — AQR Origins and AMC Meme-Stock Controversy [00:00:00]

Cliff Asness opens by recounting leaving Goldman Sachs (with some tensions) to found AQR, initially funded from personal savings. He describes a CNBC 'three stock lunch' appearance where he shorted AMC at 12 basis points and called it poor on every quantitative metric; the meme-stock community reacted with intense hostility, illustrating the tribal, internet-amplified dynamics he later formalizes as a cause of market inefficiency.

### Michael Saylor and MicroStrategy [00:11:33]

The hosts play a clip of Michael Saylor arguing that operating companies are constrained from holding liquid assets, making MicroStrategy a superior leveraged Bitcoin vehicle. Asness dismisses the reasoning as 'the dumbest thing I've ever heard,' noting that winning a lottery does not validate the ticket-buying strategy; Saylor may be right on Bitcoin for idiosyncratic reasons, but the theoretical framework is incoherent.

### The Less-Efficient Market Hypothesis — Core Thesis [00:23:04]

Asness presents his paper arguing markets have grown more susceptible to prolonged bubbles, not necessarily less efficient day-to-day. He distinguishes between information speed (clearly faster) and pricing accuracy (not clearly better), and places himself between Fama (markets mostly price things right) and Shiller (markets are driven by emotion). Long-run value strategies should be more rewarding in a less-efficient world, but the drawdowns will be more severe and lasting.

### Value Investing Underperformance — What AQR Found [00:38:49]

Asness reviews the post-GFC underperformance of value strategies, noting that AQR's industry-adjusted, multi-metric approach fared better than simple price-to-book indices. After two years of rigorous internal testing — stripping out tech, mega caps, adjusting for intangibles — AQR found no evidence that expensive stocks have become better predictors of future earnings growth, and no evidence that value spreads are more rational today than in the past. The value spread remains wide by historical standards.

### Did Quants Ruin Value? And the Role of Indexing [00:52:28]

Josh Brown raises the hypothesis that quants arbitraged away the 'embarrassment premium' in cheap stocks. Asness counters that this should show up in compressed value spreads or improved earnings predictability — neither of which appears in the data. He then addresses passive indexing: while it cannot be 100% of the market without destroying price discovery, he is cautious about quantifying the damage, calling Michael Green's views directionally correct but too extreme. He recounts Jack Bogle admitting his 75% passive-market threshold was intuitive.

### Three Hypotheses for Lower Efficiency [00:59:50]

Asness summarizes his three causes: (1) passive indexing weakens the tether to fundamental value; (2) decade-plus of ultra-low real rates, which correlated with speculative excess including virtual-land purchases adjacent to Snoop Dogg's metaverse plot; (3) social media and zero-commission trading that can convert the wisdom of crowds into a coordinated mob — the same dynamic, he argues, that has degraded political discourse. Both the dot-com bubble and the 2019-2021 growth mania coincided with proto- and full social media respectively.

### Private Equity and Volatility Laundering [01:25:52]

Asness criticizes the presentation of private equity risk, not necessarily its returns. Using Bloomberg quant research, he shows that reported volatility for private assets is a fraction of their true economic volatility. He notes one prominent private credit firm displayed a Sharpe ratio of 10.0 on its tearsheet — far above what Renaissance's Medallion fund achieved. His core objection: the illiquidity that was once a bug investors demanded compensation for is now marketed as a feature (psychological stability), and investors are now almost certainly paying a premium for it rather than earning a premium.
