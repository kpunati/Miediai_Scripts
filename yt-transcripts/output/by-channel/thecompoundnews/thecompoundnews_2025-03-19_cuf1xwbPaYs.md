# Will Tariffs Tank the Economy?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cuf1xwbPaYs
**Duration:** 33:26

## Summary

Ben Carlson, Duncan Hill, and RWM adviser Blair duQuesnay work through five listener questions on Ask The Compound. They dissect why Wall Street price targets are structurally unreliable, walk through historical tariff episodes from McKinley to Smoot-Hawley to frame the 2025 trade policy debate, and offer practical guidance on adviser-client conversations during corrections. The episode closes with a frank discussion of experiential spending — specifically boats — and the mechanics and trade-offs of using a 401k loan for a home down payment.

## Key Takeaways

- Wall Street consensus forecasts almost always predict a 5-10% gain; historically, actual returns rarely land in that range because markets tend to overshoot in both directions.
- Historical tariff episodes — McKinley in 1890 and Smoot-Hawley in 1930 — produced immediate inflation spikes and contributed to significant economic harm, including reduced global trade by up to 50%.
- Advisers handling concentrated-stock clients during corrections should acknowledge emotions first, avoid 'I told you so,' and use the market drawdown as a teaching moment rather than a tactical exit trigger.
- Gradual diversification out of concentrated positions — the reverse of dollar-cost averaging — spreads tax impact and reduces the psychological pressure of timing an exit.
- Experiential purchases like boats can be financially appropriate when a client has met their long-term goals; the true cost is maintenance and ongoing expenses, not just purchase price.
- A 401k loan of up to $50,000 (or 50% of vested balance) can bridge a home down payment gap, but risks include mandatory repayment if employment ends and the double-taxation on interest repayments.
- IRA accounts do not permit loans; Roth IRA contributions (not gains) can be withdrawn penalty-free, offering a separate strategy for first-time home buyers.

## Detailed Notes

### The Inaccuracy of Wall Street Price Targets [00:02:02]

Ben Carlson cites Bloomberg data showing that over 375 tracked firms, more than half of annual forecasts cluster in the 0-10% gain range. In seven of the past eight years, actual returns fell entirely outside the forecast range. He argues analysts should instead forecast a 20% gain three out of four years and a 15% loss one out of four years, which would historically outperform consensus accuracy. Goldman cut its 2025 S&P target from 6,500 to 6,200 mid-year as markets fell, illustrating the 'moving target' problem.

### Tariffs: Historical Lessons and 2025 Context [00:07:26]

Drawing on JP Morgan's Michael Cembalest and William Bernstein's book A Splendid Exchange, Ben outlines three tariff episodes. McKinley's 1890 tariffs caused immediate inflation and cost the GOP 100 House seats. The 1930 Smoot-Hawley tariffs reduced global trade volume by one-third to one-half and are estimated to have cut world GDP by 3-5%, while potentially contributing to conditions that led to World War II. Ben argues broad blanket tariffs hurt consumers, suppress corporate investment, and compress earnings margins — explaining recent market weakness.

### Adviser Conversations Around Diversification During Corrections [00:12:14]

Blair duQuesnay advises younger advisers to resist lecturing concentrated-stock clients; the correction itself does much of the persuasion work. Key tactics include reminding clients of total returns since inception (many growth stock holders remain up 400%+ over five years), avoiding all-or-nothing framing, and using incremental selling to spread both tax impact and exit timing. Over time, consistent and calm messaging across multiple market cycles builds client trust and reduces panic-driven calls.

### Experiential Spending and the Boat Question [00:19:14]

Ben explains he mentioned buying a boat as a legitimate use of Palantir windfall gains, sparking listener pushback. Blair and Ben agree that for clients who have achieved core financial goals, experiential spending — boats, travel, vacation homes — is a legitimate advisor recommendation. The real financial planning challenge is accounting for ongoing costs: maintenance, fuel, insurance, and storage often exceed the purchase price impact. Blair sets a $99,000 ski boat as a personal financial goal, illustrating the adviser modeling their own advice.

### 401k Loans for Home Down Payments [00:26:09]

Blair recounts taking a $50,000 401k loan at 7% interest to bridge the gap when buying a new home before selling her prior one. She notes the loan took five years to pay back after the house sale took two years instead of the expected one. Key mechanics discussed: maximum loan is $50,000 or 50% of vested balance; interest is paid back to yourself but on an after-tax basis; loans do not stop additional contributions; and the loan becomes taxable income if employment ends. IRAs do not permit loans, but Roth IRA contributions can be withdrawn penalty-free.
