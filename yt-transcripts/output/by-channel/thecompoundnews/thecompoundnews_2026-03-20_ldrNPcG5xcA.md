# Are We in a Bear Market? | TCAF 234

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-03-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ldrNPcG5xcA
**Duration:** 86:35

## Summary

In episode 234 of The Compound and Friends, Josh Brown, Michael Batnick, and Jim Lebenthal discuss a stock market where 40% of S&P 500 names are in individual bear markets despite the index being only 5% off its highs, driven by the Iran-Strait of Hormuz conflict, AI disruption fears, and private credit stress. The conversation covers whether earnings revisions will deteriorate, the outsized impact of AI on white-collar and blue-collar employment, and why seasoned investors see current conditions as a healthy reset rather than a systemic collapse. The trio also reviews specific stock positions in Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Citigroup, and Oracle, evaluating valuations and catalysts.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500 index is only ~5% from its all-time high, but roughly 40% of individual stocks within it are already in bear market territory, driven by sector-by-sector corrections.
- The Iran conflict and elevated oil prices are the near-term catalyst for market weakness, but a single diplomatic tweet could reverse sentiment rapidly and the Strait of Hormuz carries significant macro uncertainty.
- AI-driven job displacement is hitting recent college graduates hardest, with their unemployment rate at 6% versus 4% overall, raising concerns about long-term consumer demand and political ramifications.
- Private credit faces headline risk and quarterly gating pressure throughout 2026 due to SaaS exposure concerns, but defaults are currently concentrated in fraudulent underwriting rather than broad credit deterioration.
- Microsoft and Apple are highlighted as long-term buys at current valuations; Apple is described as a stealth AI winner collecting App Store fees from generative AI apps with little CapEx exposure.
- Jim Lebenthal favors Amazon for AWS demand dynamics, Citigroup for the pending Banamex IPO catalyst, and Oracle as a coiled spring if OpenAI contract concerns abate.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Conditions and the Bear Market Question [00:08:21]

Despite S&P 500 being only 5% from all-time highs, 203 stocks (~40%) are in individual bear markets, with 61% of tech and 57% of discretionary names down over 20%. The hosts argue this rolling sector-level correction is healthy rather than systemic, and that VIX reaching 35 briefly is a reasonable reset. Buyback blackouts covering 45% of the S&P are expected to begin that week, removing a key demand source.

### Iran Conflict and Macro Risks [00:15:24]

The Strait of Hormuz closure is cited as the primary near-term macro overhang, with oil at elevated levels threatening business and consumer confidence. Lebenthal argues that if traffic through the Strait resumes quickly, oil would normalize and the economic damage need not be lasting. However, prolonged high energy prices could tip businesses toward hiring freezes and consumption declines, making weekly jobless claims (250,000 threshold) the key watch variable.

### AI Disruption and Job Market Implications [00:33:27]

The hosts debate the Citrini 'software apocalypse' thesis, with Lebenthal drawing parallels to NYSE floor trader displacement but noting financial industry employment grew 20% over 30 years despite automation. Brown emphasizes recent college graduate unemployment at 6% is already rising before autonomous vehicle and broader AI deployment arrives, creating political and consumer sentiment headwinds. Batnick is more optimistic that AI expands capabilities rather than just replacing workers.

### Private Credit Stress [00:55:43]

Private credit funds with SaaS exposure are facing quarterly gates (5% per quarter as written in prospectuses) as redemption requests pile up, and this is expected to continue through 2026. Lebenthal notes that at $2.5 trillion in private credit versus $8.9 trillion in private equity, equity must be wiped out before loans face losses, making systemic risk unlikely. However, headline risk and chilled fundraising could reduce capital availability for loan refinancings, creating a secondary knock-on problem.

### Individual Stock Picks [01:14:11]

Lebenthal and Brown discuss several names: Microsoft at 22x forward earnings with a sub-1 PEG ratio is seen as a potential buy despite weak technical momentum; Apple is described as the stealth AI winner collecting App Store fees from generative AI apps with no CapEx burden and a $20 billion/year Google deal for iPhone Gemini access; Amazon is favored for AWS demand-exceeding-supply dynamics and the Anthropic strategic partnership; Citigroup is highlighted for valuation at book value and an upcoming Banamex IPO catalyst; Oracle at ~$160 is viewed as a coiled spring contingent on OpenAI's ability to honor its contract.
