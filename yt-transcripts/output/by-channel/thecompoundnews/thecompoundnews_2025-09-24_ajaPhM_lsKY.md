# Where Should I Put Cash After the Fed Rate Cut?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ajaPhM_lsKY
**Duration:** 31:21

## Summary

Episode 190 of Ask The Compound features Ben Carlson and Duncan Hill fielding five listener questions in the context of a Federal Reserve rate-cutting cycle. Topics range from practical cash management (Roth IRA as a cash vehicle, money market funds, mortgage rate mechanics) to portfolio construction (Social Security versus bonds, return expectation methodologies) to personal finance and career strategy. The show combines data-driven chart analysis with conversational, experience-based advice.

## Key Takeaways

- Parking cash in a Roth IRA is not a meaningfully better strategy than a high yield savings account or brokerage account; the Roth's real value lies in long-term tax-deferred compounding of higher-returning assets.
- Social Security shares income characteristics with fixed income but not portfolio characteristics — it cannot be rebalanced or liquidated — so it is better viewed as a floor income source that may allow a slightly higher stock allocation.
- Mortgage rates follow the 10-year Treasury rather than the Fed funds rate because the average mortgage duration is roughly 7-10 years, not 30.
- A 22-year-old with $275k already saved is effectively 'coast FI' — compound growth alone could reach $7.5 million by retirement without additional savings.
- The most important career skill in finance, even for introverts, is the ability to communicate and persuade; once aligned with genuine passion, this no longer feels like traditional sales.
- When deploying a financial windfall, treating the allocation like an asset allocation across multiple buckets (investments, renovation, travel, emergency fund, discretionary spending) reduces decision paralysis and guilt.

## Detailed Notes

### Cash Management After Fed Rate Cuts [00:00:01]

With the Fed cutting rates 25 bps and signaling more cuts, yields on money markets, T-bills, CDs, and high yield savings accounts are heading below 4%. The $7.3 trillion in money market assets may prove sticky (baby boomer demographics) or could eventually rotate. Using a Roth IRA as a cash vehicle provides no practical advantage over a brokerage account and introduces unnecessary complexity for emergency access.

### Does Social Security Count as a Bond? [00:07:03]

Social Security represents roughly 20% of US household wealth per CBO data, and is disproportionately important for lower-wealth households. Carlson's view: Social Security is better classified as a pension-like income stream than as a bond substitute, because it is illiquid and cannot be used to rebalance a portfolio. Its main planning implication is allowing a modestly higher equity allocation (e.g., shifting from 60/40 to 70/30).

### Mortgage Rates and Fed Policy [00:11:13]

After the Fed's prior 50 bps cut, mortgage rates actually rose immediately afterward. Mortgage rates are priced as a spread over the 10-year Treasury (not the overnight rate) because the average mortgage duration — accounting for refinances and moves — is roughly 7-10 years. Unless the Fed explicitly purchases long-dated bonds or MBS, short-term cuts do not guarantee lower mortgage rates.

### Career Advice for a 22-Year-Old CFA Candidate [00:13:45]

A listener with $250-300k net worth at 22, a CFA Level 3 candidacy, and family stress asks about taking a break. Carlson endorses the idea: compounding alone on ~$275k at 8% reaches $7.5 million by 65, making a short break financially harmless. Career paths discussed include risk management (quant-heavy), analyst roles at fund companies or family offices, and institutional endowment work. Core advice: introverts still need to develop selling and communication skills — once aligned with genuine conviction, it stops feeling like sales.

### Deploying a Real Estate Windfall and Return Expectation Methodology [00:21:46]

For a couple that sold a home with $107k profit on top of $120k in pre-saved down payment funds, Carlson recommends a bucket approach: renovations, vacation, 529, HSA, brokerage top-up, and a discretionary spending allocation. On financial planning return assumptions, Carlson argues no model — historical averages or capital market assumptions — is reliably better; the right approach is a range of scenarios with embedded margin of safety and ongoing plan adjustments as actual returns unfold.
