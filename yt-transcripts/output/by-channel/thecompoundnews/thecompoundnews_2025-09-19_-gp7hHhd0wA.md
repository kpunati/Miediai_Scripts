# How to Become a Super Trader | TCAF 209

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=-gp7hHhd0wA
**Duration:** 72:44

## Summary

In TCAF episode 209, Josh Brown and Michael Batnick host Tom Sosnoff, the serial fintech entrepreneur who built thinkorswim and tastytrade. Sosnoff recounts his early years as a market maker in the CBOE S&P 100 options pit, the founding and sale of thinkorswim to TD Ameritrade in a unique three-way deal, and the subsequent creation and sale of tastytrade to IG Group for $1.1 billion. The conversation covers the philosophy that anyone can become a competent self-directed options trader using probabilistic, math-based approaches, the explosion of retail options volume, and the structural flaws of covered-call ETF products that use OTC rather than listed markets.

## Key Takeaways

- Sosnoff's trading philosophy is rooted in 20 years as a floor market maker: take the other side of every order without forming directional opinions, making him a pure contrarian.
- tastytrade teaches high-probability trading strategies not to guarantee returns but to build consistent winning habits; law of large numbers then delivers the expected win rate over time.
- Approximately 16% of tastytrade customers blow out their accounts and a separate 16% outperform risk-free rates by multiples, with the broad middle delivering mediocre results.
- Covered-call and options-income ETFs underperform their stated strategies because fund size forces them into illiquid OTC markets where banks extract large edge that listed-market retail traders avoid.
- The sale of thinkorswim involved a three-way structure: Sosnoff received TD Ameritrade stock, TD provided cash, and the Ricketts family used that cash to purchase the Chicago Cubs.
- Retail investors have become significantly more financially sophisticated since the early 1990s, as evidenced by TD Ameritrade's options and futures mix rising from 7-8% to over 70% of volume between 1999 and its Schwab acquisition.
- Sosnoff argues that TD Ameritrade sold to Schwab due to internal dysfunction and board preferences, not because commission-free trading made the business unviable.

## Detailed Notes

### Tom Sosnoff's Trading Origin and Chicago Pit Career [00:10:42]

Sosnoff graduated in 1979 into a recession with 19-20% interest rates, joined Drexel Burnham, then moved to Chicago in 1981 with $50,000 in backing that was lost within two weeks. He spent 20 years as a market maker in the S&P 100 options pit at the CBOE, learning to take the other side of every order without forming market opinions, which shaped his core contrarian philosophy.

### tastytrade Business Model and Options Market Structure [00:18:27]

tastytrade is described as the largest derivatives boutique with 95% of customer activity in options and futures, explicitly not competing with Schwab or Fidelity for stock trading. Sosnoff explains the firm's 'premium selling' orientation and its math-based content marketing strategy designed to teach retail investors probabilistic thinking. He notes that options pricing is always theoretically correct, but implied volatility can be high or low relative to itself, which creates optimization opportunities.

### thinkorswim and the TD Ameritrade Sale [00:47:22]

thinkorswim was founded in 1999-2000 by Sosnoff and Scott Sheridan as the floor trading business went electronic. The platform grew into a cult firm built with Russian developers and was ultimately sold to TD Ameritrade around 2009 in a three-way deal: Sosnoff received TD stock (which he considered undervalued), TD provided $350 million cash to the Ricketts family to purchase the Chicago Cubs, and the acquisition allowed Sosnoff to continue building what became tastytrade in 2011.

### Retail Trading Renaissance and Customer Distribution [00:33:11]

Sosnoff presents internal tastytrade data: roughly 16% of customers blow out accounts, 16% earn multiples of the risk-free rate, and the middle 68% cluster around or below risk-free returns. He views the meme-stock era of 2021 as a transformational inflection point that brought 30-50 million new retail participants into markets, crediting the democratization of financial education as broadly positive despite individual losses.

### Covered-Call ETF Structural Problems [01:03:27]

Sosnoff argues that options-income ETFs like YieldMax products underperform because their large asset bases force them into OTC markets where bank counterparties (Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan) extract 10-20 ticks of edge versus the fraction-of-a-penny spreads retail traders pay on listed exchanges. He draws a historical parallel to covered-call closed-end funds that proliferated in 2007-2008 and did not survive the financial crisis due to structural and counterparty risk.
