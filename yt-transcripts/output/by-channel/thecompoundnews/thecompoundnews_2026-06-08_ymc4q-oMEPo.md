# A 6 Standard Deviation Tech Event Just Happened. Now What? | WDWL?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-06-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ymc4q-oMEPo
**Duration:** 34:38

## Summary

Josh Brown hosts DataTrek Research co-founders Nick Colas and Jessica Rabe to unpack a statistically extreme tech-sector rally: US large-cap tech outperformed the S&P 500 by 29.3 percentage points over 50 days, a six-standard-deviation event unprecedented in the past decade. The conversation covers the semiconductor/software divergence, the upcoming SpaceX IPO as a potential bubble-calibration signal, rising interest rates as a new risk factor, and a data-driven comparison to the 1999-2000 dot-com bubble that concludes current conditions are not comparable in magnitude.

## Key Takeaways

- The 50-day tech outperformance of 29.3 points is a six-standard-deviation event with no historical analog in DataTrek's dataset, exceeding even pandemic and trade-shock recovery periods.
- Semiconductors beating software by 44 points over 50 days is four standard deviations above average, though late in the period software names began catching up alongside semis.
- The S&P 500's two-standard-deviation 50-day move historically precedes further gains, with every prior instance since 2015 showing positive 50-day forward returns averaging 7.3%.
- SpaceX's fixed-price $135/share IPO at $1.8 trillion bypasses traditional price discovery; the first-day close will serve as a real-time calibration of whether markets are in 1997-98 or 1999 territory.
- Goldman Sachs removed any interest rate cut from its year-end forecast; rising two-year yields to 12-month highs add a macro headwind to tech valuations.
- The 1999 dot-com bubble saw the Nasdaq up 84% in under six months followed by a 34% decline in five weeks — a volatility profile the panelists say is simply not present today.
- Algo-driven market makers were aware of the statistical extreme heading into Friday's selloff, amplifying the cascade through widened bid-ask spreads rather than stepping in.

## Detailed Notes

### A Six-Standard-Deviation Tech Event [00:01:51]

Jessica Rabe presented a chart showing XLK's rolling 50-day return versus the S&P 500 from 2015 to present. The average outperformance is 1.6 points with a 4-point standard deviation; the six-sigma threshold sits at 25.6 points. As of June 2nd, tech beat the S&P by 29 points — the most extreme reading in the dataset and inclusive of the late-March 2025 lows, meaning it covered the biggest selloff of the year. Rabe's conclusion: there is no historical playbook for what tech has done, and traders with risk management systems will treat this as a reason for caution.

### Semiconductor vs. Software Divergence [00:06:12]

Using SMH and IGV as proxies, DataTrek found semis outperformed software by 44 percentage points over 50 days ending June 3rd — four standard deviations above the long-run mean of 2.7 points. Near the end of the period, software names including Oracle and cybersecurity stocks (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto) began rallying alongside semis, turning it into a broadening tech rally. Rabe recommended tactical investors reduce semi exposure and look at software, while long-term AI believers should hold an index-weight approach to manage concentration risk.

### Interest Rates as Emerging Risk [00:14:29]

The strong jobs report on Friday pushed two-year yields to a new 12-month high, reinforcing fears of a Fed rate hike rather than cuts. Goldman Sachs removed any rate cut from its year-end forecast ahead of the June 16-17 FOMC meeting led by Kevin Warsh. Colas noted rates only become a serious problem for tech if they rise fast enough to rationally fear recession, as happened in 2022; the current move alone is not necessarily fatal for AI-theme confidence.

### SpaceX IPO: A Bubble Barometer [00:19:45]

SpaceX is pricing at $135 per share ($1.8 trillion valuation) via fixed-price issuance — bypassing traditional book-building and leaving price discovery to the first trade. Jay Ritter's data shows average day-one IPO gains of 14-23% in 1997-98 (implying a close of ~$154-166) versus 71% in 1999. A close around $231 would, per Colas, signal a legitimate 1999 bubble. SpaceX will enter the Nasdaq 100 on a fast-track inclusion rule within 15 days, adding roughly $15-20 billion in forced demand against a $75 billion float, but shorter-than-usual lockups will offset that over subsequent quarters.

### Why Today Is Not 1999 [00:30:09]

Using a Nasdaq chart from 1999-2000, Colas showed the index rose 84% in under six months (Q4 1999 through March 10, 2000) then fell 34% in five weeks to April 14. Today's one-year Nasdaq 100 gain of roughly 34% is nowhere near that scale. Colas argued that bubble classification requires prices to first double and then fall sharply, and that the sheer dollar size of the largest mega-cap tech companies makes an 84% six-month Nasdaq rally nearly impossible today.
