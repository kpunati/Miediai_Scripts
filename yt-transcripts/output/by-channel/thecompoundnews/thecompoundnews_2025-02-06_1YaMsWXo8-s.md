# The Dumbest Trade War | Animal Spirits 398

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-02-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=1YaMsWXo8-s
**Duration:** 61:09

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 398 (February 6, 2025) focuses primarily on the tariff threats announced by the Trump administration against Canada, Mexico, and China, which were subsequently paused for 30 days. Hosts Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson assess the economic impact of tariffs on US consumers — covering imported goods, food prices, and trading partner relations — while arguing that the market's relatively muted reaction reflects both the negotiating-ploy narrative and the countervailing tailwind of AI-driven earnings. The episode also digs into Vanguard's historic fee reductions, the growth and proper use of leveraged ETFs, DOGE's access to Treasury payment systems, and an aging US housing stock that the hosts believe will drive a multiyear home renovation boom.

## Key Takeaways

- Tariffs on Canada, Mexico, and China would directly affect roughly 5% of US GDP and raise prices on food, beer, lumber, and consumer goods for American households.
- The stock market's muted response to tariff news may be explained by AI earnings acting as a structural offset, with AI-related themes potentially dominating equity returns for the rest of the decade.
- Vanguard's decision to cut fees from 11 to 9 basis points — claimed to be the largest fee reduction in history — pressured competitors like BlackRock and reflects the ongoing power of the Vanguard Effect.
- Leveraged ETFs generated roughly $940 million in fees in 2024 but delivered reported 43% returns to investors; the hosts caution that these products are misunderstood by retail investors but can have a role in sophisticated portfolios.
- Crypto acted as a real-time risk barometer over the weekend the tariff news broke, with Bitcoin falling roughly 15% and $2 billion in leverage wiped out, undermining the crypto-as-tariff-hedge narrative.
- Over 60% of US homes are 35-plus years old, and rising home equity (now ~$35 trillion) combined with young buyers' higher design standards points to a significant remodeling boom ahead.
- Employees on average accept roughly an 8-12% pay cut to work remotely, with 11.8% of full-time US workers fully remote and 29% partly remote as of early 2025.

## Detailed Notes

### Tariff Threats on Canada, Mexico, and China [00:00:04]

Batnick and Carlson analyze the February 2025 tariff announcement targeting the US's three largest trade partners. They cite data showing goods imports make up 11% of US GDP, with 43% coming from Canada, Mexico, and China, putting 5% of GDP at risk. Specific goods highlighted include 90% of fresh vegetables, 84% of beer, and 67% of berries sourced from Canada or Mexico, as well as 73% of toys and sports equipment from China. Both hosts argue tariffs are economically destructive and represent an unforced error given a strong economy and receding inflation, though they acknowledge the 30-day pause may reflect a negotiating tactic.

### Market Reaction and AI as Counterbalance [00:00:04]

The S&P 500 opened down about 2% and the NASDAQ down 3% when tariff news broke, but the VIX only rose to 20, which the hosts characterize as a muted reaction. They suggest AI earnings are the dominant factor for equity markets, potentially overwhelming tariff and interest-rate headwinds for the rest of the decade. The risk is a triple negative scenario where tariffs, presidential volatility, and AI disappointment converge simultaneously.

### DOGE and Treasury Payment System Access [00:00:04]

The hosts discuss news that Elon Musk's DOGE was granted access to the Treasury's payment system, which handles approximately 88% of all federal payments including 81 million monthly payments for Social Security, Medicare, and Veterans Benefits. Carlson expresses discomfort with potential conflicts of interest while acknowledging the federal government's infrastructure is outdated and modernization is overdue.

### Vanguard Fee Cuts and the Vanguard Effect [00:00:04]

Vanguard announced a fee reduction across hundreds of mutual funds and ETFs, cutting average expense ratios from 11 to 9 basis points — described as the largest cost cut in Vanguard's history, saving investors over $350 million annually. BlackRock fell 5% on the news, illustrating competitive pressure. The hosts credit Jack Bogle and note that $5 trillion has shifted from high-cost active to low-cost passive management over the prior decade.

### Leveraged ETFs: Fees vs. Returns [00:00:04]

Bloomberg data showed leveraged ETF providers earned ~$940 million in 2024 (up 37%), but Morningstar analyst Jeffrey Ptak's analysis showed investors in these products earned an aggregate 43% return — $16 billion in income and gains versus $1 billion in fees. Hosts caution that leveraged ETFs are misused by beginners who hold them long-term but acknowledge sophisticated investors can use them for intelligent leverage and portfolio diversification.

### Crypto as Risk Asset, Not Tariff Hedge [00:00:04]

Bitcoin fell roughly 15% and Ethereum fell up to 30% at one point during the tariff weekend, with $2 billion in leverage wiped out — the largest wipeout on record at the time. The hosts conclude crypto is a pure risk asset that functions as a real-time sentiment gauge on weekends when equity markets are closed, but is not an inflation or tariff hedge as originally narrativized.

### Housing Stock Aging and Renovation Boom [00:00:04]

More than 60% of US homes are at least 35 years old (built in 1989 or earlier) and nearly 12% date to 1939 or earlier. US home equity has risen from approximately $20 trillion in 2021 to $35 trillion. Median home renovation costs have risen from $15,000 to nearly $25,000 since before the pandemic. Carlson holds Home Depot and Sherwin-Williams and argues the renovation boom will persist as young buyers with higher design standards inherit older housing stock.

### Personal Finance: Remote Work, College Savings, Bitcoin [00:00:04]

A National Bureau of Economic Research survey found employees accept an average 8-12% pay cut for remote work; 11.8% of full-time workers are fully remote and 29% are partly remote. The hosts also discuss a Wall Street Journal story about parents replacing 529 college savings plans with Bitcoin, including a financial planner targeting one full Bitcoin per child by age 18; hosts acknowledge the appeal but question the risk for college-specific savings.
