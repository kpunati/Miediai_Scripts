# Greed Is Good | TCAF 184

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=5w2q2yNlxnE
**Duration:** 97:21

## Summary

TCAF episode 184 brings together Michael Batnick, Josh Brown, Andrew Beer of DBI, and Sam Ro of the Tker newsletter for a wide-ranging discussion on the ideological shift toward unfettered capitalism under the Trump administration — framed through the 'greed is good' analogy from the 1987 film Wall Street. The conversation covers Trump-era tariff uncertainty, why the equity and credit markets have not yet priced in recessionary risk, the decades-long failure of liquid alternative mutual funds to deliver on their diversification promise, and why hedge fund replication via managed futures ETFs represents a more credible approach. The panel also debates the structural risks and moral hazards embedded in the new wave of private credit ETFs, and questions whether the traditional 60/40 portfolio remains fit for purpose in the current rate regime.

## Key Takeaways

- Andrew Beer argues that the 'greed is good' ethos — controversial in 1987 — has become the animating philosophy of the current administration, widening the Overton window for both policy upside and downside.
- The panel broadly agrees that while tariff noise is real, equity and credit markets are currently 'looking through' the disruption, with junk spreads and jobless claims showing no distress signals as of late March 2025.
- Liquid alternative mutual funds have been a near-total failure for retail investors, delivering roughly 2% annually after fees while maintaining high correlation to equities — negating their core diversification pitch.
- CTA replication via low-cost ETFs is presented as the most defensible way to access genuine zero-correlation diversification for the typical adviser portfolio, with DBI's DBMF cited as the proof-of-concept.
- Private credit ETFs like PIV (Apollo/State Street) face a structural conflict of interest: Apollo originates and prices the illiquid loans that underpin the fund, creating a potential adverse-selection problem in stress scenarios.
- The 60/40 portfolio is increasingly viewed as outdated; a 60/20/20 split — with the 20% non-bond sleeve drawn from private credit, managed futures, commodities, or gold — is where most practitioners are migrating.
- S&P 500 bottom-up EPS growth estimates for 2025 have already fallen sharply, from around 12% to 7%, reflecting tariff uncertainty without yet signaling outright earnings decline.

## Detailed Notes

### Greed Is Good — The Philosophical Shift [00:10:32]

Andrew Beer draws an analogy between the shock of Gordon Gekko's 'greed is good' speech in 1987 and the current Trump administration's unapologetic embrace of profit-maximization and deregulation. He argues that the LBO and stock-option revolution of the late 1980s — initially vilified — ultimately drove US corporate exceptionalism. Josh Brown pushes back, distinguishing genuine market capitalism from the cronyism of picking winners via government contracts.

### Tariffs, Market Resilience, and Second-Order Risk [00:27:10]

The panel reviews S&P buy ratings near multi-year highs and bottom-up EPS estimates that have fallen from roughly 12% to 7% growth for full-year 2025, but without signaling recession. Andrew Beer notes that tariff policy is impossible to read as a clear signal given the negotiating theater around it, while Sam Ro highlights that most companies have not factored tariffs into their guidance. The group agrees that market stress historically arrives from convergence of multiple shocks rather than a single visible escalation, and that Apple and Nvidia represent key pressure points for China to exploit.

### Liquid Alts — A 22-Year Track Record of Failure [00:55:16]

Andrew Beer presents Morningstar category data from 2002 to 2024 showing that liquid alt mutual funds — covering real estate, long/short equity, macro, and multi-strategy — cluster around 0.8 correlation to the S&P 500 with negative alpha after fees near 200 basis points. He attributes this to the structural difficulty of replicating hedge fund strategies with daily liquidity constraints, and to the 'spaghetti cannon' business model of large fund complexes.

### CTA Replication and Managed Futures ETFs [01:09:28]

Beer distinguishes CTA hedge funds — a $340 billion industry with near-zero correlation to both equities and bonds and positive alpha even after fees — from the failed liquid alt universe. DBI's approach is to replicate CTA returns cheaply in an ETF wrapper (DBMF), occupying a position in portfolio construction as the 'kale' of asset allocation: genuinely diversifying but hard to sell in bull markets. BlackRock, Invesco, and Fidelity have all entered the managed futures ETF space following DBI's proof-of-concept.

### Private Credit ETFs — Innovation or Structural Risk? [01:14:36]

The panel debates PIV, the State Street/Apollo private credit ETF. Beer identifies the core conflict: Apollo originates, prices, and makes markets in the underlying loans, creating adverse-selection risk in a stress scenario where every other investor also wants to exit. He uses the analogy of relying on a 'vulture investor' to buy your house in a hurricane. Sam Ro notes the broader appeal — retail investors want access to an asset class that has compounded at roughly 12% — while acknowledging that the ETF structure forces daily mark-to-market on inherently illiquid assets.

### The 60/40 Portfolio and Where to Allocate the 40 [01:02:23]

Beer argues that bonds were an exceptional asset in the 2000s but have since seen maximum drawdown quintupled and now carry a positive correlation to equities, undermining their core role in balanced portfolios. The consensus in the conversation is that a 60/20/20 structure — with the non-bond sleeve allocated across private credit, managed futures, gold, commodities, or other diversifiers — better serves investors in the current regime.
