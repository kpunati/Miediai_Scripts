# Why We're Electing Socialist Mayors With Stocks At An All Time High

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-08-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=VsTxRl1wlAw
**Duration:** 40:42

## Summary

Josh Brown hosts Garrett Baldwin for a wide-ranging discussion on the socioeconomic forces driving anti-capitalist politics in US cities despite equity markets at record highs. Baldwin argues that 1993 was a pivotal year for six concurrent policy shifts—including the Clinton tax change that spurred stock-based executive compensation, the first passive ETF (SPY), and the Fed's adoption of inflation targeting at Jackson Hole—that collectively began bifurcating the US economy into asset-owning haves and wage-dependent have-nots. The conversation spans the Cantillon effect, K-shaped recoveries, the Genius Act's role in sustaining Treasury demand, and the political consequences of persistent real-world inflation being invisible in official statistics.

## Key Takeaways

- Six simultaneous 1993 policy events—including the shift to stock-based CEO pay and the Fed's inflation targeting at Jackson Hole—set the structural conditions for today's wealth inequality.
- The Cantillon effect ensures newly created capital flows first to financial centers and assets, compounding wealth for those already near capital while raising the cost of living for everyone else.
- Every major market crisis since 2008 has been met with policy accommodation that disproportionately benefits corporate insiders and wealthy investors who buy during panics.
- The rise of socialist municipal candidates in New York, LA, and Chicago reflects a generational reaction to being priced out of housing, burdened by student debt, and excluded from the stock-market wealth effect.
- The Genius Act's stablecoin framework may function as a mechanism to sustain demand for short-term Treasuries as foreign central banks diversify away from US debt.
- Higher Fed rates paradoxically increased wealth for asset-heavy households via money-market yields and stock gains while failing to address the supply-side housing constraints that drive urban unaffordability.
- Only 6% of Americans understand the Fed's dual mandate, making it easy for politicians to redirect economic frustration toward corporations or neighbors rather than monetary policy.

## Detailed Notes

### 1993 as the Structural Origin of Today's Inequality [00:03:07]

Baldwin identifies six simultaneous 1993 events: the Clinton tax change incentivizing stock-based CEO pay; Executive Order 12866 putting Al Gore in charge of supply-side policy; Bob Rubin joining the economic council; Andrew Cuomo joining HUD; the launch of SPY, the first passive ETF; and the Fed's Jackson Hole embrace of 2% inflation targeting. Three concurrent deflationary forces—the Berlin Wall's fall, China's entry into global markets, and the internet—meant the Fed's inflation-targeting regime pumped money into real assets rather than consumer prices, devaluing the dollar roughly 55%.

### Stock-Market Americans vs. Everyone Else [00:06:39]

The shift to stock-based compensation drove CEO-to-worker pay from 100-to-1 to 400-to-1 in roughly six years. Combined with the 401k replacing defined-benefit pensions, the US economy split into 'stock-market Americans'—those compensated in equity, invested in markets, and shielded from inflation—and everyone else, who absorb rising rents, food, healthcare, and college costs with stagnant wages. Brown cites the Figma IPO as a vivid illustration of thousands of millionaires being created in real time while most young people feel the economy is unaffordable.

### Crisis Accommodation and the K-Shaped Recovery Pattern [00:09:51]

Baldwin describes a recurring pattern: markets sell off sharply, policy accommodation arrives (FDIC backstops, Fed lending facilities, presidential rhetoric), corporate insiders buy heavily at the trough, and markets rip back to all-time highs within weeks. He argues insiders have correctly called the bottom of every major downturn since 2008, and those who panic-sell are consistently on the wrong side. April 7, 2025 is cited as the most recent example, where a tariff-rhetoric softening produced a V-shaped recovery to new highs within two months.

### The Genius Act, Stablecoins, and Treasury Demand [00:13:59]

Brown and Baldwin discuss the Genius Act as a mechanism to onboard millions of stablecoin users whose portfolios are backed by short-term T-bills, thereby creating persistent new demand for US Treasuries at a time when China and other foreign nations are diversifying away from US debt. Baldwin frames this as part of a broader pattern of exotic financial innovation designed to sustain demand for the currency and suppress yields.

### Political Fallout and the Socialist Mayor Phenomenon [00:21:13]

Baldwin traces socialist mayoral victories in New York (Mamdani), LA (Karen Bass), and Chicago to the Cantillon effect concentrating new capital in financial centers, driving up housing and living costs. Brown adds historical context: post-WWII suburban policy deliberately made people property owners to reduce susceptibility to anti-capitalist messaging, a strategy that has broken down as urban renters—holding no assets—embrace anti-capitalist candidates. Both agree the proximate cause is the Fed's failure to recognize and address the 2020–2021 inflation surge, which produced the highest inflation in 50 years and destroyed purchasing power for non-asset-owners.
