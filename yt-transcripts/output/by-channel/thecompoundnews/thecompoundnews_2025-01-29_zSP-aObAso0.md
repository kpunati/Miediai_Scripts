# Your Budget Sucks | Animal Spirits 397

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=zSP-aObAso0
**Duration:** 61:44

## Summary

Episode 397 of Animal Spirits centers on the January 2025 DeepSeek AI shock, analyzing what the Chinese AI model's efficiency means for Nvidia, the Magnificent 7, and the broader AI investment thesis. Hosts Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson also discuss Howard Marks's view that behavioral signs of a full bubble are not yet present, a deep-dive into decades of hedge fund fee extraction, and why leveraged ETFs are trading vehicles rather than long-term holdings. The episode closes with an examination of personal-finance topics including egg prices, credit card stress signals, rental inflation trends, and the viral Brooklyn couple's $400k-income budget.

## Key Takeaways

- DeepSeek's efficient AI model raises the possibility that hardware spend requirements for AI may be overstated, which pressured Nvidia and semiconductor stocks sharply.
- Ben Thompson argues the biggest long-run beneficiaries of model commoditization are large consumer-tech platforms like Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Apple, not chip makers.
- Howard Marks distinguishes between high valuations (present) and true bubble behavior (not yet present), noting the lack of widespread speculative mania outside pockets like meme coins.
- Hedge fund managers have captured roughly half of all profits earned since 1969, with concentration among the top 20 managers making selection critical.
- Three-times-leveraged S&P ETFs significantly underperform their stated leverage over multi-year periods due to volatility drag, and are designed as short-term trading instruments only.
- Rental inflation is running negative year-over-year per the new-tenants rent index, a leading indicator that reduces near-term inflation concerns.
- Insider sell-to-buy ratios are at record lows going back to 1988, reflecting concentrated equity compensation rather than necessarily bearish fundamentals.

## Detailed Notes

### DeepSeek Shock and Mag 7 Implications [00:00:04]

The hosts describe January 27, 2025 as an unusually eventful market day driven by the emergence of DeepSeek, a Chinese AI model that appeared to match frontier performance at far lower cost. Nvidia fell nearly 20% and the SMH semiconductor ETF fell roughly 10%. Ben Thompson's analysis is cited at length: model commoditization is net-positive for large consumer-tech platforms (Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Apple) while Google and chip-focused pure plays face headwinds. Hosts emphasize broad uncertainty and criticize overconfident narratives in either direction.

### Howard Marks on Bubble Conditions [00:00:04]

Howard Marks, appearing on Odd Lots, argued that current markets show numerical signs that resemble bubbles but lack the behavioral hallmarks — extreme disregard for discipline and caution — that define true manias. Hosts agree that while pockets of mania exist (meme coins, leveraged ETFs, quantum-computing stocks), the broader market does not exhibit the universal speculative fervor seen at past peaks.

### Hedge Fund Fees and Seth Klarman [00:00:04]

A Reuters report is discussed showing hedge funds collected $1.8 trillion in fees from 1969 to end-2024, retaining roughly half of all trading profits. Seth Klarman's Baupost Group is highlighted: after annualizing 20% returns for 26 years pre-crisis, his fund gained only about 4% per year since 2014, likely reflecting an overly cautious post-crisis posture. Hosts note that Klarman's $2,500 margin-of-safety book's mystique exceeds its actual originality.

### Leveraged ETF Risks [00:00:04]

Hosts examine ProShares' 3x S&P 500 ETF, noting that despite the S&P being up 45% over three years the 3x product is up only about 70% — barely more than double — due to volatility drag, and carried a 45-50% maximum drawdown. A 2020 example is cited where the 2x Nasdaq ETF lost 77% in three weeks. Both hosts and the ETF purveyors themselves state these are trading vehicles, not buy-and-hold instruments.

### Economic Indicators: Rents, Eggs, and Credit [00:00:04]

Colin Roach's new-tenants rent index shows rents down 2.5% year-over-year, which the hosts view as a key reason not to fear a re-acceleration of inflation. Egg prices doubling from $4 to $8 per dozen are discussed as symbolically irritating but adding only about $16 per month to a typical household's budget. JP Morgan and Capital One earnings reports show the share of credit card holders making only minimum payments rising to around 11%, near cycle highs, though the hosts view this as historically within normal range.

### Personal Budgets, Work Preferences, and Crypto [00:00:04]

A viral Brooklyn couple earning $400k with a $6,500 mortgage and $2,500 monthly retirement contributions sparks debate; hosts argue no budget survives public scrutiny and emphasize that responsible saving matters more than specific spending choices. A viewer poll shows 53% prefer hybrid work, 33% remote, and 15% fully in-office. Brian Armstrong's claim of 1 million new crypto coins created per week is highlighted as a regulatory challenge, while Tuttle Capital's leveraged crypto ETF filings are cited as examples of speculative product proliferation.
