# Four Rules for Negative Market Catalysts | WDWL

**Channel:** The Compound

## Summary

Josh Brown sits down with DataTrek Research co-founders Nick Colas and Jessica Rabe to work through three major investment themes as of February 3, 2025. They apply a systematic four-rule framework to assess whether Trump's new tariffs on Canada and Mexico constitute a genuine market risk, explore the disruptive innovation parallels between DeepSeek AI and historical industry disruptions, and conclude with a data-driven bull case for large-cap US healthcare as a rotation trade given extreme underperformance relative to technology.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump's 25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico surprised on timing but not substance — markets had largely priced in tariff risk from Trump's stated positions.
- A four-rule framework (recession risk, financial stability, policy-maker response, market reaction via VIX levels) helps investors triage negative market catalysts objectively.
- DeepSeek fits the classic Christensen disruptive innovation model: low-cost entrant targeting an underserved market that will improve and move up the value chain, analogous to Japanese automakers versus Detroit.
- Cheap AI is not existential for US big tech — Meta, Google, and Amazon can redirect capital toward monetizable applications rather than infrastructure buildout.
- Healthcare (XLV) is near a historically rare three-standard-deviation underperformance versus the S&P, with Wall Street projecting 21% earnings growth in 2025 on positive operating leverage.
- Healthcare trades at only 17.8x 2025 earnings versus 22x for the S&P and 28x for Tech, providing valuation support for a rotation trade.
- VIX levels of 27.3, 35.1, and 42.9 (1–3 standard deviations above the mean of 19.5) serve as thresholds for gauging severity and likelihood of policy-maker intervention.

## Detailed Notes

### Trump Tariffs: Threat Assessment Using the Four-Rule Framework [00:02:22]

Trump announced 25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico, effective Tuesday, linked to fentanyl and illegal immigration concerns. Nick Colas walked through four rules for evaluating negative catalysts: (1) recession risk — current tariffs are unlikely to cause a recession unless oil prices double; (2) financial stability — tariffs pose no systemic financial risk; (3) policy-maker response — here the policy makers are themselves the catalyst; (4) market response via VIX thresholds at 19.5, 27.3, 35.1, and 42.9. The VIX briefly crossed 20 before pulling back, suggesting markets were not deeply surprised. Mexico's agreement to supply 10,000 troops to the border led to a quick tariff pause.

### DeepSeek and Disruptive Innovation [00:14:55]

Nick Colas applied Clayton Christensen's disruptive innovation framework to DeepSeek: a low-cost Chinese LLM that is 'good enough' and will improve over time, analogous to Japanese automakers entering the US market post-1973 oil shock. OpenAI is cast as GM — well-funded but increasingly the incumbent retreating to the high end. Jessica Rabe noted that big tech was roughly a wash in S&P contribution during January 2025, with Meta and Google hitting new record highs even post-DeepSeek announcement, while Nvidia was the worst MAG-7 performer. Cheap AI benefits companies that can monetize via applications rather than infrastructure.

### Healthcare Sector: Contrarian Rotation Trade [00:27:40]

Jessica Rabe presented a quantitative case for large-cap healthcare using XLV. As of January 6, 2025, XLV had underperformed the S&P by 27.6 percentage points over the prior year, close to a three-standard-deviation event that has only occurred twice since 2000. Since the October 2022 S&P low, XLV is up only 21% versus the index's 69% gain. Wall Street consensus projects 21% earnings growth for healthcare in 2025 on 7% revenue growth, implying positive operating leverage reversal. XLV trades at 17.8x 2025 earnings versus 22x for the S&P and 28x for Tech, and the sector outperformed in January 2025 up 6.8% versus a 7% Tech decline.
