# Everything I Learned From 20 Years Running Research at JPMorgan | LFTC

## Summary

Michael Cembalest joins The Compound to celebrate the 20th anniversary of Eye on the Market, his influential research publication at JP Morgan Asset & Wealth Management. The conversation spans his best and worst calls, including an early 2006 subprime warning, a 2011 rebuttal of Meredith Whitney's municipal bond Armageddon thesis, and a contrarian no-recession call in 2023 based on corporate sector surpluses. Cembalest also discusses the role of humor in his work (tariff penguins, European Lego dioramas), the mechanics of how markets bottom before bad news peaks, leadership lessons from Jamie Dimon, and his due diligence encounter with a Madoff feeder fund. The episode also touches on geopolitical risk as a poor market signal, the structural problems of the eurozone, and the current landscape of M&A, private credit, and potential future crises.

## Key Takeaways

- The stock market reliably bottoms well in advance of economic data troughs and even before all financial casualties are known, a pattern visible from the Great Depression through 2009 and 2012 in Europe.
- Geopolitical risk indices have a negative relationship with equity returns over long periods, meaning high geopolitical fear is generally not a reliable reason to reduce market exposure.
- In December 2008, credit markets were pricing investment grade bonds at a 14% implied default rate (actual historic worst: 3%) and high yield at 55% (actual worst: 34%), creating a compelling buying opportunity.
- The 2023 yield curve inversion did not produce a recession partly because corporate sectors were in surplus and had termed out liabilities during the zero-rate era, breaking the traditional transmission mechanism.
- Cembalest argues the 2008 financial crisis was significantly driven by government-mandated GSE affordable lending targets, predating and ultimately luring the private sector into subprime, contrary to the dominant post-crisis narrative.
- European equity underperformance since 2010 was anticipated through structural eurozone flaws: the currency union caused macroeconomic divergence rather than convergence between northern and southern members.
- Strong opinions and willingness to be wrong publicly—combined with clear post-mortem analysis—are what built Eye on the Market's credibility over 20 years, contrasted with strategists who produce intentionally bland, inoffensive commentary.

## Detailed Notes

### Origins of Eye on the Market and Best Calls [00:00:16]

Eye on the Market started in 2005 at Mary Callahan Erdos' request to externalize JPMorgan Private Bank's investment thinking. Cembalest credits early credibility to his 2006 subprime warning to Jamie Dimon and the 2007-2008 decision to enter that year underweight credit and equities. He notes the publication accelerated to near-weekly frequency during the financial crisis, reaching 584 issues and 4,500 pages by the 20th anniversary.

### Controversies and Shelved Pieces [00:00:16]

Only two Eye on the Market pieces were ever killed: a March 2021 piece presenting scientific evidence for a COVID lab leak origin (shelved due to China business concerns) and a 2025 piece on executive orders targeting law firms (shelved to avoid putting a target on JPMorgan's back). Cembalest distinguished these policy overreach actions from standard political disagreements, arguing the law firm orders were not policies the president ran on.

### Debunking Armageddon Calls: Meredith Whitney and the Armageddonist Chart [00:00:16]

When Meredith Whitney predicted 50-100 large municipal bond defaults on 60 Minutes, Cembalest responded with a data-intensive report 'How Different Is Detroit,' analyzing 350 metropolitan statistical areas. Detroit ranked below the 5th percentile on labor, income, and crime metrics and was shown to be an extreme outlier, not a bellwether. The subsequent 12-15 year municipal default rate was 0.1%. Cembalest also produced the 'Armageddonists' chart, which tracked equity and bond portfolio outcomes for investors who followed high-profile bearish macro calls, illustrating cumulative destruction of wealth for those who stayed short equities.

### Market Signals and the 2023 No-Recession Call [00:00:16]

Cembalest tracks a dashboard of indicators including the leading indicator index, CEO confidence, payroll growth, and forward profit growth expectations; notably, the geopolitical risk index shows a negative sign, meaning high geopolitical risk historically predicts worse, not better, returns if acted upon. In August 2023 he wrote the 'Rasputin' piece arguing the inverted yield curve would not cause a recession because prior curve-inversion recessions required positive real rates to shock corporate borrowers, whereas in 2022 real rates barely turned positive; additionally, the corporate sector was in surplus rather than deficit, removing the mechanism of capital spending retrenchment.

### How Markets Bottom: December 2008 Credit Pricing and the Bank Stock Signal [00:00:16]

Cembalest describes a December 2008 Eye on the Market as a once-in-a-career document, showing investment grade bonds implied a 14% default rate (historic worst was 3%) and high yield implied 55% (historic worst was 34%), using a conservative 20% recovery assumption. Separately, the S&P bank index bottomed in early 2009 with only 8% of eventual bank failures realized—mirrored in Europe where equities bottomed in 2012 while unemployment kept rising for two more years—illustrating the market's anticipatory function.

### Tariffs, Humor, and European Allocation [00:00:16]

Cembalest used penguin stuffed animals as co-panelists to satirize the liberation day tariff announcement after learning the formula used to calculate them was misapplied—the tariffs were roughly four times higher than the originating academic's intended model. His Lego stormtrooper diorama of the European bailout hierarchy was produced during a multi-day Long Island blackout and underpinned a long-running decision to be massively underweight European equities versus the US from 2010 onward, one he considers the most profitable asset allocation call of his career.

### Leadership, Madoff, and the GSE Role in the Financial Crisis [00:00:16]

Cembalest traces the financial crisis in part to the 2004 repeal of the 12-to-1 broker-dealer net capital rule, after which the big five broker-dealers rapidly reached 30-plus leverage. He contrasts Jamie Dimon's hands-on risk culture with predecessor bank CEOs, describing the JPMorgan halo effect that grants him access across industries. He recounted his own Madoff due diligence: he visited a feeder fund, found the custodian listed at the back of a Chinese restaurant on early Google Maps, drew a unicorn on the return chart for its impossibility, and was later deposed by the SEC. His favorite piece in the compilation shows GSE subprime loading preceded private sector activity by years and was driven by HUD low-income lending mandates, directly contradicting the dominant narrative that blamed only reckless private bankers.
