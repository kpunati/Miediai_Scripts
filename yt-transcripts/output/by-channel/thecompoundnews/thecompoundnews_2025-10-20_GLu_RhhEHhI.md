# A Year-End Rally Could Double The S&P 500's Gain This Year

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=GLu_RhhEHhI
**Duration:** 38:05

## Summary

Nick Colas and Jessica Rabe of DataTrek Research join Josh Brown to assess Q3 2025 earnings season, observing that while the nominal beat rate has moderated from the exceptional first two quarters, margin expansion is broad-based across five S&P sectors. They argue the Shiller PE overstates valuation risk because it uses decade-old earnings data, and that a 5-year earnings average reduces the effective CAPE to roughly 28-29. Historical seasonality strongly favors a December peak for the S&P 500, and detailed cash flow analysis of the five hyperscalers suggests their AI capex is comfortably funded by organic free cash flow.

## Key Takeaways

- S&P 500 earnings beat percentages in Q1 and Q2 2025 were the highest since 2021, though Q3 beat rates have normalized as analysts stopped sandbagging estimates.
- Five of eleven S&P sectors are expanding net margins in Q3, with financials (+1.7 pts) and technology (+1.0 pts) contributing most, undermining the narrative that strength is tech-only.
- The Shiller CAPE near 40 is misleading because it incorporates low-earnings years from the mid-2010s; using only the last five years produces a more moderate reading near 28.
- Seasonal data going back to 1980 shows the S&P peaks in December 53% of years with average returns of 22%, giving the current 13.3% YTD gain significant upside potential.
- The five largest hyperscalers generated ~$570 billion in operating cash flow in 2025, comfortably covering ~$325 billion in capex while still returning ~$210 billion to shareholders.
- Amazon is the only major tech company with no dividend and no buyback, reinvesting all operating cash flow into capex.
- OpenAI's lack of public financial disclosures and unknown auditor is flagged as a key transparency risk in the AI investment ecosystem.

## Detailed Notes

### Q3 Earnings Season Assessment [00:02:45]

DataTrek presented FactSet data showing S&P 500 earnings beat percentages by quarter. Q1 and Q2 2025 beat by 8.1% and 7.6% respectively, the highest since 2021's 14%+ beats. Q3 is tracking at ~5.9%, with analysts having stopped sandbagging estimates after getting beaten badly twice. Jessica Rabe noted that five of eleven sectors are expanding net margins, led by financials at +1.7 percentage points, followed by technology and utilities at +1.0 and +0.5 points each.

### Shiller PE and Valuation Debate [00:11:27]

The Shiller CAPE currently reads near 39-40, a level last seen during the dot-com bubble. However, Colas argues the metric is distorted because the 10-year average EPS denominator of ~$167 includes the low-earnings years of the mid-2010s. The 5-year average EPS (2021-2025) is $232, bringing the effective CAPE to 28-29. The hosts also discussed that a 24x multiple on the 2026 consensus EPS estimate of $304 would yield ~9% S&P upside, which DataTrek views as justified given the ROE profile of the top seven companies.

### Market Seasonality and Year-End Outlook [00:18:43]

Jessica Rabe presented seasonality data from 1980-present: the S&P 500 peaks in December 53% of years with average annual total returns of 22%, while October peaks have occurred only four times (less than 10% of years). With the S&P up 13.3% YTD and its current peak on October 8th, DataTrek remains bullish through year-end and views near-term weakness as buying opportunities. Year-end dynamics including institutional window dressing, share buyback resumption after earnings blackout periods, and options desk position unwinds are cited as structural drivers.

### Big Tech Cash Flow and AI Capex Analysis [00:26:26]

Colas analyzed the five largest hyperscalers (Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta), showing combined 2025 operating cash flow of ~$570 billion (up ~$38 billion from 2022). Of this, ~$325 billion is allocated to capex, ~$170 billion to buybacks, and ~$40 billion to dividends. Amazon stands out as the only company with zero buybacks or dividends, reinvesting all ~$100 billion of cash flow. A required-return analysis applying a 15% ROI hurdle shows most hyperscalers need only a 2-15% incremental return on their capex to justify AI investment, given the size of their underlying cash flows.
