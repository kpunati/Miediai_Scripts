# The AI Bull Market Is Perfectly Tracking the Original 90’s Internet Boom | WDWL?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-07-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=jSyd0pAxmnM
**Duration:** 37:37

## Summary

Josh Brown, Nick Colas, and Jessica Rabe of DataTrek Research examine the current US equity market environment through three lenses: S&P 500 valuation math showing limited upside without peak-cycle sentiment, a compelling chart overlay showing the NASDAQ's AI-era rally tracking the 1990s internet bull market almost exactly, and a present-versus-future-value framework applied to major technology stocks. The guests argue the US market warrants structurally higher multiples due to lower recession risk and an accelerating shift of European retail capital into US equities. They conclude that while valuations are stretched, the AI cycle is still early — analogous to mid-1997 relative to the March 2000 peak — and that dispersion within large-cap tech is increasing as investors now distinguish between AI winners and laggards.

## Key Takeaways

- At current S&P 500 earnings estimates of $264 (2025) and $300 (2026), meaningful upside requires holding a 24-26x PE multiple, leaving almost no margin for error.
- The NASDAQ's path since December 2022 overlays the 1995-2000 internet bull run almost exactly, placing today at roughly the mid-1997 equivalent — suggesting years of runway remain if the analogy holds.
- Every S&P 500 sector except healthcare and energy is trading above its 10-year average forward PE, indicating broad multiple expansion rather than a purely tech-driven phenomenon.
- Approximately $600 billion of foreign private capital flowed into US stocks in the 12 months ending May, contradicting the post-tariff narrative of foreign divestment.
- Computing power is roughly 33,000 times greater than during the 1990s bubble, which creates much higher barriers to entry for Gen AI, potentially sustaining the scarcity premium in large-cap tech for longer.
- Using a present-versus-future value framework, over 90% of Tesla's market cap depends on future earnings (autonomous vehicles, Optimus robot), while Alphabet has the lowest future-value dependence among big tech at 45%.
- The Magnificent 7 is fragmenting into clear AI winners (Nvidia, Meta, Microsoft, Broadcom) and relative laggards (Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Tesla on current-business metrics).

## Detailed Notes

### Q2 Earnings Season: Strong Beats but Valuation Overhang [00:00:11]

Q2 earnings growth consensus rose to 5.6%, though beat rates (83%) and average surprise magnitude (7.9%) are tracking below the 5-year norm of 9.1%. High-profile reporters like Netflix, JPMorgan, and Bank of America posted strong results but saw stock prices fade, reflecting a sell-the-news dynamic driven by valuation concerns rather than earnings disappointment.

### S&P 500 Valuation Grid and Upside Math [00:02:06]

DataTrek presents a fair-value grid spanning PE multiples from 14x (trough confidence) to 26x (beyond the 1990s bubble peak) against earnings scenarios from below-consensus to current estimates of $264 for 2025 and $300 for 2026. At a 22x multiple and $264 earnings, the market is at fair value; upside to 26x would imply only a 5% return on 2026 estimates. The team argues the market has re-rated because investors now price recession probability at roughly 5% rather than the historical 15-25%.

### European Capital Rotation into US Equities [00:08:40]

European wealth managers and retail investors — particularly in the UK, France, and Italy — are shifting away from domestic assets (Italian sovereigns, utility stocks) toward US large-cap tech, which they view as globally scalable with superior returns on equity. Foreign investor flows into US assets totaled approximately $1.7 trillion in the 12 months ending May, with $600 billion going directly into equities, a trend that could persist for 20 years. ASML's 14% earnings-related decline last week was cited as reinforcing European investors' preference for US tech.

### NASDAQ 1990s Parallel and AI Bull Market Timeline [00:17:48]

Jessica Rabe presents a chart comparing the NASDAQ composite's performance from January 1995 with its performance from the December 2022 low; the two lines track almost identically through 639 trading days, both showing roughly 100% gains. The macro parallels are strong: both were preceded by Fed rate shocks (1994 and 2022), both were catalyzed by a disruptive new technology (Netscape/internet in 1995; ChatGPT/Gen AI in late 2022). Current position in the analog is approximately mid-1997, implying years of potential runway before a hypothetical 2000-style peak.

### Structural Differences from the 1990s [00:24:53]

Key differences from the original dot-com era include 33,000x more computing power due to Moore's Law advances and dramatically higher barriers to entry (years and billions of dollars to build large language models vs. a few months and millions for Netscape Navigator). These factors could sustain a scarcity premium in Gen AI names longer than the 1990s. Nick also highlights the AI cycle's likely evolution into physical AI and robotics — analogous to how the 1990s internet boom was simultaneously driven by the mobile phone buildout — as a potential second leg of the secular theme.

### Present vs. Future Value Framework for Major Tech Stocks [00:29:38]

Nick applies a perpetuity-based framework (forward earnings divided by 10%) to separate how much of each stock's current price reflects current earning power versus future growth expectations. Tesla stands at 91% future value (driven by Optimus robots and Cybercab autonomy), while Alphabet is lowest at 45%. Big tech averages 67% future value versus roughly 52% for the broader S&P 500. Josh also notes that names like Palantir (122x trailing price-to-sales, up 400% year-over-year), Cloudflare, CrowdStrike, and Snowflake demonstrate the AI bull market has expanded well beyond the MAG7.
