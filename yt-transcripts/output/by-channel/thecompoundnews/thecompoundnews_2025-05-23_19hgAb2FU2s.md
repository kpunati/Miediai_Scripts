# Real Men Don't Panic Over Bonds | TCAF 193

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-05-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=19hgAb2FU2s
**Duration:** 74:01

## Summary

In this 74-minute episode, Josh Brown and Michael Batnick interview Richard Bernstein, CEO and CIO of Richard Bernstein Advisors, about the current macro and market environment. Bernstein argues that rising Treasury yields reflect a decade-long slow deterioration in US creditworthiness rather than a sudden crisis, illustrated by the persistent yield spread over German bonds since the 2011 US debt downgrade. He sees the Big Beautiful Bill as stimulative but deficit-widening, warns that corporate profit growth is peaking (from ~14% to potentially 1-2% by year-end), and advocates for international quality stocks and gold as portfolio diversifiers. Bernstein also shares structural concerns about the small-cap market, the limits of Fed policy in a private-credit-dominated economy, and AI adoption timelines being slower than market enthusiasm implies.

## Key Takeaways

- The US pays ~190 basis points more on debt than Germany—a penalty that has compounded since the 2011 debt downgrade and acts as a quiet tax on the entire economy via higher mortgage, corporate, and muni rates.
- Bond vigilante dynamics are present but slow-burning; a single bad CPI print could accelerate a bond selloff significantly.
- Corporate earnings growth momentum is peaking and Bernstein's models project a drop from ~14% growth in Q1 to roughly 1-2% by year-end, suggesting a more defensive market posture is warranted.
- Individual investors are increasingly shunning diversification, with average Merrill Lynch private client portfolios running beta above 1.4—a contrarian warning sign.
- The small-cap market is structurally impaired because high-quality new companies are bypassing the Russell 2000 lifecycle and entering the S&P 500 directly as large-caps.
- International quality stocks offer higher dividend yields and comparable growth trajectories at far cheaper valuations than Mag 7 names.
- Gold is a strong hedge against uncertainty, not a momentum trade, and the NFIB uncertainty index tracks closely with gold's year-over-year price change.

## Detailed Notes

### Big Beautiful Bill and Fiscal Outlook [00:06:21]

Bernstein characterizes the tax bill as stimulative but internally inconsistent with tariff policy, since cutting taxes while trying to reduce trade deficits works at cross-purposes. The Joint Committee on Taxation projects $3.8 trillion in additional deficits through 2034, and Bernstein says the idea that tariffs will pay for it has quietly been abandoned by the White House.

### Treasury Yields and Creditworthiness [00:10:40]

Bernstein's central chart shows the spread between US 10-year and German 10-year yields. After the 2011 US debt downgrade the US began consistently paying more than Germany, now ~190 basis points, despite de facto safe-haven status. This is a slow structural bleed of higher borrowing costs that flows through to mortgages, corporate bonds, and munis—not a sudden crisis. Fed Governor Waller confirmed markets expect heavier Treasury supply from the bill and want a lower price, i.e., higher yield.

### Bond Market, Fed Policy, and Recession Risks [00:20:46]

The hosts discuss a bond auction that produced the third-worst stock market reaction in history the prior day. Bernstein argues the Fed has not hiked because it is politically impossible under Trump, but from a pure economic standpoint rate cuts are unlikely and a hike is not off the table. He also flags rising mortgage delinquencies as an early warning that the consumer's largest liability—70% of household debt—is beginning to stress, with implications for Fed cutting timelines.

### Diversification vs. Mag 7 Concentration [00:38:38]

Bernstein notes that advisors are having increasingly difficult conversations with clients who want to abandon diversification after years of Mag 7 outperformance. He argues that while these companies have genuinely superior earnings, they appear near peak momentum—his models show S&P 500 EPS growth decelerating sharply—and that international quality stocks (European pharma, luxury, food) trade at a significant discount with better dividend yields and comparable growth.

### Small Cap Structural Decline and IPO Market [00:52:24]

Bernstein and Brown observe that the Russell 2000 is becoming a graveyard of fallen angels because high-quality companies now emerge from VC at valuations that immediately qualify for the S&P 500, skipping the small-cap lifecycle. Examples include Airbnb, Snowflake, and Core Weave. This structural shift means historical small-cap recovery patterns may not repeat.

### AI Adoption and Market Implications [01:06:56]

Bernstein thinks AI will transform the economy but that the transformation will be fast yet slower than current market enthusiasm implies, constrained by data center build-out capacity and corporate CapEx comfort levels. He draws a parallel to the internet bubble: overcapitalization of a sector that genuinely transformed the economy but produced poor investor returns.

### Gold as Uncertainty Hedge [01:00:19]

Bernstein presents a chart overlaying the NFIB uncertainty index with gold's year-over-year change, showing a strong correlation over 10 years. He advocates holding gold as portfolio ballast against uncertainty, not as a momentum trade, and notes that in 2011 GLD briefly exceeded SPY as the largest market-cap ETF—a signal that proved to be a generational top for gold and a buy signal for equities.
