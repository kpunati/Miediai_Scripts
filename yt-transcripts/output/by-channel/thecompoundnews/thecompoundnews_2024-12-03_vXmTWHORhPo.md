# Introducing the Degen Dow

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vXmTWHORhPo
**Duration:** 41:44

## Summary

Josh Brown and Michael Batnick cover a broad range of market topics on their 'What Are Your Thoughts' episode from December 3, 2024. The centerpiece is the debut of the 'Degen Dow,' a tongue-in-cheek equal-weighted index of 30 speculative, high-beta stocks — including MicroStrategy, GameStop, Coinbase, and Nvidia — that has returned roughly 44% year-to-date, far outpacing the S&P 500, and serves as a barometer of retail speculation. The episode also examines BlackRock's aggressive acquisition-led push into private credit and infrastructure, contrasts its fee structure with Blackstone's, and discusses what this means for the asset management industry. Additional segments honor the late Art Cashin, critique bearish Twitter commentary, make the bull case for Amazon's AI positioning, and use Intel's 25-year underperformance as a cautionary tale about single-stock risk.

## Key Takeaways

- Year-end seasonality data shows the S&P 500 rises in December about 74-75% of the time regardless of prior-year performance, casting doubt on the idea of an outsized 'Santa Claus rally' in strong years.
- The 'Degen Dow' — an equal-weighted basket of 30 meme, crypto-adjacent, and high-beta stocks — is up 44% year-to-date, illustrating the 2024 theme of elevated retail speculation and leverage.
- BlackRock's acquisitions of Prequin, Global Infrastructure Partners, and HPS Investment Partners reflect a strategic pivot to private markets, where fee-related earnings are higher and stickier than in its dominant but low-margin public ETF business.
- Blackstone's larger market cap despite managing one-tenth of BlackRock's AUM demonstrates the valuation premium the market assigns to private-market fee structures.
- Among the 'Magnificent 7' minus Tesla and Nvidia, only Meta and Apple have outperformed the S&P 500 over the past three years, with Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft all lagging the index.
- Amazon's Trainium chip development positions it as a potential 'AI arms dealer' through AWS, offering a differentiated angle beyond its retail and cloud businesses.
- Intel's inclusion in the Dow Jones from November 1999 to November 2024 bookends a 57% total return decline, making it a canonical example of single-stock concentration risk over a 25-year horizon.

## Detailed Notes

### Year-End Seasonality and Performance Chasing [00:02:23]

Callie Cox's data shows the S&P 500 rises in December 74-75% of the time in strong years and 74% of all other years, nearly eliminating the supposed 'strong year' seasonal edge. However, Brown argues the chase dynamic is real, manifesting as portfolio managers seeking catchup trades in laggards rather than doubling down on leaders. January historically sees the highest median monthly equity fund inflows, while December is often negative due to withdrawals and tax-related selling.

### Introducing the Degen Dow [00:08:44]

Brown and Batnick unveil the Degen Dow, a 30-stock equal-weighted index constructed from the most speculative, meme-driven, and high-beta names popular on Robinhood, StockTwits, and WallStreetBets. Starting from 45 names, the 15 smallest by market cap were removed. Components include MicroStrategy, GameStop, Coinbase, Carvana, DraftKings, Reddit, Nvidia, Riot Blockchain, Roblox, Lucid, and Vistra Energy, among others. Year-to-date return stands at approximately 44%, more than doubling the S&P 500's gain, with extreme prior drawdowns (80%+) visible on the longer-term chart.

### BlackRock's Push into Private Markets [00:21:29]

BlackRock has acquired Prequin (private market data), Global Infrastructure Partners, and HPS Investment Partners ($150 billion in private credit) for approximately $12 billion in stock. The strategic rationale is that private-market vehicles generate substantially higher and stickier fees than public ETFs, where BlackRock charges single-digit basis points. The AUM/market cap comparison with Blackstone ($11.5 trillion vs. $1.1 trillion in AUM but $140 billion vs. $187 billion in market cap) makes the fee-premium case starkly. Brown notes Vanguard is unlikely to follow this path, creating a long-term divergence between the two index giants.

### Art Cashin Tribute [00:27:47]

The hosts pay tribute to Art Cashin, the longtime UBS director of floor operations and CNBC fixture, who passed away. Brown emphasizes Cashin's universal respect — a rare quality in finance — his steadfastness during the 9/11 exchange reopening, and his generosity with time and knowledge. The segment is brief but emotionally substantive, with both hosts noting plans to discuss him further on The Compound and Friends podcast.

### Amazon Bull Case and Mega-Cap Tech Relative Performance [00:33:05]

Brown makes the case for Amazon, noting it trades at about 40x next year's earnings but has multiple earnings levers via AWS, its own Trainium AI chips, and a proven enterprise customer base capable of demonstrating AI ROI today. Among the Magnificent 7 (ex-Tesla, ex-Nvidia), only Meta and Apple have outperformed the S&P 500 over three years; Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft have lagged. Brown mentions selling Alphabet to buy more Amazon. Intel's 25-year arc — added to the Dow in November 1999, removed in November 2024, down 57% in total return over that period and having lost $147 billion in market cap under Pat Gelsinger's tenure — closes the episode as a warning on single-stock concentration.
