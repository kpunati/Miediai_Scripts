# Which Beaten Down Stocks Are Worth a Closer Look?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=svPDff_TSc0
**Duration:** 36:32

## Summary

Ask The Compound episode 216, recorded March 27, 2026, features Ben Carlson and Duncan Hill reviewing the Magnificent Seven sell-off, which has driven the Mag 7 basket down roughly 14% year-to-date while energy and hard-asset sectors have kept the equal-weighted S&P 500 flat. The hosts survey beaten-down stocks across software, private equity, credit cards, and fintech, ultimately arguing that Meta and Microsoft represent the highest-probability recovery plays. A second segment debates the inevitability of 24/7 equity trading, and a third segment presents Ben's three-factor explanation for structurally higher market valuations: the 401k automatic-investing revolution, higher household equity allocations, and improved corporate profit margins. The episode closes with advice for financial advisors on building a newsletter-first content platform.

## Key Takeaways

- The equal-weighted S&P 500 is flat or positive YTD because gains in energy, utilities, and materials are offsetting deep losses in tech and communication services.
- Private equity managers (KKR, Apollo, Blackstone) and software names (Adobe, Salesforce, CoreWeave) have seen the steepest drawdowns, in the 40-60% range.
- Ben Carlson identifies Meta and Microsoft as the highest-probability 'plug your nose and buy' candidates given their scale and earnings power, despite further downside risk.
- The hosts are skeptical of 24/7 stock trading, warning that off-hours markets will carry wider spreads and attract sophisticated arbitrageurs at the expense of retail investors.
- Higher CAPE ratios since the 1990s are best explained by automatic 401k enrollment, rising equity allocations, and structurally superior corporate margins—not by a shrinking supply of public equities.
- Financial advisors building a content presence should own their platform via a Substack or Beehive newsletter, write in plain English for clients rather than peers, and build a searchable library of expertise.

## Detailed Notes

### Mag 7 Drawdown and Equal-Weight Divergence [00:02:41]

Every Mag 7 stock is in a correction (down 10%+); Tesla, Meta, and Microsoft are in bear markets (down 20%+). The Mag 7 basket is down ~14% YTD versus the S&P 500 total return of -5%, while the equal-weighted S&P is roughly flat because energy stocks (up ~40% YTD despite only ~4% of the index) and other hard-asset sectors are offsetting tech weakness. Carlson notes that rising rates and private-credit concerns are also weighing on financials.

### Bottom Fishing: Beaten-Down Names Across Sectors [00:07:14]

Software names Adobe, Salesforce, and CoreWeave are down 50-60%+. Private equity giants KKR, Apollo, and Blackstone are off 40-50%. Credit-card names (Capital One, Ally, Amex, Visa, Mastercard) are down 17-30% on fears of AI disruption and a credit cycle. Fintech (Robinhood -55%, Coinbase -60%, Block -80%) tracks closely with Bitcoin's ~50% drawdown from highs. Carlson argues Meta and Microsoft are the most defensible recovery bets; Duncan mentions nibbling at Microsoft and Nvidia.

### 24/7 Trading: Inevitable But Risky for Retail [00:14:39]

Both hosts are skeptical of around-the-clock equity markets, citing wider spreads, lower liquidity off-hours, and the likelihood that sophisticated traders will exploit retail activity. Robinhood already offers 24/5 trading. Carlson advises that retail investors simply don't have to participate in after-hours sessions and can remain low-frequency traders in a high-frequency world.

### Why Valuations Are Structurally Higher Since the 1990s [00:19:44]

Carlson presents three drivers: (1) the 401k automatic-investing revolution—default enrollment and target-date funds push money into equities regardless of market conditions; (2) rising household equity allocations, now averaging ~70% across age groups versus much lower in prior decades; and (3) structurally higher corporate profit margins as the economy shifts to asset-light, intangible-heavy tech businesses. He rejects the supply-of-equities argument, noting that micro-cap proliferation in the 1990s was the anomaly and that mega-caps now bundle many formerly separate public companies.

### Content Strategy for Financial Advisors [00:27:43]

Carlson recommends starting with a Substack or Beehive newsletter rather than guest-posting in trade publications, since the goal is to own the distribution channel and build an email list. He advises writing weekly in plain English for current and future clients, building a searchable library of answers to perennial questions, and treating content as a trust-building tool rather than a direct client-acquisition funnel. Blogging should be enjoyable first; business benefits follow organically.
