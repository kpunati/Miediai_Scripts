# These Are the Stocks to Buy In 2026 | TCAF 225

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=urIbSAmx2jw
**Duration:** 83:15

## Summary

In episode 225 of The Compound and Friends, JC Parets (Trend Labs) joins hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to present his 2026 market outlook using technical analysis. The episode covers JC's four groups investors should fade (sell-side analysts, asset managers, journalists, and economists), then moves to specific 2026 trade ideas across energy, small caps, financials, semiconductors, and commercial real estate. The discussion includes a detailed post-mortem of 2025 asset class returns showing international equities and commodities dramatically outperformed US mega-cap tech, and closes with a skeptical debate on prediction markets.

## Key Takeaways

- JC Parets identifies four 'fade groups' whose consensus views tend to be wrong: sell-side analysts (especially at bearish consensus), asset managers in futures markets, mainstream journalists (magazine cover indicator), and economists who prioritize theory over observable price action.
- International equities outperformed the US in 2025 for the first time in roughly a decade, and JC believes the trend has multiple years left given how far behind cumulative returns remain versus the S&P 500.
- Energy and oil services stocks are identified as the highest-conviction 2026 opportunity, with small-cap energy, Baker Hughes, Halliburton, and Exxon all cited; the sector is near zero index weight and draws a commodity super-cycle analogy.
- Semiconductors continue making all-time highs on a relative basis even as Magnificent 7 mega-cap stocks face headwinds, suggesting the technology weakness is concentrated in software and large-cap names rather than the sector broadly.
- Small caps (particularly financials, industrials, and discretionary sub-sectors) show multi-year technical bases that JC believes are resolving higher, supporting a rotation from mega-cap dominance.
- CBRE is highlighted as a commercial real estate breakout candidate that has transitioned to an ARR model and is trading like a tech company, with a near-term target around $185.
- Prediction markets are dismissed as illiquid binary bets akin to rebranded binary options, with no structural basis to become larger than the securities market despite promotional claims from exchange operators.

## Detailed Notes

### LTCM, Isaac Newton, and the Limits of Intelligence [00:10:48]

The episode opens with a Warren Buffett video clip analyzing Long-Term Capital Management's collapse, noting the firm's 16 partners had roughly 350-400 combined years of experience, Nobel Prize-winning theoreticians, and their own capital at risk—yet went broke by risking what they had and needed to make money they didn't need. JC draws the parallel to Isaac Newton's South Sea bubble losses in the 1700s, arguing intelligence is no edge against human emotions like FOMO and comparison with peers.

### The Four Fade Groups [00:18:58]

JC outlines four groups whose consensus views should be faded: (1) sell-side analysts, who face career risk and conflicts of interest from M&A work; (2) asset managers and hedge funds, whose futures positioning provides contrarian signals (citing a historic Russell 2000 short position in summer 2025 that preceded a rally); (3) mainstream journalists, whose magazine covers lag price action by the time articles are published and approved; and (4) economists, who ignore observable price data in favor of models that may be based on revised or lagged data. The fade signal is strongest when all four converge.

### Magazine Cover Indicator in Action [00:29:25]

JC reviews recent examples: a November cover depicting a skier with red-arrow ski trails predicting markets would topple the economy was followed by micro-caps up 15%, transports up 12%, and small caps up 12%. A current cover featuring 'Beware the Bubble' is cited as similarly actionable because it fails to specify any particular bubble, making it effectively a reflexive endorsement of the bull case. The MicroStrategy/Forbes cover featuring Michael Saylor is noted to have coincided with the peak of the MSTR-to-Bitcoin ratio on November 20, 2024—the day after IBIT options began trading.

### 2025 Asset Class Review and International Thesis [00:39:17]

The hosts recap 2025 returns: silver +150%, gold +65%, emerging markets +34-35%, developed international +30-32%, versus S&P 500 with a strong but relative underperformance versus non-US assets. JC reiterates his year-ago call that international would outperform and the dollar would weaken, both of which proved correct. He argues the 15-year divergence between US and non-US stocks means the rotation could persist for years even after one strong year.

### 2026 Stock Picks: Financials and Semiconductors [01:07:09]

Josh presents Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs as best-in-class financials, with JC noting the entire global financial sector—US, European, and Japanese banks—is in strong uptrends that historically correlate with healthy bull markets. KLA Corporation and Lam Research are highlighted as semiconductor equipment leaders; while both are extended near-term, JC sees KLAC above $1,525 and LRCX above $236 as structurally bullish. CBRE is presented as a commercial real estate breakout candidate transitioning from transactional commissions to an ARR model.

### Energy as the 2026 Macro Trade [01:00:02]

JC argues energy is the highest-conviction 2026 sector, analogizing the current setup to where gold was before its breakout. Small-cap energy, oil services (Baker Hughes, Halliburton, Schlumberger), and Exxon are all cited. The energy weighting in major US indexes is approximately 2% for the S&P 500 and essentially zero in the NASDAQ, suggesting institutional underownership and potential for a squeeze. Crude oil has not yet rallied materially, making services names particularly attractive as leveraged plays.

### Small Caps and Prediction Markets Debate [00:55:07]

JC presents small-cap sub-sector analysis showing all-time highs in small-cap momentum, industrials, materials, technology, and financials, arguing the negative narrative around small caps reflects selective framing. The S&P 600 (requiring profitability) and Russell 2000 both show multi-year technical bases that JC believes signal a sustained breakout. The episode closes with a debate on prediction markets, which JC dismisses as illiquid binary bets resembling rebranded binary options with no structural reason to displace securities markets, while acknowledging they may grow as a niche gambling product.
