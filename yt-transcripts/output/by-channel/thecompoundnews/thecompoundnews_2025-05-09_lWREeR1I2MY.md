# The Soul of a Short-Seller | TCAF 191

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-05-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lWREeR1I2MY
**Duration:** 88:56

## Summary

Carson Block, founder of Muddy Waters Capital, sits down with The Compound hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick for a deep dive into the world of activist short selling. Block explains the mechanics of finding and publishing on fraudulent or scammy public companies, the legal and financial risks of the business, and why he believes today's market environment creates unusually strong incentives for corporate misconduct. The conversation covers notable cases—Sino Forest, Luckin Coffee, e.l.f. Beauty, AppLovin, and Chinese reverse mergers—illustrating how frauds are structured and ultimately exposed. Block also reflects on the career of Nate Anderson of Hindenburg Research and the broader decline of investigative financial journalism.

## Key Takeaways

- Activist short sellers differ from traditional short sellers by requiring provable misconduct and publicly publishing their findings to catalyze market and regulatory action.
- Block estimates only about 5% of shortable companies fit the activist short profile, requiring proof of fraud, misrepresentation, or deceptive accounting rather than merely poor fundamentals.
- The probability of getting caught for corporate fraud has declined since the post-Enron era while potential rewards have grown massively due to inflated asset values and stock-based compensation.
- Passive investing flows and relentless 401k inflows can sustain overvalued or fraudulent stocks for extended periods because most buyers are price-insensitive index buyers who don't research individual names.
- Companies facing short reports respond in two ways: quietly unwinding the problematic behavior while denying wrongdoing, or doubling down and denying while continuing the behavior—as Block alleges AppLovin is doing.
- Muddy Waters uses SAIC (State Administration of Industry and Commerce) filings to cross-check Chinese company financials and has found systematic discrepancies between US-reported revenues and China-reported revenues.
- The business model of Muddy Waters involves shorting stocks before publishing research, with the fund earning returns when the market validates their findings—a structure that attracts regulatory scrutiny despite being analogous to what long-biased analysts do.

## Detailed Notes

### Introduction and Market Structure Discussion [00:00:00]

The episode opens with a cold-open discussion about whether large-cap tech companies have become de facto consumer staples. Block argues that US equity markets are increasingly driven by passive flows and 401k inflows, with fundamentals mattering more at the margin. He notes that among S&P 500 companies many names are down 30% or more over three years, showing that within-index dispersion is large even as headline indexes rise.

### What Activist Short Selling Is and Why It Exists [00:09:44]

Block defines activist short selling as focusing on provably scammy or fraudulent companies, taking short positions, and then publishing findings publicly. He contrasts this with traditional short sellers like Chanos who run diversified short books. Block argues the activist approach is necessary because financial investigative journalism has collapsed and regulators act as 'financial archaeologists' who wait for blowups rather than proactively policing fraud.

### Incentive Environment for Fraud [00:13:08]

Block argues that since the post-Enron era, regulators have been reluctant to prosecute gray-zone cases. Combined with inflated asset values—meaning the payoff for a successful fraud is orders of magnitude larger than in 2010—and stock-based compensation that allows executives to monetize quickly, the risk-reward calculus for committing fraud has rarely been more favorable. Investors anesthetized by emergency monetary policy and passive indexing are also less likely to scrutinize individual holdings.

### Sourcing Short Ideas and Activist Process [00:28:41]

Ideas come mostly from long-short fund managers pitching Muddy Waters on companies they are already short. Block is skeptical of quantitative screens, preferring to require civil-standard provability: a preponderance of evidence that disclosed information is materially false. The firm typically covers five to six names a year and uses 'schmuck insurance' (small initial positions) to hedge against being preempted by other short sellers.

### Case Studies: e.l.f. Beauty and AppLovin [00:32:12]

Muddy Waters published on e.l.f. Beauty after customs import data could not be reconciled with reported cost of sales, suggesting inventory and revenue misstatements; the company subsequently lowered guidance significantly, which Block interprets as unwinding the inflation. On AppLovin, Block alleges the company builds persistent identifier graphs that track users across apps without opt-in consent, violating platform terms of service, and that management lied in their public denial—yet the stock was up 12% on strong earnings the day of recording.

### Chinese Reverse Mergers and the Sino Forest Short [00:49:57]

Block recounts how Muddy Waters began with Chinese reverse-merger frauds, using SAIC government filings as a primary verification tool that US law firms ignored. Orient Paper reported roughly $100 million in revenue to US investors versus approximately $3 million to Chinese authorities. Sino Forest was a larger, more complex fraud with over 100 entities; forged bank letters and forestry bureau correspondence revealed the fraud dated back to the company's original listing. John Paulson was the largest shareholder; the trade was highly profitable for Muddy Waters.

### The Business of Muddy Waters and Industry Reflections [01:10:15]

Block operates a hedge fund where the short-activism and publishing are integrated—he takes positions before publishing and relies on market reaction to generate returns. He notes the business exposes him to government investigations (including a DOJ/SEC inquiry) and relentless legal pressure. Muddy Waters has expanded into long-oriented junior mining investments and public equities in Vietnam and India. Block reflects that Nate Anderson of Hindenburg is one of the very few to exit activist short selling on top rather than via blowup.
