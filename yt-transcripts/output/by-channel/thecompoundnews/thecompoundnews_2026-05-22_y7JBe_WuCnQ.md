# Why Memory Is a Bubble but Nvidia Isn’t | TCAF 243

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=y7JBe_WuCnQ
**Duration:** 87:15

## Summary

This episode of The Compound and Friends (TCAF 243) brings together hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick with VanEck CEO Jan Van Eck for a wide-ranging 87-minute conversation on semiconductors, AI, and capital markets. Van Eck argues that Nvidia is transitioning from a high-growth stock to a blue-chip compounder on the strength of its CUDA software ecosystem, while memory chipmakers like Micron are in a price-driven bubble with weak competitive moats. The discussion also covers the structural case for large-cap dominance via network effects and diminished antitrust enforcement, the risk of rising long-term Treasury yields to equity markets, the massive potential wealth creation event from SpaceX and Anthropic IPOs, and corporate AI adoption trends. The show closes with reflections on philanthropy, wealth inequality, and the societal effects of social media.

## Key Takeaways

- Van Eck distinguishes Nvidia from memory chipmakers: Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem gives it durable moats, whereas companies like Micron are benefiting primarily from price increases rather than differentiated technology.
- SMH's outperformance over peer semiconductor ETFs is attributed to its 20% maximum single-stock weighting, which allows Nvidia's gains to compound within the fund.
- Rising 30-year Treasury yields—at their highest since 2007—represent a structural sovereign-debt risk that equity markets are currently ignoring, in Van Eck's view.
- The SpaceX IPO is expected to create a large, rapid wealth-creation event due to an abbreviated rolling lock-up structure, with recycled capital potentially stimulating further asset price inflation.
- Corporate AI adoption in regulated financial services is expected to lag other industries because of data privacy and compliance constraints, despite growing enterprise spending on LLMs.
- Van Eck views large-cap dominance as a structural, possibly permanent, shift driven by network effects and the effective end of meaningful antitrust enforcement over the past 25 years.
- The conversation on philanthropy highlights a perceived gap between the scale of modern wealth creation and the level of civic institution-building seen in the Gilded Age robber-baron era.

## Detailed Notes

### SMH Origins and ETF Construction Debate [00:09:14]

Jan Van Eck explains that SMH originated as a Merrill Lynch HOLDR product that generated commission revenue rather than management fees, and VanEck acquired it when Merrill exited asset management during the financial crisis. The fund's outperformance is traced to its cap-weighted construction allowing Nvidia to reach up to a 20% weighting, in contrast to equal-weight semiconductor ETFs that diluted Nvidia exposure. The hosts debate whether equal-weighting makes sense for sector ETFs, with Brown arguing market-cap weighting has been superior over 15 years while Van Eck notes this has historically been cyclical.

### Large-Cap Moats, Network Effects, and Antitrust [00:17:52]

Brown and Van Eck argue that large-cap dominance is more durable today than in prior eras due to network-effect business models enabled by the internet and effectively dormant antitrust enforcement for roughly 25 years. Van Eck notes that mega-caps compete intensely against each other (e.g., Waymo vs. Tesla vs. Uber) but rarely face existential threats from smaller challengers. The sole credible mechanism cited for large-cap underperformance is a bear market, where lower-multiple small caps have less downside.

### Bond Market, Sovereign Debt, and the New Fed Chair [00:22:05]

Van Eck reiterates a longstanding concern that U.S. fiscal deficits—running at roughly 5-6% of GDP—will eventually cause a loss of confidence in long-term bonds, similar to dynamics already visible in UK and Japanese long-end yields. The 30-year Treasury reaching its highest yield since 2007 is attributed to a combination of elevated inflation expectations, strong nominal economic growth, and rising defense spending. Incoming Fed Chair Kevin Warsh is expected to communicate less and intervene less than Powell, which Van Eck sees as broadly positive for market stability.

### IPO Wealth Creation: SpaceX, Anthropic, and Reflexive Markets [00:29:41]

The hosts discuss the forthcoming SpaceX IPO, expected at roughly a $2 trillion market cap with only about 3-3.5% initial float and an abbreviated rolling lock-up structure. Brown describes a reflexive loop where IPO-generated liquidity finances real estate, luxury goods, and further financial market activity. Van Eck notes that index rules are being updated to accommodate rapid inclusion of companies like SpaceX, which SpaceX management is actively managing to attract ETF-buying demand at listing.

### Why Memory Is a Bubble but Nvidia Isn't [00:40:38]

Van Eck's central thesis is that Nvidia's CUDA software platform—widely adopted across AI training and inference—gives it durable pricing power and enterprise lock-in, analogous to IBM's multi-decade survival through hardware-to-software-to-services pivots. Memory companies like Micron, by contrast, are exploiting a supply-demand imbalance to raise prices without adding proportional value; Van Eck views this as classically bubble-like because margins are vulnerable to efficiency improvements in model architecture or new entrants such as Chinese memory manufacturers. Nvidia's recent earnings beat—revenue up ~80%, earnings up 125%, new $80B buyback—is interpreted as the stock transitioning from a growth to a blue-chip shareholder base, explaining why the share price barely moved post-print.

### Corporate AI Adoption and Agentic AI [00:45:26]

Van Eck shares internal VanEck data showing Claude token usage doubling roughly every three to four months since February, with total LLM spend around $750,000 per year. He and Brown agree that financial services firms face structural barriers to rapid AI deployment—proprietary client data, regulatory constraints, and data security concerns—meaning finance will be a late adopter relative to other sectors. The discussion notes the distinction between interactive prompt-based AI usage and true agentic AI, with Van Eck saying VanEck only allows agentic experimentation in sandboxed environments.

### Philanthropy, Wealth Inequality, and Social Media [01:09:34]

The conversation broadens to a discussion of whether modern billionaires match the civic institution-building of Gilded Age philanthropists like the Rockefellers and Carnegies. Brown argues a gap exists between modern wealth creation and public goods provision, while Van Eck suggests more time is needed to evaluate current philanthropists and that capitalism as a system deserves defense distinct from consumerism. Van Eck describes a deliberate effort to limit social media use via a device called a 'brick,' and both hosts note that phone bans in schools have been well received by students and parents.
