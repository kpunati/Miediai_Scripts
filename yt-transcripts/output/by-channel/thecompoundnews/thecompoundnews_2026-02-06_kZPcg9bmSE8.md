# What to Buy in the Momentum Stock Slaughterhouse | TCAF 228

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=kZPcg9bmSE8
**Duration:** 83:49

## Summary

Episode 228 of The Compound and Friends brings in John Mowrey, CIO of NFJ Investment Group, to analyze the dramatic momentum-driven selloff in software and high-growth stocks occurring while the broader S&P 500 holds near all-time highs. The conversation examines factor decay in value investing, how ETF proliferation eroded traditional value premiums, and why Mowrey's firm is positioned in energy, banks, and materials. The hosts and guest debate whether the current dislocation marks the beginning of a sustained multi-year value rotation, and contrast this episode with the 2000 tech crash and 2022 bear market.

## Key Takeaways

- Software stocks experienced their worst 7-day decline outside of 2008 and COVID, yet the S&P 500 remains near all-time highs — an unprecedented divergence by magnitude of the affected sector.
- Value factor premiums largely decayed post-2008 due to ETF proliferation converting what were alpha sources into beta, forcing value managers to rethink their frameworks.
- NFJ Investment Group addressed factor decay by building customized peer groups for valuation rather than relying on arbitrary GICS classifications or single-factor screens.
- Mowrey argues that financials, given their large index weight, are the most critical sector for any sustained value rotation to gain traction — materials and energy are too small.
- Consumer staples are rallying not on fundamentals but purely on the narrative of being 'undisruptable,' with some now trading at the same forward PE as tech, which Mowrey views as a warning sign.
- Bitcoin's correlation with tech stocks has increased significantly since the introduction of spot ETFs, undermining its prior status as an uncorrelated asset.
- The Russell 2000 has outperformed the S&P 500 by over 10% since the Liberation Day lows, suggesting a value/small-cap rotation may already be underway.

## Detailed Notes

### Background: Factor Decay and NFJ's Evolution [00:14:23]

Mowrey traces how value investing thrived from 2000–2007 but suffered post-2008 as ETF proliferation turned factor-based alpha into beta. He explains that back-tests using low P/B and low P/E metrics that worked up to 2008 no longer hold, and describes how he took over as CIO in 2017 to overhaul both process and culture. His solution was constructing customized peer groups to identify relative valuation dislocations rather than relying on static factor screens.

### The Unprecedented Software Selloff [00:28:57]

Brown and Mowrey document a situation where application software (Adobe, Salesforce, Datadog, Workday, Applov) entered a 30% drawdown and fell 18% in just 7 days — a pace only seen during 2008 and COVID. Critically, the S&P 500 remained within 1% of all-time highs, and Warren Pies noted this is the first time a sector representing more than 8% of market cap has fallen 25%+ while the broad market held near highs. The discussion highlights that unlike biotech or individual stocks, software has no near-term proof point to resolve disruption fears.

### Rotation Into Value: Energy, Banks, and Materials [00:42:22]

Mowrey highlights that refiners are growing earnings at 40% year-over-year driven by expanding crack spreads, regional banks are growing earnings at 30% aided by a steepening yield curve, and materials are showing strong performance. He argues that energy and materials are too small a share of the S&P 500 to anchor a true value rotation, so financials — the largest weight in value indexes at roughly 40% of the Russell 1000 Value — must lead. The firm entered the week already overweight banks, energy, and materials.

### Consumer Staples Warning and Bullish Market Take [00:56:15]

Staples recorded their best January-February performance on record with massive fund inflows, and the forward PE of staples has converged with tech forward PE — both around 23x — despite staples reporting negative earnings estimate revisions. Brown is bullish on the broad market overall, viewing the software selloff as cathartic and healthy, while Mowrey is cautious on both staples (bad fundamentals, premium valuation) and software near-term. Both agree Microsoft is a buy, not a sell, at current levels.

### Bitcoin, Crypto, and the ETF Effect [01:12:30]

The hosts observe Bitcoin in an approximately 50% drawdown and Ethereum down roughly 60% from its 2021 high. Mowrey and Brown agree that the introduction of spot Bitcoin ETFs has changed crypto from a potentially uncorrelated asset into one that now trades in sync with high-risk tech — the ETFs made it part of the broader risk-on/risk-off trade. Robinhood and Coinbase are highlighted as effectively functioning as de facto crypto proxies.

### Bull Market Dating and Looking Ahead [01:05:56]

Brown argues the current secular bull market began in 2013 when the S&P 500 broke out to new all-time highs above the 2007 peak, not in 2009. He contends that 2022 was a severe cyclical bear within a secular bull but did not reset the cycle because unemployment never spiked. Looking ahead, Mowrey sees rate-sensitive names — financials, materials, real estate — continuing to benefit if the yield curve steepens, and warns that any value rotation will need fundamental earnings support rather than just cheap multiples.
