# Liberation Day or Red Wedding? | WAYT?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=-8H638x8fxk
**Duration:** 69:37

## Summary

Recorded on April 1, 2025 — the eve of Trump's Liberation Day tariff announcement — Josh Brown and Michael Batnick assess the macro backdrop including Goldman Sachs' raised recession odds, Atlanta Fed GDP tracking at -3.7% for Q1, and deteriorating consumer fundamentals. The hosts analyze where market stress is concentrated (Mag 7 in bear market territory while S&P 493 is flat on the year), discuss the CoreWeave IPO, OpenAI's record $40B funding round, and Elon Musk's merger of X and xAI. They close with a deep dive on Nike's multi-year business deterioration and a mystery chart on the 10-year Treasury yield.

## Key Takeaways

- Tariff uncertainty is acting more like a dial than a switch, preventing any single Liberation Day announcement from being a definitive market-clearing event.
- Mag 7 stocks are in a bear market while the rest of the S&P 500 is roughly flat, with sector rotation flowing toward healthcare, utilities, and energy.
- Goldman Sachs pegs a 35% recession probability and projects a potential 17% further S&P drawdown from then-current levels to 4,600 if recession materializes.
- CoreWeave's 27% post-IPO rally and OpenAI's record $40B funding round signal continued private and public appetite for AI infrastructure despite macro headwinds.
- Nike is described as 'just another company' after alienating key retail distribution partners, losing relevance in athleisure, and facing rising competition from On Cloud, Hoka, and others.
- The Dorsey Wright Momentum and Low Volatility ETF (DVAL) is highlighted as a defensive positioning idea for late-cycle equity exposure.
- The 10-year Treasury yield approaching the December 2024 double-bottom near 4.10% is flagged as a key watch level; a break to 4% likely requires meaningful economic deterioration.

## Detailed Notes

### Liberation Day Tariff Outlook and Macro Risks [00:02:00]

The hosts discuss the eve of Trump's Liberation Day tariff announcement, with a whisper number of ~20% across the board. Michael highlights a non-farm payrolls report on April 4th as potentially more consequential. Neil Dutta of Renmac is quoted noting rising gas prices, declining job postings, and a 9% stock market decline all weighing on consumer spending; Atlanta Fed GDP nowcast stood at -3.7% for Q1.

### Goldman Sachs Forecasts and S&P 500 Downside Scenarios [00:07:34]

David Kostin cut Goldman's S&P 500 3-month target to 5,300 and 12-month to 5,900, with EPS growth forecasts trimmed to +3% for 2025. Goldman now assigns 35% odds of a US recession and estimates a 25% peak-to-trough drawdown in a recession scenario, implying a trough around 4,600 at roughly 17x forward earnings. The hosts note prior bear-market trough multiples of 13-15x and emphasize they do not expect that outcome.

### Mag 7 Bear Market and Sector Rotation [00:17:16]

Every Mag 7 stock except Apple is in a 20%+ drawdown while the S&P 493 is flat on the year. The Mag 7's market cap share fell from ~35% to ~30.5% of the S&P 500. Money is rotating broadly into healthcare, utilities, energy, and financials rather than concentrating in a new leadership sector. Alphabet's forward PE compressed to 16.5x, and the hosts debate AI search disruption risk to Google's core business.

### CoreWeave IPO and OpenAI Funding Round [00:25:07]

CoreWeave's stock surged ~27% on its second trading day, vindicating its infrastructure-as-a-service model for AI compute. OpenAI closed a $40B round — the largest private tech deal ever — valuing the company at $300B post-money, with SoftBank contributing $30B largely to fund the Stargate AI infrastructure project. The hosts contrast the $1.5B raised in CoreWeave's public offering against the massive private-market flows going into AI.

### Elon Musk Merges X and xAI [00:37:03]

Musk merged X (formerly Twitter) with xAI, combining data, employees, and revenue with no cash changing hands. The hosts and Matt Levine's commentary frame it as a formality given Musk's majority control of both entities. XAI was valued at $80B (up from $50B four months prior) and X at $33B in the transaction. The hosts draw a parallel to the Solar City/Tesla merger and argue Musk operates essentially without regulatory constraint.

### Gen X Career Disruption and AI Labor Impact [00:45:11]

Prompted by a New York Times article, the hosts discuss how Gen X workers who followed conventional career paths now face peak-career disruption from AI. Ad agencies alone are projected to lose 32,000 jobs (7.5% of workforce) by 2030. The hosts contrast this with younger generations' entrepreneurial orientation and acknowledge the asymmetry: AI lowers the cost of starting a business while eroding traditional employment in knowledge-work sectors.

### Low Volatility and Momentum Factor Strategy [00:49:57]

Technician Ari Wald recommends the Dorsey Wright Momentum and Low Volatility ETF (DVAL) as a late-cycle positioning strategy. Holdings overlap with Josh's best-stocks list includes Brown & Brown, Welltower, T-Mobile, Cardinal Health, TradeWeb, Visa, Mastercard, and Walmart — defensive names that are holding above key moving averages and near 52-week highs despite market turbulence.

### Nike Make the Case: Falling Knife Lesson [00:57:54]

Michael used Nike as a 'make the case' candidate but ultimately sold at a 16% loss. The stock is down 56% from its highs and has closed down three consecutive years for the first time since its IPO. Core issues include alienating retail distribution (Foot Locker, Dick's) in a direct-to-consumer pivot, aging franchise athletes and sneaker lines, and competitors On Cloud, Hoka, Anta, and Li-Ning collectively approaching 50% of Nike's revenue versus 20% in 2019. The discussion underscores a rule: avoid falling knives without base formation and a stabilizing trend.

### Mystery Chart: 10-Year Treasury Yield [01:06:44]

The mystery chart revealed the 10-year Treasury yield at approximately 4.27%. The hosts note a potential double-bottom near 4.10% (matching December 2024 lows) and suggest a break toward 4% is likely only if economic data materially worsens without an inflation resurgence. Persistent four-handle yields reflect inflation data constraining Fed easing despite real growth slowdown signals.
