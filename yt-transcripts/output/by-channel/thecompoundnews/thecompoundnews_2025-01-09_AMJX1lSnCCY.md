# Predictions For 2025 | Animal Spirits 394

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AMJX1lSnCCY
**Duration:** 69:43

## Summary

In episode 394 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson record in person to review their 2024 predictions and lay out their boldest calls for 2025. Key themes include whether the AI-driven bull market extends into a late-cycle blow-off or corrects sharply, the structural impact of high mortgage rates on the economy, and the ongoing consolidation of VC funding into a handful of mega-firms. The hosts also discuss retirement regrets, personal portfolio benchmarking, the growing dominance of Mag 7 companies in absorbing innovation, and private credit stress in the media sector.

## Key Takeaways

- Batnick's boldest 2025 prediction is a double-digit correction in the back half of the year leading to a negative full-year return, though he assigns it only a 1% probability based on historical precedent.
- Carlson's 2025 prediction is a 60/40 split between the AI bubble accelerating into a dot-com-style melt-up versus Nvidia falling sharply and bonds outperforming stocks.
- High mortgage rates near 7% are a structural drag on housing activity, and the hosts argue this 'housing is the business cycle' dynamic will eventually weigh on broader economic growth.
- VC funding has contracted severely, with more than 2,000 firms going dormant since 2021 and the $71 billion raised in 2024 representing a seven-year low — concentrating innovation funding inside the Mag 7.
- The hosts agree that wealthy clients will not trust AI to replace human financial advisors, though AI will augment advisor efficiency and serve lower-wealth segments not currently served by traditional wealth management.
- Money market funds are expected to remain elevated in 2025 absent a substantial Fed cutting cycle beyond current expectations.
- GMO data shows the S&P top 10 stocks have underperformed the equal-weighted rest since 1957 in aggregate, but outperformed by 5% annually since 2013 — illustrating extreme recency bias risk in current positioning.

## Detailed Notes

### 2024 Prediction Review [00:00:04]

Batnick grades himself a B-minus on 2024 predictions. He correctly called Bitcoin hitting $100K and media streaming consolidation being limited (only Paramount/Ellison deal). He was wrong that Apple would be dropped from the Magnificent 7 — Apple was up 30% despite no revenue growth. He correctly predicted money staying in money market funds but was incorrect that the other 493 S&P stocks would catch up to large-cap tech for the full year.

### 2025 Bold Predictions [00:00:04]

Batnick assigns +10,000 odds (implied 1% probability) to a scenario where the market gains 10%+ in the first half and then ends the year down — noting this has happened only once in history (1928). Carlson frames his outlook as a hedge: either the AI bubble accelerates into a late 1990s-style melt-up, or Nvidia falls sharply and bonds outperform stocks with the S&P posting a rare 0-10% 'average' year. Both hosts emphasize extreme recency bias in current investor positioning.

### Housing as the Business Cycle [00:00:04]

Referencing Luke Kawa's Sherwood piece and economist Ed Leamer's 2007 paper, Carlson argues that 6-7% mortgage rates are a slow-burning drag that must eventually affect economic growth. Home builder margins are under pressure from rate buy-downs, and residential construction employment is flagged as a vulnerability. The effective average mortgage rate on outstanding loans remains well below current market rates, meaning the pain is hitting a growing but still small share of borrowers.

### VC Contraction and Mag 7 Consolidation [00:00:04]

More than 2,000 VC firms have gone dormant since the 2021 peak of 8,300 active investors. The $71 billion raised in 2024 is a seven-year low, with over half captured by nine firms (a16z, Thrive, and similar giants). The hosts argue this structurally reinforces Mag 7 dominance: innovation that would have been funded by independent VCs now happens inside Apple, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Nvidia — citing Apple Vision Pro and CoreWeave (Nvidia-backed) as examples. Big Tech has made 870 acquisitions across the five largest companies.

### Private Credit Stress and Other Market Data [00:00:04]

J.P. Morgan's Guide to Alternatives shows media as the largest share of leveraged loan defaults at 23%, followed by healthcare at 20%, reflecting social media disruption of traditional media. The AGG bond ETF remains in a multi-year drawdown, with bonds returning roughly 1.3% annually over 10 years on a total return basis. The hosts note VOO surpassed SPY in annual inflows at $116 billion, with IVV also expected to surpass SPY in total AUM.

### Personal Finance: Benchmarking, Retirement Regrets, and Sudden Wealth [00:00:04]

A listener email prompts discussion of portfolio benchmarking — both hosts admit they do not rigorously benchmark against the S&P 500 and focus instead on net worth growth relative to savings goals. A WSJ piece on Americans over 80 surfaces retirement regret themes; one subject retired with only $50,000 and a $2,900/month pension. The Loom founder's viral post about existential drift after an estimated $50-100 million liquidity event prompts discussion of wealth's psychological complexity and the hosts' view that $10 million is a 'sweet spot' before wealth becomes a burden.
