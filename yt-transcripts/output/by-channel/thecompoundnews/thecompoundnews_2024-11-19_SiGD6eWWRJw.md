# Something's Going to Break, We Don't Know What

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-11-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=SiGD6eWWRJw
**Duration:** 78:01

## Summary

Michael Cembalest, Chairman of Market and Investment Strategy at JP Morgan Asset Management, joins Michael Batnick and Josh Brown on The Compound to discuss the aftermath of the 2024 U.S. presidential election. Cembalest argues that despite a historically strong economic backdrop, the Biden-Harris campaign failed to effectively communicate on inflation and the economy, contributing to their loss. The conversation then turns to key risks for 2025 markets, including the inflationary potential of tariffs and deportations, the structural dysfunction in U.S. housing supply caused by zoning regulations, uncertainty around AI infrastructure ROI, and the limits of the DOGE efficiency agenda given the dominance of entitlement spending in the federal budget.

## Key Takeaways

- Cembalest argues Democrats experienced political malpractice by failing to communicate a strong economic record, particularly on jobs, manufacturing reshoring, and stock market gains.
- Housing unaffordability traces primarily to zoning regulations that have reduced starter homes from roughly 40% to 8% of new construction, a supply-side problem Democrats did not address.
- The Trump trade post-election (financials, energy, dollar, Bitcoin up) was rational given pre-election probabilities, but universal tariffs or mass deportations could reignite inflation and put the Fed in a difficult position.
- Cembalest predicts a 12-15% market correction in 2025 even if year-end returns are positive, as markets price in zero risk of policy disruption going wrong.
- Hyperscalers face a $400 billion annual gap between AI capex spend and current AI revenue, and the biggest 2025 market risk is uncertainty creeping into AI adoption timelines.
- DOGE faces structural limits: non-defense discretionary spending is at 60-year lows and the real budget is dominated by entitlements, making significant cuts politically and practically difficult.
- Small modular reactors have never been delivered on time or under budget, making the green-power commitment of data centers dependent on natural gas in the near term.

## Detailed Notes

### Election Aftermath and Democratic Messaging Failures [00:02:16]

Cembalest argues that despite near all-time high labor force participation, record manufacturing reshoring, and highest year-to-date equity gains in an election year since 1936, the Harris campaign failed to put these economic wins in front of voters. He criticizes the choice of Yellen over more visible communicators and contrasts this with the Clinton-era approach of having surrogates like Bob Rubin actively sell economic achievements. He notes that economic messaging was the central determinant of election outcomes historically, citing Nixon's 49-state win before Vietnam-era inflation.

### Housing Affordability and Zoning Crisis [00:14:58]

Cembalest presents data showing the home price-to-income ratio has nearly doubled since 1995, now at roughly 10x median income. The root cause he identifies is a surge in zoning regulations and land use litigation that has reduced starter homes (under 1,400 sq ft) from about 40% of new construction to 8%. He argues deregulation, not demand subsidies, is the correct policy response, and that the Biden administration's housing subsidy approach would have worsened the supply-demand imbalance.

### Immigration Economics and Labor Market Risks [00:33:51]

Cembalest discusses immigration dispassionately, noting the U.S. needs immigration given low organic birth rates, but the Biden administration's open-border approach imposed a $10-15 billion fiscal hit on New York City alone. He warns that aggressive deportations could tighten low-wage labor markets, potentially prompting the Fed to hike rates again, and that many Republican-state industries like agriculture and construction are already struggling with labor shortages.

### Trump Trade and 2025 Market Risks [00:43:18]

The post-election market rally in financials, energy, and Bitcoin was rational given prior probability pricing. Cembalest warns that universal tariffs of 5-10% on all imports would be unprecedented since the 1930s and could cause an immediate market gap-down. He predicts a 12-15% correction in 2025, driven by the realization that 'something is going to break' as the administration pursues disruption, though he expects markets to end the year higher.

### AI Infrastructure and Big Tech Risk [01:07:26]

Hyperscalers are spending roughly $500 billion annually on GPU and data center infrastructure while generating about $100 billion in AI revenue, creating a $400 billion funding gap. Cembalest draws parallels to Corning's fiber optic overinvestment in 1999-2000, noting it took Corning 20 years to match its 1999 revenues. He considers the biggest market risk for 2025 to be uncertainty creeping in about AI adoption timelines, as current market pricing assigns near-zero probability to a 'metaverse-style' disappointment scenario.

### DOGE, Federal Budget, and Entitlements [01:01:09]

Cembalest notes that federal civilian employment is at 2% of total workforce, the lowest in 85 years, and non-defense discretionary spending is at its lowest level since the early 1960s. The real money is in entitlements (Medicare, Medicaid, Social Security), which have grown to roughly 5x non-defense discretionary spending since the 1960s. He suggests DOGE may serve politically as a scapegoat regardless of outcome, since Trump can either credit it for cuts or discard it if results are minimal.

### Energy, Nuclear, and Data Center Power [01:12:59]

Cembalest argues that hyperscaler commitments to green power face structural constraints: only three decommissioned U.S. nuclear plants could potentially be recommissioned, and no small modular reactor has ever been delivered on time or under budget. Data centers will likely have to rely on natural gas for the near term, making the current political environment a convenient cover for quietly abandoning renewable commitments.
