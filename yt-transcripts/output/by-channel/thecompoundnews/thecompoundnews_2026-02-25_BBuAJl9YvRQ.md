# Everyone Hates AI | Animal Spirits 453

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=BBuAJl9YvRQ
**Duration:** 76:59

## Summary

Episode 453 of Animal Spirits opens with Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson dissecting a widely circulated Catrini Research think piece that envisions AI-driven white-collar unemployment reaching 10% and equity markets falling 40% by 2028. The hosts debate whether this scenario is realistic or overhyped, with Carlson offering the more optimistic counterargument that human preferences, cost constraints, and market dynamism will blunt the worst outcomes. They then survey the current stock market environment, noting a paradox where global breadth is near multi-decade highs even as Mag 7 and software stocks suffer their sharpest drawdowns in years. The episode closes with an in-depth look at the Blue Owl private credit (BDC) debacle, warning that poor crisis communication and structural mismatches in private credit vehicles could slow wealth-channel adoption of the asset class for years.

## Key Takeaways

- The Catrini Research AI doom scenario went viral enough to move markets, temporarily sending American Express and Capital One down roughly 8% on concerns about AI disrupting financial services and stablecoins.
- Batnick views real estate as the best AI hedge because physical scarcity persists in a digital world and a deflationary AI shock would likely force fiscal stimulus that lifts asset prices.
- Despite high-profile software drawdowns, 66% of S&P 500 members are above the index's all-time high—an unusually wide breadth reading that gives the bulls a macro foundation for staying invested.
- Blue Owl's handling of OBDC2 redemptions was widely criticized as a PR disaster; the hosts argue the stock's 60% decline implies GFC-level default assumptions that may be excessive but are hard to dismiss given management's deflective tone.
- OpenAI's projected $218 billion cash burn through 2030 dwarfs any prior tech build-out, raising questions about whether its bet on AGI compute will prove as well-timed as Netflix's content spend.
- Both hosts agree that AI will cause transition pain for white-collar workers, but neither expects unemployment to breach 10% absent a concurrent recession, because AI-driven productivity typically expands total work volume rather than eliminating it outright.
- The episode reflects a broader sentiment shift: retail call-option activity has peaked, rotation into defensive sectors is accelerating, and investor psychology is fragile enough that continued Mag 7 weakness could drag down the broader market.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Doom Scenario and Market Reaction [00:00:49]

Batnick describes a Catrini Research piece projecting a doom loop: AI exceeds expectations, companies shed white-collar workers, consumer spending falls, and margins compress in a negative feedback loop reaching 10% unemployment and a 40% market decline by 2028. The piece went viral and briefly dragged American Express and Capital One down roughly 8%, with Carlson noting the market reaction was probably an overreaction even if some AI disruption is inevitable.

### Counterarguments: Human Element, Network Effects, and Cost Constraints [00:06:33]

Carlson argues the piece underestimates human preference for relationships and the staying power of network effects, pointing out that internet-era disruption left realtors intact despite full information availability on Zillow. He and Batnick note that running AI agents is expensive and companies will create countervailing friction, and Gavin Baker's tweet cited insufficient compute as a near-term ceiling on the doomsday timeline.

### AI Backlash and Public Sentiment [00:20:44]

Time and the New York Times ran pieces on growing public hostility to AI, with one academic noting this is the first major technology boom accompanied by widespread active hostility rather than enthusiasm. The hosts attribute this partly to tech leaders failing to articulate consumer benefits and to the framing of AI as something happening to people rather than for them, with Batnick predicting a socialist political backlash if mass unemployment materializes.

### Stock Market: Breadth vs. Mag 7 Divergence [00:26:38]

Despite Microsoft, Amazon, and Tesla each down double digits year-to-date (Microsoft the worst, down roughly 30% from its high), 66% of S&P constituents are above the index's all-time high—the most in 50 years per Ned Davis. Global stock markets are similarly near multi-decade breadth highs, giving Carlson reason to stay broadly invested even as he worries about how much Mag 7 damage the index can absorb before psychology tips.

### Private Credit and the Blue Owl Fiasco [00:55:38]

Batnick details the triple whammy hitting BDC stocks: floating-rate income eroding as rates fell, concerns about underwriting quality after rapid inflows, and now AI-related fears about software exposure (Blackstone's BCred is 26% software). Blue Owl's OBDC2 redemptions were particularly poorly handled—management's claim to be 'completely surprised' by the negative market reaction to a $1.6 billion asset sale at 99.7 cents on the dollar struck both hosts as tone-deaf; the stock is down 60% and one covenant-light analysis implies GFC-level or worse default assumptions are already priced in.

### AI CapEx Burn and OpenAI vs. Anthropic [00:51:05]

A comparison chart shows Netflix burned $11 billion, Tesla $9 billion, and Uber $18 billion in free cash flow before reaching profitability; OpenAI is projecting $218 billion in burn through 2030. Batnick jokes that if one could short OpenAI and go long Anthropic (Claude), it would be the trade—referencing Anthropic's Claude product by name as the reason AI anxiety has intensified after a period when ChatGPT's errors made people feel safe.
