# Will AI Displace Financial Advisors? | Animal Spirits 455

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-03-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=xuCzf3fnpEc
**Duration:** 45:56

## Summary

Recorded live at Future Proof Citywide in Miami, this episode of Animal Spirits features two substantive interviews alongside a hosts' roast segment. Michael Kitces uses 25 years of advisory industry benchmarking data to argue that technology has historically deepened advisor services and reduced client loads rather than eliminating jobs or compressing margins, suggesting AI will follow the same pattern. Phil Huber of Cliffwater counters negative private credit narratives by presenting index data back to 2004 showing historically low credit losses even through the GFC, while acknowledging legitimate concerns about software-sector concentration and the need for sound liquidity management in evergreen structures.

## Key Takeaways

- Do-it-yourself investors who will use AI tools were never going to hire advisors in the first place—AI expands that pool marginally but does not convert existing advisor clients.
- Advisory industry benchmarks (fees, overhead, margin) have been static for 30 years despite each major technology wave, suggesting AI will compress margins no more than prior innovations did.
- The productivity gains from technology have historically been consumed by advisors going deeper with existing clients rather than adding more clients.
- Private credit is not in a bubble; negative media coverage conflates isolated defaults with systemic risk, while the Cliffwater index shows realized credit losses were far below return income even in 2008-2009.
- Software-sector concentration in private credit (~20% of the index) is a legitimate concern, but senior lenders are structurally protected versus equity holders who absorb first losses.
- Effective evergreen private credit structures manage redemption risk through revolving credit facilities and planned liquidity programs, not fire sales of illiquid loans.
- Advisors gain efficiency from AI but tend to reallocate that time to deeper client service, not client load expansion, once past a financial security threshold.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Context and Episode Introduction [00:01:21]

### Michael Kitces on AI and the Future of Financial Advisors [00:08:30]

Kitces argues that DIY investors who use AI tools were never going to hire advisors, so the addressable market is not shrinking. He draws on 25 years of advisory firm benchmarking: fees, overhead expense ratios, and profit margins have been constant at approximately 1%, 40%, and 30% respectively, even through the internet, smartphones, and robo-advisor eras. The only metric that changed materially is average client load, which declined as advisors used freed time for deeper service rather than volume growth. He predicts AI will follow the same trajectory, with the 'power planner' back-office role being eliminated but advisor headcount remaining stable.

### Phil Huber on Private Credit: Bubble Narrative vs. Data [00:20:53]

Phil Huber of Cliffwater, which manages nearly $40 billion across two credit funds and has been active in private credit for 20 years, rebuts the bubble narrative by citing index data back to 2004. In 2008, the worst year, the Cliffwater index was down only 6.5% while realized credit losses were ~60 basis points; the index was then up meaningful double digits in 2009 and 2010 despite higher realized losses, because income more than offset credit losses. Huber acknowledges software-sector concentration (~20% of index) is a valid concern but notes senior lenders are insulated from terminal value declines that devastate equity holders. He distinguishes a credit cycle—characterized by lender dispersion and eventual weaker-lender failures—from a bubble, which would require valuations that cannot support any reasonable return expectation.

### Liquidity Management in Evergreen Private Credit Structures [00:32:00]

Huber explains that evergreen structures are designed for semi-liquidity: with effective loan maturities of 3-4 years and about 30% of a portfolio repaying annually, there is organic liquidity without forced selling. Well-run funds use pre-established revolving credit facilities to meet elevated redemption periods without distressed asset sales. Prorated redemptions (when they occur) are not the same as full gating—most investors still access most of their capital. He expresses confidence that the current period will prove a proof point for the structure's resilience.
