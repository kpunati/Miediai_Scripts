# Why Every CEO is Secretly Pumped For Trump

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Sjj0om4sz5c
**Duration:** 38:47

## Summary

Professor Jeffrey Sonnenfeld of Yale's School of Management gives Josh Brown a detailed breakdown of how major US CEOs are positioning themselves for Trump's second term, covering tariff policy, the shift in Fortune 500 CEO political support, Trump cabinet appointments, immigration's economic consequences for key industries, and regulatory policy implications. Sonnenfeld draws on decades of direct access to top CEOs and cabinet officials to offer nuanced assessments of which cabinet picks signal stability versus risk for markets. The discussion covers Tim Cook's artful navigation of Trump 1.0 as a model for corporate diplomacy, as well as the risks of Trump's divide-and-conquer tactics with the business community.

## Key Takeaways

- Fortune 500 CEO public support for the Republican presidential candidate dropped to zero in 2020, and while there is now renewed willingness to engage with Trump, CEOs remain wary of a repeat of the divisive tactics from his first term.
- Tariffs are widely viewed by CEOs as a negotiating tool rather than permanent policy, but history shows that bark-and-bite negotiating can backfire, as evidenced by Harley-Davidson being forced to close a US plant in response to EU retaliatory tariffs.
- Tim Cook is held up as the model of corporate diplomacy with Trump: ego-suspension, avoiding public confrontation, and quietly securing supply-chain concessions while managing a pivot of 25% of Apple's China production to Vietnam.
- Scott Bessent is viewed as a stabilizing force at Treasury, while Howard Lutnick's volatile personality and desire to control the trade representative function raises concerns for market stability.
- The antitrust battles facing Microsoft, Alphabet, and Meta are unlikely to simply disappear under Trump because populist elements within the MAGA coalition support antitrust enforcement against big tech.
- Immigration crackdowns pose serious economic risks to agriculture, construction, and tech, with H1B restrictions in Trump's first term having been successfully pushed back via Business Roundtable lobbying that demonstrated companies would simply move jobs to Canada.
- Paul Atkins' nomination as SEC chair is broadly viewed positively even by some exchange participants who disagreed with him historically, given his deep knowledge of securities markets and cryptocurrency regulation.

## Detailed Notes

### CEO Political Realignment and Tariff Posture [00:02:19]

Sonnenfeld explains that Fortune 500 CEO support for GOP presidential candidates dropped to zero in 2020, but has returned under Trump 2.0 as business leaders engage pragmatically. Tariffs are seen by the CEO community as primarily a negotiating tactic, not final policy—but Sonnenfeld cautions that this assumption has backfired before, citing China halting farm-product purchases in retaliation for 2018 steel and aluminum tariffs, and Harley-Davidson closing its Kansas City plant after EU retaliation.

### CEO Diplomacy: The Tim Cook Model [00:18:06]

Tim Cook is highlighted as the most artful corporate diplomat through Trump's first term, successfully navigating tariff exposure on Apple's China-heavy supply chain and its status as a major player in the Chinese consumer market. Cook's approach—humility, no public confrontation, willingness to be called 'Tim Apple'—is contrasted with more combative approaches. Cook is in the process of moving about 25% of Apple's China production to Vietnam, a supply-chain pivot that required ongoing political access.

### Cabinet Assessment: Fortifiers, Detractors, and Wildcards [00:25:27]

Sonnenfeld categorizes Trump cabinet appointments into three groups. Scott Bessent at Treasury is a clear positive for markets—sophisticated, stable, loyal to Trump. Howard Lutnick at Commerce is more volatile and concerning, particularly his reported desire to have the US Trade Representative report to Commerce rather than directly to the president. Marco Rubio at State and Elise Stefanik at the UN are described as appropriate for Trump's foreign policy vision.

### Immigration Economics and Business Risk [00:29:37]

Sonnenfeld details the economic dependency of US industries on immigrant labor: roughly 30-40% of farm workers are undocumented, over 20% of construction workers are foreign-born with up to 40% undocumented in some cities, and 25% of the tech workforce are immigrants. He recounts how Business Roundtable lobbying around H1B visas in Trump's first term succeeded only after demonstrating that companies were already leasing space in Toronto and Vancouver, preparing to shift jobs across the border.

### SEC Chair Nomination and Crypto Regulation [00:33:47]

Paul Atkins was nominated to replace Gary Gensler, who announced his departure as Bitcoin approached $100,000. Sonnenfeld discloses he sits on the IEX board, an exchange that had historical disagreements with Atkins, but notes the IEX team is optimistic because Atkins has genuine expertise and is not a flamethrower. Concerns exist around Howard Lutnick's stake in Tether and potential conflicts in crypto regulation, which Atkins is seen as being free of.
