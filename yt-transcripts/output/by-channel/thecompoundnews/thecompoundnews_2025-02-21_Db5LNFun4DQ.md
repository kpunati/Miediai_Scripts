# Live in Naples with Brian Belski | TCAF 179

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-02-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Db5LNFun4DQ
**Duration:** 66:38

## Summary

In this live episode recorded at the Alamo Drafthouse in Naples, Florida, Brian Belski joins hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to outline his market outlook, portfolio strategy, and long-term investment thesis. Belski reaffirms his 25-year secular bull market call beginning from around 1999-2000, arguing the US remains the world's highest-quality equity market. He details how he barbells sector exposures—neutralizing expensive Magnificent Seven names to overweight fundamentally sound companies—and explains his conviction in dividend growth as both offensive and defensive. Key risks cited include sticky inflation above 2% and excessive market noise from sentiment surveys and media, which he dismisses in favor of direct client conversations and fundamental analysis.

## Key Takeaways

- Belski maintains a 25-year secular bull market call, viewing current volatility as cyclical noise within a longer uptrend.
- The US remains the premier equity market globally; Belski is skeptical of rotating into Europe or China on a sustained basis.
- Barbelling sector exposures—neutralizing high-priced Magnificent Seven components to overweight fundamentally sound mid-tier names—is the core portfolio strategy for 2025.
- Dividend growth investing outperforms both in bull and bear markets and is not correlated with high nominal yields; it requires companies with free cash flow yields above dividend yield.
- AI's biggest beneficiaries extend well beyond hardware into retailers, banks, and experiential consumer companies like Costco, Walmart, and Shake Shack.
- The number one historical factor in US equity outperformance since 1990 per Belski's research is low share price, not momentum.
- Sticky inflation above 2% is Belski's primary macro concern for 2025, not tariffs or tech earnings per se.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Context and Brian Belski's Background [00:03:11]

Belski is introduced as BMO's chief investment strategist and leader of the investment strategy group, with prior roles at Oppenheimer, Merrill Lynch, and Piper Jaffray. He has been forecasting the US market since 1998 and the Canadian market since joining BMO in 2012. BMO crossed $10 billion AUM at the start of 2025.

### Secular Bull Market Thesis and Dismissal of Sentiment Noise [00:04:42]

Belski reiterates his 25-year secular bull market call, noting cyclical bears and bulls can occur within it. He dismisses the Bank of America Fund Manager Survey as largely irrelevant, observing that 89% of respondents claim equities are overvalued while cash allocations are at record lows near 3.5%. He argues sentiment is better assessed through direct conversations, not surveys, citing that managers rarely complete them honestly.

### Why Markets Feel Perpetually Noisy [00:13:10]

Despite the S&P at all-time highs, low credit spreads, and a VIX around 15, markets feel chaotic. Both hosts and Belski attribute this to the shift from subscription to attention-based media models post-1999, plus the scarring from two consecutive 50% bear market drawdowns in the 2000s. Belski adds emotional damage from COVID and the 2008 financial crisis as contributing factors.

### Bull Case for US Equities and Domestic Revenue Focus [00:20:28]

Belski advocates focusing on US companies with more than half of their revenue derived domestically as a way to dilute tariff risk. He notes that from the pandemic to today, companies with more than half of revenue outside the US have outperformed (19% vs. 17% returns), largely driven by tech and cap size effects. Looking forward, he expects financial sector earnings to be the big driver, as financial stocks remain undervalued.

### Portfolio Construction: Barbelling and Stock Selection Process [00:24:37]

The 2025 strategy is to barbell—staying exposed to Magnificent Seven sectors (tech, communication services, consumer discretionary) while neutralizing the most expensive names like Amazon, Tesla, Meta in favor of Lululemon, Home Depot, TJ Maxx, Marriott, Spotify, Reddit, and AT&T. Belski uses four-sheet analysis per sector covering valuation, earnings growth, operating performance, and price performance. He will immediately sell a stock if management lies on a quarterly call, or sell dividend growth names on dividend cuts.

### AI Beneficiaries, Dividend Growth, and Canadian Stocks [00:35:50]

Belski argues AI's biggest beneficiaries are not just semiconductor companies but retailers (Walmart, Costco) and banks that were early fintech adopters. On dividends, he distinguishes between high nominal yield (can indicate lack of growth) and dividend growth (companies with free cash flow above dividend yield, positive earnings, and consistent payout history). His Canadian investment case rests on cheap valuations, long-term corporate management, and the fact that companies like Circle K (Couche-Tard), Waste Connections, and Shopify generate most revenues in the US.

### Risks, S&P 500 Target, and Factor Research [00:54:25]

Belski's 2025 S&P 500 target is 6,700 with $275 EPS. His primary concern is sticky inflation not returning to 2%, not tariffs or tech earnings. He downplays the Fed funds futures narrative. His proprietary 57-factor research since 1990 shows low share price is the top-performing factor (25% CAGR) and momentum ranks outside the top 25, while estimate dispersion is the worst factor.
