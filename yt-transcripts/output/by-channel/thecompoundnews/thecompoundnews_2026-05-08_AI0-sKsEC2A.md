# The Investor Utopia is Here | TCAF 241

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AI0-sKsEC2A
**Duration:** 85:42

## Summary

TCAF episode 241 brings Bloomberg ETF analyst Eric Balchunas onto The Compound to survey the current state of the investment industry. The conversation covers the unusual market dynamic of individual stocks blowing up while indexes hold at all-time highs, the competitive landscape emerging around Nasdaq 100 funds and SpaceX's anticipated index inclusion, and how the fee-compression era has given way to an explosion of high-fee 'hot sauce' active and leveraged ETFs. The episode also explores Bitcoin's growing mainstream acceptance, the mechanics and investor appeal of prediction market ETFs, and why direct indexing has failed to dent ETF flows despite years of hype.

## Key Takeaways

- ETF flows and launches are at all-time highs, with the industry on pace to surpass last year's record 1.5 trillion in inflows.
- The number of active ETFs now exceeds passive ETFs by count, though passive funds still hold roughly 90% of assets.
- The race to zero on fees has ended; the average asset-weighted expense ratio has bottomed and is edging higher as leveraged and thematic 'hot sauce' products gain traction.
- SpaceX's anticipated IPO and near-immediate Nasdaq 100 inclusion is driving competing fund launches from BlackRock, State Street, and Invesco, with index eligibility seen as critical for IPO exit liquidity.
- Prediction market ETFs tracking political and economic outcomes are nearing launch from issuers including Round Hill, Bitwise, and Granite Shares, and could eliminate the guesswork around macro event trading.
- Direct indexing has grown to roughly a trillion dollars but remains a niche offering for high-net-worth clients, far short of the disruption to ETFs that was once predicted.
- Bitcoin is characterized as a democratized hard asset hedge against currency debasement, with an estimated 50 million US holders, though its lack of cash flows remains a genuine objection.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Conditions: Index Strength vs. Stock-Level Carnage [00:19:03]

Josh Brown highlights an unusual pattern where dozens of individual stocks are falling sharply on a given day while the S&P 500 and equal-weight indexes still print gains. Balchunas attributes this to mega-cap concentration and the Bessenbinder finding that 4% of stocks create virtually all equity wealth. The hosts argue this is precisely why market-cap-weighted indexing makes sense.

### Tech Dispersion and the Bubble Question [00:26:42]

Big-tech earnings dispersion hit a record high in Q1 2026, with software names like Datadog and Fortinet surging while others collapsed after earnings. Brown argues true bubbles require prices with no plausible fundamental justification, and the current NASDAQ does not meet that bar: daily 1% swings are running at 17 year-to-date versus 103 in calendar year 2000. The group is cautious but not bearish, with Balchunas recommending hard assets or put options rather than outright pessimism.

### SpaceX IPO and Competing Nasdaq 100 Funds [00:34:51]

NASDAQ announced it will include SpaceX in the Nasdaq 100 within 15 days of its IPO, a rule change that coincided with iShares and State Street filing competing QQQ-equivalent products. Balchunas frames the likely motivation as major asset managers wanting SpaceX exposure through index products rather than discretionary mandates. The IPO's primary purpose is exit liquidity for long-time private investors; the size of the public float rather than valuation is what matters to markets.

### ETF Industry Growth and the Hot-Sauce Era [00:43:46]

After a decade of fee-war 'boring' that Balchunas calls 'punk rock disruption,' the average ETF expense ratio has bottomed around 17 basis points and is rising. New launches are overwhelmingly active, leveraged, or thematic products charging 70-100 basis points. The hypothesis is that investors have locked in a low-cost core portfolio (VOO, VTI) and now want a speculative 'hot sauce' sleeve, fueling demand for products like 2x single-stock ETFs and sector plays. Balchunas introduces a framework: to succeed as an active ETF issuer, be very differentiated from beta and price accordingly, because investors have zero fee sensitivity in the speculative tier but extreme sensitivity in the core.

### Prediction Market ETFs [01:06:24]

Round Hill, Bitwise, and Granite Shares have filings pending for ETFs tracking political prediction markets (2028 presidential, Senate, House outcomes). The appeal is that ETFs sit in existing brokerage accounts, carry equity-market transparency and liquidity, and allow investors to express macro views without complex derivatives. Balchunas and Brown see potential for economic prediction markets (CPI, Fed decisions, recession probability) to eventually provide better real-time signals than broken survey-based data. Schwab's CEO did not rule out offering prediction market products for economic releases.

### Direct Indexing: Segway, Not Revolution [01:19:35]

Custom and direct indexing has reached an estimated 1 trillion in AUM but represents roughly 1-2% of all fund assets, far below the hype. Balchunas was skeptical from the start: the trend toward simplicity and low cost runs counter to a strategy that is more expensive and more complex. He compares it to the Segway — a product with genuine utility for a niche (wealthy investors with tax-loss-harvesting needs) but not the mass-market revolution its boosters predicted.

### Bitcoin Discussion [00:07:02]

Balchunas is researching a book titled 'Both Sides of the Coin' on Bitcoin. He notes an estimated 50 million US holders and frames Bitcoin's value proposition as a democratized hard asset hedge against currency debasement, analogous to gold or real estate for wealthy individuals. He identifies three archetypes of Bitcoin haters: those who missed it, those who see it as lottery-ticket luck, and those who never experienced a currency collapse. He acknowledges the absence of cash flows is a legitimate objection and describes Bitcoin holders who survived multiple 80%+ drawdowns as demonstrating a degree of conviction that deserves respect.
