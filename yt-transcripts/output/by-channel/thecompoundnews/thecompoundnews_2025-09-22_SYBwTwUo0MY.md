# The Truth About How Wall Street Moves Mortgage Rates

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=SYBwTwUo0MY
**Duration:** 41:23

## Summary

In this live interview, Josh Brown hosts Telis Demos, Heard on the Street columnist at the Wall Street Journal, to explore what actually moves mortgage rates and whether the housing market can thaw. Demos demystifies the mortgage bond market, explaining how MBS prepayment risk and the Fed's exit from agency MBS purchases have driven elevated spreads between the 10-year Treasury and 30-year mortgage rates. The conversation also covers the TikTok ownership restructuring involving Oracle and a consortium of US investors, JP Morgan's aggressive push in the premium credit card segment, and the counterintuitive behavior of long-term rates following Fed rate cuts.

## Key Takeaways

- Retail mortgage rates are set by the mortgage bond market, not directly by the Fed funds rate; the 10-year Treasury yield is the most relevant benchmark.
- MBS prepayment risk creates an air pocket dynamic: as rates fall, institutional buyers prefer lower-coupon MBS to avoid reinvestment risk, which paradoxically affects the rate at which new mortgages are priced.
- The Fed's wind-down of its agency MBS portfolio has contributed to persistently wide mortgage-to-Treasury spreads since March 2022.
- Restoring pre-pandemic housing affordability would require mortgage rates near 2.23%, making a true affordability recovery unlikely without a financial crisis.
- JP Morgan Chase's 21% ROTCE gives it a profitability cushion that allows sustained, patient investment in its credit card business that rivals cannot match.
- The TikTok deal involves Oracle hosting US user data and a consortium of US investors taking over operations, with ByteDance licensing its algorithm technology.
- A surprise Fed rate cut can actually push long-term yields higher if markets interpret it as signaling economic weakness or future inflation concerns.

## Detailed Notes

### TikTok Deal Structure and Social Media Implications [00:01:12]

Telis Demos explains the emerging TikTok restructuring: Oracle will host US user data in a US-based cloud center, ByteDance will license algorithm technology to a new US-controlled entity, and a consortium including SilverLake and reportedly Lachlan Murdoch via Fox Corp will hold ownership stakes. Demos flags a conflict of interest as News Corp owns the Wall Street Journal. The deal is framed as addressing both data-privacy and foreign-influence-on-algorithm concerns.

### Mechanics of Mortgage Rate Formation [00:14:10]

Mortgage rates are determined by the mortgage bond market, not directly by Fed policy. When a mortgage is made it is typically sold into the agency MBS market, so the yield investors demand on those bonds sets the rate. The 10-year Treasury is the practical benchmark because the effective duration of a 30-year mortgage (accounting for prepayments and refis) is closer to 5-10 years. Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey is backward-looking; Mortgage News Daily provides real-time tracking.

### MBS Prepayment Risk and the Spread Compression Story [00:16:57]

MBS bonds are bucketed by coupon in half-point increments (5%, 5.5%, 6%, etc.). When rates fall, investors anticipate that high-coupon mortgage holders will refinance, prepaying principal early and eliminating expected future income. As a result, investors rotate toward lower-coupon MBS to reduce prepayment exposure, creating an 'air pocket' in higher-coupon demand that affects the rates banks can offer. The Fed's exit from agency MBS purchases and reduced bank buying since 2023 have been the primary drivers of the elevated Treasury-to-mortgage spread that has persisted since March 2022.

### Fed Cuts and Counter-Intuitive Mortgage Rate Behavior [00:28:16]

Following the Fed's surprise 50 basis point cut in September 2024, the 10-year Treasury yield rose approximately 0.5 percentage points and 30-year mortgage rates increased about 75 basis points over the subsequent two months. This illustrates that Fed funds rate cuts do not mechanically translate into lower mortgage rates; market expectations about economic trajectory, inflation, and fiscal conditions drive long-term yields independently. A deep rate cut can even signal concern about the economy that triggers a 'bear steepener.'

### JP Morgan's Credit Card Dominance Strategy [00:31:42]

JP Morgan is already the number-one US issuer by card spending volume, with consumer card sales growing from under $200 billion pre-pandemic to nearly $350 billion. Its 21% Q2 ROTCE—highest among the big-six banks and well above its 17% through-cycle target—gives it a profitability cushion to treat card investment as a loss leader and outspend competitors on rewards without near-term profit pressure. The credit card business is also a customer-acquisition funnel for mortgages, checking accounts, and merchant partnerships.
