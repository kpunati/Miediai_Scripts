# Uber is the Most Misunderstood Stock in America | LFTC

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Baq0DE2heD0
**Duration:** 41:54

## Summary

Josh Brown and Mark Mulhern (Manu Invests) present a detailed bull case for Uber, arguing the market misunderstands autonomous vehicles as an existential threat when they actually represent a structural cost tailwind. The conversation examines Uber's asset-light demand-aggregator model, its expanding network of AV partnerships with Waymo, BYD Apollo, Volkswagen, Pony AI, Lucid, Nuro, and others, and the Nvidia collaboration that could enable every OEM to deploy autonomous vehicles on Uber's platform. They also discuss Bill Ackman's large disclosed Uber position and parallels to Dara Khosrowshahi's Expedia playbook as a demand aggregator against direct-to-consumer competition.

## Key Takeaways

- Uber trades at roughly 16x forward earnings despite ~25% free cash flow growth, reflecting market fear that autonomous vehicles (Waymo, Tesla Cyber Cab) will displace Uber rather than serve as suppliers on its platform.
- Removing the human driver from a ride eliminates approximately 55% of Uber's pass-through costs (insurance ~30%, driver incentives ~25%), potentially allowing Uber to charge a lower headline take rate while expanding actual margins.
- Dara Khosrowshahi's strategy mirrors his Expedia playbook: aggregate demand from 190 million MAUs and make it irrational for any AV provider to leave vehicles sitting idle rather than listing them on Uber.
- Uber's AV partnership roster spans Waymo, BYD Apollo, Volkswagen, Pony AI, Lucid/Nuro, Aurora, Motional, Wabi, and others, making it a global platform rather than a single-vendor bet.
- The Nvidia partnership provides an end-to-end AV brain and unified architecture allowing any OEM to plug in and instantly produce Uber-ready autonomous vehicles at scale.
- Over 50% of Uber's current revenue is international, yet the AV debate is almost entirely US-focused, leaving successful Waymo partnerships in the UAE and Saudi Arabia largely unpriced.
- Bill Ackman's disclosed Uber stake is roughly 20% of Pershing Square's 13F at an estimated average cost in the high $60s to $72, providing a potential activist floor if the stock revisits that level.

## Detailed Notes

### Why the Market Misunderstands Uber [00:03:36]

Uber trades at roughly 16x next-year earnings with ~25% free cash flow growth—about half the multiple of comparable tech-growth peers. Mulhern and Brown attribute this to surface-level fear that Waymo and Tesla Cyber Cab will simply replace Uber rather than serve as fleet suppliers. Uber spent a decade and enormous cash building a 190-million-MAU consumer platform that cannot be easily replicated by any single AV company.

### AV as Cost Tailwind, Not Existential Risk [00:22:37]

Uber's revenue is grossed up to include insurance it charges drivers and driver incentive payouts; both disappear with autonomous vehicles. Eliminating the human driver removes roughly 30% of take rate in insurance costs and ~25% in driver incentives, so Uber could charge a lower headline take rate on AV rides while actually improving margins. This is not reflected in current Street estimates.

### Uber's AV Partnership Strategy and the Nvidia Deal [00:18:06]

Uber is acting as a strategic investor and demand aggregator, taking equity in AV startups (Lucid/Nuro 20,000-vehicle deal, Wabi, Aurora backed by Nvidia) in exchange for exclusivity and platform listing. The Nvidia partnership is central: Nvidia's L4 chip and unified AV architecture allows any OEM—Mercedes, Cadillac, Volkswagen—to plug in and deploy autonomous vehicles on Uber's network. Mulhern argues Uber can succeed even without Waymo or Tesla by listing tier-2 AV providers across its global footprint.

### International Markets and the Expedia Parallel [00:29:50]

Over half of Uber's business is international; no AV competitor has comparable global infrastructure. Waymo and BYD Apollo are already operating commercially in the Middle East and Asia through Uber. Brown draws an explicit parallel to Khosrowshahi's tenure at Expedia, where hotels initially feared disintermediation yet continued working with Expedia because filling idle capacity at a discount beat leaving rooms empty—precisely the dynamic Uber is replicating with AV providers.

### Bill Ackman's Stake and Activist Floor [00:36:06]

Ackman's Uber position represents approximately 20% of Pershing Square's disclosed 13F and is estimated to have been built at an average cost in the high $60s to $72. Brown and Mulhern agree a decline back to cost basis would likely trigger a 13D filing and board seat demand, effectively creating an activist support floor. Ackman has already been in informal contact with Khosrowshahi regarding partnership opportunities including Hertz.
