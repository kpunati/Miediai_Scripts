# Everyone Back in the Boat | Animal Spirits 460

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FeJxk81ppEM
**Duration:** 73:05

## Summary

In episode 460 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson record on Tax Day 2026 and reflect on how the stock market has recovered despite geopolitical fears, with the S&P 500 near all-time highs. They trace the dramatic rotation from software to semiconductors, the mounting evidence that AI is disrupting entry-level hiring, and how record Q1 2026 venture capital — dominated by AI mega-rounds — reflects the sector's dominance. The episode also covers Nike's prolonged stock underperformance, housing market regional bifurcation, and generational wealth data showing millennials ahead of boomers on several metrics.

## Key Takeaways

- The stock market proved pessimists wrong after geopolitical fears: oil did not spike and stocks did not want to go lower, with the S&P near all-time highs.
- Software stocks lost significant ground to semiconductors — software fell from 54% to 25% of the tech sector since 2020, while IGV returned only ~6% total over five years versus ~260% for SMH.
- AI disruption of the entry-level job market is visible in data and anecdotes, with young non-college workers and even recent college graduates struggling as companies slow junior hiring.
- Q1 2026 set an all-time record for venture capital at $242 billion, driven almost entirely by massive AI rounds (OpenAI, Anthropic), versus $16 billion in Q1 2024.
- Nike's stock has declined 66% over five years and 16% over ten years as its direct-to-consumer strategy backfired, despite remaining a respected brand.
- Housing markets are bifurcated — homes sell quickly in California, New York, and the Midwest, but slowly in Sun Belt markets where correction started earlier.
- Consumer spending data (BofA card spending +4.3% YoY) contradicts gloomy sentiment surveys, and the fact that ~60-65% of households own stocks implies latent optimism.

## Detailed Notes

### Tax Day, IRS Workforce, and Tax Alpha [00:00:04]

The hosts open on Tax Day, praising Ritholtz's in-house tax team and discussing demand for CPAs and enrolled agents. They note IRS workforce has fallen from ~90,000 to ~70,000 employees while the US population and tax complexity have grown. Reducing the IRS by this amount is estimated to save $46 billion over a decade but cost $643 billion in lost enforcement revenue. Advisors are increasingly focused on after-tax alpha as a controllable edge.

### Stock Market Recovery and Sector Rotation [00:00:04]

Despite geopolitical shock, the S&P 500 is up ~1% YTD with the market near all-time highs, while Emerging Markets (+12-13%) and EAFE (+8%) outperformed. Oil did not spike and stocks did not want to go lower — the hosts argue AI optimism is winning out over geopolitical fears. Software names like Snowflake fell from $280 to $120 in six months, and IGV is up only ~6% total over five years versus ~260% for SMH; software's tech-sector weight dropped from 54% to 25% since January 2020.

### AI, Venture Capital, and Labor Market Disruption [00:00:04]

Q1 2026 set an all-time VC record at $242 billion, dominated by AI mega-rounds from OpenAI and Anthropic, versus $16 billion in Q1 2024. AI is beginning to show up in entry-level hiring data: young non-college adults aged 22-25 are disproportionately leaving the labor force, and anecdotes from computer science professors indicate only ~25% of students are getting internships or jobs post-graduation. Ramp's AI index shows 50% of US businesses have paid AI subscriptions, with Anthropic seeing its largest monthly increase. Companies like Cursor report $6.1M in ARR per full-time employee, illustrating AI-enabled efficiency gains.

### Generational Wealth, Housing, and Consumer Health [00:00:04]

WSJ data shows millennials have higher inflation-adjusted incomes and higher average net worth than boomers did at similar ages. Housing affordability in the late 1970s-80s was comparably poor for boomers due to high mortgage rates. Current housing market is bifurcated: fast sales in the Northeast and Midwest, slow in the Sun Belt (70+ days to pending). Bill McBride (Calculated Risk) forecasts a pickup in boomer home sales lasting through 2040 and a second large millennial cohort aging past peak home-buying, both likely suppressing price appreciation. BofA credit/debit card spending rose 4.3% YoY — the strongest since early 2023 — contrasting with University of Michigan sentiment at an all-time low.

### Nike, Software Stocks, and Stock-Picking Lessons [00:00:04]

Nike's quarterly revenues were flat to down 3% currency-neutral, with Nike Direct down 7% and gross margins declining 130 bps. Over five years Nike is down 66% vs. the market up 76%; over ten years down 16% vs. market up ~300%. The hosts use Nike as a cautionary tale: its direct-to-consumer pivot was a major strategic mistake, and the market generally does not get things this wrong without good reason. The same dynamic applies to trying to pick AI winners and losers among software names that are down 50-70%.
