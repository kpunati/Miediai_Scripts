# The Fat Pitch For Bears | Animal Spirits 465

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vj-hZaGuieE
**Duration:** 65:15

## Summary

In episode 465 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson debate the current bear case for equities, arguing that while risks like sticky inflation, high yields, and AI capex overbuild are visible, their very visibility makes them less likely to be the catalyst for a crash. They use Michael Burry's repeated failed crash calls and an unnamed NYU professor's market-timing mistake to illustrate the futility of acting on smart-sounding macro predictions. The conversation broadens to cover AI's accelerating impact on financial jobs, investment strategy, and society — including humanoid robotics, wealth inequality in San Francisco tech circles, and the mechanics of private market SPV risks.

## Key Takeaways

- The current bear case (AI capex overbuild, sticky inflation, high yields) is widely known, which historically reduces its power to crash markets.
- Nvidia's enormous size puts a practical ceiling on further multiple expansion regardless of earnings growth.
- Even highly intelligent market commentators and academics consistently fail to time the market, as illustrated by Michael Burry's repeated wrong crash calls.
- AI is already automating high-skilled finance work previously requiring master's and PhD holders, with Ken Griffin calling the impact 'depressing' after witnessing it firsthand.
- The housing market is a major driver of wealth inequality: existing homeowners with locked-in low mortgage rates have dramatically lower housing costs as a share of income than new buyers.
- SPVs used to access private companies like SpaceX and OpenAI carry serious counterparty risk — investors may discover they do not actually own shares when those companies IPO.
- Momentum strategies like Porter House are by definition crowded when working, and AI tools can now rapidly analyze portfolio exposures versus an investment thesis.

## Detailed Notes

### Bear Case Setup and Market Risks [00:00:43]

Batnick and Carlson discuss the 'fat pitch for bears': a combination of potential AI capex overbuild, sticky inflation, high Treasury yields (~5.1-5.2% on the 30-year), and a possible new Fed chair. However, Carlson argues yields at 5.2% are below the 50-year average of ~6.2% and that repeated 'boy who cried wolf' rate scares have not produced crises. The distribution of S&P 500 forward P/Es shows most stocks (by count) trade below 20x earnings, with only the large-cap AI names distorting the index multiple.

### Nvidia Valuation Ceiling and Earnings Growth [00:11:21]

Batnick highlights that Nvidia's forward P/E looks low but the forward net income has surged from $15 billion in 2023 to $223 billion, making any multiple expansion nearly impossible due to the resulting market-cap size. At 25x forward earnings Nvidia is already 8.5% of the market; at 35x it would be 12%. The hosts discuss whether this is the primary cap on the stock rather than valuation concerns.

### Market Timing and Smart Money Failures [00:17:35]

The episode reviews an NYU professor (unnamed) who publicly warned of an AI bubble and raised cash last November, then acknowledged on a follow-up appearance that timing the market is nearly impossible. Michael Burry's long track record of crash calls is mapped against market performance, with the hosts noting his predictions have consistently been early or wrong, and that macro predictions rarely align with actual portfolio positioning for professionals like Druckenmiller and Dalio.

### AI Impact on Finance Jobs and Investment Strategies [00:28:03]

Batnick demonstrates using Claude AI to analyze a technical article and then audit the Porter House SMA portfolio's exposure to the 'AI as new Netflix' infrastructure thesis, completing in 10 minutes what might have taken teams days. Ken Griffin is quoted saying work previously requiring man-years by PhDs is now done in days by AI agents, calling it depressing. The hosts debate whether AI is automating tasks or full jobs, and whether tinkering with AI-built trading screens will become a behavioral pitfall for retail investors.

### Humanoid Robots, Demographics, and Political Risk [00:39:29]

Brett Adcock's humanoid robot company is highlighted for running 48+ hours of fully autonomous package sorting at human parity speed (~3 seconds per package). The hosts discuss whether robots are a necessary response to demographic decline, and flag the political unpredictability of AI disruption — referencing students booing AI at commencement speeches and potential regulatory responses such as taxing automation layoffs.

### Crypto, Real Estate, and Private Markets [00:49:54]

Julian Emanuel's chart showing Bitcoin and software stocks moving in tandem since 2021 prompts discussion of Bitcoin as a software proxy rather than a distinct asset class. On real estate, the hosts call the locked-in low-rate mortgage divide between existing and new homeowners the biggest wealth inequality driver of the decade. A public service announcement on SPV risks warns that layered special purpose vehicles used to access SpaceX and OpenAI may leave investors without actual equity when those companies go public.
