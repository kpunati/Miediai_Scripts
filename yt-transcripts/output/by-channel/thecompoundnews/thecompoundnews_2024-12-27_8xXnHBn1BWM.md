# From Fitness Trainer to Wall Street CEO, the Incredible True Story of Harvey Schwartz | TCAF 171

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=8xXnHBn1BWM
**Duration:** 49:22

## Summary

Harvey Schwartz, CEO of Carlyle and former Goldman Sachs President, appears on The Compound and Friends to discuss his non-linear path from fitness instructor and cold-caller to leading one of the world's largest alternative asset managers. The conversation covers his early career failures, firsthand experience navigating the 2008 financial crisis at Goldman, the regulatory environment that catalyzed private equity's rise at the expense of bank balance sheets, and Carlyle's strategic pivot toward the wealth management channel as the next major growth frontier for private markets.

## Key Takeaways

- Post-2008 banking regulation that restricted proprietary risk-taking directly created the opening for alternative asset managers like Carlyle to fill the capital formation role previously held by bank balance sheets.
- The migration of private market products into the wealth channel—RIAs, wirehouses, and eventually 401k plans—represents what Schwartz calls the most significant trend in capital formation for the next 10-20 years, and remains early innings globally.
- Investors' behavioral tendency to hold wealth rather than spend it means illiquid private allocations make more portfolio sense than previously assumed, as most household wealth functions as long-term capital regardless of account type.
- The speed and transparency of modern information flow means financial crises will resolve (or accelerate) far faster today than in 2008, where key policy responses unfolded over several months.
- Carlyle's largest business is now credit and insurance rather than private equity, reflecting the broader evolution of alternative asset management beyond buyouts.
- Schwartz's leadership approach at Carlyle prioritized consensus-building with founders and largest shareholders over asserting a new direction, recognizing that credibility must be earned before intensity is deployed.
- Excess alpha in any financial market segment eventually compresses as information and capital efficiency improve, so the private market opportunity must be evaluated on portfolio construction fit rather than absolute return comparisons to past decades.

## Detailed Notes

### Early Career: From Cold-Caller to Citibank [00:02:20]

Schwartz describes starting at a municipal bond shop in New Jersey in 1987 making cold calls from Dun & Bradstreet index cards on the day of the Black Monday crash. After failing to build a book of business and nearly filing personal bankruptcy, a friend secured him a temp position at Citibank's back office. A mentor intervened to get him into Citibank's six-month credit analyst training program, which he identifies as the real beginning of his conventional Wall Street career.

### Goldman Sachs and the 2008 Financial Crisis [00:13:51]

Schwartz joined Goldman Sachs at age 33, eventually co-heading trading across equities, commodities, and fixed income during the financial crisis. He was at the Federal Reserve the weekend Lehman Brothers failed, and he reflects on how close the financial system came to systemic collapse. Three turning points he identifies: TARP approval roughly two weeks after Lehman, Goldman and Morgan Stanley converting to bank holding companies, and the Fed's bank stress tests and quantitative easing program in spring 2009. He also notes the suspension of mark-to-market accounting coincided almost precisely with the S&P 500 bottom.

### Private Equity's Rise from Banking Regulation [00:20:11]

Post-crisis regulation restricting bank balance sheet risk directly opened the door for alternative asset managers to provide capital that banks had previously supplied. Schwartz frames this as businesses always needing capital and capital finding its way regardless of regulatory structure. He notes IPOs have roughly halved since 2000 because private capital offers an alternative path, and characterizes Carlyle's own evolution from a pure private equity firm to one where credit and insurance are now its largest businesses.

### Carlyle CEO Transition and Strategy [00:26:33]

Schwartz took the Carlyle CEO role after a period of deliberate decompression following his 2018 Goldman departure. He chose Carlyle for its brand, its position in industry trends he had already identified (wealth channel, insurance, private credit growth), and alignment with the founders. He immediately prioritized the wealth management channel, changed carry protocols to better align incentives, and personally made cold calls to financial advisers to understand how they think about alternatives in client portfolios.

### Wealth Channel and the Future of Private Markets [00:35:04]

Schwartz argues the shift from institutional to wealth channel investors is the most significant capital formation trend of the next decade, currently in early stages globally. He notes Carlyle's CATAC credit product and CAPM secondaries/co-invest product have had rapid adoption. He addresses skeptics who question whether private market returns can sustain historical levels as competition increases, arguing the relevant question for advisers is portfolio construction fit and compounding consistency rather than comparison to the exceptional returns of early private equity vintages.
