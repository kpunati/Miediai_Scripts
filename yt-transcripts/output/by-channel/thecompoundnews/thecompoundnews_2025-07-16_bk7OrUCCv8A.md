# There Are Too Many Rich People | Animal Spirits 421

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-07-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=bk7OrUCCv8A
**Duration:** 70:48

## Summary

Episode 421 of Animal Spirits finds hosts Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson characterizing the current stock market rally as 'nervous' — with Schwab clients remaining cautious even as the S&P 500 hits record highs and pockets of euphoria exist in Bitcoin and AI names. The central thesis of the episode is that there are now too many wealthy Americans, a trend evidenced across multiple segments: rich households that do not feel rich, crowded luxury resorts, fierce competition for Manhattan one-bedrooms, and private equity muscling into youth sports. Batnick also presents data showing the 2020s have produced the worst real returns for bonds of any decade on record, while Bitcoin surpasses $2 trillion in market cap. The episode closes with media recommendations and personal anecdotes, threading the 'too many rich people' theme throughout.

## Key Takeaways

- Schwab trading activity data shows clients are selling technology stocks for the fifth consecutive month even as the S&P 500 reaches record highs, indicating a nervous rather than euphoric rally.
- Real (inflation-adjusted) bond returns this decade are worse than any prior decade, including the 1940s-1970s, with TLT still down approximately 40% from its October 2022 peak.
- US corporate America is highlighted as genuinely exceptional — returning $1.67 trillion to shareholders via buybacks and dividends in the past year — even if other aspects of the country are not.
- Bitcoin at a $2 trillion market cap is now comparable in size to silver and approaching US small-cap equities, yet generates less emotional market reaction than when it first hit major milestones.
- The upper-middle-class wealth surge — top 10% net worth up 49% since 2007 versus 11% for the median — is crowding premium experiences and making objectively wealthy people feel relatively less special.
- Baby boomers own 41% of all US residential real estate value; their gradual demographic decline over the next 20 years will create a slow-moving generational wealth transfer of unprecedented scale.
- Private equity is entering the $40 billion youth sports industry, with investors like Josh Harris and David Blitzer acquiring camps, fields, and leagues to build scalable sports property portfolios.

## Detailed Notes

### Nervous Rally and Investor Caution [00:01:59]

Batnick describes the current rally as 'nervous,' citing Schwab's Trading Activity Index showing clients sold technology stocks for the fifth consecutive month in a row, and that the index barely rebounded from two-year lows. Nvidia set new highs but Schwab clients were net sellers. The thesis is that this caution may itself be providing a floor under the market, as sidelined investors continue to chase a market that refuses to let them back in.

### US Corporate Exceptionalism and Shareholder Returns [00:09:40]

Carlson references BofA analyst Savita Subramanian's view that while the US is not exceptional in all ways, corporate America is, owing to index survivorship and post-COVID productivity gains. Over the past 12 months, S&P 500 companies returned $1.67 trillion to shareholders via buybacks and dividends. Carlson argues the dividend yield metric is no longer meaningful in isolation because buybacks — being more tax-efficient — now dominate capital return, requiring investors to look at combined shareholder yield.

### Worst Decade Ever for Bonds [00:17:35]

Batnick presents a comparison of nominal and real bond returns by decade going back to the 1920s for 5-year, 10-year, and 20-plus-year Treasuries. On a real (inflation-adjusted) basis, the 2020s stand apart as the worst decade on record — worse even than the 1940s through 1970s, when real returns were also negative. TLT is cited as still approximately 40% below its peak despite yields now sitting at 4-5%, which has softened the psychological blow for investors.

### Too Many Rich People: The Central Theme [00:24:04]

Batnick introduces the episode's recurring theme through a listener anecdote: a couple earning $600K per year with five kids, student loans, and a rental property who does not feel wealthy. Nick Maggiulli's piece on 'The Death of the Amex Lounge' is cited, showing the top-10% net worth rose 49% since 2007 to $1.9 million, creating too many wealthy people competing for the same premium goods and experiences. The Bloomberg analysis of college financial aid shows families earning $150K-$400K are squeezed — too rich for aid, too poor to pay sticker price comfortably.

### Bitcoin, Crypto, and the AI Discussion [00:37:18]

Bitcoin's $2 trillion market cap is noted as roughly equivalent to silver and approaching the $3 trillion US small-cap equity market. The hosts observe that milestone market cap numbers for Bitcoin no longer generate the same excitement they once did. Michael Saylor's Strategy is credited with creating a flywheel of corporate Bitcoin adoption. On AI, Jensen Huang's comment that workers will not lose jobs to AI but to workers who use AI is discussed, with Carlson suggesting AI will simply make people work more rather than less, mirroring prior productivity technologies like email and smartphones.

### Real Estate, Youth Sports, and Boomer Wealth Transfer [00:41:34]

Baby boomers own $20 trillion or 41% of US residential real estate despite being one-fifth of the population; their share will decline as roughly 39 million boomers die between 2025 and 2045. The hosts expect the transition to be slow and glacial rather than a sudden supply shock. On youth sports, private equity investors including Apollo's Josh Harris are acquiring the $40 billion youth sports industry's facilities and leagues, drawn by reliable demand from affluent families willing to spend heavily on travel teams and trainers.
