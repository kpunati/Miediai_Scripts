# Stocks React to Negative Economic Surprises

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-02-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vvkzSXYfYWE
**Duration:** 32:05

## Summary

Josh Brown interviews Callie Cox, Chief Strategist at Ritholtz Wealth Management, about the growth scare taking shape in early 2025. Cox argues that while inflation remains the dominant narrative, the more pressing risk is a softening job market, with DOGE-related federal job losses potentially the largest in US history. The City US Economic Surprise Index has crossed back below zero, signaling that economic data is now disappointing relative to Wall Street consensus. Cox recommends investors set a 'crash plan' with predefined response thresholds, consider reducing concentration in high-multiple growth stocks, and hold some shorter-duration Treasuries as an economic hedge given that high policy rates leave the economy vulnerable to shocks.

## Key Takeaways

- Economic data is surprising to the downside for the first time since summer 2024, with the Citi US Economic Surprise Index crossing back below zero.
- The biggest risk is not inflation but a job market crack: federal layoffs and a hiring freeze could hit the data in March 2025 in a way markets are not positioned for.
- Inflation expectations are rising in surveys (UMich) and in bond market break-even rates, but actual broad-based inflation is not at crisis levels yet.
- High-multiple tech and growth stocks face the greatest downside risk in a growth scare because they are the most sensitive to sentiment reversals.
- Having a written crash plan with predefined action thresholds (5%, 10%, 20% declines) converts fear into a framework and preserves investor agency.
- The Fed remains above neutral and is constrained from cutting by lingering inflation, leaving the economy more vulnerable to economic shocks than in a normal environment.
- Investor sentiment has come down from euphoric levels at year-end 2024, which is a healthy reset even though CEO confidence remains elevated.

## Detailed Notes

### Inflation: Narrative vs. Data [00:01:15]

Cox distinguishes between actual inflation data (PCE/CPI near 3%, above the 2% target but far from the 9% peak of 2022) and elevated inflation expectations driven by recency bias and tariff/immigration policy headlines. Break-even rates in the TIPS market are also rising, giving the Fed additional reason for caution. Egg prices (up ~20% year-over-year) are cited as a psychologically potent but narrow driver of consumer inflation anxiety.

### The Growth Scare: Job Market Cracks [00:07:36]

Cox argues the primary risk is a softening job market, not runaway inflation. Consumer spending constitutes 70% of the US economy, and most recent hiring has been concentrated in less-cyclical government, education, and health sectors. DOGE-related actions—a federal hiring freeze, ~75,000 buyout acceptances, and ~200,000 probationary terminations—could represent the largest federal job loss in history, yet the impact has not yet appeared in reported data as of late February 2025.

### Citi Economic Surprise Index and Market Expectations [00:10:45]

The Citi US Economic Surprise Index has crossed back below zero, meaning economic data is now disappointing relative to Wall Street consensus, reversing the positive surprise trend from late 2024. GDP expectations going into 2025 were elevated after two straight years of upside surprises. The 10-year Treasury yield is at approximately 4.4%, high relative to the prior decade.

### Investor Sentiment and Portfolio Positioning [00:21:15]

AAII survey data shows bulls retreating from euphoric year-end levels, which Cox views as a healthy normalization. However, global portfolio managers (per the Merrill Lynch survey) hold low cash while simultaneously believing stocks are overvalued—an unusual and unstable combination. Growth scare sell-offs historically are indiscriminate and hit high-multiple stocks hardest.

### Portfolio Recommendations and Crash Plan Framework [00:26:31]

Cox recommends trimming concentrated positions in high-multiple growth/tech stocks and holding shorter-duration Treasuries rather than long-duration bonds, since long-duration prices in policy expectations and is less reliable as a pure economic hedge. She outlines a 'crash plan' framework: at a 5% decline, stay put; at 10%, consider deploying cash; at 20%, consider tax-loss harvesting or Roth conversions. Investors with 100% equity portfolios have no agency—their only option is panic-selling. The S&P 500 has had 91 instances of a 5% or greater pullback since 1950, roughly once every 10 months.
