# Retail Investors Just Plowed $100 Billion Into Stocks. THIS MONTH. | TCAF 212

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Q1DsajlXrcQ
**Duration:** 83:14

## Summary

Scott Nations, president of Nations Indexes and author of 'The Anxious Investor,' joins Michael Batnick and Josh Brown to examine the record surge in retail stock buying and the proliferation of speculative instruments including leveraged ETFs and quantum computing names. The conversation covers structural changes in market speculation—from meme stocks to zero-DTE options—and examines how volatility dynamics have changed in modern markets. Nations and the hosts also flag early stress in private credit markets following the First Brands bankruptcy as a potential systemic risk, and close with observations about K-shaped consumption patterns illustrated by Delta's premium cabin revenue overtaking coach.

## Key Takeaways

- Retail investors purchased over $100 billion in US stocks in a single month—a record—though normalized against a ~$50 trillion market cap this is approximately 0.2% of total market value.
- Speculative quantum computing names like Rigetti and Aklo trade roughly the same daily dollar volume as Google and Netflix despite being a fraction of their market cap.
- The ratio of leveraged-long to leveraged-short ETF assets stands at roughly 10:1, near cycle highs, with single-stock leveraged ETF assets approaching $30 billion.
- Robinhood's market cap converged to within 1.3x of Schwab's from a 23x discount just two years prior, reflecting a dramatic revaluation of retail brokerage business models.
- Scott Nations views private credit and BDCs as the most likely vector for the next financial stress event, citing opacity, high fees, and the First Brands double-collateral scandal as early warning signals.
- The Federal Reserve's tendency to keep rates too low for too long is identified as a greater systemic risk than excessively tight policy.
- Delta's premium cabin revenue approaching parity with main cabin illustrates the broader K-shaped economy where high-end spending is robust while mass-market demand softens.

## Detailed Notes

### Chicago Mercantile Exchange and Market Evolution [00:09:10]

Scott Nations describes his career as a trader in the S&P options pit at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange starting in 1989. He notes that while the pit provided an accessible career on-ramp for young traders, modern markets are far more efficient and offer opportunity based on merit rather than physical proximity or lineage. The entry point to professional trading now typically requires advanced quantitative education, with physics PhDs increasingly valued over math PhDs.

### Retail Buying Surge and Speculative Behavior [00:15:39]

Richard Thaler and Alex Imas's updated 'Winner's Curse' is cited to explain how internet coordination platforms extend bubble durations via 'diamond hands' and tribal identity around stocks. The hosts present data showing retail purchased over $100 billion of US stocks in a single month, the largest on record. Speculative quantum computing names (Rigetti, Aklo, IonQ) and digital asset companies trade daily dollar volumes comparable to trillion-dollar companies like Google, illustrating extreme concentration of speculative activity in small-cap names.

### Leveraged ETFs, Options, and Volatility Dynamics [00:21:19]

Long leveraged ETF assets outnumber short by roughly 10:1 near all-time highs, with nearly $30 billion in single-stock leveraged ETFs. Nations explains that leveraged and VIX-linked ETFs are often money incinerators due to contango roll costs and volatility crush following catalysts. He notes that the options market now recovers from volatility spikes in roughly 18 hours versus years after the 1987 crash, creating a feedback loop where traders reflexively sell vol spikes—a pattern that could backfire if a major down leg occurs.

### Robinhood vs. Schwab and Brokerage Market Dynamics [00:47:34]

The market cap gap between Schwab and Robinhood has compressed from 23x in 2023 to approximately 1.3x, with Robinhood at $135 billion and Schwab at $170 billion. Nations notes Robinhood's 'free' model obscures payment-for-order-flow revenue, but acknowledges other brokerages now do the same. The hosts suggest this convergence represents a fundamental repricing of retail brokerage—though a bear market could reverse sentiment toward Schwab's perceived stability.

### Private Credit Stress and First Brands [00:57:49]

Nations flags private credit as the most likely source of the next financial shock, citing opacity, high fees, and illiquidity. The VanEck BDC Income ETF (BIZD) has fallen below April lows as dividend cuts loom from declining rates and rising non-accruals. First Brands filed for bankruptcy with $11 billion in liabilities on $3 billion revenue after reportedly double-pledging approximately $2.3 billion in collateral—drawing comparisons to the Bear Stearns hedge funds in 2006-07 as an early warning signal rather than a systemic crisis in itself.

### Gold, Bitcoin, and the K-Shaped Economy [01:10:23]

Gold is outperforming Bitcoin year-to-date despite the broader bull market, a dynamic the hosts find puzzling but attribute to falling interest rates lifting all asset classes simultaneously. The largest ETF inflow category year-to-date is Treasury bills—an unusual combination with equity and crypto at all-time highs. Delta's premium cabin revenue rose 9% to $5.8 billion while coach fell 4%, epitomizing the K-shaped economy where premium spending remains robust.
