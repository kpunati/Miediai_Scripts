# The Zero Dollar Club | Animal Spirits 435

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=x23ikFPMCzA
**Duration:** 80:27

## Summary

In Animal Spirits episode 435, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examine whether current market sentiment indicators are reliable given the saturation of financial media commentary, drawing parallels to Jesse Livermore's contrarian approach in 1929. They analyze Yuri Timmer's bull market charts showing that the current rally is unusually earnings-driven with minimal multiple expansion, and debate the pros and cons of gold ownership given its performance. The hosts also discuss speculative 'Zero Dollar Club' stocks — companies with little to no revenue that have seen massive price appreciation — and raise concerns about newly filed 5x single-stock leveraged ETFs. Consumer health data from American Express and Bank of America earnings calls is cited to argue that aggregate credit metrics remain solid despite media narratives about consumer stress.

## Key Takeaways

- Sentiment indicators have become harder to interpret as reliable contrarian signals because financial commentary is now ubiquitous across all media channels.
- The current bull market is historically unusual in being driven almost entirely by earnings growth rather than PE multiple expansion, with PE up only about 1% year-over-year.
- Hosts do not own gold for clients and explain this through historical return analysis, noting three out of four decades from the 1980s through 2010s saw flat or negative gold returns.
- The 'Zero Dollar Club' — speculative stocks with no meaningful revenue — has seen extraordinary gains, raising concerns about a potential flash-crash scenario if sentiment turns.
- Proposed 5x single-stock leveraged ETFs filed with the SEC are characterized as potentially dangerous from a market-structure perspective, though a government shutdown could allow them to become effective automatically.
- Aggregate consumer credit data from American Express, Bank of America, and Ally Financial shows no meaningful deterioration, with charge-off rates declining or flat despite media narratives about consumer distress.
- The US housing market faces a shrinking buyer pool, with median down payments at record highs and homes sitting on market longer, but hosts expect activity to rebound meaningfully once mortgage rates fall below 5%.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Bubble Sentiment and Historical Parallels [00:01:01]

Hosts debate whether AI bubble warnings are meaningful given the current media saturation. Batnick references a New York Times article from 1929 describing how 'people who know the least about the stock market have made the most money,' which Jesse Livermore used as a contrarian short signal. The key question posed is whether sentiment indicators have lost predictive value when everyone is simultaneously a commentator.

### Gold Ownership Debate [00:09:37]

Carlson explains why neither host owns gold for clients, citing three consecutive loss decades (1980s, 1990s, 2000s) and the distorting effect of the 1970s Nixon-shock catch-up run. Many listeners responded that they hold 5-10% gold as insurance or diversification, which the hosts acknowledge as a reasonable rationale. Bitcoin is noted as having failed to displace gold as a store-of-value alternative as Carlson had expected.

### Bull Market Structure: Earnings vs. Multiples [00:18:24]

Citing Yuri Timmer's Fidelity research, hosts note the current three-year bull market has followed the median cyclical bull market path almost exactly. Unusually, PE expansion has been only about 1% year-over-year while EPS grew 11%, making this rally fundamentally driven. ETF flows hit $1 trillion for the year with the top four being large stock market ETFs and Bitcoin.

### Speculative Stocks and Leveraged ETF Risks [00:29:24]

The 'Zero Dollar Club' — a list of companies with near-zero revenue such as Serve Robotics, AST SpaceMobile ($35B market cap), and Rigetti Computing — is highlighted as an example of speculative excess. Separately, 13 new 5x single-stock leveraged ETFs (covering names like Nvidia, Tesla, Amazon, and Coinbase) were filed with the SEC; a government shutdown could allow them to go live automatically in 75 days under ETF Rule 6C-11 without additional review.

### Consumer Credit Health and Economy [00:50:21]

American Express results show no credit deterioration; card-member loans 30+ days past due remain at 1.3%, net write-offs are flat, and Gen Z spending is up 39% YoY. Bank of America data shows total net charge-offs declined quarter-over-quarter and credit card charge-off rates fell from 98 to 82 basis points. Ally Financial's CEO similarly reported no impact from macro uncertainty on credit performance. Hosts argue aggregate data does not support widespread consumer distress narratives.

### Housing Market and Auto Prices [01:00:20]

The average new car price reached $50,000 per Kelly Blue Book, with more than 60 models averaging above $75,000, though the Toyota Camry is more affordable in labor-hours terms than in 1995. In housing, median days on market rose from about 20 to 50, median down payments hit a record $70,000, and most homes sell below list price. Hosts expect a meaningful activity rebound once 30-year mortgage rates fall below 5%, though the gap between buying and renting costs has pushed many potential buyers to rent indefinitely.

### China Trade War and Macro Context [00:36:43]

Hosts discuss a Wall Street Journal article noting the US is backing away from many tariffs on goods it cannot domestically produce. They argue China has a structural advantage in trade wars given its tolerance for economic pain, and that small businesses have borne the brunt of tariff impacts while large-cap companies have largely been insulated — explaining why the stock market has been relatively unconcerned.
