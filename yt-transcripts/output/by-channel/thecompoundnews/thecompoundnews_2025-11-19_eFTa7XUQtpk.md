# Is the AI Trade Over? | Animal Spirits 439

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=eFTa7XUQtpk
**Duration:** 73:44

## Summary

Episode 439 of Animal Spirits features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson debating whether the AI trade has peaked, with both hosts concluding the recent selloff in speculative stocks is a healthy correction rather than a bubble top. They contrast the candor of Apollo's Mark Rowan — who acknowledged private credit was a better business in prior years — with the evasive language of Blue Owl's Mark Lipschultz, using the comparison to illustrate why CEO transparency matters for investors. The episode also covers Michael Burry closing his hedge fund as a case study on the difference between making a legendary trade and being a legendary investor, and closes with personal finance reminders on maximizing retirement contributions.

## Key Takeaways

- The speculative-stock selloff (quantum computing, meme stocks) is characterized by both hosts as a healthy correction; they argue the AI bubble, if it existed at all, was unusually weak and likely not finished.
- Michael Burry's pattern of repeated crash calls illustrates the gap between making a single legendary trade and sustaining a track record as a legendary investor.
- Mark Rowan's honest acknowledgment that private credit offered more value in prior years contrasts sharply with Blue Owl's defensive earnings-call language, a signal hosts use to assess management credibility.
- Bitcoin's volatility profile closely mirrors that of large-cap tech stocks; IBIT was down nearly 20% through April then up 35%, underscoring that ETF wrapping has not reduced crypto volatility.
- The NAR's widely-cited statistic that the median first-time homebuyer age is now 39-40 is disputed by competing surveys, suggesting the 'millennials are locked out of housing' narrative may rest on flawed data.
- Maxing out a 401(k) — contribution limit rising to $24,500 in 2026 — matters more to long-run wealth than most investment strategy decisions.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Trade and Speculative Selloff [00:00:04]

Batnick argues there was never true euphoria in this bull market; Carlson pushes back, pointing to Oracle's 37% single-day gain on its OpenAI deal as a genuine euphoric moment. Both agree the subsequent correction is healthy and that the AI investment cycle is far from over, with hyperscaler CEOs showing no sign of pulling back capital spending despite broad public skepticism about job displacement and rising energy costs.

### Market Skepticism and Media Narrative [00:00:04]

The hosts note a stark contrast with the 1990s dot-com era: mainstream media is actively skeptical of AI rather than stoking enthusiasm, which they argue keeps a lid on excess and prevents a 1999-style melt-up. Brad Gerstner's observation that AI has become 'deeply unpopular' in America due to job-loss fears and rising electricity bills is cited as a structural headwind to retail enthusiasm.

### Michael Burry and Legendary Trades vs. Legendary Investors [00:00:04]

Burry's announcement of closing his hedge fund prompts a discussion on how the 2008 crisis made certain investors famous for a single trade rather than sustained performance. Carlson draws a parallel to John Paulson, who could not replicate his subprime short in subsequent years. The hosts argue these 'legendary trade' figures are often publicly bearish in part because they are psychologically anchored to the moment of peak recognition.

### Private Credit: Apollo vs. Blue Owl [00:00:04]

Apollo's Mark Rowan candidly states that private credit was a better business in prior years, likening regret about not owning it earlier to regret about not owning Nvidia earlier. In contrast, Blue Owl's Lipschultz responds to questions about BDC underperformance and a reported fund merger leaving investors with a 20% hit with vague 'execute, execute, execute' language. Hosts view this candor gap as a key signal for manager selection.

### Bitcoin, Housing, and Consumer Data [00:00:04]

Bitcoin's drawdown and recovery profile is described as nearly identical to Meta's, suggesting ETF adoption has not changed the asset's risk character. On housing, the hosts plan a dedicated episode to examine whether the NAR's median first-time buyer age statistic is methodologically sound; Carlson expresses little sympathy for pandemic-era buyers who feel 'trapped' by low-rate mortgages on appreciated homes. A brief note on the 50-year mortgage suggests the monthly payment savings versus a 30-year are modest and the product mainly benefits lenders.
