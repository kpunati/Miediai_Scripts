# It’s Only a Bubble If You Panic | TCAF 214

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=RZEhwdSYIos
**Duration:** 69:27

## Summary

This episode of The Compound and Friends brings together Sonali Basak of iCapital, Ben Carlson of Ritholtz Wealth Management, Josh Brown, and Michael Batnick for a wide-ranging examination of the private credit market following high-profile BDC-related bankruptcies. The panel concludes that systemic risk fears are overstated: losses from the First Brands and Envision Healthcare bankruptcies were de minimis across the BDC universe, and payment-in-kind ratios and non-accruals remain stable according to Houlihan Lokey data. However, the hosts acknowledge real risks including rapid capital deployment by less-experienced managers, manager quality dispersion, and the absence of a genuine recessionary stress test for the expanded private credit market. The conversation also covers the structural democratization of private assets, with minimums falling and 401k inclusion on the horizon, and ends with a debate on whether this accessibility actually benefits retail investors.

## Key Takeaways

- BDC losses from recent bankruptcies were highly dispersed, with most exposed funds suffering less than 0.05% drawdowns, undermining systemic-risk narratives.
- Manager selection is the dominant driver of outcomes in private credit; dispersion between top and bottom managers is far wider than in public credit markets.
- Payment-in-kind levels and non-accrual rates remain stable according to current industry data, contradicting bearish narratives popularized in financial media.
- The private credit market has expanded well beyond traditional direct lending into data center leases, asset-backed lending, and consumer finance, introducing different risk profiles.
- Illiquidity is a feature as much as a bug: it prevents investor panic-selling, but advisers who over-allocate clients to illiquid assets face serious relationship risk in any future recession.
- Secondaries are viewed as a compelling strategy to exploit future dislocations, buying distressed positions at a discount when funds are forced to liquidate.
- The democratization trend (lower minimums, semi-liquid structures, 401k inclusion) may compress illiquidity premiums over time, and the best managers may not make their top funds available to retail channels.

## Detailed Notes

### Private Credit Landscape and BDC Bankruptcy Fallout [00:00:00]

Sonali Basak reviews iCapital analysis of BDC exposure to First Brands and Envision Healthcare bankruptcies. Out of 165+ BDCs examined, only a dozen had any exposure, and most losses were around 0.05% of total assets. The panel attributes fraud and inadequate collateral disclosures — not reckless lending — as the primary factors in these specific cases.

### Structural Differences: Direct Lending vs. Public Credit [00:00:00]

The group traces private credit's growth through post-GFC bank regulation, COVID disruption, and the 2023 SVB regional banking crisis. Direct lending now exceeds broadly syndicated loans in total volume at an estimated $1.7 trillion. Unlike public bond funds with QIPS enabling investor lookups, private credit funds have no standardized transparency mechanism, though monthly reporting is becoming more common as the market democratizes.

### Manager Quality, Dispersion, and Capital Deployment Risk [00:00:00]

Dispersion of returns across fund managers is identified as the central risk, not asset-class volatility. Basak argues that top managers like Blackstone, Ares, KKR, Blue Owl, and Apollo bring scale, experienced workout teams, and deep diligence. Brown warns that PE-style pressures to deploy capital are creating sloppy underwriting in lower-tier managers, particularly in wealth management roll-ups at excessive multiples.

### BDCs, Banks, and Systemic Risk Assessment [00:00:00]

The panel revisits BDC vulnerabilities: dividend yields will compress as rates fall, and fraud discoveries may multiply. Brown draws a rhetorical parallel to mortgage fund defenses in 2008 but concedes the underlying collateral quality and structure are materially different. Basak disputes Moody's $300 billion bank-exposure figure, arguing it conflates specialty lenders with private credit and that true fund exposure is closer to $96 billion.

### Democratization, 401k Inclusion, and Outlook [00:00:00]

Discussion of falling minimums (now as low as $25,000) and potential 401k private-asset allocations. Basak and Brown agree that Schwab, Vanguard, and similar platforms will only onboard marquee managers, reducing but not eliminating poor product risk. The illiquidity premium may compress as more retail capital enters, and truly elite opportunities will remain inaccessible to most individual investors.
