# Trump Coin | Animal Spirits 396

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=nvTgMZ64Qr4
**Duration:** 59:09

## Summary

In episode 396 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson open with an extended analysis of the Trump meme coin launched days before the 2025 inauguration, arguing it represents a new low for crypto markets and signals a potential golden age of financial fraud with regulators standing down. They cover a range of economic and market topics including the historical decline in US recession frequency, a sharp rise in income thresholds for top earners, risks of a Chinese consumer recovery stoking inflation, and the collapse of Sonos's stock following a disastrous app update. The episode closes with observations on Gen Z's heightened money focus, institutional investors signaling US housing overvaluation, and media recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- The Trump meme coin, where Trump reportedly controls ~80% of the float, is viewed by hosts as an unprecedented conflict of interest and a signal that crypto fraud will intensify under the new administration.
- Hosts argue that meme coins have become the defining narrative of the current crypto bull cycle, crowding out legitimate blockchain development and damaging the industry's credibility.
- The US has spent only two months in recession over the past 15+ years, with the 2010s being the first decade in modern history to see neither a recession nor a bear market.
- The income threshold to reach the top 10% of individual earners rose approximately 50% from 2014 to 2024, reflecting income growth that has outpaced inflation for high earners.
- A Chinese consumer spending recovery could introduce an unexpected inflationary impulse for the US and other central banks, a scenario not widely discussed.
- Sonos lost roughly 70% of its market cap from its highs after a catastrophic app redesign that broke basic user functionality.
- Institutional single-family landlords like Invitation Homes trade at 20-35% discounts to NAV, which analysts use to argue US home prices are overvalued by a similar magnitude.

## Detailed Notes

### Trump Meme Coin and Crypto Concerns [00:00:04]

Hosts describe the Trump meme coin launched just before the inauguration as the craziest event they have seen in financial markets, noting Trump reportedly holds roughly 80% of the supply. They argue the coin exemplifies pure gambling with no underlying utility, and that even many established crypto participants are embarrassed by it. Jim Chanos and Noah Smith are cited warning of a golden age of financial fraud, with the hosts noting that the removal of regulatory guardrails makes the situation harder to contain.

### Regulatory Context and Crypto Industry Damage [00:00:04]

Batnick draws a parallel to the Roaring 20s, suggesting the current deregulatory environment will eventually require a new wave of financial regulation after widespread losses. The hosts note that Gary Gensler's aggressive stance against crypto ETFs inadvertently galvanized political opposition that contributed to Trump's election victory, producing an outcome worse for consumer protection than if the ETFs had been approved earlier. They acknowledge legitimate crypto innovation—Bitcoin, stablecoins, tokenization—will continue despite the meme coin sideshow.

### Recession Frequency and Economic History [00:00:04]

Using NBER data going back to the 1900s, the hosts show the US spent roughly 40% of the early 20th century in recession, a share that has fallen dramatically as the economy matured. The 2010s were the first decade on record with neither a recession nor a bear market. They acknowledge constant financial media noise may make investors feel worse off despite objectively better macro outcomes.

### Income Thresholds and Rising Earnings [00:00:04]

Drawing on 'Don't Quit Your Day Job' data, Carlson notes the individual income needed to reach the top 10% rose from $100,000 in 2014 to $150,000 by 2024, and the top 5% threshold moved from roughly $130,000 to $200,000. He attributes this partly to the expansion of high-paying roles beyond traditional professions like medicine and law, and notes that inflation-adjusted figures still show real income gains for upper cohorts.

### China Inflation Risk and Consumer Spending [00:00:04]

Citing Matthew Klein's piece in The Overshoot, the hosts discuss how Chinese per capita discretionary spending has fallen sharply since the pandemic, acting as a deflationary force globally. Klein warns that if Chinese policymakers successfully stimulate consumer spending, it could create an inflationary impulse that catches Western central banks off guard—a scenario absent from most forecasters' models.

### Sonos App Disaster and Housing Market [00:00:04]

Sonos's stock has fallen roughly 70% from its highs after a software update broke basic app functionality, stranding users and exposing the company's over-reliance on software quality. On housing, Wall Street Journal data on Invitation Homes trading at 20-35% discounts to NAV is cited as an implicit signal that residential property prices are overvalued; however, Batnick distinguishes owner-occupied housing from investment properties, noting cap-rate analysis does not directly translate to home price valuation for individuals.

### Gen Z, Millennials, and Cultural Observations [00:00:04]

Survey data shows 36% of 12th graders now rate money and wealth as 'extremely important,' up from roughly 25% for prior generations, which the hosts link to social media exposure to instant wealth. They discuss how Millennials were scarred by the financial crisis into risk aversion, while Gen Z has not yet experienced a comparable shock. The segment closes with observations on overcrowded youth sports infrastructure and media recommendations including 'A Real Pain' on Hulu and 'American Primeval' on Netflix.
