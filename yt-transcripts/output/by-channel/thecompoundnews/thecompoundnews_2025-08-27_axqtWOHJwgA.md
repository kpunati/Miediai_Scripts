# State of the American Investor | Animal Spirits 427

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-08-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=axqtWOHJwgA
**Duration:** 64:48

## Summary

In episode 427 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson survey market conditions in late August 2025, arguing that broad participation (9-to-9 breadth thrust, equal-weight performance) supports continued optimism while acknowledging real-world headwinds including a slowing labor market and cumulative inflation pressure on low-income consumers. They review the Fidelity 2025 State of the American Investor survey, which shows younger investors are more optimistic, more familiar with options strategies, and more engaged with crypto than older cohorts. The hosts analyze Strategy's Bitcoin treasury model in depth, crediting its execution while questioning whether its premium over Bitcoin NAV is sustainable. They also critique YieldMax ETF yield mechanics, discuss Ivy League endowment illiquidity problems, and cover real estate commission rigidity.

## Key Takeaways

- Broad market participation—high percentage of S&P 500 stocks above key moving averages and a rare 90/90 breadth thrust day—supports the bull market case over narrow leadership concerns.
- The debate over whether 2025 resembles 1996 (still early) or 1999 (near a top) is the central narrative framing investor conversations with clients.
- Younger investors are more optimistic, more familiar with options and income strategies, and far more crypto-engaged than older investors, according to Fidelity's 2025 survey.
- YieldMax ETFs generate yield by selling options, but an analysis shows simply holding two-thirds stock plus one-third cash would have outperformed most of these strategies on a total-return basis.
- Strategy's Bitcoin treasury model is described as sophisticated financial engineering—refining Bitcoin into tiered fixed-income instruments—but its premium to Bitcoin NAV has compressed and MSTR has not made new highs since November 2024.
- Ivy League endowments holding only 2% in cash and 6% in bonds while mostly locked into private equity illustrates dangerous groupthink, as some had to borrow hundreds of millions for operational needs.
- Real estate agent commissions remained sticky or rose after the NAR settlement that was supposed to create more fee competition, suggesting the industry is structurally resistant to disruption.

## Detailed Notes

### Stock Market Breadth and the 1996-vs-1999 Debate [00:02:10]

Batnick and Carlson argue that broad participation—59% of S&P 500 stocks above 50-day and 200-day averages, plus a rare 90/90 breadth thrust day—is bullish. They frame the current moment as the '1996 or 1999' question: Tom Lee's view is that it is still 1996 (early cycle), while some clients are beginning to worry about an AI bubble. Historical analysis from Carson Group shows S&P 500 forward returns after 90/90 days average over 22% with a hit rate above 90%.

### Value vs. Growth and Market Composition Shift [00:12:42]

The hosts examine rolling three-year value versus growth performance and note that 77% of mega-cap market cap is now classified as growth—up from roughly 50% in 2003-2014—per Adam Parker's proprietary model, leaving only 3.9% classified as value. Carlson suggests value underperformance may be structural: fewer economic recessions remove the cyclical pivot points where value historically re-rates. Value still works in international markets and may be better treated as a trend-following allocation in the US.

### Fidelity 2025 State of the American Investor Survey [00:21:58]

Fidelity surveyed 2,007 adults with at least $25,000 investable assets in mid-April 2025. Younger investors (0-5 years experience) are more optimistic about future portfolio performance (56% vs. 34% for 11+ year investors). Newer investors are more familiar with options strategies like covered calls (43% vs. 33%), while older investors show less crypto interest (23% vs. 56%). Hosts conclude younger investors are orders of magnitude more sophisticated than prior generations and are battle-tested through multiple recent drawdowns.

### YieldMax ETFs: Income or Return of Capital? [00:19:06]

Jeff Ptak's analysis compared YieldMax ETFs on Tesla, Coinbase, and Nvidia to a simple portfolio of two-thirds stock and one-third cash, rebalanced periodically. The simpler strategy outperformed, net of YieldMax's fees and the tax drag from large distributions. Hosts note distributions are set for 'shock and awe' rather than as genuine income, and total return is largely dependent on the underlying stock's appreciation—not the option strategy.

### Strategy (MicroStrategy) Bitcoin Treasury Model [00:39:12]

Batnick listened to Strategy's earnings call, the first opened to Q&A, which ran two hours. CEO Fong Lee outlined ambitions for Strategy to be the world's largest treasury company, surpassing Mag 7 cash positions and eventually Berkshire Hathaway's $348 billion capital base via Bitcoin. Strategy is pivoting from convertible notes to preferred equity issuance. Saylor compared the model to refining crude oil—stripping Bitcoin's volatility into graduated fixed-income instruments while leaving excess volatility to common shareholders and derivative ETFs. Despite stock being up roughly 2,000% since 2023, MSTR has not made new highs since November 2024, trading 20% off its highs, while Bitcoin itself is up 24.5% year-to-date.

### Real Estate, Housing, and Endowment Liquidity [00:46:31]

Logan Motoshami's data shows the 30-year mortgage/10-year Treasury spread has narrowed in 2025 versus prior years, though the hosts debate whether Fed rate cuts will translate into lower mortgage rates given inflation concerns. ARM share of bank-held mortgages appears elevated at 41% per Zero Hedge, but Logan's data shows actual borrower ARM origination is near 8%. On real estate commissions: after the NAR settlement, average commissions rose rather than fell, as buyers declined to negotiate. Separately, Ivy League endowments averaging 2% cash and 6% bonds have had to borrow for operational expenses, reflecting dangerous illiquidity from over-allocation to private equity and venture capital.

### Consumer Pressures and Economy [00:32:55]

McDonald's same-store sales were negative for four consecutive quarters as low-income consumers cut visits by double digits. Walmart reported 4.6% same-store sales growth but warned of rising post-tariff inventory costs continuing into Q3 and Q4 2025. Hosts note the bottom 20% of income earners contribute only about 20 basis points of S&P 500 earnings, so their financial stress has limited direct market impact, though it signals ongoing consumer bifurcation.
