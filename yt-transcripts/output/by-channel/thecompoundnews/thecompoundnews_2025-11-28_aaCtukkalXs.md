# Why oil could be next year’s gold | TCAF 219

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=aaCtukkalXs
**Duration:** 65:30

## Summary

Peter Boockvar joins The Compound and Friends to share his views on markets, inflation, and energy. The conversation covers the bifurcation within AI stocks between Google-aligned and OpenAI-aligned infrastructure plays, the dramatic reversal in Google's narrative from perceived laggard to one of the best performing stocks of 2025, and serious questions about the sustainability of mega-cap tech capex spending running 25-52% of revenues. Boockvar argues the Fed risks reigniting inflation by cutting rates prematurely, and makes a strong contrarian case for oil as perhaps the cheapest major asset class given declining US shale output and near-record-low energy sector weighting in the S&P 500.

## Key Takeaways

- The AI trade has split into Google-aligned versus OpenAI-aligned infrastructure winners, with correlation among Mag 7 stocks breaking down as company-specific narratives diverge.
- Oracle's capex as a percentage of revenue has surged from 10% in 2021 to an expected 52%, raising questions about returns on capital and whether picks-and-shovels companies face collateral damage if hyperscalers pull back.
- Long-end bond yields in the US, Europe, and Japan are rising or flat even as central banks cut short rates, signaling that markets are pricing in fiscal risk and debt sustainability concerns.
- Boockvar argues the Fed should prioritize taming inflation over supporting the labor market, warning that cutting rates risks repeating Powell's transitory error and could reignite housing and rental inflation.
- Oil at roughly $60 per barrel is Boockvar's top contrarian call: energy is below 3% of the S&P 500, US shale basin production is rolling over, and rig counts are near post-2021 lows.
- AI utility value is real and users would pay significantly more than current pricing, suggesting the concern is not demand but over-investment in physical infrastructure relative to near-term revenue.
- A more dovish Fed replacement for Powell could weaken the dollar, benefiting precious metals, international equities, and other hard assets.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Stock Bifurcation and Google's Narrative Reversal [00:13:08]

The market has shifted from treating AI as a monolithic theme to differentiating winners and losers. A clear divergence emerged between Google-exposed and OpenAI-exposed infrastructure stocks following Sam Altman's podcast appearance. Google's PE expanded from roughly 14x to 31x forward earnings and the stock added $1.5 trillion in market cap—its best year since 2009—after the company pivoted away from internal controversy and committed aggressively to AI integration in search.

### Capex Sustainability and Picks-and-Shovels Risk [00:25:49]

Boockvar walked through capex as a percentage of revenue across hyperscalers: Oracle at ~52%, Meta at ~35%, and Microsoft and Google at 25-27%, all dramatically higher than 2021 levels of low-to-mid teens. The key question is whether this level of spend becomes permanent—impacting free cash flow and return on equity—or whether companies can ratchet back as AI models become more efficient. If spend declines, companies selling into the physical buildout (networking, data center components) could face significant revenue headwinds.

### Fed Policy, Inflation Risks, and the Housing Market [00:34:21]

Boockvar argued that cutting the Fed funds rate too aggressively risks stoking another round of inflation, pointing to European and Japanese central bank examples where rate cuts did not lower long-end yields. He identified housing and rental costs as the core inflation pressure for young consumers, warning that lower mortgage rates could stimulate demand without commensurate supply increases, raising home prices and negating affordability benefits. The drop in multifamily construction starts could sow the seeds for renewed rental inflation in coming years.

### Fed Leadership Change and Asset Class Implications [00:53:40]

With Kevin Hassett likely to replace Powell, the hosts and Boockvar discussed the probability of more aggressive rate cuts regardless of inflation data. Boockvar's recommended positioning in that scenario includes precious metals, international equities (European and Asian markets are up 30-50% in dollar terms in 2025), and oil. He specifically noted gold as a beneficiary if the dollar weakens further on rate differentials.

### Oil as a Contrarian Hard Asset [01:01:25]

Boockvar's top asset class call is oil at approximately $60 per barrel, which he views as one of the cheapest assets in nominal terms. Energy represents less than 3% of the S&P 500, positioning is extremely light, and fundamental supply headwinds are building: US shale production in major basins (Eagle Ford, Haynesville, Bakken) is rolling over per EIA data, rig counts are near post-September-2021 lows, and ExxonMobil's push into Guyana signals that domestic fields are maturing. Venezuela, holding the world's largest proven reserves, was also mentioned as a longer-term supply wildcard.
