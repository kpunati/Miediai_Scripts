# The Stock Market Crashed. Now What? | Animal Spirits 407

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=XuDjC64rn50
**Duration:** 73:48

## Summary

Recorded on April 8, 2025, this Animal Spirits episode captures Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson's real-time reaction to the market crash triggered by the Trump administration's sweeping tariff announcements. The hosts assess the economic logic of tariffs as deeply flawed, place recession probability at approximately 75%, and debate whether investors should buy the dip or wait for clearer policy signals. They also explore the political economy behind tariff support among Americans who feel economically left behind, and close with media consumption and travel observations.

## Key Takeaways

- Both hosts view the tariffs as a self-inflicted economic wound, likening them to the worst policy mistake in 95 years and comparing the execution to the Luca Doncic-for-Anthony Davis trade.
- Recession probability has risen sharply to an estimated 60-75%, with Larry Fink and CEO surveys already describing conditions as recessionary.
- The stock market's behavior—sharp drops followed by extreme intraday volatility on rumors of a 90-day pause—reflects binary outcomes: either a resolution lifts markets sharply or tariffs as proposed could push the S&P 500 down 30-50%.
- Historically, the S&P 500 bottoms on average 9 months before earnings trough and 5 months before GDP trough, arguing for buying panic even when macro news worsens.
- Retail investors demonstrated classic 'buy the dip' behavior: VTI saw one of its largest inflows in three years, and Bank of America recorded the fourth-largest client equity inflows in its data history.
- The societal frustration driving tariff support is real and rooted in deindustrialization, even if the policy remedy is economically counterproductive.
- Automated, pre-committed investment plans (401k, IRA, brokerage auto-invest) allowed the hosts to stay the course during the selloff without emotional decision-making.

## Detailed Notes

### Tariff Policy Critique and Economic Context [00:01:34]

Hosts open with Cullen Roche's data showing US household net worth at record highs, real GDP at all-time highs, and inflation-adjusted median income up 50% since 1975, arguing the narrative that America is being exploited is misleading. Jeremy Siegel called this the biggest policy mistake in 95 years, comparable to 1930s Smoot-Hawley tariffs, made worse by how globally interconnected supply chains now are. The tariff formula was criticized as arithmetically flawed and targeting every nation indiscriminately rather than strategically.

### Political Motivations and the Navarro vs. Musk Dynamic [00:05:19]

The hosts dismiss the theory that Trump is engineering a recession to force lower interest rates for debt refinancing, arguing recessions are nonlinear and unemployment reduces Treasury revenues. Elon Musk publicly criticized Peter Navarro while privately appealing to Trump, but the market-focused advisers appeared to be losing out to the hardline trade faction. The hosts characterize Trump's approach as 'Russian roulette, not chess,' believing the tariffs reflect genuine ideology rather than negotiating posture.

### Market Reaction: Historical Context and the Fake-News Rally [00:37:26]

The two-day post-announcement decline ranked among the 11 worst three-day S&P performances ever, with mega-caps Apple (-16%), Nvidia (-15%), and Boeing (-19%) hit hardest. An anonymous Twitter account, Walter Bloomberg, misread a CNBC Q&A and posted a false 90-day tariff pause, briefly adding 1-2 trillion dollars in market cap within minutes before the report was debunked, illustrating extreme market sensitivity to policy headlines. VIX spiked above 45, and historical data shows the S&P 500 positive 82% of the time 12 months after such spikes.

### Investor Behavior and Buy-the-Dip Thesis [00:46:07]

Despite deep uncertainty, retail flows were strongly positive: VTI recorded one of its three-year largest inflows and Bank of America reported the fourth-largest US equity client inflows since 2008. Both hosts bought during the selloff, acknowledging they may be early given that a 30-40% further decline remains plausible if tariffs are not reversed. They emphasize that the stock market historically bottoms months before economic data improves, so waiting for macro clarity typically means missing the recovery.

### Structural Economic Concerns and Deindustrialization Context [00:25:45]

A listener email from a Quad Cities resident crystallizes the frustration of Americans who feel economically displaced: high-skill workers can succeed, but community life, fertility rates, and manufacturing employment have hollowed out. The hosts empathize with the sentiment while maintaining that blanket tariffs will raise prices and reduce competition rather than restore factory jobs, especially as AI and robotics make manufacturing employment structurally declining regardless of trade policy.
