# Eff You Money | AS 391

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 391, recorded December 19, 2024, is a wide-ranging finance and culture discussion hosted by Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson. The core financial topics include whether the U.S. can sustain a third consecutive year of strong equity gains, the structural shift away from traditional boom-bust business cycles, and a detailed examination of John Hussman's decade-plus bearish record. The hosts also explore the concept of 'eff you money'—the optimal level of wealth that provides freedom without becoming a psychological burden—anchored around a $10 million threshold. Lighter segments cover retail trader behavioral patterns, chart crimes involving inflation comparisons, market internals weakness in energy and materials, and pop culture recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500 posted back-to-back gains near 25% in 2023 and 2024, a historically rare event that sets uncertain odds for 2025.
- John Hussman has maintained a bearish stance since approximately 2011, and his fund has been essentially flat since inception in 2000 despite managing billions at peak.
- A Purdue University study found retail traders on StockTwits systematically lost money on stocks they identified as technically bullish and would have profited by holding those they identified as bearish.
- Removing Nvidia alone from the S&P 500 since October 2022 would have put European equities ahead of the U.S. index, illustrating extreme single-stock concentration.
- The hosts argue that 'eff you money' has an optimal range around $10 million, above which wealth tends to become a source of stress rather than freedom.
- Corporate balance sheets are lower leverage, higher credit quality, and more asset-light than in prior decades, potentially dampening the severity of future recessions.
- Recession consensus among economists has moved to zero globally for 2025, a complete reversal from prior years of persistent calls for downturns.

## Detailed Notes

### 2025 Market Outlook and Historical Parallels [00:01:08]

Batnick and Carlson examine the setup for 2025 after what appears to be back-to-back roughly 25% S&P 500 gains. Historical precedents for back-to-back 25%+ years are rare—occurring in the 1930s, 1950s, and 1990s—with highly variable outcomes in the subsequent year ranging from minus 35% to plus 53%. Michael predicts a strong first half, a volatility spike in the second half, and a net gain of around 3% for the year.

### John Hussman and the Persistent Bear Syndrome [00:11:54]

The hosts detail how Hussman Strategic Growth Fund avoided losses in 2008 and the dot-com bust, attracting billions in AUM, but has since been persistently bearish. The fund has returned roughly 3% total since inception, while the S&P 500 is up nearly 500% since 2012. Batnick cites Jesse Livermore's critique of Hussman's data-torturing models and notes Hussman changed his model's time horizon and fund name to accommodate the extended bull market.

### Market Internals and Sector Weakness [00:29:39]

Batnick flags unusual internal weakness: value stocks have declined for 10 consecutive days (fewer than 10 occurrences since 1926), the equal-weight S&P (RSP) has closed below its open for approximately 12 straight days, and energy (XLE), materials, and healthcare are severely underperforming on a relative basis. He notes this is a cautionary signal even if energy is only about 3% of the S&P by market cap.

### Eff You Money: Optimal Wealth Level [00:43:14]

Prompted by CEOs bending the knee to president-elect Trump, the hosts discuss how extreme wealth can remove rather than grant personal freedom due to stakeholder obligations. Carlson argues $10 million is roughly the sweet spot based on client experience—enough to live well without accumulating obligations that create new stresses. Stuart Butterfield's three levels of wealth (no debt stress, don't care about restaurant prices, don't care what a vacation costs) are cited as a practical framework.

### Structural Economy Changes and Recession Probability [00:03:19]

The hosts discuss Rick Rieder's view that the physical-to-digital transformation has changed the business cycle, making traditional inventory-driven recessions less likely. They also reference Torsten Slok's 2025 outlook describing the economy as 'firing on all cylinders,' with personal consumption rising as a share of GDP and corporate profits near all-time highs. Economists globally now forecast zero countries to enter recession in 2025, a complete reversal from prior years.

### Retail Trader Behavior and Social Media Influence [00:27:33]

A Purdue University study analyzing 77 million StockTwits messages and correlating them with Robinhood trading data found that stocks retail traders identified as technically bullish systematically lost money, while those they marked as bearish gained. Over 10 years, retail technical analysis buyers lost nearly 40% of initial investment and would have gained 30% holding their sells. The hosts attribute this partly to chasing stocks that had already rallied.

### Chart Crimes and Inflation Analogies [00:33:49]

Batnick calls out a Deutsche Bank chart comparing 1970s inflation to current inflation as a chart crime, noting the axes are truncated to make the similarity appear more dramatic. A corrected version with proper scaling still shows some similarity but far less alarming. The broader point is that visual data manipulation is common and readers should scrutinize axis scales.
