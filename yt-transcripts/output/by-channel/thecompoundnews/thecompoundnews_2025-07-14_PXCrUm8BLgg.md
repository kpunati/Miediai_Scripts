# Stablecoins Explode Across the Financial System | LFTC

**Channel:** The Compound

## Summary

Sam Broner of a16z crypto joins Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to explain why stablecoins have become a hot Wall Street investment theme following Circle's explosive IPO. Broner defines fiat-reserve stablecoins as dollar-denominated assets backed by short-dated U.S. treasuries that can be transferred near-instantly for fractions of a cent, contrasting this with traditional correspondent banking that takes three to seven days and levies opaque fees. The discussion covers the three-wave adoption story (technology, integration, regulation), the Genius Act's treatment of stablecoin yields, the threat to incumbent payment rails, the dollar-penetration dynamic in high-inflation economies, and the systemic risk Broner sees as insufficient collaboration between stablecoin infrastructure and traditional finance.

## Key Takeaways

- Fiat-reserve stablecoins like USDC and USDT are backed 1:1 by short-dated U.S. Treasuries and can settle near-instantly for less than a cent, addressing long-standing friction in global payments.
- Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, MoneyGram, and JP Morgan all announced stablecoin initiatives in spring 2025, signaling mainstream financial system adoption.
- If stablecoins grow 10x, they could become one of the largest buyers of short-dated U.S. treasuries, with meaningful implications for the treasury market.
- Western Union and legacy remittance providers face existential disruption as startups using stablecoin rails are growing remittance volumes 10-30% per month.
- The Genius Act, as proposed, would prevent stablecoins from bearing native yield, though Broner argues this is not a significant limitation given the ease of routing into DeFi yield products.
- Broner's primary systemic risk concern is insufficient collaboration between stablecoin startups and incumbent financial institutions, not a collapse of the banking system.
- Circle's IPO was dramatically underpriced by underwriters, reflecting Wall Street's earlier underestimation of demand for crypto infrastructure investment vehicles.

## Detailed Notes

### What Stablecoins Are and Why Now [00:02:27]

Sam Broner defines fiat-reserve stablecoins as money stored on a blockchain but backed by U.S. treasuries or cash equivalents, making them functionally similar to a money market fund balance. Three adoption waves are identified: a technology improvement 18 months prior that brought transaction costs below a cent and settlement times below one second; integration of stablecoin infrastructure into fintech apps; and recent U.S. regulatory progress under what is referred to as 'crypto week' in Congress.

### Use Cases: Remittances and B2B Cross-Border Payments [00:11:25]

A Mexican garment manufacturer paying a Vietnamese textile supplier today routes through multiple correspondent banks, takes three to seven days, and incurs opaque foreign exchange fees. Using USDC, the same transaction can settle in one second for one cent. Remittance companies built on stablecoin rails are growing 10-30% per month; traditional remittance fees average 6.65% versus near-zero for stablecoins, representing a meaningful cost savings for low-income senders.

### Dollar Penetration and Geopolitical Dimension [00:07:27]

Approximately 750 million people live in high-inflation economies and structurally prefer dollar savings. Mass stablecoin adoption effectively exports U.S. dollars digitally, increasing demand for short-dated U.S. treasuries that back these coins. Broner expects local-currency stablecoins to coexist with USD stablecoins rather than full dollarization, as countries retain interest in controlling monetary policy for local supply shocks.

### Circle IPO, Visa/Mastercard, and Investment Landscape [00:20:36]

Circle's IPO saw the stock run dramatically above its offer price, surprising underwriters and reflecting strong investor demand for crypto infrastructure exposure. Broner expects most fintechs to become stablecoin companies over time because stablecoin rails allow global day-one launch and faster product development than legacy banking integration. Visa and Mastercard's existing merchant networks are valuable but could face margin compression as take rates fall, though Broner believes the total addressable market will expand to offset per-transaction fee declines.

### Regulatory Framework and Systemic Risk [00:39:21]

The Genius Act would separate stablecoin activity from banking and restrict native yield on payment stablecoins, though Broner does not see this as a major barrier given easy composability with DeFi yield protocols. The biggest systemic risk he identifies is that traditional payment incumbents may not adopt stablecoin infrastructure fast enough, leading to fragmented reinvention rather than a coordinated upgrade of global payment rails.
