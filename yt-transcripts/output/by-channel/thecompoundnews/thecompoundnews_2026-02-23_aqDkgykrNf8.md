# Big Tech Has Til Year-End…or Else.

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=aqDkgykrNf8
**Duration:** 27:01

## Summary

Nick Colas of DataTrek Research argues that Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta have fundamentally transformed from capital-light businesses into capital-intensive enterprises comparable to automotive manufacturers, with CapEx-to-cash-flow ratios exceeding 100% in 2026. The bull case for US equities rests entirely on these AI investments generating demonstrable ROI within 12 months, or investors face a structurally compelling reason to rotate to international equities. Jessica Rabe presents data showing the S&P 500 carries a 17-percentage-point overweight to technology versus ACWX, and that the index's long-run outperformance depends on Big Tech's ability to prove AI monetization. A recent 11-percentage-point outperformance by non-US stocks over 100 days represents two to three standard deviations, signaling unusual and potentially durable capital rotation.

## Key Takeaways

- Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta now allocate 100% or more of operating cash flow to CapEx, a dramatic shift from 44% in 2023, making their investment thesis more similar to capital-intensive industrials than traditional software platforms.
- Asset efficiency (revenue divided by PP&E) for the three hyperscalers will fall 42% from 2023 levels by 2026, with Alphabet expected to be half as efficient as it was just three years prior.
- Ford Motor Company's revenue-to-PP&E ratio now exceeds that of any major Big Tech hyperscaler, illustrating how dramatically the sector's capital intensity has shifted.
- The S&P 500's 17-percentage-point tech overweight versus ACWX means that if AI investments fail to deliver, US large-cap equity outperformance over the next decade is mathematically at risk.
- An agency problem exists between Big Tech management and shareholders: management faces existential competitive risk from under-investing, while shareholders can simply diversify into international equities.
- Non-US stocks must be bought at a two-to-three standard deviation outperformance level, making timing of any rotation into international equity challenging for new entrants.
- Big Tech's current high P/E multiples (25-30x) implicitly validate continued high capex, as investors are paying for future AI monetization; management can cite valuations as a mandate to keep spending.

## Detailed Notes

### Big Tech's Capex Transformation [00:02:58]

Nick Colas applies automotive-sector capital analysis to Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta, noting that these companies have shifted from high-margin, capital-light models to capital-intensive businesses. The revenue-to-PP&E ratio averaged 2.2x in 2023 across the three companies but will fall 42% by 2026, with Ford now showing a higher ratio than any of the hyperscalers.

### CapEx as a Percentage of Cash Flow [00:10:05]

CapEx as a share of operating cash flow has risen from 44% in 2023 to 50%, then 70%, and now to 103-106% or more for Alphabet and Meta in 2026. The companies effectively surveyed their budget departments for projected cash flow and allocated the entire amount to AI infrastructure, leaving no margin of safety.

### Margin Compression and the Investment Test [00:06:45]

Operating profit margins for the group are expected to fall five percentage points to approximately 34% in 2026. Colas argues that the only way to validate the CapEx is incremental margin improvement beginning in 2027; revenue growth alone will not satisfy investors without accompanying profitability evidence.

### The Agency Problem Between Management and Shareholders [00:09:03]

A subtle agency conflict exists: shareholders can own diversified portfolios and are agnostic on which company wins AI; management faces an existential risk from falling behind competitors. High current valuations (25-30x earnings) effectively mandate continued spending, as management can argue the stock price implies investor endorsement of the AI bet.

### International Equity Rotation and the ACWX Alternative [00:13:15]

Jessica Rabe frames the choice for equity investors as remaining overweight US large-cap tech or rotating to ACWX, which has a 17-percentage-point underweight to technology and greater exposure to financials, industrials, and materials. The S&P's long-run outperformance over three-, five-, and ten-year periods by four to six-and-a-half percentage points is structurally dependent on Big Tech's AI bet paying off.

### Statistical Significance of the Recent International Outperformance [00:16:48]

Non-US stocks recently outperformed US large caps by 11 percentage points on a trailing 100-day basis, a two-to-three standard deviation event. Rabe notes that international outperformance tends to be mean-reverting but can run for extended cycles, and investors entering after such an extreme move face unfavorable timing.
