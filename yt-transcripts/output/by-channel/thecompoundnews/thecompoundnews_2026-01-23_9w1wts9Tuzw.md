# Stop Ruining My Perfectly Good Bear Market | TCAF 226

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=9w1wts9Tuzw
**Duration:** 84:17

## Summary

In this 84-minute episode of The Compound and Friends, Jeremy Grantham joins Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to discuss his autobiography 'The Making of a Perma Bear,' tracing his career from early indexing pioneer and quant innovator to his well-known bubble calls in Japan, the dot-com era, and the post-2009 market. Grantham argues the current bull market is unprecedented, characterized by four distinct phases—post-crisis bounce, buyback era, COVID stimulus, and AI capex—that have collectively suppressed mean reversion for over 15 years. He identifies structural forces—monopoly creep, Citizens United, and outsourcing—as drivers of a permanent shift in profit margins and deepening wealth inequality, and closes with cautious optimism about geothermal energy and nuclear fusion as potential civilizational solutions.

## Key Takeaways

- Grantham distinguishes himself from 'perma bears' by pointing to periods of outright bullishness (e.g., his 2018 melt-up call and GMO's emerging markets position in the 2000s), arguing his real flaw was a time horizon too long for client patience.
- The AI theme interrupted what Grantham believed was a developing bear market in 2022, taking the Shiller PE from a world-record level down only partway before reversing, leaving valuations still deeply overpriced in his view.
- The bubble in AI is structural—billions in Nvidia chips purchased without yet generating revenue—rather than primarily reflected in stretched PE multiples, which Grantham regards as a classic bubble signature.
- Decades of antitrust inaction, Citizens United, and global labor arbitrage have allowed monopoly concentration to permanently elevate corporate profit margins, breaking the historical mean-reversion pattern Grantham long relied on.
- Demographic decline is accelerating far faster than projections across China, South Korea, Japan, and now globally, with potential long-term drag on animal spirits and GDP growth that markets are not pricing.
- Grantham is most optimistic about geothermal energy, arguing that drilling technology from the fracking revolution can be transferred to geothermal and could expand the viable resource from roughly 1% to 13% of the planet's surface within a decade or two.
- Energy storage costs have fallen approximately 95% over 20 years, with expert forecasters underestimating the improvement for 17 consecutive years, pointing to continued deflationary trajectory for renewables.

## Detailed Notes

### The 'Perma Bear' Label and Career Retrospective [00:02:05]

Grantham explains the title of his book is partly ironic: the perma-bear label sticks when a bubble outlasts investors' patience, which he estimates at roughly two years. He recounts being bearish well before the 2000 dot-com peak, losing half GMO's AUM during the late-1990s bubble run-up, then making money in 2001-2002 but failing to recover those clients. He frames his core flaw as having a time horizon—several years—that fundamentally mismatched the market's shorter-term dynamics.

### The Four-Phase Bull Market Since 2009 [00:06:24]

Josh Brown proposes that the post-2009 bull market is actually four overlapping cycles: the crisis bounce, the buyback era, COVID stimulus, and AI capex. Grantham endorses this framing, noting that the third and fourth phases entered bubble territory. He observes that AI specifically arrived just as the 2022 bear market was gaining traction, lifting only the Mag 7 for much of 2023 before broadening, and resetting the Shiller PE from a record high to merely 'badly overpriced' rather than back to fair value.

### Bubble Anatomy and the AI Capex Cycle [00:08:14]

Grantham describes a recurring pattern at bubble peaks: speculative fringe stocks peak and fall before blue chips, citing 1929, 2000, and QuantumScape in late 2020 as examples. He argues the current AI bubble is genuine—comparable in historical significance to the railroads—but unusual in that the bubble is in capital expenditure (massive chip purchases with no revenue) rather than in stretched PE multiples, since companies like Nvidia at $4.5 trillion already trade at ~54x earnings.

### Profit Margins, Monopoly, and Wealth Inequality [00:49:55]

Grantham acknowledges that the long-expected mean reversion of profit margins—a cornerstone of his investment framework—has not materialized in the 21st century. He attributes this to: reduced antitrust enforcement allowing industry concentration to rise in every sector; Citizens United enabling the wealthy to capture regulatory and legislative outcomes; and global outsourcing compressing labor's share of income. He notes real wages for the median US worker are up only ~15% since 1975 versus ~140% for the average French worker, and warns that financially squeezed lower-income households defaulting on auto loans represent a potential systemic crack.

### Demographic Decline and Long-Term Growth Headwinds [01:06:00]

Grantham presents accelerating fertility decline as a structural risk that markets and policymakers are not adequately pricing. Japan's 20-year-old workforce cohort is 50% of its 1948 peak; China's total population began declining ahead of schedule; and global annual births have fallen from ~150 million to ~137-138 million over 14 years. He argues that societies experiencing multi-decade population decline lose the animal spirits needed for risk-taking and investment, using Japan's technology leadership in the 1980s versus its current competence-without-ambition as an illustration.

### Geothermal, Fusion, and Renewable Storage as Sources of Optimism [01:14:23]

Grantham identifies geothermal energy as the most actionable near-term opportunity, arguing that horizontal drilling techniques developed for fracking can be transferred to capture deep heat, potentially expanding viable geothermal territory from ~1% to ~13% of the planet's surface. He also highlights rapid and underestimated progress in nuclear fusion and the ~95% cost decline in renewable energy storage over 20 years. His Grantham Foundation is actively investing in geothermal. He frames cheap, infinite green energy combined with declining population as potentially providing a path through the resource and climate constraints that have historically brought down civilizations.
