# Never Pay Off Your Mortgage | Animal Spirits 436

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=04j_d4nCUu4
**Duration:** 75:01

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 436 features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson reviewing the dramatic 2025 stock market recovery from April lows, noting that technical indicators like the breadth thrust signaled the rebound accurately. The hosts debate whether AI-driven capital expenditures represent a true bubble, drawing parallels to the dot-com era while acknowledging the superior fundamentals of today's mega-cap technology companies. Discussion also covers systemic risks building in private credit markets via private letter ratings and insurance company exposure, and the duo debates the personal finance merits of paying off a low-rate mortgage early.

## Key Takeaways

- The 2025 April drawdown recovery was historically strong, with the S&P up ~24% in six months following a breadth thrust signal that has been 100% accurate since 1950.
- Narrow market leadership with very few stocks beating the index resembles 1998-1999 conditions, though the hosts note this pattern typically coincides with strong bull markets rather than warning of imminent collapse.
- AI capital expenditure is likely a capex bubble, but the hosts argue it differs from dot-com because the underlying companies have real earnings and lower relative valuations.
- Private credit markets show a growing use of private letter ratings from small agencies rather than major credit raters, raising systemic risk concerns flagged by the IMF and FT.
- Despite declining mortgage rates, housing markets are not showing meaningful pickup in activity, with the hosts arguing lower prices are more necessary than lower rates.
- Private credit is described as the easiest sell to retiring baby boomers seeking yield, but retail investor demand via RIAs appears overstated by asset managers.
- The mortgage payoff debate illustrates a broader theme: mathematically suboptimal financial decisions can be valid personal finance choices when psychological benefits outweigh the spread.

## Detailed Notes

### Stock Market Recovery and Breadth Thrust [00:00:35]

The hosts open by reviewing the year-to-date returns chart showing the S&P was down 15% as of April 8th before recovering to gains. A breadth thrust triggered on April 24th led to the fourth-best six-month return in history at 24%, and all prior signals since 1950 have been followed by positive returns six months and one year later. The hosts note that most contrarian indicators were flashing green coming out of April but many investors dismissed them as unreliable in a new environment.

### Narrow Market Leadership and Stock Concentration [00:05:49]

A chart showing the number of stocks outperforming the S&P 500 on a calendar-year basis reveals concentration near historical lows, similar to 1998-1999. The hosts observe that the lowest stock-outperformance counts coincide with strong bull markets like 2013-2017, while the highest counts occur during down markets like 2022. Active managers' track records are argued to be understated due to trading activity, though picking outperforming managers is harder than picking outperforming stocks.

### AI Capex Bubble Debate [00:25:32]

Referencing Kai Wu's research paper, the hosts discuss AI capex as a percentage of GDP, depreciation-adjusted, which shows levels comparable to or exceeding the railroad and internet eras. The Mag Seven's ROIC of 22.5% versus 6% for the rest of the S&P 493 is highlighted as historically unprecedented. The hosts draw a distinction between a price bubble (dot-com era, 80%+ declines) and a capex bubble, arguing current valuations are more reasonable and a potential washout would be less severe. Andrej Karpathy's podcast view that AI will maintain the same 2% GDP growth trajectory is discussed with skepticism.

### Private Credit and Systemic Risk [00:43:59]

Citing a Wall Street Journal article and FT reporting, the hosts cover private credit's expansion into 401k plans and insurance company portfolios. The FT piece highlights that most private credit classified as investment grade by insurers uses private letter ratings from small agencies rather than Moody's, S&P, or Fitch. Global Atlantic (KKR-owned) has 25% of its $100 billion portfolio in private letter-rated securities. The hosts note potential misclassification risks flagged by the IMF, though they express confidence systemic players are too interconnected to be allowed to fail.

### Housing Market and Mortgage Debate [00:41:19]

Neil Dutta data shows mortgage purchase applications slid 3% despite declining mortgage rates from 8% to 6%, and Carlson argues lower home prices are more important than lower rates. The hosts debate whether increased housing activity would lead to lower prices, not higher, as the true market-clearing price is revealed. The viral tweet about paying off a 2.625% mortgage generates debate about mathematical suboptimality versus psychological value of debt freedom.

### Economy, Tariffs, and Consumer Spending [00:17:23]

Goldman's analysis shows US consumers absorbing 37% of tariff costs currently versus businesses eating 51%, with a projected long-run consumer share of 55%. The hosts discuss how big companies are using AI as cover to reduce headcounts, predicting overcorrection and subsequent rehiring. Americans now spend $108 per week on weekday lunches, up from $88 the prior year, and food-away-from-home spending has skyrocketed versus food at home, illustrating consumer behavior persistence despite inflation complaints.
