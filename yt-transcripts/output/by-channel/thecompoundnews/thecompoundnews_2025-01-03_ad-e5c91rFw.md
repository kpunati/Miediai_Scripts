# Why Brian Wesbury is Bearish on 2025 | TCAF 172

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ad-e5c91rFw
**Duration:** 89:27

## Summary

In this 89-minute episode of The Compound and Friends, Brian Wesbury, Chief Economist at First Trust Advisors, lays out his bearish case for 2025. He contends that post-COVID fiscal and monetary stimulus functioned like morphine — masking economic pain while creating structural distortions that have yet to fully unwind. Wesbury argues that a capitalized profits model now signals overvaluation for the first time in 15 years, driven by multiple expansion rather than earnings growth, and that unsustainable government spending and job creation are propping up GDP at the expense of long-run health. He also addresses Bitcoin, Federal Reserve accountability, Modern Monetary Theory, and the cultural value of leadership institutions like the Boy Scouts.

## Key Takeaways

- The U.S. equity market is overvalued per Wesbury's capitalized profits model, primarily due to multiple expansion rather than earnings growth, with the S&P 500 up ~62% in two years while earnings rose only ~5%.
- A recession is likely in 2025 according to Wesbury, driven by withdrawal from fiscal stimulus and an unsustainable composition of job growth dominated by government and healthcare sectors.
- The 30% spike in M2 money supply growth in 2020-2021 was the primary cause of subsequent inflation, yet the Federal Reserve rarely acknowledges or discusses the money supply in public communications.
- Nvidia's continued earnings beats are critical to sustaining market enthusiasm, but hyperscaler AI capex spending could slow if revenue payoff disappoints, creating a chain reaction risk.
- The Treasury General Account holds approximately $750 billion, functioning as an off-M2 reserve that allowed the Treasury to participate in monetary policy alongside the Fed — a historically unprecedented arrangement.
- Wesbury argues that Bitcoin and gold standards would constrain government borrowing by eliminating the ability to fund deficits through quantitative easing.
- Agentic AI is expected to materially reduce white-collar employment in 2025, raising questions about whether productivity gains will offset job losses fast enough to avoid demand contraction.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Wesbury Turned Bearish and the Capitalized Profits Model [00:11:38]

Wesbury describes turning bearish roughly a year before this episode after being a consistent bull for 15 years. He uses a capitalized profits model that discounts NIPA-based corporate profits by the 10-year Treasury yield; the model now shows the S&P 500 is overvalued for the first time since the model became bullish. He acknowledges AI and Nvidia explain part of the overvaluation and that earnings-driven bulls could still be right if AI productivity materializes.

### Fiscal Deficits, Government Job Growth, and Recession Risk [00:28:27]

Wesbury presents a chart showing government jobs growing faster than private-sector jobs since 2022-2023, tied directly to $2 trillion annual deficits. He argues roughly half of GDP growth over the past two years stems from government spending, and that unwinding this stimulus — whether through DOGE cuts or natural expiration — could directly cause a recession. He uses New York's Medicaid home-care program as an illustrative example of fiscally-driven job creation that would not exist in a balanced-budget environment.

### M2 Money Supply, Inflation, and Federal Reserve Accountability [00:44:13]

A chart of year-over-year M2 growth going back to 1960 shows a spike to 30% that Wesbury calls the obvious cause of subsequent inflation. He is sharply critical of the Fed's dismissal of money supply as a relevant variable, noting Powell told Senator Kennedy that we should 'unlearn' what we know about M2. Wesbury argues the Fed has moved to an 'abundant reserves' model that effectively makes it a proto-national bank, with its balance sheet growing from $800 billion to over $7 trillion.

### Nvidia, AI Capex, and Market Concentration Risk [00:24:16]

Wesbury identifies Nvidia's late-February earnings report as the biggest near-term market risk, not because it will miss but because the bar for upside surprises keeps rising. Microsoft, the top Nvidia customer through its OpenAI relationship, was the worst-performing Mag-7 stock in 2024, suggesting AI capex spending does not automatically reward shareholders. He warns of a potential chain reaction if hyperscalers slow investment, which would hit Nvidia last — as the 'caboose' of AI spending.

### Bitcoin, Modern Monetary Theory, and the Treasury General Account [01:06:21]

Wesbury discusses Bitcoin ownership distribution including ETF holders and Satoshi's alleged holdings, and argues Bitcoin adoption is partly a response to monetary debasement. He rejects Modern Monetary Theory, arguing that inflation itself disproves the claim that governments can borrow and print indefinitely without consequence. He describes the Treasury General Account — currently at ~$750 billion — as a mechanism that removes money from M2 and enables the Treasury to function as a quasi-monetary authority, which he views as historically unprecedented and problematic.

### DOGE, Federal Budget Trajectory, and Historical Parallels [00:36:50]

Wesbury presents a chart of historical budget deficits showing the last two years' deficits of 6.2% and 6.4% of GDP are the largest outside of World War II, the 2008 crisis, and COVID. He is skeptical but hopeful that DOGE can meaningfully cut spending, citing the Reagan-era Grace Commission as a cautionary precedent where 2,000 spending cut proposals yielded little. He predicts Trump would likely abandon DOGE cuts if they triggered a stock market decline or recession.
