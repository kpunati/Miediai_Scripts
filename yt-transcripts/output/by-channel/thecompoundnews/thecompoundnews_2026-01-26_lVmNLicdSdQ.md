# Building the Perfect Portfolio

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lVmNLicdSdQ
**Duration:** 33:56

## Summary

In this episode of Live from the Compound, Josh Brown speaks with Cullen Roche about his new book 'Your Perfect Portfolio,' which surveys over 20 portfolio strategies to help investors find an approach suited to their own behavior and time horizon. Roche argues that the 'perfect' portfolio does not exist; instead, a portfolio investors can stick with through volatile periods is superior to a theoretically optimal one they will abandon. The conversation extends to macro topics including the bifurcated economy, the housing affordability crisis, and the concentration of AI-related wealth.

## Key Takeaways

- There is no universally perfect portfolio; the best portfolio is one an investor can behaviorally sustain through downturns.
- Diversification inherently means tolerating some underperforming positions at all times — 'good diversification is learning to hate some part of your portfolio all the time.'
- Trend-following CTA strategies can provide genuine diversification because they can go long or short and are not structurally correlated to stocks or bonds.
- Investors should think in time horizons: T-bills are predictable over one year, stocks become more reliable over 5-20 year periods but are unpredictable over 12-24 months.
- Real returns after inflation, fees, and taxes are significantly lower than nominal figures suggest, potentially around 4% for equities.
- The housing market is likely to remain sluggish for 5-10 years as wages slow and rental costs creep upward, while AI-driven wealth creates a parallel boom for those exposed.
- Attempting overly complex or concentrated portfolios often makes outcomes worse; simplicity and discipline tend to win over time.

## Detailed Notes

### Introduction and Book Overview [00:02:48]

Cullen Roche introduces 'Your Perfect Portfolio,' explaining it is structured in two parts: ten foundational principles and then analysis of 20+ strategies. Unlike his earlier macro-focused writing, this book aims to be practical and accessible to any investor, presenting each strategy with its origin, historical performance, mechanics, and suitability.

### Key Portfolio Strategies Discussed [00:05:23]

Roche outlines the breadth of strategies covered, including the Warren Buffett approach, 100% stocks, T-bill and chill, 60/40, factor investing, risk parity, the permanent portfolio, Boglehead three-fund, income strategies, trend following, endowment portfolios, and retirement planning. Strategies range from extremely simple (Boglehead) to highly complex (endowment, full risk parity), and Roche intentionally avoids over-complicating things to keep strategies achievable for individual investors.

### Diversification and Behavioral Challenges [00:10:27]

Both Roche and Brown discuss how diversification fails emotionally when correlations between asset classes spike during crises — as experienced in 2022. The permanent portfolio (stocks, long-term bonds, gold, cash) and trend-following CTAs are highlighted as more robust diversifiers. Roche emphasizes that 'perfect is the enemy of the good' and that a portfolio a client cannot endure for five years is, by definition, not perfect.

### Trend Following and Truly Uncorrelated Strategies [00:13:30]

Trend-following CTAs are described as Roche's favorite truly uncorrelated strategy. These funds seek small trends that develop into large moves — currently expressed as overweights in precious metals. After a decade-long underperformance following the GFC, they have re-emerged as strong diversifiers. However, investors must accept periods of apparent underperformance as part of the strategy's nature.

### Macro Outlook: K-Shaped Economy and AI Wealth [00:20:31]

Roche and Brown discuss a bifurcated economic environment where young renters face years of housing unaffordability as prices stagnate while rents creep up, while those exposed to AI and technology assets have experienced transformational wealth accumulation. Roche does not see quick fixes for housing and notes that anyone who has been underweight technology for the past decade has missed a generational return, illustrated by the example of NASDAQ bubble investors still earning roughly 8% annually since 2000.

### Realistic Return Expectations [00:31:00]

Roche deliberately presented all returns in his book in real (inflation-adjusted) terms and after fees and taxes, yielding figures around 4% for equities rather than the commonly cited 10% nominal. This approach is intended to set practical expectations and prevent investors from chasing unrealistic benchmarks such as the Magnificent Seven's recent performance.
