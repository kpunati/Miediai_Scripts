# Cut Your Losses, Size up Your Winners | TCAF 170

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=CuZu_8TSwm4
**Duration:** 79:56

## Summary

Nick Colas of DataTrek Research joins Michael Batnick and Josh Brown to analyze the December 2024 Fed rate cut and the subsequent sharp equity sell-off, explaining why the move was orderly and likely a buyable dip. The conversation covers the extreme bullishness in investor sentiment surveys heading into the event, the structural case for US over international equities, and the long-running dominance of growth over value stocks. Colas also shares investment lessons from his time reporting directly to Steve Cohen at SAC Capital, particularly the discipline of cutting losses quickly and sizing up winning positions.

## Key Takeaways

- The December 2024 post-FOMC sell-off was concentrated in rate-sensitive stocks and prior big winners, not a fundamental deterioration signal.
- A VIX spike to 27.6 has historically been a buying opportunity outside of a confirmed bear market.
- US equities are rational to overweight given that every US sector outperformed the rest of world over the past 10 years except energy and real estate.
- Growth consistently outperforms value since 2008 because high-innovation tech companies generate more frequent and larger earnings surprises.
- Steve Cohen's core trading rule: cut losses immediately and size up winners—fear is the worst trading motivator on both sides of a position.
- Tesla's stock price embeds 92% future growth expectations versus only 68% for Nvidia, making Tesla more of a call option than a traditional equity.
- Bessembinder's research shows just 2.4% of US stocks created all equity market value from 1990 to 2020, justifying a growth-stock concentration approach.

## Detailed Notes

### Fed Reaction and Market Sell-Off [00:11:29]

Colas argues the December FOMC sell-off was orderly and driven by two distinct buckets: rate-sensitive names such as home builders and small caps, and stocks with the largest prior gains that traders sold without needing to explain. Google Trends data showed unusual retail interest in the market into the close, suggesting panic selling by retail investors. The biggest yield jump on an FOMC day since 2013 caused a full reset of 2025 rate-cut expectations, with January cut probability falling below 9%.

### Sentiment Indicators and VIX Context [00:23:00]

The Conference Board survey of respondents expecting stocks to rise in 12 months hit 56%, well above the prior ceiling of 45%, while Shiller's survey showed 45% of investors believe a crash has less than 10% probability in six months. Colas views the sell-off as a healthy reintroduction of risk into an overly euphoric market. The VIX closing at 27.6—exactly one standard deviation above the long-term mean—has historically been followed by S&P gains of 3.5%–5.2% one month later.

### US vs. International Equities [00:34:35]

Every US sector outperformed international peers over 10 years except energy and real estate; the US is now 66% of global market cap. Colas views international exposure as tactical rather than strategic, arguing the key unanswered question is what catalyst could cause international stocks to outperform US stocks over a full year. A scenario where US inflation rebounds and forces rate hikes while Europe slips into deep recession could be one such trigger.

### Growth vs. Value and Market Structure [00:47:09]

Bessembinder's research shows 2.4% of US equities and 1.4% of global equities created all shareholder value from 1990 to 2020, with the top 10 names accounting for 14% of all wealth creation. Colas attributes sustained growth outperformance since 2008 to the accelerating pace of tech innovation making future earnings surprises far more likely in growth names. The extreme value underperformance—down 13 consecutive days at the time of recording—reflects forced redemptions from value managers rather than fundamental deterioration.

### Tesla Valuation and Elon Musk Premium [00:55:34]

Colas frames Tesla as a call option on Elon Musk's ability to influence policy, not a traditional car company. At current prices, 92% of Tesla's value reflects future growth expectations versus 8% from present-value of current earnings; for Nvidia the split is roughly 68%/32%. Traditional automakers like GM and Ford trade at 5x earnings because the market prices them as terminal businesses with no meaningful growth prospects.

### Steve Cohen Trading Principles [01:08:09]

Colas recounts Cohen's approach at SAC Capital: when a trader was down 30% in a day, Cohen would say 'you're making this harder than it has to be,' and the trader would then liquidate the entire book and start fresh. The guiding question was: 'If you could start from zero, would you put that position back on?'—usually the answer was no. SAC traders also met weekly with a psychologist to review every trade, asking why positions weren't cut on losses or added to on winners, to ingrain the discipline of cutting losses and sizing up winners.
