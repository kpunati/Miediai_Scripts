# Hi-Yo Silver! | Animal Spirits 449

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=6VfHAmgosr8
**Duration:** 73:03

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 449 features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examining the divergence between household and government debt trends in the US, with household balance sheets at historic strength despite rising government debt-to-GDP. The hosts discuss 2026's early diversification story where small caps, value, and emerging markets are all outperforming the Mag 7, and explore Jeremy Grantham's long career and why his recent bearishness has been wrong. The episode also covers precious metals, particularly silver's meme-stock-like rally and gold's outperformance of Bitcoin, and touches on K-shaped economic data, private credit media narratives, and prediction markets.

## Key Takeaways

- US household balance sheets are at historic strength, with net worth growing far faster than liabilities since the GFC, creating a wealth effect unlike any previous cycle.
- Early 2026 shows a broadening market where small caps, value stocks, and emerging markets are all outperforming the S&P 500 and Mag 7.
- Jeremy Grantham had a remarkable career calling Japan, dot-com, and GFC bubbles, but his long-running bearishness on US stocks has been wrong because margin mean reversion never happened.
- Silver is up dramatically year-to-date in 2026, outperforming both gold and Bitcoin, which the hosts find surprising given prior expectations that crypto would be the safe-haven play.
- The K-shaped economy is becoming more pronounced, with top 20% earners accounting for nearly 60% of total spending outlays.
- Private credit critics in financial media may be overstating risks; the nature of private credit as buy-and-hold loans means daily mark-to-market volatility is largely irrelevant.
- Prediction markets are seen as here to stay but unlikely to scale as aggressively as current adoption rates suggest, given their zero-sum nature.

## Detailed Notes

### Household vs. Government Debt [00:04:24]

Government debt as a percentage of GDP stands at 121% and rising, but household debt as a percentage of GDP has declined meaningfully over the past 15 years. Matt Klein's analysis shows household assets are 53% higher than end of 2019 while liabilities are only 28% higher, representing $66 trillion of net wealth added — more than three years' worth of personal consumption. The hosts note this wealth-to-liability dynamic is historically unprecedented.

### Market Diversification and Stock Performance [00:08:23]

In early 2026, value stocks, small caps (Russell 2000), and emerging markets are all outperforming the S&P 500, continuing a broadening trend. The Mag 7 has gone essentially sideways for six to seven months while the percentage of stocks above their 50-day moving average is rising. Historical data from Robert Shiller shows that in nearly half of all years since 1930, earnings and stocks have diverged — one up while the other is down.

### Jeremy Grantham Discussion [00:16:52]

Ben Carlson reviewed Grantham's career, noting he correctly called the Japan, dot-com, and GFC bubbles and was an early pioneer of index funds and small cap value investing. His long-running bearishness on US stocks over the past 12-15 years has been wrong primarily because corporate margin mean reversion — a core thesis — never materialized, as the Mag 7 continued expanding profits. The hosts discuss how being right three times can create overconfidence about a fourth call.

### Silver, Gold, and Precious Metals [00:33:26]

Silver surged dramatically in early 2026, described as behaving like a meme stock. Year-to-date silver is up approximately 55%, with historical context showing extreme volatility including a 90% drawdown from 1980 to 2000 and a 66% correction in the 2010s. Gold is up 90% since start of 2025 while Bitcoin is down 4%, surprising the hosts who expected crypto to be the primary beneficiary of dollar weakness and fiscal concerns. Central banks are identified as the largest buyers of gold.

### Economic Indicators and Personal Finance [00:38:52]

Daily and weekly economic indicators from Apollo's tracker show solid restaurant bookings, air travel, and debit card data. Job openings have fallen back below 2019 levels, which is concerning for job seekers, though layoffs remain low. Moody's data shows the top 20% of income earners now account for nearly 60% of total spending outlays, a record K-shaped divergence since 1989. The Kiel Institute estimates that 96% of tariff costs are passed through to US buyers, with $200 billion in 2025 tariff revenue paid almost entirely by Americans.

### Private Credit and Prediction Markets [00:55:53]

Media coverage of BlackRock cutting a private debt fund value and private credit investors cashing out is framed as potentially overhyped; Matt Levine's take is that private credit is inherently buy-and-hold, so marks matter less than for traded assets. The risk emerges when retail investors with shorter time horizons try to add liquidity to an illiquid space. On prediction markets, the hosts view them as here to stay but caution that their zero-sum nature will limit scale, as losers eventually stop participating.
