# The Truth Behind Private Equity’s Megaboom | TCAF 207

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=EyIkATsABXk
**Duration:** 76:16

## Summary

Michael Sidgmore of Broadhaven Ventures and Alt Goes Mainstream discusses the rapid growth of private markets and the structural shift toward democratizing alternatives for wealth management clients. The conversation covers the mechanics of evergreen and interval fund structures, the business case for why large alternative asset managers are targeting the wealth channel, and whether the lack of daily mark-to-market is a feature or a flaw. The episode also examines the wave of consolidation between traditional and alternative asset managers exemplified by the Goldman Sachs and T. Rowe Price partnership and BlackRock's acquisitions of Global Infrastructure Partners and HPS.

## Key Takeaways

- Private markets total approximately $25 trillion in AUM, with private wealth representing 1-3% allocated versus 15-20% for institutions, suggesting massive runway for growth.
- Evergreen and interval fund structures have become the primary vehicle for the wealth channel because they offer continuous investment opportunities and limited redemption windows versus traditional closed-end drawdown funds.
- The T. Rowe Price/Goldman Sachs deal and BlackRock's acquisitions of HPS and Global Infrastructure Partners signal that the convergence between traditional and alternative asset managers is accelerating.
- Private credit growth to $40 trillion is a widely cited long-term target from Apollo's CEO, though rising covenant-light loans and PIK activity are cited as warning signs of overheating.
- The 10% of advisors doing 90% of alternatives flows are disproportionately former wirehouse advisors who built practices around performance pitches rather than financial planning.
- Technology infrastructure platforms like iCapital were essential enablers of the wealth channel buildout by creating straight-through processing and standardized fund access.
- Sports team ownership is emerging as a new private markets asset class, with alternative managers taking minority stakes in franchises like the New York Giants.

## Detailed Notes

### Size and Structure of Private Markets [00:11:14]

Private markets total roughly $25 trillion, encompassing private equity, private credit, hedge funds, infrastructure, secondaries, real estate, and GP stakes. Private equity is the largest sub-class but secondaries are cited as the most likely candidate for a $50 billion fund. Institutions are 15-20% allocated while private wealth sits at only 1-3%, implying trillions of potential inflows.

### Why Alternative Managers Are Targeting Wealth Clients [00:13:01]

Institutional LPs have reached full allocation levels and have increasingly sought co-investment rights and lower fees by bypassing fund structures. Wealth management represents the next major pool of capital, alongside insurance which is also structurally underallocated. The guest acknowledges the cynical read that this is simply about finding new buyers, while also arguing there is genuine economic logic given the concentration and correlation risk in public equity indexes.

### Evergreen Funds and Interval Fund Mechanics [00:28:51]

Traditional closed-end drawdown funds lock capital for 10 years with J-curve dynamics and no redemption. Evergreen funds allow continuous capital deployment and limited redemption windows (typically quarterly for interval funds, or via board-governed tender offers). The structure suits wealth management clients who may need partial liquidity and cannot commit capital in multi-year tranches, and also makes business administration easier for advisors adding new clients.

### Private Credit Growth and Risks [00:41:56]

Private credit has grown from a few hundred billion to approximately $2 trillion and Mark Rowan of Apollo argues it could reach $40 trillion as banks retrench. However, rising covenant-light loan percentages approaching 100% and increased PIK activity are cited as concerning signs. Banks including JPMorgan are beginning to re-enter the space following deregulation, which may compress terms and returns for current private credit managers.

### Asset Manager Consolidation: Goldman/T. Rowe Price and BlackRock [00:45:37]

Goldman Sachs agreed to purchase up to $1 billion of T. Rowe Price stock and the two firms will co-distribute private market products to retail investors, advisors, and plan participants. BlackRock's Larry Fink declared the firm has fundamentally transformed itself through acquisitions of Global Infrastructure Partners, HPS, and Prequin. The pattern reflects traditional managers acquiring alternative distribution and alternative managers acquiring traditional distribution capability.

### Brand, Culture, and the Image Problem [01:01:05]

A viral Johnny Hillbrandt parody Instagram character lampoons private equity culture, prompting discussion of whether the industry has a real image problem. Sidgmore argues the authentic leaders such as David Rubenstein and John Gray who participate in unscripted media are winning with the advisor audience, while scripted PR-driven appearances are losing. Josh Brown counters that advisors primarily care about brand recognition and price rather than corporate culture statements.
