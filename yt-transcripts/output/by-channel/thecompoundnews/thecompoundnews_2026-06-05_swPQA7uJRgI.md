# The Recession Signals Are All Wrong | TCAF 245

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-06-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=swPQA7uJRgI
**Duration:** 69:53

## Summary

Episode 245 of The Compound and Friends brings together macroeconomists Neil Dutta and Skanda Amarnath to assess an economy defined by a historic AI investment boom alongside a sluggish labor market and rising inflation pressures. The guests debate whether traditional recession indicators remain useful when non-residential business investment — not housing — is driving the cycle, and highlight the unusual divergence between soaring corporate profits and stagnant worker compensation. Fed policy under incoming Chair Kevin Warsh is examined, with the guests split on whether supply-driven inflation warrants rate hikes or patience. The conversation concludes that credit spreads and corporate willingness to sustain AI capex spending are the key variables to watch for cycle turn signals.

## Key Takeaways

- AI capital expenditure has eclipsed the 2005 housing bubble peak as a share of US GDP, fundamentally altering traditional business cycle dynamics.
- The labor market is stabilizing rather than deteriorating, but nominal wage growth remains well below nominal GDP growth, creating stress for household spending over time.
- Labor share of GDP has hit a post-1947 low while profit share is at a post-1950 high, a divide that is shaping political sentiment and policy debates.
- Industrial companies like Caterpillar, Vertiv, and GE Vernova now trade like semiconductors because their order books are dominated by AI data center capex.
- The S&P 493 is outperforming the Mag 7 year-to-date, but the broadening may reflect AI capex diffusion rather than a classic healthy-breadth signal.
- Scanda Amarnath believes the Fed will hike 75-100 basis points because persistent supply-side inflation will be misread as demand-driven, while Neil Dutta is more dovish given weak labor income.
- Credit spreads and sustained spending by non-hyperscaler companies on AI are identified as the leading indicators most likely to signal a genuine cycle turn.

## Detailed Notes

### Economic Overview: Soggy Labor Market Meets Inflationary Pressures [00:10:08]

Scanda Amarnath describes the macro picture as one where the distribution of risks has shifted: inflation is trending higher (not just due to oil), while the labor market, though not in the red, is signaling solid rather than strong growth. Neil Dutta agrees the labor market has improved from late-2025 lows but notes total wage and salary growth remains sluggish relative to the overnight rate and price inflation, creating building stress for households. Both guests express surprise that the consumer has held up given a housing market that remains largely frozen.

### The AI Investment Boom and GDP Accounting [00:16:37]

Skanda Amarnath notes that AI-related investment (tech equipment, software, industrial equipment) has ripped past 7% of GDP, exceeding the peak residential investment share during the 2005 housing bubble, the 1990s dotcom boom, and the telecom structures boom. Neil Dutta highlights that a portion of AI spending leaks abroad via imports (TSMC, Japanese and Korean hardware suppliers), partially suppressing the US GDP figure, but argues this misses the broader economic linkages: RSU vesting income for tech workers, the wealth effect on consumer spending, and the impact on municipal tax revenues. The guests agree GDP accounting understates the true economic footprint of the boom.

### Market Breadth and Industrial Stocks as AI Proxies [00:37:32]

Neil Dutta's research shows that 15 non-tech S&P 500 companies with roughly $2 trillion in market cap now exhibit correlations of 0.5 or higher with the semiconductor ETF SMH, with 12 of the 15 being industrials — Caterpillar (backup generators and engines for hyperscalers), Vertiv (cooling and power management), Eaton (components), and GE Vernova (gas turbines for data center power). Michael Batnick highlights that the S&P 493 is up 12.6% year-to-date versus 7.3% for the Mag 7, though guests caution this may reflect AI capex diffusion rather than traditional broad-market health.

### Corporate Profits vs. Worker Pay: A Historic Divide [00:45:03]

The hosts discuss a Greg Ip Wall Street Journal piece citing Q1 data showing labor share of gross domestic income at 51% — the lowest since 1947 — and profit share at 12.1%, the highest since 1950. Worker compensation grew just 0.8% in Q1 while domestic corporate profits jumped 2.7%. Amarnath attributes part of the divergence to tax code incentives favoring capital over W2 income and the rise of superstar firms with persistently high margins; Dutta adds that nominal GDP is growing around 6% while nominal wage and salary growth is running well below that, a historically unusual disconnect.

### Fed Policy Under Kevin Warsh [00:50:24]

Neil Dutta expresses skepticism about Kevin Warsh's credibility, noting his Fed tenure was characterized by philosophical speeches rather than substantive policy analysis, and that his pivot to data-dependency coincided with his public campaign for the Chair role. Skanda Amarnath argues Warsh cannot unilaterally dictate policy given the committee voting structure, and notes four dissents at the most recent meeting. Amarnath predicts the Fed will hike 75-100 basis points because persistent supply-driven inflation will be misread as requiring demand restraint, while Dutta is more dovish given weak labor income and sluggish residential investment.

### Labor Market Signals and Cycle Turn Indicators [00:58:44]

A listener question prompts discussion of the 7.6 million job openings figure, with Neil Dutta dismissing openings as an unreliable signal and preferring hires and quits data. Amarnath notes stabilization in professional and business services hiring as a positive sign, attributing a three-year white-collar tech-session to pandemic overhiring and AI uncertainty rather than fundamental weakness. Both guests agree that layoffs only become a reliable signal when a downturn is already confirmed. For cycle turn indicators, Dutta cites fading fiscal tailwinds and passive policy tightening via unchanged rates against weak nominal wage growth; Amarnath points to credit spreads and the willingness of non-hyperscaler companies to sustain AI capex as the variables most likely to flag a genuine inflection.
