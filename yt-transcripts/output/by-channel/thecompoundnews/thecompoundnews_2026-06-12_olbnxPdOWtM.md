# Why the Knockout Punch Never Comes | TCAF 246

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-06-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=olbnxPdOWtM
**Duration:** 67:38

## Summary

In TCAF episode 246, Brian Levitt of Invesco joins Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to analyze the current bull market's AI concentration, arguing that earnings growth justifies elevated valuations and that the broadening cycle was twice disrupted by policy shocks (the Iran war and Liberation Day tariffs). Levitt explains why investors perpetually searching for a 'knockout punch' misunderstand bear market mechanics — the catalytic event typically arrives after a bear market is already underway, not before. The conversation also dissects the SpaceX IPO hype, the validity of dot-com comparisons, and what indicators — credit spreads, bank lending standards, and inflation break-evens — actually matter for cycle analysis.

## Key Takeaways

- Earnings breadth was broadening in early 2026 until the Iran oil price shock disrupted it, with seven of eleven S&P sectors posting double-digit Q1 earnings growth.
- The 'knockout punch' bear market narrative is backwards: major shock events like Lehman Brothers followed existing downturns rather than causing them.
- The 5-year inflation break-even near 250 basis points signals price stability, supporting the case against rate hikes and continued cycle expansion.
- Household net worth at all-time highs and historically low liability ratios make the consumer sector more resilient than bearish narratives suggest.
- SpaceX IPO oversubscription figures are unreliable because indications of interest routinely overstate actual demand by large multiples.
- The most underrated AI opportunity may be in companies consuming AI to improve margins — such as Travelers in insurance underwriting — rather than the picks-and-shovels sellers.
- Levitt's preferred cycle-monitoring framework focuses on credit spreads, bank lending standards, dollar strength, inflation expectations, and leverage — not PE ratios or strategist price targets.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Concentration and Earnings Broadening [00:00:00]

Michael Batnick highlights that while S&P 500 earnings are rising, most companies' earnings remain below their 2022 starting levels — only the new-era AI sectors are driving growth. Levitt counters that Q1 2026 data shows genuine broadening, with seven to ten of eleven S&P sectors recording positive earnings growth. The broadening trade was developing twice in the past two years but was interrupted each time by a policy shock — the Iran conflict in 2026 and Liberation Day tariffs in 2025.

### What Needs to Happen for Broader Market Participation [00:00:00]

Levitt argues that oil prices peaking, inflation expectations peaking, and the Fed resuming its easing stance are the three preconditions for market breadth to expand beyond AI. The Russell 2000 vs. S&P 500 ratio chart was showing signs of a major breakout coinciding with the oil price peak. The 5-year break-even rate near 250 basis points — down from a 265 peak — indicates the bond market is pricing in price stability, reducing the risk of rate hikes that would end the cycle.

### Why Investors Keep Looking for a Market Crash [00:00:00]

Josh Brown explains that the average investor expects a news event to 'knock out' the market and cause a bear market, but historically the knockout punch arrives only after the bear market is well underway — citing Lehman Brothers, which collapsed because of the bear market, not as its cause. Levitt notes the market has climbed through the European debt crisis, Brexit, COVID, Liberation Day, and the Iran conflict — 18 years since the last actual recession (excluding COVID). The real bear market sequence involves good news failing to lift stocks, six months without new highs, then the economic surprise index rolling over.

### Dot-Com Bubble Comparisons and Savita Subramanian's Warning [00:00:00]

Savita Subramanian of Bank of America flags that seven of her ten bear market signposts have triggered, including the spread between top and bottom tech performers matching February 2000 levels. Levitt acknowledges the similarities but points to the critical difference: 1999-2000 featured a tightening rate cycle while the current environment anticipates Fed easing, fiscal stimulus, and no debt-overhang in the real economy. The dispersion within tech can resolve through broadening upward rather than a crash.

### Consumer Strength and the K-Shaped Economy [00:00:00]

Household net worth at roughly $175 trillion — an all-time high — combined with historically low liability ratios supports consumer resilience. Levitt frames the K-shaped economy practically: when the upper income quintiles maintain spending, the S&P 500 earnings are largely insulated, since the bottom quintile's spending is partly government-subsidized. Hotel stocks are cited as a clean indicator of upper-income consumer health, with Hilton-type names near all-time highs.

### SpaceX IPO: Bubble Signal or Bullish Catalyst? [00:00:00]

Josh Brown dismisses the 3.3x oversubscription claim as fabricated — standard IPO practice inflates indications of interest because investors request five times what they actually want to ensure allocation. He predicts SpaceX will open 30-40% above the $135 offering price but will likely give back much of that gain. The broader debate: if the IPO goes off without incident, bears lose another 'asteroid' argument and the market could interpret it as validation of healthy risk appetite. Potential $4 trillion in new index-eligible market cap from SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI IPOs is float-adjusted and thus less disruptive than headline numbers suggest.

### Indicators That Actually Matter and the AI ROI Thesis [00:00:00]

Levitt's preferred cycle dashboard: credit spreads, bank lending standards, dollar strength, inflation expectations, and leverage — explicitly excluding PE ratios and sell-side price targets. The episode closes with the optimistic AI thesis: Travelers' 21% underwriting profit jump driven by AI is the leading edge of a wave of earnings surprises from AI consumers (not just producers), which could power a broader and more durable bull market beyond the chip-and-infrastructure phase.
