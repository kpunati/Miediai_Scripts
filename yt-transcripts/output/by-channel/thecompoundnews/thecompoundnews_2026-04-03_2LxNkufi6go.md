# Why Gas Prices Can’t Wreck the Market | TCAF 236

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=2LxNkufi6go
**Duration:** 72:42

## Summary

Episode 236 of The Compound and Friends features Dan Greenhaus (Solus Alternative Asset Management) and Alexandra Semenova (Bloomberg News) alongside hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick discussing a wide range of market topics amid an ongoing Middle East conflict. The central thesis is that despite oil surging past $100/barrel due to Strait of Hormuz disruptions, the S&P 500 has been remarkably resilient because energy's share of corporate earnings and consumer spending has declined structurally over decades. The panel also examines private credit redemption pressures at Blue Owl, argues the situation is not analogous to 2008 given the absence of deposit-taking leverage, and discusses SpaceX's confidential IPO filing at a $1 trillion valuation. The episode closes with a dissection of Snap's chronic underperformance and the arrival of an activist investor.

## Key Takeaways

- Oil above $100/barrel has not caused meaningful S&P 500 damage because energy is a de minimis share of index earnings and consumer energy spending as a percent of income has roughly halved since 2012.
- The Strait of Hormuz supply disruption has mitigating factors — large pre-war oversupply, floating storage, and pipeline bypasses via Saudi Arabia's East-West pipeline and UAE's Fujairah route — but Greenhaus warns these buffers are being worked off.
- V-shaped recoveries have conditioned institutional and retail investors to hold through drawdowns, and midterm election years historically see average max drawdowns of 16% but are positive one year later 75% of the time.
- Blue Owl's OCIC private credit BDC faced redemption requests but 90% of shareholders stayed, and inflows of $872 million nearly offset the $988 million in tenders, suggesting the redemption panic is overstated versus 2008 comparisons.
- Retail investor animal spirits have cooled — single-name retail buying is at post-COVID lows and speculative ETF volumes have dried up, partly as gas price increases absorb discretionary spending.
- SpaceX's confidential IPO filing targeting $50-75 billion raised at over $1 trillion valuation would be the largest IPO ever; hosts note that a simultaneous SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic IPO wave could be a classic market-top signal.
- Snap, in a 94% drawdown from its high, has an activist investor (Irenic Capital) proposing a path to a $35 billion market cap, but Spiegel and co-founder control 95% of voting power via a tri-share-class structure.

## Detailed Notes

### Oil Prices and Market Resilience [00:00:01]

Brown opens by noting the S&P shrugged off an 8-point WTI spike, reversing gap-down lows in just 2 hours. Greenhaus explains that the market entered the conflict with ~2 million bpd oversupply, significant floating storage, and alternative pipeline routes (Saudi East-West pipeline at ~4 million bpd capacity, UAE Fujairah bypass at ~1.5 million bpd), which have buffered prices. He warns, however, that ships and inventories take 20-30 days to clear, and Asian markets are beginning to see rationing as the supply chain effects materialize.

### Energy's Declining Weight in Consumer Spending and S&P Earnings [00:00:01]

Semenova and Brown highlight that energy's share of consumer spending fell from ~6% in the early 1990s to roughly 2-3% in January 2026, reducing the macro drag of higher gas prices. Greenhaus shows data by income decile: even the lowest earners now spend roughly half the share of after-tax income on gasoline versus 2012. However, the panel flags that the projected $4.30/gallon pump price translates to a ~$120 billion shock equal to 0.5% of disposable income, which could offset the benefit of the 'big beautiful bill' tax refunds.

### Drawdowns, V-Shaped Recoveries, and Investor Behavior [00:00:01]

The hosts contextualize the current drawdown as the fifth largest this decade, with the VIX only briefly hitting 35. Brown argues the pattern of rapid recoveries from COVID, the debt ceiling, Silicon Valley Bank, and Liberation Day has trained investors not to panic-sell. Greenhaus notes that midterm election years historically average a 16% max drawdown but are positive 100% of the time one year later. The panel also warns that many large-cap 'brand name' stocks are down 10-20% beneath the resilient headline index, while the outperforming stocks are obscure names in industrials and memory chips.

### Retail Investor Activity and Speculative Cooling [00:00:01]

Brown and the panel note retail single-name stock buying is at post-COVID lows per Vanda Research data; retail investors are still buying ETFs but not Palantir, Nvidia, or similar momentum names. Options volumes and speculative ETF flows have reversed. The $120 billion gas price shock is cited as one mechanism absorbing money previously available for speculation, though the panel agrees retail traders will likely return once sentiment improves and attractive new single-stock narratives emerge.

### Private Credit Redemptions and Blue Owl [00:00:01]

Brown dissects Blue Owl OCIC headlines: 41% of one legacy fund faced redemption requests, but gross inflows of $872 million nearly offset $988 million in tenders, for net outflows of only $116 million (<1% of NAV). Greenhaus argues the situation is structurally dissimilar to 2008 — there are no deposit-taking institutions, leverage is far lower, and 93% of OCIC loans are senior secured at 40% loan-to-value. The key risk is concentrated software exposure (~25% of BDC portfolios) where recovery rates in distress are materially lower than the broadly syndicated loan market.

### SpaceX IPO Filing [00:00:01]

SpaceX filed confidentially for an IPO targeting a $1 trillion-plus valuation, seeking to raise $50-75 billion in June, with Musk aiming for 30% retail allocation. The panel notes this would surpass Saudi Aramco's $29 billion 2019 raise as the largest IPO ever. Brown raises the question of whether a simultaneous SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic IPO wave inside 6 months would constitute a classic market-top signal, drawing parallels to Blackstone's 2007 IPO.

### Snap's Activist Investor and Chronic Underperformance [00:00:01]

Snap, at $3.93 per share and in a 94% drawdown from its $80+ high, received an activist letter from Irenic Capital Management (which holds 2.5% of Class A shares) outlining a path to $35 billion market cap. However, Spiegel and co-founder Bobby Murphy collectively control over 95% of total voting power through a tri-share-class structure. The panel notes Snap has never monetized its massive engaged user base and describes it as possibly 'the worst stock ever' in terms of value destruction despite continued user adoption.
