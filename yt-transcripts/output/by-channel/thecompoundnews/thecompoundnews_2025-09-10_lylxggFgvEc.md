## Summary

Recorded live at Future Proof 2025 in Huntington Beach, this episode of Animal Spirits features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson reflecting on the ten-year journey of building Ritholtz Wealth Management from a bootstrapped startup to a near-80-person independent RIA. They discuss the firm's client-fit philosophy, the unexpected power of the content flywheel for client acquisition and hiring, the role of luck—particularly the pandemic-driven shift to remote work—and the firm's commitment to growth over fixed financial goals. The episode is part retrospective, part business philosophy, and part live conference entertainment.

## Key Takeaways

- RWM started in 2015 managing $140 million with seven employees and grew by staying bootstrapped and remote-first.
- COVID-19 accelerated RWM's growth because the firm was already paperless and remote before the pandemic made those qualities industry-wide necessities.
- Client fit is treated as a two-way requirement from day one; turning away misaligned prospects, even large ones, protects the entire team and long-term culture.
- The content flywheel—blogs, podcasts, YouTube—drove organic client acquisition and talent recruitment far beyond initial expectations, working like compound interest over time.
- Setting a growth goal of $3 billion proved misguided because it ignored the team infrastructure required to serve that level of AUM.
- The firm's mantra 'if it's not an obvious yes, it's a no' applied to hiring has resulted in very low turnover and a highly motivated staff.

## Detailed Notes

### Ten Years at RWM: Startup Origins [00:03:24]

Carlson joined in fall 2015 when the firm had seven employees and $140 million AUM. Barry Ritholtz set the tone by framing the firm as building infrastructure for a billion-dollar firm while acknowledging they could barely afford Carlson's salary. The founding team was bootstrapped, leasing computers from the Apple Store, with remote work adopted informally when Carlson declined to relocate from Grand Rapids.

### Client Fit as Core Philosophy [00:08:11]

Wealth management architect Chris Van enforced a strict client-fit standard from day one, turning away a prospect claiming $25-100 million in assets because his expectations were unrealistic. Carlson initially doubted this approach but recognized its value after wasting an entire conference doing back-tests for prospects who never became clients. The lesson: accepting bad-fit clients harms traders, operations staff, and overall firm culture.

### COVID and the Remote-Work Inflection Point [00:07:09]

RWM accidentally became a remote-first, paperless firm before COVID when Carlson stayed in Grand Rapids and the firm served clients nationwide via the internet. When the pandemic forced the industry to adopt virtual client relationships, RWM was already operating that way, making 2020 by far their largest growth year.

### The Content Flywheel and Unintended Consequences [00:13:26]

Carlson expected early blog readers to become immediate clients, then assumed the pipeline would dry up and referrals would dominate. Instead, consistent content production built an evergreen pipeline—clients come when life events arise, not just because they like the brand. The podcast also became an unexpected recruiting tool: nearly all adviser hires came through listeners who found the firm via Animal Spirits or related content.

### Growth Over Goals and Hiring Philosophy [00:16:57]

Batnick recalls a backyard meeting where he set a $3 billion AUM goal, which in hindsight ignored the staffing required to serve that scale. The firm never set formal asset milestones; discussions focused on what was working and where to invest. Today with nearly 80 employees, leadership's primary motivation is delivering on the promise made to every hire that the firm will never stop growing.
