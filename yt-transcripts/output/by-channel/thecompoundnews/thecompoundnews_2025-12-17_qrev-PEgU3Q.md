# All-Time Highs Should Feel Better Than This | Animal Spirits 443

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-12-17
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qrev-PEgU3Q
**Duration:** 79:28

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 443, recorded mid-December 2025, finds hosts Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examining a paradox: stocks at all-time highs despite sharp declines in high-profile tech names like Oracle, Nvidia, and Netflix. The broadening of market leadership to equal-weight, small-caps, and international equities is framed as a healthy sign rather than cause for alarm. The episode covers Howard Marks's nuanced stance on AI bubble risk, the slowing monetization cycle in private markets, housing affordability constraints, and the hosts' views on leasing versus owning cars and rising streaming prices.

## Key Takeaways

- Equal-weight S&P 500, Russell 2000, and all-country world index hit all-time highs even as major tech names sold off sharply, signaling healthy market breadth.
- European financials and international equities outperformed US tech benchmarks significantly over a five-year period, confounding the narrative that non-US markets are uninvestable.
- Howard Marks recommends a moderate, selective approach to AI exposure — neither going all-in nor staying entirely out — given the inability to call bubble tops definitively.
- Private equity buyout funds have slowed monetization dramatically; funds from 2016 vintage have returned only about 30% of capital where prior norms were closer to 70% by this stage.
- AI enterprise software is projected to grow from near zero in 2022 to a $100 billion category, but OpenAI's business model faces scrutiny due to massive stock-based compensation relative to revenue.
- Cumulative inflation since 2020 of roughly 25% overall, with housing and transportation up more, continues to weigh on consumer sentiment even as nominal wages have risen.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Breadth and Tech Selloff [00:01:05]

Hosts detail how major tech stocks including Oracle (-45%), Netflix (-30%), Costco (-20%), Meta (-20%), and Nvidia (-15%) pulled back significantly while the equal-weight S&P 500, Russell 2000, and ACWI reached all-time highs. European financials (EUFN) and international stocks rose roughly 30%, defying the prevailing narrative that only US mega-cap tech drives returns. Batnick argues this broadening is the most bullish possible internal structure for equity markets.

### AI Bubble Debate and Howard Marks [00:16:33]

Howard Marks's latest memo is reviewed: he advises neither going all-in nor all-out on AI given irreducible uncertainty, recommending moderate and selective positioning. The hosts note that Nvidia's forward P/E has contracted from roughly 40x at ChatGPT launch to 24x today, which is atypical of bubble dynamics, though the sheer scale of the market cap ($4-5 trillion) remains historically extraordinary. Gavin Baker's view that Taiwan Semi's capacity restraint is a healthy governor on AI spending is highlighted.

### Investor Behavior and Concentration Risk [00:19:35]

The episode explores retail investors rotating away from Mag 7 into broader ETFs. An anecdote about a math teacher who moved 80% of savings from tech into gold illustrates the tension between justified concentration profits and prudent diversification. T. Rowe Price manager Alan Boen's research is cited: in normal decades roughly 40 stocks compound at 20% per year, versus 120 during the low-rate era, suggesting many portfolio winners are 'impostors' that may not persist.

### Private Markets and Labor Market [00:26:46]

JP Morgan research shows private equity buyout funds have sharply slowed cash distributions; 2016-vintage funds have only returned about 30% of invested capital versus historical norms near 70% at this stage, forcing managers to seek retail capital channels. The US unemployment rate at 4.6% (up 50 basis points in 5 months) is noted but bond and equity markets appear unconcerned, as corporate profit margins benefit from slower hiring.

### Housing, Streaming, and Consumer Topics [00:45:08]

Housing analyst Mike Sas projects flat home prices in 2026; 86% of homeowners now have at least 30% equity, a dramatic improvement from 2013 when negative equity was near 15%. On streaming, Disney, Max, and others have raised prices multiple times since launch; Bloomberg data shows US pay TV subscribers rose for the first time since 2017. Hosts debate Netflix's potential acquisition of Paramount and the implications for the Hollywood studio model, with discussion of the narrow theatrical window strategy.
