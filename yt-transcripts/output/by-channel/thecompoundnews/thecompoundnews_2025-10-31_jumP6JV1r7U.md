# What Did the Fed Actually Say? | TCAF 215

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-31
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=jumP6JV1r7U
**Duration:** 63:35

## Summary

In this episode of The Compound and Friends, hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick interview Stephanie Roth, Chief Economist at Wolfe Research, on Halloween 2025. The conversation covers major themes including strong tech earnings from Meta, Microsoft, and Alphabet confirming continued AI capex acceleration, and Federal Reserve Chair Powell's comments effectively taking a December rate cut off the table. Roth argues the AI investment cycle is historically unprecedented in scale — comparable only to China's infrastructure supercycle — but not yet in bubble territory given its cash-flow-funded nature and relatively modest share of GDP. The discussion also addresses the wealth effect's growing importance to consumer spending, cooling shelter inflation, tight credit spreads, and US-China trade dynamics.

## Key Takeaways

- Powell signaled a December rate cut is far from certain, triggering an immediate repricing of expectations and a sharp sell-off in housing and mortgage-related stocks.
- Meta, Microsoft, and Alphabet all reported strong revenue growth and confirmed significantly higher capex spending in 2026, validating continued AI infrastructure investment.
- AI capex spending currently represents about 1.3% of GDP and roughly a quarter of economic growth — historically large but not yet at bubble-level proportions seen in prior booms.
- The wealth effect is a more powerful economic driver than traditionally modeled, as equity ownership has broadened dramatically through 401ks, retail investing, and stock-option compensation.
- Shelter inflation is finally cooling in official CPI data, but tariff pass-through is only about 50% complete, keeping overall inflation above the Fed's target.
- Tight credit spreads reflect a healthy economic backdrop; isolated credit stress events remain concentrated in auto subprime and fraud-related cases rather than systemic sectors.
- China has roughly equal trade leverage to the US, and a trade truce — not a deal — was achieved, paving the way for a year-end equity rally as underperforming investors catch up.

## Detailed Notes

### Tech Earnings and AI Capex Confirmation [00:07:08]

Meta, Microsoft, and Alphabet all reported strong revenue growth — Meta up 26%, Microsoft up 18%, and Google posting its first $100 billion quarter. All three companies confirmed that AI-related capital expenditure will accelerate meaningfully in 2026, with Meta's capex running at approximately $116 billion for 2025. Roth estimated AI capex is contributing roughly a quarter of current GDP growth, or about 1.3% of GDP, representing above-trend spending on data centers and compute infrastructure.

### AI Bubble Risk and Historical Context [00:17:56]

Roth and the hosts compared the current AI investment cycle to historical booms, noting it is most comparable in scale to China's infrastructure supercycle ($14 trillion in today's dollars) rather than the dot-com bubble ($1 trillion). AI spend has not yet reached the 3-5% of GDP peaks seen in prior bubbles like railroads, dot-com, and housing. The primary risk indicator to watch is when spending shifts from cash-flow-funded to debt-financed, which has begun with large infrastructure deals involving Oracle and others but has not yet reached systemic scale.

### Federal Reserve Policy and December Rate Cut Uncertainty [00:23:23]

Powell's key statement was that a December rate cut is far from a foregone conclusion, sharply reversing market expectations that had priced in a near-100% probability of a cut. Roth explained there are two dissenting Fed members pulling in opposite directions — one favoring a 50 basis point cut and another favoring no cut at all — reflecting genuine uncertainty about the policy path. With the economy performing steadily, inflation above target at roughly 3%, and labor markets intact, the case for further easing is weak unless labor conditions deteriorate.

### Wealth Effect and Consumer Spending Resilience [00:33:52]

Josh Brown argued the wealth effect is the most important and underappreciated driver of consumer spending, citing Ford and GM reaching 52-week highs despite tariff headwinds as evidence that consumers are spending because stock portfolios are at record highs. Roth confirmed the view, noting that equity net worth rose by approximately $6 trillion in 2025, adding roughly 40 basis points to growth. Millennials now hold 23% of their net worth in equities vs. only 6% for boomers at the same age, meaning the wealth effect is broader and stronger than historical models suggest.

### Shelter Inflation, Tariffs, and Credit Conditions [00:44:40]

Shelter inflation in CPI is finally declining toward levels last seen in 2021, though the Fed's owners equivalent rent measure has lagged real-time data by more than 18 months. On tariffs, Roth noted that goods price inflation is running about 2% year-over-year versus a typical 0 to negative 1%, representing real but contained pass-through; she estimated only about 50% of tariff costs have been passed to consumers so far. Credit spreads remain tight, reflecting a broadly healthy economy; isolated stress events remain concentrated in auto subprime and fraud-related cases and have not spread to unrelated sectors.

### AI Labor Market Impact and US-China Trade [00:56:18]

The discussion highlighted that about 10.6% of tech sector revenues come from China, with Apple at 16%, Tesla at 21%, and Nvidia at 13% — giving China meaningful leverage in trade negotiations. A US-China trade truce (not a full deal) was reached, including reductions in fentanyl-related tariffs. On the labor market, Roth described a low-hire, low-fire environment expected to persist, with AI displacement starting visibly among recent college graduates in white-collar tech roles but unlikely to become a broad recessionary layoff cycle for at least a decade.
