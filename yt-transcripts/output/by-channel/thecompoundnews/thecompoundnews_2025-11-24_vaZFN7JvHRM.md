# The Flying Car Moment is Now Months Away

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vaZFN7JvHRM
**Duration:** 33:18

## Summary

Charles Lemonides, founder of ValueWorks hedge fund, joins Josh Brown to make the investment case for Joby Aviation as the leading eVTOL company following the Dubai Airshow. Lemonides argues that Joby's live flight demonstrations, Blade acquisition giving it existing revenue and infrastructure, Toyota manufacturing partnership, and FAA certification progress set it apart from all competitors. The conversation also covers ValueWorks' broader investment philosophy of finding quality assets at compelling valuations in out-of-favor growth companies, with Rivian and Instacart cited as analogous holdings.

## Key Takeaways

- Joby was the only eVTOL company to fly a piloted aircraft at the Dubai Airshow, completing a point-to-point flight at UAE—a concrete operational proof point that competitors lacked.
- Joby's acquisition of Blade gives it existing revenue (~$100M annual run rate), operating infrastructure, heliport access in New York, and management expertise in urban air mobility logistics that competitors must build from scratch.
- A 6-year exclusive commercial air taxi agreement with Dubai's RTA targets a commercial launch in the second half of 2026, which Lemonides views as the first real cash-flow-generating milestone for the sector.
- Toyota's ~15% ownership and manufacturing guidance, plus Baillie Gifford's ~6% stake, provide Lemonides with institutional validation for Joby's execution credibility.
- Lemonides values Joby on a capital-invested basis: when he entered, two-thirds of the share price was in cash and total investment implied a steep discount to already-spent development capital.
- The operating model matters more than vehicle sales: Joby intends to operate aircraft rather than sell them, which creates accelerating revenue as the fleet scales, versus a linear sales model like Archer's.

## Detailed Notes

### Joby at the Dubai Airshow [00:01:06]

Lemonides attended the Dubai Airshow, one of three major global aviation events alongside Paris and Farnborough. Joby was the standout eVTOL company, conducting daily live piloted flights while all other OEMs displayed only models or prototypes. Joby completed a 17-minute point-to-point flight between Margam and Al Maktoum International Airport, the first piloted eVTOL flight in the UAE. The aircraft's near-silence relative to jets overhead was a notable demonstration of its commercial viability in urban environments.

### Joby's Competitive Advantages [00:03:44]

Joby's SR-4 aircraft features six rotors with double redundancy, a fixed wing for fuel-efficient cruise flight, and electric propulsion that eliminates helicopter noise and exhaust. The Blade acquisition provides ~$100M annual revenue, existing heliport infrastructure at Hudson and East River ports in Manhattan, and a management team experienced in deploying urban air mobility logistics. Lemonides argues competitors like Archer (partnering with Stellantis for manufacturing), Eve (backed by Embraer), and Wisk (backed by Boeing) are meaningfully behind Joby in both product and commercial readiness.

### Commercial Milestones and Regulatory Path [00:09:18]

Joby is producing roughly one aircraft per month and has sold units to the US Defense Department. FAA certification is estimated to be approximately one year away. The Trump administration's EIPP program will facilitate closer FAA collaboration for eVTOL integration into the national airspace. A 6-year exclusive RTA agreement targets commercial service in Dubai in the second half of 2026, which Lemonides characterizes as a pivotal moment when revenue will first be generated from passenger operations.

### Operating vs. Selling Model and Scaling Economics [00:12:32]

Lemonides emphasizes that Joby's decision to operate aircraft rather than sell them creates superior revenue scaling: doubling a fleet from 200 to 400 operating aircraft doubles revenue, whereas a sales model grows linearly with unit volume. Given that supply will be constrained for at least 5 years with production limited to perhaps 200-300 aircraft per year, operators who hold and run the aircraft will capture far more value than vehicle sellers.

### ValueWorks Investment Philosophy and Portfolio Context [00:25:53]

Lemonides describes ValueWorks' approach as seeking quality assets at compelling valuations, specifically targeting out-of-favor growth companies where significant capital has already been deployed and the business is operationally proven but investors have moved on. Joby was attractive when cash represented two-thirds of market cap and remaining implied valuation was a discount to development capital spent. Rivian and Instacart are cited as analogous positions; Lemonides is also long Spirit AeroSystems as a back-door Boeing acquisition play. He explicitly avoids Lucid as a potential failure case.
