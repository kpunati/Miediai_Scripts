# Why Paramount Should Beat Netflix | TCAF 222

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-12-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=obaCZs7BiiU
**Duration:** 55:22

## Summary

Episode 222 of The Compound and Friends brings financial journalist Bill Cohan onto the show to break down one of the largest media deals in recent history: competing bids from Netflix and Paramount for Warner Bros. Discovery. Cohan explains the deal mechanics, economics, and personalities at the center of the acquisition race, including the significant breakup fees, the Ellison family's equity backstop, and why the WBD board has thus far favored Netflix's bid. The conversation also covers the broader renaissance for large US banks under deregulation, the growth of private credit structures through alternative asset managers like Apollo and Blackstone, and speculation about which Kevin — Warsh or Hassett — will be named the next Federal Reserve chair.

## Key Takeaways

- Warner Bros. Discovery's board favors the Netflix bid because on economics alone, the Netflix offer plus the value of the spun-off global networks stub exceeds Paramount's $30-per-share all-cash offer.
- A $5.8 billion regulatory breakup fee protects WBD if the Netflix deal fails regulatory approval, while WBD owes Netflix $2.8 billion if it switches its recommendation to Paramount.
- Cohan assigns 55% odds to Paramount winning by raising its bid to $34 per share, arguing Netflix is near its debt capacity limits and would rather take the breakup fee and a long-term supply deal.
- Major US banks including JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and Bank of America have hit record highs driven by a deregulatory environment, rising capital markets activity, and strong M&A and IPO pipelines.
- Large banks have shifted risky lending into private credit structures through alternative asset managers like Apollo and Blackstone, creating a cleaner but potentially fragile system if performance deteriorates.
- Kevin Warsh is favored to become the next Fed chair due to his personal connections to Trump's circle and his expected willingness to lower interest rates.
- Movie theater ticket sales peaked in 2002 and are now at roughly two-thirds of that level, with Netflix's US and Canada revenue running at approximately 2x domestic box office, showing that streaming has clearly won the content consumption war.

## Detailed Notes

### Netflix vs. Paramount: Deal Mechanics and Current State [00:02:35]

Bill Cohan explains that Zaslav put WBD into play after a Reverse Morris Trust two-year tax waiting period expired in April 2025. WBD's net debt was reduced from $55 billion to $30 billion, the stock rose from roughly $7.25 to $30 per share, and after Paramount's bid arrived two weeks after closing its own deal, Netflix emerged as a serious competing suitor. Comcast briefly entered then dropped out, leaving a two-horse race with Netflix currently holding a signed merger agreement.

### Breakup Fees, Bid Economics, and the Ellison Equity Question [00:13:34]

Netflix must pay WBD $5.8 billion if the deal collapses due to regulatory failure; WBD owes Netflix $2.8 billion if it switches to Paramount. The WBD board's valuation of the global networks stub at roughly $3-per-share means the Netflix deal exceeds Paramount's $30-per-share all-cash offer. A key uncertainty is whether the Larry J. Ellison revocable trust backstopping the $41 billion equity commitment is properly reflected in SEC 13G filings, as it appears in Oracle's proxy but not the filings.

### Cohan's Odds and the Netflix Upside Scenario [00:31:26]

Cohan puts 55% odds on Paramount ultimately winning by raising its bid to $34 per share, arguing Netflix is near its debt ceiling and a higher bid would push it into junk-bond territory. If Paramount wins, Netflix can collect the $2.8 billion breakup fee, negotiate a long-term content supply deal, and see its share price recover. Kalshi prediction markets show Netflix at 72% and Paramount at 25% as of recording, which Cohan disagrees with.

### Hollywood Ramifications and the Theater Industry [00:34:37]

Either deal eliminates one content buyer from Hollywood, further pressuring labor markets that are already strained. A Paramount deal would involve more duplicative studios and larger headcount cuts, while a Netflix deal is worse for movie theater operators since Netflix historically withholds theatrical releases. Ticket sales have been in secular decline since peaking in 2002, and Netflix's UCAN revenue is now approximately 2x domestic box office, confirming streaming's dominance.

### US Bank Renaissance and Private Credit Ecosystem [00:44:14]

Large US banks have reached record valuations driven by a deregulatory environment, booming M&A and IPO activity, and strong capital markets. JP Morgan is on track for roughly $60 billion in net income. Banks have effectively outsourced risky middle-market lending to alternative asset managers like Apollo, Blackstone, KKR, and Ares — retaining client relationships and origination fees while passing credit risk. Cohan notes Apollo's structure through Athene (an annuity insurer) creates a potential vulnerability if loan performance deteriorates, though it is not yet showing up in results.

### Next Federal Reserve Chair [00:51:43]

Cohan predicts Kevin Warsh will be named the next Fed chair, citing Trump's preference for someone with a strong presence ('central casting'), Warsh's Morgan Stanley background, and his family connection to Ron Lauder who is a close personal friend of Trump. Warsh is also expected to support lower interest rates. Kevin Hassett had been seen as the frontrunner but appears to be losing ground.
