# You Don’t Want a 50 Basis Point Rate Cut - They Always Mean Recession

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=gAxH4LE8WKk
**Duration:** 42:25

## Summary

Nick Colas of DataTrek Research presents data-driven analysis on the September 2025 Fed rate cut decision, arguing a 50bp cut would wrongly signal recession. He compares current Magnificent 7 companies to their 1990s tech counterparts to argue that elevated S&P multiples are justified by structurally superior business models. The conversation closes with a sector-level decomposition of 2025 returns showing financials as an underappreciated opportunity due to earnings growth without corresponding multiple expansion.

## Key Takeaways

- Historical data shows 50bp Fed cuts are almost exclusively recession-era events; the market would likely interpret an unexpected 50bp cut as a panic signal rather than good news.
- A 25bp cut is overwhelmingly expected and already priced in; what matters more is whether Powell signals a pace of cuts that matches futures markets or is more hawkish.
- Nvidia is a structurally far superior business to Intel in 1999 on every financial metric, supporting higher multiples even if the absolute PE multiple seems elevated on a historical basis.
- Meta's 39% net margins versus IBM's 9% in 1999, combined with aggressive reinvestment, justify a meaningfully higher valuation than IBM commanded at peak dot-com.
- Microsoft's 10x revenue growth above inflation since 1999 illustrates how technology compound effects can make historical PE comparisons misleading.
- Financials have experienced 10% earnings estimate upgrades year-to-date but only 1% multiple expansion, making the sector a compelling re-rating candidate into year-end.
- A grid analysis of S&P 500 fair value shows only 7 of 49 scenarios deliver upside from current levels, requiring either higher multiples or earnings beats to justify long exposure.

## Detailed Notes

### Fed Rate Cut Sizing and Recession Signaling [00:01:45]

Nick Colas presents data showing 55 Fed rate cuts since 1990: 60% were 25bp, 33% were 50bp, and 7% were 75-100bp. The 50bp cuts have historically occurred only in recession or immediately post-recession years (1991, 1992, 2001, 2002, 2007, 2008, 2020), with 2024 as the only exception due to normalization from an extreme hiking cycle. A surprise 50bp cut in September 2025 would therefore likely trigger recession fears in markets.

### Earnings Estimates and S&P Valuation Framework [00:11:42]

Unlike the typical pattern where analyst estimates drift down through the year, S&P 500 forward earnings estimates have risen every week since Q2 earnings season began. DataTrek's sensitivity grid shows 49 valuation scenarios: only 7 deliver upside from current levels, requiring either PE expansion toward 24x or meaningful earnings beats. The simplest 10-11% upside scenario requires $235 EPS next year at a 24x multiple, two dollars above prior estimates.

### Nvidia vs Intel 1999: Justifying Elevated Multiples [00:19:49]

Nvidia's operating margins are 1.8x Intel's in 1999, net margins 2.3x (57% vs 25%), ROE 4.7x higher (106% vs 23%), and asset intensity 8x better due to fabless model. On an inflation-adjusted basis, Nvidia is three times Intel's 1999 revenue size and 7.5x more profitable. Colas argues this justifies a materially higher multiple than Intel's peak of 24-25x, though not the full 7.5x differential.

### Meta vs IBM 1999 and Microsoft Then vs Now [00:24:09]

Meta's 39% net margins versus IBM's 9% in 1999 indicate a far stronger competitive moat; both companies have similar inflation-adjusted revenues (~$190B vs ~$170B), but Meta earns five times more. Microsoft has grown revenues 10x above what CPI inflation would have implied since 1999, reaching $300B versus an expected $38B, demonstrating the compounding power of technology platform reinvention (cloud pivot). Colas's thesis is that modern big-tech companies are orders of magnitude better businesses than their 1990s benchmarks, justifying a 22x S&P multiple.

### Sector Analysis: Financials as the Standout Opportunity [00:34:12]

A decomposition of 2025 sector returns into PE multiple expansion and earnings estimate changes shows winning sectors (communications, tech, industrials, utilities) average 7% PE expansion and 9.4% earnings growth. Financials stand out as an anomaly: 10% earnings estimate upgrades but only 1% PE multiple expansion. Colas attributes the multiple suppression to persistent concerns about bank balance sheets and lending risk, but sees deregulation, fintech re-ratings (Visa, Mastercard, Coinbase, Robinhood), and deal activity as catalysts for re-rating into year-end.
