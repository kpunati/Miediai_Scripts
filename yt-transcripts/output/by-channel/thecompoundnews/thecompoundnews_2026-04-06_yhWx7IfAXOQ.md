# Private Credit Is the Fuse, Insurance Companies Are the Bomb, Redemptions Are a Lit Match

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=yhWx7IfAXOQ
**Duration:** 41:32

## Summary

In this interview, Josh Brown hosts Nick Nemeth, author of the Mispriced Assets Substack, to examine systemic risks embedded in private credit markets and their spillover into the US life insurance sector. Nemeth contends that private credit NAVs are materially misstated due to sponsor-marked, not market-marked, valuations especially in software-heavy portfolios, and that escalating redemptions could force mark-to-market reckoning. He argues the true bomb is the life insurance industry, which sits on $10 trillion in assets with only $658 billion in capital surplus, backed by an offshore reinsurance system he believes is severely underfunded. The discussion covers leverage stacking across borrowers, fund vehicles, and CLO structures, and calls for congressional oversight of the offshore reinsurance arrangements currently invisible to US regulators.

## Key Takeaways

- Private credit funds use sponsor-marked, not market-marked, valuations that Nemeth believes materially overstate NAV, particularly for software company loans facing AI disruption.
- BXSL, the publicly traded BDC, trades at roughly a 12% discount to NAV and shares 80% of the same loans as private product BCRED, illustrating how public markets are repricing private credit risk.
- Leverage in the system is multi-layered: borrower operational leverage, 7-9x fund-level EBITDA leverage, 1.5-2x fund borrowing, and potentially 10x CLO leverage, meaning the end investor bears far more risk than a stated 10% yield suggests.
- Life insurers, levered 17x with $658 billion in surplus against over $10 trillion in assets, have reinsured their balance sheet risk to a small group of offshore entities that Nemeth estimates are significantly under-reserved.
- Redemption pressure in semi-liquid private credit funds is the key near-term trigger: outflows force asset sales at real marks, compressing NAVs and potentially triggering insurance company downgrades and capital calls.
- Nemeth distinguishes PE-backed life insurers (most aggressive asset allocators) from mutual insurers like New York Life (more conservative) as a practical guide for consumers.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Private Credit Marks Are Unreliable [00:01:47]

Nemeth opens by explaining that private credit grew from near zero to a multi-trillion-dollar asset class with valuations set by fund managers rather than market clearing. He argues this 'volatility laundering' masks true credit risk, and that software company loans, which may represent 33-35% of BCRED's actual exposure versus the disclosed 25-26%, are especially mispriced given the public equity decline in SaaS stocks.

### Leverage Stacking and EBITDA Manipulation [00:10:23]

Private equity sponsors run 7x leverage on EBITDAs that include aggressive add-backs Nemeth says regulators and independent analysts would reject; Damodaran's bond-rating methodology would assign far lower credit ratings than the investment-grade labels these loans receive. These loans are then packaged into AA-rated CLOs, replicating the structured credit dynamics of 2008. End investors receiving a ~10% yield are bearing leveraged exposure amplified multiple times across the stack.

### Liquidity Mechanics and the Redemption Risk [00:15:26]

Nemeth highlights that private credit funds are allergic to cash drag and have unfunded commitments they are contractually obligated to meet, leaving minimal liquidity buffers. Cliffwater, for example, claimed two years of liquidity without counting its contractual funding obligations. Blue Owl recently disclosed a nearly 40% redemption request from a small subset of investors, and Nemeth warns that game theory favors early exit at stated NAV before asset sales compress values further.

### Life Insurance Industry as the Systemic Weak Link [00:26:13]

The US annuity and life insurance sector holds over $10 trillion in assets against roughly $658 billion in capital surplus, implying average 17x leverage versus Lehman's 30x at failure. PE-backed insurers are the most aggressive asset allocators, while mutuals like New York Life are more conservative. With only a 6% asset decline needed to trigger industry-wide receivership, any credit shock across private credit, high yield, or investment-grade corporates simultaneously could cascade. Offshore reinsurers backstop these balance sheets, but US regulators cannot examine their holdings due to lobbying-secured carve-outs, and Nemeth believes actual reserves cover roughly half of stated liabilities.
