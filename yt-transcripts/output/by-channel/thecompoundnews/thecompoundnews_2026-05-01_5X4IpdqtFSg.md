# How to Find 100 Bagger Stocks

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=5X4IpdqtFSg
**Duration:** 93:04

## Summary

Episode 240 of The Compound and Friends features Neeraj Khemlani and Matt Ankrum, discussing Ankrum's book The Coffee Can Investor and his strategy for identifying 100-bagger stocks. The conversation draws on Bessembinder's research that only 4% of stocks create net wealth, Charlie Munger's inversion framework, and Ankrum's own decades of research to argue that focusing on eliminating the low-quality 96% improves an investor's odds substantially. The episode also addresses AI disruption of incumbent SaaS companies, using TechnologyOne as an example of a high-consequence vertical software firm unlikely to be easily displaced. A live appearance by Ankrum's daughter Morgan grounds the abstract strategy in its real-world purpose: building generational wealth and financial literacy.

## Key Takeaways

- Only 4% of stocks from 1926-2018 created net wealth, so eliminating the low-quality 96% is more actionable than searching for the 4%.
- High-quality companies (defined by consistent returns on tangible assets above 15-30%) demonstrate an anomalous market inefficiency: higher returns with lower volatility, contradicting standard finance theory.
- Nearly every 100-bagger in Ankrum's study experienced a peak drawdown of approximately 70%, requiring investors to endure existential-seeming declines.
- The law of large numbers is frequently misapplied to argue high-growth companies cannot sustain growth; Amazon's AWS continuing to accelerate at $150 billion annualized is a counterexample.
- AI disruption of SaaS is real but indiscriminate market selling ignores the distinction between low-consequence software (easily replaceable) and high-consequence software (deeply embedded, regulated, mission-critical).
- The coffee can strategy requires 20 concentrated positions held for 30 years, with an extremely high barrier to sell to prevent emotional decision-making.
- Financial literacy—teaching children how to invest in businesses rather than trade stocks—is positioned as a generational obligation that school systems fail to fulfill.

## Detailed Notes

### The Coffee Can Origin Story [00:07:55]

The coffee can concept traces to 1980s portfolio manager Robert Kirby at Capital Group, who noticed a client's husband had piggybacked on buy recommendations but never sold. A single position in what was then Xerox grew from $5,000 to $800,000. Kirby wrote about the experience for the Journal of Portfolio Management, inspiring Ankrum to study stocks that multiplied 100 times.

### Defining and Visualizing 100-Baggers [00:12:27]

A 100-bagger over 30 years requires roughly 16.6% annual compounding, approximately double the S&P 500's long-run average. The gap between the 100-bagger and market index appears small for the first decade but becomes an 80-fold difference by year 30, illustrating why the strategy feels unrewarding early. Warren Buffett's wealth creation—98-99% generated after age 65—is cited as the canonical example of compounding's back-weighted nature.

### The Fastenal Epiphany: Psychology of Holding [00:16:30]

As a Janus analyst, Ankrum correctly predicted Fastenal would miss earnings and sold the position, which subsequently fell 55% while the market fell 15%. The stock then recovered and rose 19-fold from the sale price (vs. the S&P rising 4x over the same period). The lesson: superior fundamental knowledge did not prevent a costly sale, because psychology—not analysis—drives sell decisions during drawdowns. Amazon was initially feared to be an existential threat to Fastenal but ultimately became one of its largest customers.

### Bessembinder Research and the Quality Anomaly [00:29:06]

Bessembinder's study of 1926-2018 found 71% of individual stocks failed to match rolling 10-year index returns, only 1 in 5 survived and outperformed over 20 years, and only 4% created net wealth over T-bills. The top 50 wealth-creating companies accounted for 50% of all net wealth; 84% of those were high-quality firms by Ankrum's definition. GMO's Jeremy Grantham separately documented that high-quality stocks deliver higher returns with lower volatility—an anomaly that should not exist under efficient market theory but persists because quality companies are boring and structurally underpriced.

### AI Disruption and High-Consequence SaaS [00:59:24]

The discussion distinguishes low-consequence software (marketing tools, websites—replaceable if AI alternatives are close enough) from high-consequence software where errors carry financial, regulatory, or personal liability. TechnologyOne, serving Australian local governments and universities, is highlighted as an example: a tax-assessment error would mean $14 million in miscollected funds and personal CEO accountability. Incumbents also benefit from years of AI integration (C3.ai cited as 11-12 year user of Nvidia GPUs) and are expanding AI capabilities on top of existing system-of-record data advantages. Historical analogy: in 1999-2000, Amazon was expected to destroy brick-and-mortar retail, but today 15 of the top 20 e-commerce websites are traditional retailers who adapted.

### The Portfolio Structure and Generational Purpose [01:16:30]

Ankrum's coffee can consists of 20 stocks at $250,000 each ($5 million total) for his three daughters, with a 30-year horizon and a stated target of approaching $500 million. As of writing, 13 of the planned 20 positions are held, with 8 in software stocks now under AI-disruption pressure. The family's experience—sharing the portfolio with daughters and involving them in research—is offered as a model for teaching financial literacy, which Ankrum argues is conspicuously absent from school curricula.
