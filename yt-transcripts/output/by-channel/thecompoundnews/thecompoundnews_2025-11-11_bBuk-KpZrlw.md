# US Stock Market Flashes Dreaded “Titanic” Signal

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=bBuk-KpZrlw
**Duration:** 68:08

## Summary

Josh Brown and Michael Batnick co-host a wide-ranging market discussion covering technical signals, valuation, consumer health, and speculative stocks. They critically examine the 'Titanic signal' in the context of a top-heavy index dominated by Mag 7 names, arguing that breadth deterioration in smaller caps is less meaningful than it would historically appear. The episode also debates whether fast-casual restaurant weakness reflects genuine lower-income consumer stress or simply overvaluation and overexpansion in those companies. Key segments cover Schwab's public rejection of sports-betting integration, Tesla's pay package for Musk, a 'buy the dip' review of MSTR, DKNG, and CoreWeave, and a make-the-case analysis concluding investors should monitor but not own standalone quantum computing stocks.

## Key Takeaways

- The Titanic signal's historical predictive value may be diminished because index concentration in Mag 7 names creates structural noise in broad breadth metrics like the S&P 1500.
- Mag 7 earnings growth—not multiple expansion—has been the primary driver of elevated valuations since 2022, with Jurrien Timmer's data showing a fourfold price gain alongside a threefold earnings gain.
- Charles Schwab's CEO publicly drew a line against integrating prediction markets or sports betting, while DraftKings is pivoting to offense by launching its own predictions platform.
- Prediction market growth (led by Kalshi and Polymarket) is a genuine competitive threat to DraftKings and Flutter, but Brown and Batnick believe the 43% and 25% drawdowns respectively may be overdone.
- Tesla's trillion-dollar pay package for Musk requires extraordinary milestones over 10 years; both hosts argue shareholders who oppose it are misunderstanding Tesla's core investment thesis around autonomy and robotics.
- Standalone quantum computing stocks (Rigetti, IonQ, D-Wave) are viewed as too early-stage to invest in, with IBM and Alphabet seen as better risk-adjusted ways to gain quantum exposure.
- Robinhood was revealed as the S&P 500's best-performing stock year-to-date, up roughly 300% with a market cap of approximately $118 billion.

## Detailed Notes

### Titanic Signal and Market Breadth [00:02:37]

The hosts discuss the 'Titanic signal' from Turning Point Market Research, which flags when S&P 1500 stocks record more 52-week lows than highs for five consecutive sessions near a multi-year high. Historical results since 1928 show only 40% positive outcomes over the next four weeks. Brown and Batnick push back by examining what stocks are actually on the 52-week low list (Airbnb, Adobe, Trade Desk, Chipotle, Nike) and argue these are largely idiosyncratic situations, not systemic stress.

### Index Concentration and Valuation Context [00:09:46]

A chart showing the growing spread between median stock drawdown and the S&P 500's overall drawdown since early 2023 illustrates why breadth signals are noisier in the current top-heavy market. Jurrien Timmer's 5-year CAPE ratio is presented as more relevant than the traditional 10-year version given how much the index composition has changed. The cap-weighted trailing PE of 26x compares to an equal-weighted PE near 18x, highlighting the valuation bifurcation.

### Schwab, Sports Betting, and Prediction Markets [00:17:36]

Schwab CEO Rick Wuester's statement at the Impact Conference is highlighted: he argued only 5% of gambling app users profit, and that conflating betting with long-term investing is harmful to young investors. Public brokerage's Wall Street Journal ad positioning itself against 'bookie' behavior is praised as on-brand. DraftKings' 43% year-to-date drawdown and Flutter's 25% decline are discussed alongside Robinhood's growing prediction markets business ($100M annualized run rate). Both hosts believe fear of prediction market disruption is overstated for DraftKings, making it the most interesting dip candidate of the three 'falling knives' discussed.

### K-Shaped Economy Debate [00:36:36]

Torsten Slok's Atlanta Fed wage growth chart showing the lowest quintile decelerating sharply is contextualized against pandemic-era gains for low-wage workers driven by tight labor supply. Brown argues the real K-shaped story is asset ownership vs. non-ownership, not just wage levels. PNC Financial's commentary showing robust consumer spending even at lower income levels is cited to counter narratives from struggling fast-casual restaurants. Stocks like DIN (Applebee's/IHOP parent, down 60% over 3 years) and DRI (Darden, near multi-month lows) are presented as potential evidence of a real bifurcation in consumer spending.

### Tesla Pay Package and Elon Musk [00:48:40]

Tesla shareholders approved Musk's trillion-dollar compensation package 75-25, contingent on targets including sextupling Tesla's market cap to $8.5 trillion, growing EBITDA 24-fold to $400 billion, deploying 1 million humanoid robots, and 1 million robo-taxis—all over 10 years with no salary or bonus. Both hosts argue opposition is misguided because the bull case for Tesla is inseparable from Musk's leadership in autonomy and robotics.

### Buyable Dips: MSTR, DKNG, CoreWeave [00:53:27]

Strategy (MSTR) is rejected: its MNAV premium has compressed from over 3x to 1.2x and Batnick sees no structural reason for a premium over direct Bitcoin ownership. DraftKings (DKNG) at a 58% drawdown and near historical support around $30 is the preferred choice, contingent on prediction market fears being overstated. CoreWeave at a 50% drawdown from its high is dismissed due to a short selling controversy over its GPU depreciation methodology (6-year life vs. an 18-month Nvidia upgrade cycle flagged by Michael Burry and Jim Chanos).

### Quantum Computing Stocks and Mystery Chart [01:00:07]

Brown makes the case to avoid pure-play quantum stocks: Rigetti (superconducting, same architecture as Alphabet and IBM but smaller), IonQ (trapped-ion, furthest commercialized but still only selling lab access), and D-Wave (annealing, nearest to practical applications). IBM and Alphabet are recommended as safer vehicles for quantum exposure. The mystery chart segment closes the show with Robinhood revealed as the S&P 500's top performer year-to-date, up approximately 300% to a $118 billion market cap.
