# The Most Confusing Rally of All Time | Animal Spirits 410

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=rZqAgXDb94I
**Duration:** 62:36

## Summary

In episode 410 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson wrestle with the paradox of a sharp stock market recovery in late April 2025 that returned the S&P 500 and NASDAQ to near pre-Liberation Day levels despite deteriorating real-economy indicators. They assess breadth-thrust signals suggesting the rally has historical precedent, while remaining skeptical that fundamental damage from tariffs is fully priced in. The hosts also cover shipping-volume collapses, recession signals from airlines and restaurant operators, and broader themes including AI adoption, wealth concentration, and the rising age of first-time homebuyers.

## Key Takeaways

- The April 2025 stock market recovery confounded both hosts; while breadth-thrust data historically signals further gains, neither believes tariff damage is fully reflected in equity prices.
- Hedge funds have been net sellers of over $1 trillion in equities year-to-date while retail investors have bought roughly $50 billion a month, raising questions about who holds the 'smart money' position.
- Shipping data is flashing severe recession warning signs: Port of LA expects a 35% import volume drop and bookings out of China fell 60% in a week, with potential empty-shelf effects in consumer staples by summer 2025.
- The hosts argue tariff damage will hit Main Street harder than Wall Street, with the Russell 2000 underperformance cited as a proxy for small-business pain.
- AI tools are subsidizing younger users and beginning to reshape analytical jobs, with the hosts predicting a significant reduction in entry-level financial analyst hiring.
- Wealth inequality data shows the top 1% owns 50% of the stock market; the number of US billionaires has risen from under 1,400 in 2021 to nearly 2,000.
- The hosts note that most people alive today have experienced only two months of actual recession over fifteen years, meaning the behavioral and psychological effects of a real downturn would likely be underestimated.

## Detailed Notes

### The Confusing Rally: Breadth Thrusts vs. Fundamental Damage [00:02:24]

The S&P 500 recovered from a worst closing drawdown of 11.2% in April to nearly flat month-to-date by Monday of recording. Ryan Dietrich's breadth-thrust data shows that reversals from panic selling to panic buying within 10 trading days have historically preceded higher markets 100% of the time at 6 and 12 months. Despite this, Batnick and Carlson remain skeptical: earnings, sentiment, and valuations have not adjusted for tariff risk, and this breadth thrust occurred before any visible economic damage.

### Retail Buyers vs. Hedge Fund Sellers [00:14:52]

Wall Street Journal reporting cited by the hosts shows hedge funds have sold over $1 trillion net in equities in 2025, while retail investors have consistently bought $50 billion per month according to JP Morgan data. The hosts attribute hedge-fund selling primarily to mechanical risk-limit triggers rather than fundamental views. Meanwhile, retail day-traders and speculative investors continue to treat volatility as a buying opportunity, with some carrying seven-figure portfolios concentrated in Bitcoin and leveraged crypto-related equities.

### Tariff Transmission: Shipping, Supply Chains, and Empty Shelves [00:22:13]

Amazon announced plans to display tariff cost components alongside listed prices, which the hosts view positively as it prevents companies from quietly retaining margin after tariff removal. Port of Los Angeles data pointed to a 35% expected drop in import volumes and a 60% weekly decline in China bookings, mirroring the weakest shipping quarter since 2015. Around 95% of US stroller imports and three-quarters of toys and infant furniture come from China, making these product categories particularly vulnerable to supply disruption in the near term.

### Recession Signals from Corporate Calls [00:31:50]

Southwest Airlines CEO described current airline-industry conditions as a recession. Chipotle cited economic concerns as the primary reason consumers are reducing restaurant visit frequency. UPS announced 20,000 layoffs citing declining demand with no stock rebound. Capital One, however, reported improving delinquency rates and described the US consumer as being in good shape, illustrating the bifurcation between upper-income resilience and Main Street stress.

### Wealth Inequality and AI Adoption Themes [00:38:44]

The Atlantic article highlighted that AI companies are subsidizing student and young-adult access in a pattern similar to VC-funded cheap ride-sharing and food delivery in the 2010s. The hosts discussed the top-1% owning 50% of the stock market versus the bottom 50% owning 1%, and noted that the 19 richest US households saw $1 trillion in wealth gains in 2024. The median age of first-time US homebuyers has risen from 32-33 pre-pandemic to 38 today, reflecting housing affordability pressure that has yet to become a major political issue.

### Behavioral Finance: Recession Inexperience and Investor Psychology [00:36:51]

Batnick and Carlson argue that Americans are out of practice with genuine recessions, having experienced only two months of contraction in fifteen years—and those months were cushioned by extraordinary fiscal stimulus. They suggest that if unemployment rises materially, the psychological and behavioral reaction could overshoot the actual economic damage. Investors who panic-sold during the April drawdown face the classic behavioral barrier of re-entry, and the episode frames the rally as psychologically difficult to capitalize on for those who exited.
