## Summary

Josh Brown interviews Yale professor Jeffrey Sonnenfeld, founder of the Chief Executive Leadership Institute, about his new book 'Trump's Ten Commandments,' which analyzes the predictable strategic patterns behind Donald Trump's leadership style. Sonnenfeld argues that Trump's seemingly chaotic behavior follows consistent strategic logic, including a strong preference for divide-and-conquer tactics against coalitions and alliances. The interview explores what these patterns mean for corporate leaders, boards, and investors when navigating political and regulatory risk.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump's leadership behavior is patterned and strategic, not random or impulsive, according to Sonnenfeld's 25 years of research.
- Trump's core strategy is divide-and-conquer: he systematically undermines collective power bases including business coalitions, NATO, and trade alliances to maximize his bargaining advantage.
- Companies that engage Trump privately in small groups tend to achieve better outcomes than those who oppose him publicly, which risks retaliation.
- Collective private action by multiple CEOs (e.g., Walmart, Home Depot, Costco post-Liberation Day) has proven effective at influencing policy without triggering Trump's ego-based retaliation.
- Public humiliation is the most dangerous strategy against Trump; he retains grievances from perceived slights for years.
- Trump prefers loyalty and audience over expertise; the absence of independent expert advisors in Trump 2.0 means fewer internal checks on potentially damaging policy decisions.
- The book is positioned as useful for both Trump supporters seeking to understand how to help him and critics seeking effective counter-messaging strategies.

## Detailed Notes

### Book Origins and Sonnenfeld's Trump Relationship [00:02:12]

Sonnenfeld has known Trump longer than anyone in either Trump administration outside of family members, having first connected when he critiqued The Apprentice weekly in the Wall Street Journal for Season 1. The book was partly encouraged by Jared Kushner, who told Sonnenfeld that people need to understand Trump before they can effectively support or oppose him.

### The Divide and Conquer Commandment [00:05:55]

The second commandment Sonnenfeld highlights is divide and conquer: Trump consistently attacks coalitions, alliances, and collective power bases — including NAFTA/USMCA (which he created), NATO, the Business Roundtable, and the EU — because he fears collective action more than individual actors. He prefers bilateral, private deals where he holds a stronger bargaining position than facing adversaries collectively.

### Corporate Responses and Case Studies [00:08:50]

The Harley Davidson case illustrates the risks CEOs face: after the company built a Thailand plant in response to EU retaliatory tariffs, Trump called for a consumer boycott, the stock plummeted, and the CEO was fired. By contrast, IBM has navigated Trump successfully for 120 years without lobbying fees by engaging in private, small-group meetings. After Liberation Day, Walmart, Home Depot, and Costco CEOs met privately with Trump, demonstrating the value of collective-but-private engagement.

### Strategic Patterns: Grandiosity, Pragmatism, and Loyalty [00:17:40]

Sonnenfeld argues Trump is fundamentally pragmatic despite surface-level volatility, citing his ability to pivot from attacking someone publicly to calling them a close friend. Trump values audience size and winning above ideology, and can work with people across political lines including Mayor Mondavi. In Trump 2.0, the absence of independent expert voices (unlike Trump 1.0 where challengers like Jared Kushner helped moderate H-1B restrictions) means policy decisions rely more on loyalists who may reinforce rather than correct poor choices.

### Implications for Investors and Business Leaders [00:30:10]

The book's audience includes both Trump supporters who want to understand how to advance his agenda and critics who want to develop effective counter-strategies. Sonnenfeld suggests that understanding the commandments — rather than being repeatedly surprised by them — is the fundamental prerequisite for effective engagement. Scott Bessent's policy influence is cited as a positive market signal.
