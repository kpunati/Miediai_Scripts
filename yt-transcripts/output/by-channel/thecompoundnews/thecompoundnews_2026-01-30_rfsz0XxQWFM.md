# What Tom Lee's Worried About in 2026 | TCAF 227

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=rfsz0XxQWFM
**Duration:** 77:40

## Summary

In episode 227 of The Compound and Friends, Fundstrat's Tom Lee presents a constructive but cautious 2026 outlook: he expects at least 10% full-year gains in equities but anticipates a painful mid-year drawdown — potentially 20% — driven by policy shocks or a new Fed being tested rather than an economic recession. Lee highlights precious metals as the standout bull market of the moment, argues the software sector's bear market reflects AI disruption rather than broader economic weakness, and maintains that market breadth is broadening in a healthy way. He also addresses the cryptocurrency bear market, Bitmine's Ethereum treasury strategy, and why robotics may be the next major secular investment theme.

## Key Takeaways

- Tom Lee's 2026 base case mirrors 2025: strong start, a bear-market-feeling drawdown (possibly 20%), then a strong year-end recovery for a net gain of at least 10%.
- The precious metals rally is historically extreme in trading volume and price action, with Tom DeMark still seeing room to run; gold and silver are attracting capital that historically flowed to crypto.
- Software stocks are in an accelerating bear market because AI is threatening subscription-based SaaS models and reducing tech employment — Microsoft lost $400 billion in market cap in a single session despite a largely in-line quarter.
- A massive deleveraging event on a crypto exchange — bigger than FTX — liquidated over two million accounts and crushed Coinbase and other names; Tom Lee believes the selling is near exhaustion.
- Blockchain infrastructure is being adopted by major financial institutions (Standard Chartered, UBS, Fidelity) for settlement layers, which Lee views as structurally bullish for Ethereum over Bitcoin.
- The Granny Shots strategy reached $4.4 billion AUM in roughly one year and outperformed the market by ~700 basis points in 2025, with 2026 composition tilting toward industrials and basic materials.
- Robotics and autonomy — illustrated by Tesla pivoting away from Model S/X toward humanoid robots and the Cybercab — could be the next multi-year bull market theme, though Lee acknowledges near-term labor displacement risks.

## Detailed Notes

### 2026 Market Outlook and Macro Backdrop [00:06:09]

Tom Lee expects 2026 to resemble 2025 with a bullish first half, a significant drawdown (potentially 20%) in the middle of the year caused by policy shocks from the White House or a market test of the new Fed chair, and a strong recovery to end the year with at least 10% gains. Positive indicators include the Rule of First Five Days being met (when both the first week and first month are positive, the probability of an up-year jumps to ~92% over 75 years of data), improving global earnings growth, broadening market breadth, and AI/blockchain tailwinds. Headwinds include elevated valuations after three consecutive strong years, subprime consumer stress in auto loans, margin debt at record highs (~$1.2 trillion as of November 2025 vs. $710 billion in July 2023), and aggressive White House policy actions.

### Precious Metals Rally and Commodity Sector [00:14:33]

Energy and basic materials are Fundstrat's top sector picks for 2026 following years of significant underperformance — the level of underperformance over the prior three years was at a historically rare extreme on a z-score basis. The current precious metals bull market is parabolic: daily trading volume in precious metal ETFs hit $64 billion versus prior spikes of around $20 billion. Tom DeMark, now an adviser at Bitmine, believes gold can reach $8,900 and that the move more closely resembles 1979-1980 than an early-stage trend. Lee notes the physical metals market is even tighter than the financial market, supporting the rally.

### Tech Divergence and Software Bear Market [00:22:18]

Large-cap tech correlation is collapsing: on the day of recording, Microsoft was down 12% while Meta was up 10% — an historically unprecedented divergence for names that typically move together. The application software sub-industry is in a deep bear market (IGV down ~25%), with names like Workday, ServiceNow, and Salesforce all sharply lower; the market appears to be pricing in AI as a disruptor of SaaS subscription models and reducing technology employment. Lee interprets Microsoft's $400 billion single-day market cap loss — despite a largely in-line quarter — as a negative sign that investors are punishing even modest disappointments, a signal that the sector's premium is being unwound.

### Cryptocurrency Bear Market and Bitmine Strategy [00:46:41]

Crypto entered a bear phase after October when a pricing exploit on a major exchange triggered the largest automated deleveraging event in crypto history, bigger than FTX's collapse, liquidating over two million accounts and wiping out roughly one-third of all market makers. Coinbase is in a ~50% drawdown. Lee sees the selling as near exhaustion, with Ethereum possibly touching $2,400 before bottoming, coinciding with a peak in precious metals as risk appetite rotates back. Bitmine holds ~3.52% of all Ethereum and generates ~$400 million/year in staking rewards plus $40 million from cash; institutional holders include Morgan Stanley, ARK, and Fidelity. Lee argues digital treasury companies with scale (only MicroStrategy and Bitmine have actually grown their holdings since October) offer leveraged upside when the underlying recovers.

### Robotics, AI, and the Future of Labor [01:04:58]

Tesla's decision to discontinue Model S and Model X in favor of full focus on the Cybercab and humanoid robots — with Elon Musk projecting consumer-available humanoid robots by 2027 — is cited as a major signal that robotics is transitioning from concept to product. Lee frames robotics as a force multiplier for labor in the context of a structural 10-year shortage of prime-age workers. He draws an analogy to flash-frozen food technology, which reduced farming employment from 30% to 5% of the workforce over 20 years while dramatically improving living standards, suggesting the transition could be similarly disruptive but ultimately surplus-generating. He acknowledges near-term risks of civil unrest during the adjustment period, especially given wealth concentration in the top 50% of households.

### Market Broadening and Granny Shots Update [01:11:48]

Market breadth is broadening: the equal-weight RSP is nearly flat on a day when the NASDAQ 100 was down ~96 basis points and Microsoft down 12%, illustrating rotation into the other 493 names. Tom Lee views this broadening as a healthy, bullish sign for institutional stock-pickers who cannot concentrate in Magnificent Seven. The Granny Shots strategy — now totaling $4.4 billion in AUM across three products (original Granny Shots, small-mid cap Granny J, and Income Granny) — outperformed the market by ~700 basis points in 2025 and is tilting more toward industrials and basic materials in 2026. International equity markets are also highlighted as attractively positioned, with the dollar potentially weakening serving as an additional tailwind.
