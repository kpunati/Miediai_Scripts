# Why Valuations Don't Matter | TCAF 202

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-08-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=bXfr7GZ95mY
**Duration:** 80:36

## Summary

Episode 202 of The Compound and Friends features Adam Parker, founder of Trivariate Research and former Morgan Stanley chief US equity strategist, discussing the Fed's pivot to 'efficient' monetary policy language as a substitute for 'higher for longer,' the blowout earnings from Microsoft and Meta, and a detailed thesis for why traditional valuation-based stock selection no longer works. Parker argues that cheap stocks are cheap because fundamentals are legitimately impaired, valuation-neutral quant strategies dominate short-term price action, and margin expansion at high-quality businesses makes expensive multiples rational. The episode closes with discussion of AI's measurable effect on entry-level job formation, with data showing rising graduate unemployment despite historically strong overall employment.

## Key Takeaways

- The Fed's new 'efficient monetary policy' language is functionally equivalent to 'higher for longer' and significantly reduced September cut probabilities.
- Microsoft and Meta delivered strong earnings but the market's muted intraday reaction reflects how far stocks had already rallied from April lows and the difficulty of knowing the 'whisper number'.
- Parker's core valuation thesis: cheap stocks stay cheap because the market correctly anticipates impaired fundamentals, while high-multiple stocks reflect genuine margin superiority and durability.
- Large multi-strategy quant funds run valuation-neutral books with multi-hour holding periods, meaning their activity is structurally disconnected from the fundamental work of long-term investors.
- AI capex at hyperscalers (Microsoft at ~$120B annualized run rate, Meta doubling year-over-year) is the defining investment controversy — bulls argue returns arrive by 2027-2028, bears worry about depreciation burdens on margins.
- Entry-level job displacement by AI is already measurable: 50% drop in tech entry-level hires since 2019 and graduate unemployment at 6.6% vs. 4% overall.
- Parker favors drug distributors like McKesson as AI margin-expansion plays given their thin current margins and potential for productivity-driven improvement.

## Detailed Notes

### Fed Policy and Rate Outlook [00:16:10]

The July FOMC held rates steady and introduced the term 'efficient' monetary policy, which Parker and Brown interpret as a euphemism for 'higher for longer' designed to avoid antagonizing the White House. With unemployment at 4.1% — lower than 84% of historical readings — Parker argues the Fed has little reason to cut. September cut probabilities immediately fell to roughly 45% from above 80%.

### Mag 7 Earnings: Microsoft and Meta [00:25:32]

Microsoft reported strong Azure growth with a $368 billion contracted backlog and over $30 billion in quarterly capex guidance; R&D nearly tripled over a decade but fell as a share of revenue to 68% cloud gross margins. Meta reported 3.4 billion daily active users across its family of apps, raised its capex midpoint, and is trading at roughly 27-30 times forward earnings. Both stocks rallied but faded intraday, which Parker flags as a mild concern given the magnitude of the pre-earnings run-up.

### Why Valuation Doesn't Work [00:53:20]

Parker outlines six reasons: (1) cheap stocks are cheap because fundamentals are correctly priced as impaired; (2) large quant multi-strats run valuation-neutral books on 3-hour to 2-day horizons, dominating short-term price action; (3) retail investors are less valuation-sensitive; (4) the market has become more anticipatory, correctly discounting impairment earlier; (5) high-gross-margin businesses deserve structural premium multiples; (6) AI and cloud computing have fundamentally changed cost structures. Parker recommends avoiding the most expensive and cheapest deciles but ignoring valuation in the middle, focusing instead on margin trends.

### AI's Impact on Entry-Level Jobs [01:04:48]

A Wall Street Journal data piece showed entry-level tech hires down 50% since 2019, and graduate unemployment for 21-24-year-olds at 6.6% vs. 4% overall. Parker notes firms now hesitate to train junior employees when AI can produce 70%-accurate first drafts instantly. While the long-run view is that new job categories will form (AI quality control, legal oversight of AI errors), the near-term disruption for new graduates is severe and unlikely to reverse quickly.
