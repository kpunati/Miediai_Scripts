## Summary

Episode 468 of Animal Spirits opens with Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson dissecting a sharp Friday stock market selloff driven paradoxically by a strong labor report, which raised questions about whether AI is actually disrupting employment. The hosts debate the SpaceX IPO, the Mag 7 underperformance relative to the broader S&P 500, Vanguard VOO crossing $1 trillion in ETF assets, and a deepening crypto winter amid risk-on market conditions. Additional topics include US wealth concentration at the top, real estate affordability collapse in small tourist towns, new car sticker shock, and personal finance lessons around retirement age rules.

## Key Takeaways

- A strong June 2026 jobs report triggered a selloff in AI and semiconductor stocks, with the hosts arguing the data show AI has not yet meaningfully disrupted the labor market.
- Tech and communication services now account for roughly half the S&P 500, making it nearly impossible to build a diversified US equity portfolio that avoids AI-related concentration risk.
- Vanguard VOO hit $1 trillion AUM for the first time, reflecting a broad generational shift toward passive investing among younger investors, including a doubling of Gen Z IRA participation.
- Despite a fully risk-on environment, Bitcoin and crypto are underperforming and losing mindshare to AI, which the hosts see as unusually bearish for the asset class.
- SpaceX's index inclusion in the S&P 500 was delayed due to institutional pushback; at roughly 70 basis points of the index, the financial impact on passive holders is described as negligible.
- The top 3% of US families now hold over half of national wealth, with middle-class wealth share falling significantly since 1989, raising political rather than purely economic concerns.
- New vehicle prices for mainstream SUVs have reached $1,000-$1,500 per month in lease payments, illustrating persistent consumer sticker shock even as broader inflation has moderated.

## Detailed Notes

### Friday Selloff and AI Labor Market Debate [00:01:00]

A better-than-expected non-farm payrolls report on Friday triggered a sharp selloff in AI and semiconductor names; Micron fell 13%, SMH dropped 9.2%, and the QQQ fell nearly 5% in one session. The hosts note the irony that a strong jobs report called AI's labor disruption narrative into question. Ben argues AI has not yet appeared in employment data, while Michael believes disruption is coming but will be more visible during the next recession.

### Dip Buying, Market Psychology, and Price Driving Narrative [00:08:51]

Historical data cited shows dip buyers have been rewarded: since the start of the bull market, the S&P 500 was higher four days after a 2% down day in 11 of 13 instances. The hosts observe that market price movements shape investor narrative in real time — bearish arguments feel more convincing when prices fall and bullish ones when prices rise. 2026 has had only 11 down-1% days versus a historical average of 31 per year.

### Index Concentration and the SpaceX IPO [00:12:55]

Tech and communication services now total roughly 49% of the S&P 500, while the Mag 7 is actually underperforming the broader index because the 2026 rally has been led by memory and semiconductor names in the 493. SpaceX's S&P 500 inclusion was delayed at least a year after institutional blowback; analysis suggests it will represent roughly 70 basis points of major index ETFs, making the valuation debate mostly academic for passive holders. Morgan Stanley estimates SpaceX revenue at $3.4 trillion by 2040.

### VOO Hits $1 Trillion, Retail Trading Boom, and Crypto Winter [00:41:20]

Vanguard VOO became the first ETF to cross $1 trillion AUM with roughly $70 billion of 2026 inflows; Gen Z Roth IRA contributions grew 65% in Q1 2026. Simultaneously, retail's share of stock trading has doubled to 20% since 2010, with over 20 SpaceX-linked ETFs already filed. Crypto is lagging in a fully risk-on environment — the hosts argue this is the most bearish signal for the asset class — and even Michael Saylor reportedly sold Bitcoin before repurchasing $100 million worth.

### Wealth Inequality, Real Estate, Private Credit, and Car Prices [00:44:38]

The top 3% of US families now hold 53% of national wealth versus 26% previously, while the middle-class share fell 8 percentage points since 1989. Small tourist towns like Petoskey, Michigan have seen median home prices triple from $310,000 to $1.1 million since 2020, crowding out teachers and service workers. Blackstone's private credit fund faced 10% redemption requests in Q2, up from 8% in Q1, with the gate mechanism capping outflows at 5%. New mainstream SUV lease payments now routinely exceed $1,000-$1,500 per month, reflecting persistent vehicle price inflation.
