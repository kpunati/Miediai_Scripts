# What is Return Stacking?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=6VeAsD72_ZQ
**Duration:** 34:01

## Summary

This episode of Ask The Compound covers a range of personal finance and portfolio construction topics, anchored by a guest interview with Corey Hoffstein of Newfound Research on return stacking. Return stacking employs modest leverage through futures markets to overlay diversifying assets such as bonds or managed futures on top of an existing equity portfolio without reducing core stock exposure. The episode also addresses Ben Carlson's index-fund-only philosophy versus stock picking, a retirement spending heuristic called the four-year rule, and the inadvisability of using buy now pay later schemes to fund stock market investments.

## Key Takeaways

- Return stacking is a leveraged portfolio technique that layers diversifying asset classes on top of a traditional stock-and-bond allocation without requiring the investor to reduce core equity exposure.
- The cost of leverage in return stacking products is approximately equal to T-bill rates, so the incremental asset stacked must beat cash rather than beat the bond market.
- Newfound Research's LICE framework warns against leverage that is illiquid, concentrated, or excessive — the goal is modest, liquid, diversifying leverage only.
- Ben Carlson argues that index investing sidesteps large permanent losses, citing that blue-chip names like Boeing and Nike are down 60% from all-time highs while the S&P 500 remains nearly flat.
- The four-year rule prescribes accumulating four years of living expenses in cash before retirement, using the cash reserve during market downturns and replenishing it after sustained market recoveries.
- Using buy now pay later to invest in stocks is characterized as an asset-liability mismatch given the short repayment timeline versus the risk horizon needed for equity investing.
- For late-start investors, consistently funding low-cost index funds such as Vanguard target-date funds is endorsed as a rational long-term approach, with plans expected to evolve over time.

## Detailed Notes

### Return Stacking Explained [00:02:59]

Guest Corey Hoffstein explains that return stacking derives from institutional portable alpha strategies dating to the 1980s at PIMCO. The term itself was coined by Rodrigo Gordo. The core mechanic is using futures (requiring only ~5% collateral for $100 of bond exposure) to gain diversifying exposure without reducing the existing equity allocation. The leverage cost approximates the Fed funds rate or T-bill rate.

### When Leverage Is Responsible [00:08:41]

Hoffstein introduces the LICE framework: avoid leverage that is Illiquid, Concentrated, or Excessive. He argues that using leverage to introduce truly uncorrelated diversifiers (e.g., managed futures, gold) into a portfolio is fundamentally different from borrowing to buy more of the same assets. The 2022 environment, where stocks and bonds fell together, demonstrated the portfolio vulnerability that return stacking is designed to mitigate.

### Why Ben Doesn't Pick Stocks [00:14:33]

Ben Carlson explains that concentration risk in individual stocks produces deep permanent drawdowns even among blue-chip names, while index funds capture market returns without the idiosyncratic loss risk. He acknowledges missing out on Google's 230%+ gain from its 2023 lows but emphasizes the cognitive burden of monitoring individual positions as a key deterrent. He currently holds only two small stock positions — Nvidia and Nike — alongside an otherwise all-index portfolio.

### Four-Year Rule for Retirement [00:22:18]

Originating from a retiree named John Feast who retired at the peak of the 2000 dotcom bubble, the four-year rule prescribes saving four years of living expenses (net of pension or Social Security) in cash or equivalents before retiring. During downturns, spending is drawn from cash; once the market has recovered for 18-24 months, the cash reserve is replenished. Carlson plans to publish the full strategy on wealthofcommonsense.com.

### Buy Now Pay Later vs. Credit Cards for Young Investors [00:25:54]

A college student asked whether zero-interest BNPL could be used as investment leverage using time-value-of-money logic. Carlson and Hill reject the idea: the short payback window creates an asset-liability mismatch with equity market risk, BNPL does not build credit history like a credit card, and tax implications erode any return differential. The preferred alternative is a rewards credit card paid off monthly, some of which allow cash-back to be deposited directly into brokerage accounts.
