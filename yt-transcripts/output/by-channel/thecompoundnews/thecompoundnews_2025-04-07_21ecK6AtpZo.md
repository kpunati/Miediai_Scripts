# Former Trump Trade Official on What's Really Happening

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=21ecK6AtpZo
**Duration:** 38:28

## Summary

Josh Brown interviews Steve Pavlick, former Trump Treasury official now at Renaissance Macro, during the acute market volatility surrounding the April 2025 reciprocal tariff rollout. Pavlick provides an insider perspective on the internal contradictions of the tariff strategy, explaining that the policy simultaneously pursues revenue generation, trade concessions, and industrial policy — goals that fundamentally conflict with each other. He argues that the broader strategic logic may be to isolate China by pulling other trading partners toward the US, and that the execution — particularly the surprise scale of the tariffs and the changing justifications — has undermined US negotiating credibility and business confidence.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump is the sole decision-maker on tariffs; cabinet members including Bessent and Lutnik communicate but do not set policy, and shifting public justifications reflect competing internal plans.
- The three goals of tariffs — revenue, trade concessions, and national-security industrial protection — are mutually contradictory, and the administration's failure to pick one has destroyed business confidence.
- A universal baseline rate was deliberately set alongside reciprocal rates to prevent Chinese circumvention via third-country re-routing.
- Sectoral tariffs protecting steel, aluminum, semiconductors, and pharmaceuticals are likely to endure regardless of broader negotiations, given bipartisan support for strategic manufacturing.
- The isolation of China from its trading partners may be the underlying strategic objective, which would make history judge the policy more favorably if successful.
- Unlike Trump's first term, there are no offsetting tax cuts providing a tailwind — this round is merely extending existing rates, a headwind-prevention measure rather than stimulus.
- Business investment is unlikely to respond as hoped because executive-order tariffs can be reversed by courts or a future administration, making long-term factory commitments an enormous leap of faith.

## Detailed Notes

### Current Tariff Status and Market Reaction [00:01:40]

Recording on April 7, 2025, with markets in extreme volatility; a fake headline about a 90-day pause briefly moved markets before being refuted. Reciprocal tariffs were still on track for 12:01 AM April 9. Pavlick notes Trump previously granted a 30-day pause on Canada/Mexico tariffs, so some speculation about a shorter extension was reasonable.

### Trump's Mindset and Negotiating Logic [00:04:40]

Pavlick argues Trump as a lame-duck president feels less constrained by political pressures and may be misreading his mandate — voters may have been expressing inflation frustration rather than endorsing aggressive tariffs. Trump's fundamental view is that the US consumer market gives it bilateral leverage, and that other countries will eventually come around as time is on the US side.

### Internal Contradictions and Policy Incoherence [00:08:10]

Pavlick explains tariffs can accomplish three things — generate revenue for tax-cut funding, secure trading concessions, or protect strategic industries — but these goals conflict. The daily shifting of justifications (from middle-class restoration to national security to deficit reduction) has eroded confidence. He contrasts this with Trump 1.0, where a 'team of rivals' dynamic played out more openly; now there is more public message discipline but presumably still significant private disagreement.

### Tariff Architecture: Four Buckets [00:12:11]

Pavlick categorizes the tariff regime into four buckets: (1) reciprocal tariffs plus a universal baseline designed to prevent jurisdiction-hopping; (2) Canada and Mexico, linked to USMCA renegotiation ahead of the July 2026 deadline and Chinese circumvention via Mexico; (3) sectoral tariffs on steel, aluminum, autos, copper, pharma, and semiconductors, which Pavlick believes will endure due to bipartisan support; and (4) China as a category unto itself, where the underlying goal may be to build a coalition of trading partners that pulls away from China.

### Sequencing vs. Trump 1.0 and Business Investment Implications [00:16:22]

In Trump's first term, significant corporate and individual tax cuts preceded tariffs, providing an economic tailwind. This time, Republicans are merely extending existing rates — preventing a headwind but providing no new stimulus. Pavlick also argues that because tariffs rely on executive authority and could be reversed by courts or a future administration, businesses will wait and see rather than commit billions to domestic manufacturing, regardless of agreement with the policy goals.

### Geopolitical Theater: Mexico, Canada, and China [00:27:24]

Pavlick praises Mexican President Claudia Sheinbaum as a masterclass in managing Trump, suggesting she is giving him the political optics he needs while securing domestic political cover for border measures. Canadian PM Carney benefits from opposing Trump domestically. Pavlick speculates the sequencing of deal announcements will be strategic — potentially holding back early agreements to prevent them from becoming the ceiling for all subsequent negotiations.
