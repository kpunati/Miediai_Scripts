# The Mother of All Bubbles | Animal Spirits 389

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=A_Wsj0cPTzY
**Duration:** 69:50

## Summary

In episode 389 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson examine whether US market dominance — now comprising roughly 70% of the global stock index — constitutes a bubble or reflects durable structural advantages such as productivity growth and entrepreneurship culture. They discuss record ETF flows, risks lurking in private credit, Michael Saylor's Bitcoin bet versus legendary trades, a 10-year housing price outlook, and the explosive growth of YouTube as a podcast platform. The conversation spans personal anecdotes about Black Friday spending, tipping fatigue, and travel, alongside substantive financial analysis.

## Key Takeaways

- US equities represent roughly 70% of the global stock market but only 26% of global GDP, a gap the hosts attribute partly to superior corporate profitability and productivity growth rather than simple overvaluation.
- ETF flows hit a monthly record of $155 billion in November 2024, with annual flows approaching $1 trillion for the first time, reflecting the sheer volume of capital seeking US market exposure.
- Private credit scams modeled on yield promises are expected to proliferate as opaque, illiquid products attract unsophisticated retail investors; one case study showed a retiree losing his entire $760,000 savings.
- BlackRock's acquisition of HPS signals a major push to combine public and private credit markets, with the CFO calling it 'the future of fixed income.'
- The housing market faces structural undersupply; torson slock data shows the median home buyer age is now 49, 40% of homes are owned mortgage-free, and prices could rise nearly 11% in the coming year according to one model.
- YouTube has overtaken Spotify and Apple Podcasts as the most-used podcast platform in the US, with video podcast viewership on Spotify growing 88% in one year.
- CFA exam candidates have declined significantly from late-2010s peaks, driven by the pandemic, passive investing's rise, and a cultural shift toward tech careers and speculative assets.

## Detailed Notes

### US Exceptionalism and the 'Mother of All Bubbles' Debate [00:04:17]

The hosts review a Financial Times piece arguing that global investors are committing more capital to the US than ever before in modern history. They push back on the 'bubble' framing, noting that PE multiple comparisons between US tech and European industrials are misleading due to different sector compositions. Ben cites Joey Politano's productivity data showing the US has pulled far ahead of other developed economies since the mid-2010s as a structural explanation for premium valuations.

### Global Market Cap vs. GDP and ETF Record Flows [00:09:30]

A chart comparing country-level stock market weights in the ACWI index to GDP shares reveals the US at 65% of global market cap but only 26% of GDP. The hosts note China's 17% GDP share translates to just 2.4% market cap weight, suggesting investor skepticism rather than blanket US overvaluation. ETF flows set a monthly record of $155 billion in November, with annualized flows nearing $1 trillion for the first time, attributed to post-election 'Trump bump' sentiment and continued retail adoption.

### Private Credit Risks and Yield Scams [00:17:53]

The hosts highlight a Jason Zweig story about a firm called Yield Wealth that promised 15.25% guaranteed returns with no losses; a 60-year-old retiree rolled his entire $760,000 401(k) into it and lost everything in what appears to be a Ponzi scheme. Michael predicts private credit will spawn more such scams given its opacity and illiquidity. Separately, BlackRock's $150 billion HPS acquisition signals the legitimization of private credit as a mainstream fixed-income category.

### CFA Decline, Financial Nihilism, and Enron Crypto Scam [00:25:14]

CFA exam candidate counts have fallen well below late-2010s levels, attributed to the pandemic, growth of passive investing, and generational preference for tech over finance careers. The hosts, both CFA charterholders, speculate that the designation feels antiquated in an era of index funds. A viral 'Enron is back' tweet turned out to be a crypto scam masquerading as the bankrupt energy company, which the hosts use to illustrate the decade's culture of financial nihilism among Gen Z investors.

### Michael Saylor and Bitcoin Comparisons [00:39:52]

The hosts debate whether Saylor's estimated $4.9-$15 billion gain on MicroStrategy's Bitcoin position qualifies as one of the greatest trades of all time alongside Paulson's subprime short ($4B) and Soros's pound short ($1B). Michael argues the comparison is flawed because Saylor used a publicly traded balance sheet to raise capital rather than placing a personal hedge fund bet. MARA Holdings announced a $700 million convertible note offering to acquire more Bitcoin, raising the possibility that MicroStrategy's premium over NAV could compress if competitors replicate the strategy.

### Housing Market 10-Year Outlook [00:42:00]

Nick Maggiulli's prediction that US residential real estate will underperform inflation over the next decade is debated; both hosts lean toward taking the other side. Torsten Slok data is cited: median buyer age is 49 (up from 31 in 1981), 40% of homes have no mortgage, household equity is at record highs at 73% of housing value, and a model forecasts 10.88% price appreciation in the next year. The structural thesis is chronic undersupply relative to demand, not expected to resolve within the decade.

### YouTube Dominates Podcasting; Platform and Viewing Trends [00:48:16]

YouTube has become the top podcast platform in the US with 31% of weekly listeners, surpassing both Spotify and Apple Podcasts. 150 million Americans watch YouTube on their TVs monthly, and video podcast viewership on Spotify grew 88% year-over-year. The hosts acknowledge they were slow to convert Animal Spirits to video, now posting the full episode on both YouTube and Spotify. They connect this trend to how younger generations consume content passively on screens rather than pure audio.
