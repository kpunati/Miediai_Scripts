# Stock Shortage | TCAF 177

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-02-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cdWGOM3SvlE

## Summary

David Giroux, CIO and portfolio manager at T. Rowe Price, joins The Compound and Friends to share his cautious market outlook heading into 2025, arguing the S&P 500 is priced for less than 5% annual returns through 2030 based on bottom-up company modeling. He explains how the market's composition has shifted — with high-multiple sectors dominating — making simple historical P/E comparisons misleading. Giroux discusses his approach to fixed income including leveraged loans, skepticism toward private credit, and specific stock picks in healthcare and industrial software. The episode also explores the 'stock shortage' thesis, Treasury yield dynamics, AI's impact on software company margins, and the mechanics of running an active ETF.

## Key Takeaways

- Giroux's bottom-up analysis of the top 150 S&P companies produces a 5-year forward total return estimate below 5% annually, with a year-end 2030 index target near 7,200-7,300.
- The market's sector composition has shifted dramatically since 2006, with high-multiple growth stocks now dominant, making raw P/E comparisons to historical averages misleading.
- Banks appear expensive at 17x earnings versus a 10-year historical average of 12-13x, and current valuations may reflect peak net interest margins — a potential double risk.
- Leveraged loans (floating rate, top of capital structure) are preferred over investment-grade bonds or private credit given tight credit spreads across fixed income.
- Private credit raises concerns: many mid-market companies are levered 8x with cost of debt near 11%, while pricing has compressed from SOFR+600 toward SOFR+350-400 as traditional loan markets recovered.
- AI's most tangible near-term benefit accrues to software companies, which can both sell AI-powered products and reduce their own cost structures as programming costs decline.
- The 'stock shortage' thesis — fewer publicly traded companies chasing growing retirement account flows — may structurally support elevated valuations, but Giroux sees it as insufficient to justify current multiples.

## Detailed Notes

### T. Rowe Price Culture and Active Management Edge [00:09:35]

Giroux has been at T. Rowe Price since 1998 and attributes its outperformance to a culture focused purely on investment management — no insurance or banking distractions. He contrasts T. Rowe's long-term fundamental research approach with the increasingly short-term, quantitative nature of much of the industry, arguing this differentiation creates an edge in three- to ten-year investment horizons. T. Rowe data shows outperformance versus passive in 70% of rolling 10-year periods over 20 years, with an average annual excess return of 0.83%.

### Cautious Market Outlook and Valuation Methodology [00:22:50]

Giroux's team models the top 100-150 companies individually, estimating earnings through 2030 and applying appropriate multiples to derive a market price target near 7,200-7,300 — implying under 5% annual returns from current levels. He cautions against naive P/E comparisons because market composition has changed: growth stocks (44% of S&P) trade at 32x vs. a historical 29x — slightly elevated but not extreme — while cyclicals and banks trade near cycle-high multiples. The real concern is peak earnings combined with peak multiples in rate-sensitive sectors.

### Fixed Income, Leveraged Loans, and Private Credit [00:35:33]

Despite a cautious equity view, Giroux does not view investment-grade bonds as a safe haven because credit spreads are near historic tights — a spread widening event would cause bond losses simultaneous with equity losses. He prefers leveraged loans (floating rate, senior secured) which performed well in 2022 rising rate environment. Private credit raises concerns: middle-market companies at 8x leverage with 11% cost of debt face a high hurdle, and the traditional loan market has taken back many credits that temporarily migrated to private credit, compressing private credit spreads from SOFR+600-700 to SOFR+350-400.

### AI, Software Margins, and the Stock Shortage Thesis [00:47:38]

Giroux argues software companies are best positioned for AI benefits — both from selling AI productivity products and from seeing their own cost structures fall as programming becomes cheaper (e.g., GitHub Copilot). He is skeptical that the broader AI investment wave has yet translated into measurable margin gains outside a few use cases. The 'stock shortage' thesis — that the Wilshire 5000 now holds only ~3,500 (or ~2,000 investable) names while retirement account assets keep growing — is acknowledged as a real structural tailwind, but Giroux notes growth stock multiples have not structurally re-rated versus history, suggesting the thesis has limits.

### Portfolio Positioning and Stock Picks [01:02:48]

The TCAF ETF holds Microsoft, Amazon, Becton Dickinson, Nvidia, Roper Technologies, Fortive, United Health, Revvity, Alphabet, and PTC as top positions. Giroux is overweight healthcare — not top-down but via specific names like McKesson, Revvity, and Thermo Fisher — and sees Revvity as a potential $300 stock from ~$113-120. He is underweight financials given peak NIM and elevated multiples, and is wildly underweight Apple despite understanding the services mix shift thesis, citing iPhone replacement-only growth and skepticism about Apple AI driving a new upgrade cycle.
