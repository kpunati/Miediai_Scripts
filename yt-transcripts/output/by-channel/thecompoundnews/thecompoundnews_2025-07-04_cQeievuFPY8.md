# When Will the AI Bubble Pop?  | TCAF 198

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-07-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cQeievuFPY8
**Duration:** 78:48

## Summary

Episode 198 of The Compound and Friends features Michael Batnick in conversation with portfolio manager Joe Fahmy and market strategist Shay Boloor, who together argue the AI-driven bull market is in its early innings rather than nearing a top. The hosts use technical indicators including the Zweig Breadth Thrust and historical NASDAQ patterns to support a multi-year bullish outlook, while analyzing individual Mag 7 stocks, particularly Apple's structural decline and Palantir's emerging dominance as a Stage 2 AI winner. The conversation also examines speculative growth themes including humanoid robotics via Tesla, space infrastructure via Rocket Lab, eVTOL companies like Archer Aviation and Joby, and the risks in quantum computing names.

## Key Takeaways

- Joe Fahmy places the current cycle at roughly year two of a four-to-five-year AI bull market, drawing direct parallels to the 1995-1999 internet boom.
- The Zweig Breadth Thrust triggered at end of April is cited as a historically reliable bullish signal, with 12-month forward returns positive in every prior instance.
- Shay Boloor's Rule of 40 framework identifies Palantir as the clearest Stage 2 AI winner because it is the primary interface bringing LLMs into enterprise workflows.
- Apple is assessed as permanently transitioning from growth to financial-engineering mode, with no credible path to AI leadership due to talent and cultural deficits.
- Tesla's bull case rests not on EVs but on its position as the sole scaled player in physical AI and humanoid robotics, with autonomy as a transformative long-term catalyst.
- Shay Boloor warns that agentic AI's enterprise breakthrough will trigger a multi-year digestion period for software stocks as AI agents replace human labor costs.
- Broadcom is highlighted as an underappreciated beneficiary of AI inference growth, described as the 'Uncle Sam' taxing all hyperscaler AI workloads.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Cycle and AI Bull Case [00:06:04]

Joe Fahmy argues the current bull market, powered by AI, began in late 2022 or early 2023 when Nvidia reported a record quarter. He draws a historical analogy to prior innovation-driven cycles in railroads, PCs, and the internet, estimating we are in year two of a four-to-five-year run. Shay Boloor adds that current infrastructure spend (Nvidia, hyperscalers) mirrors the first-stage 'structural arteries' of the dot-com era, with the bigger winners to come in Stage 2 application companies built on top of AI infrastructure.

### Technical Signals and Market Internals [00:29:05]

The Zweig Breadth Thrust triggered around April 25th, indicating a rapid shift from extreme oversold to extreme overbought conditions in NYSE advance/decline data in a 10-day window. Historically this signal has been followed by outsized positive returns 12 months later in all 19 prior instances since World War II. An additional chart shows the S&P up 26% in the 59 trading days from the April bottom, another rare event with a clean forward return record. Joe Fahmy also cites heavy institutional call-buying in Amazon, Google, ARM, and Nvidia dated to June-December 2027 expirations as a sign of large-money conviction.

### Mag 7 Divergence: Apple's Decline and Palantir's Rise [00:40:59]

Year-to-date relative performance shows Nvidia, Microsoft, and Meta outperforming the S&P while Apple, Tesla, and Google lag. Both guests dismiss Apple as a growth stock, citing a cultural inability to attract AI talent and a strategic drift toward financial engineering and services. Palantir is repeatedly cited as the closest analog to the Stage 2 winners of the internet era because it provides the only scalable bridge between large language models and enterprise workflows, with a recent Rule of 40 score exceeding 80.

### Tesla, Humanoids, and Physical AI [00:46:29]

Shay Boloor frames Tesla not as a car company but as the only player building a general-purpose physical AI and humanoid robotics platform. He argues autonomy, once solved, transforms vehicles into income-generating assets and that humanoid robots will face near-unlimited enterprise demand from companies like Amazon and McDonald's once commercially available. The speculative timeline remains tied to Elon Musk's execution pace, but Boloor contends buying Tesla under $2 trillion is a generational opportunity given the robotics total addressable market.

### Speculative Growth Themes and Bull Market Risks [01:00:24]

The guests discuss speculative plays including Rocket Lab (positioned as the 'FedEx of space' and closest SpaceX competitor), Archer Aviation and Joby in the eVTOL/defense-drone space, and IonQ for quantum computing. IonQ is favored for its room-temperature trapped-ion approach but both guests note quantum computing remains a science project far from its ChatGPT moment. On bull market risks, Joe Fahmy cautions that the end signal will appear as institutional distribution, declining breadth, and an acceleration phase where everyone including 'the mailman' is making money, which he does not see yet.

### Valuation Philosophy and Jeremy Grantham Parallel [01:07:47]

Joe Fahmy discusses Jeremy Grantham's reflection that rigid adherence to pre-1997 average S&P P/E of 14 would have destroyed returns, because profit margins and interest rate structures permanently shifted the equilibrium to roughly 24 times. He argues today's investors must remain open to the possibility that average P/E settles at 28-30 over the next decade driven by AI productivity gains, rather than fighting the tape by anchoring to older valuation norms.
