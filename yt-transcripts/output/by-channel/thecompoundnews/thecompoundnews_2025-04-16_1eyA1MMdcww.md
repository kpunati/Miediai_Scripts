# The Dollar Dump | Animal Spirits 408

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=1eyA1MMdcww
**Duration:** 64:20

## Summary

In episode 408 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson unpack the extraordinary market conditions of April 2025, centered on the simultaneous decline of the US dollar and rise in Treasury yields during the tariff-driven equity selloff — a break from historical safe-haven patterns. The hosts debate whether the shock phase of tariff-induced panic is over while acknowledging that recession risk remains elevated and corporate earnings guidance will matter more than backward-looking data. They also address the unintended consequences of trade policy, including European travel cancellations, shifting foreign asset allocations, and the impossibility of reviving US manufacturing at scale. The episode closes with practical guidance on portfolio rebalancing for nervous investors and personal recommendations including Apple TV shows.

## Key Takeaways

- The simultaneous dollar decline and yield spike during the April 2025 selloff was historically anomalous, signaling a potential structural shift in global capital flows away from US assets.
- The acute tariff-panic phase is likely over, but a double-dip correction remains possible if the economy slides into recession.
- Consumer and CEO sentiment has fallen to levels not seen since the GFC, but sentiment can reverse quickly and may not fully translate to economic activity if jobs remain intact.
- US manufacturing revival via tariffs faces structural barriers: the service economy now dominates, and most major corporations can and will route around tariffs while small businesses bear the brunt.
- Historical data shows that buying after a 15% drawdown produces positive returns 100% of the time over 10-year horizons, but shorter-term volatility remains significant.
- Nervous investors are better served by reducing equity allocation (e.g., 90/10 to 70/30) than attempting to time a full exit and re-entry.
- European consumer behavior — canceling US travel plans — illustrates how unintended consequences of tariff policy can affect service-sector GDP in ways that are difficult to model.

## Detailed Notes

### Dollar and Yield Divergence [00:08:47]

During the April 2025 tariff selloff, the US dollar fell sharply while 10-year and 30-year Treasury yields rose — the opposite of typical safe-haven dynamics. Jim Bianco's chart showed these two series, which historically track closely, diverging hard. The 30-year yield rose 48 basis points in one week, the largest weekly gain since the 1980s, while the 10-year saw its largest weekly gain since the early 2000s. The euro-dollar rate also diverged from the interest rate differential, illustrating a broad asset-allocation shift away from US assets.

### Tariff Policy Chaos and Recession Odds [00:08:15]

China tariff rates moved from 54% on April 2nd to 104% on April 8th, then 145% on April 9th, with electronics later partially exempted. Betting markets (Kalshi) showed recession probability reaching 65% before retreating to around 52%. The hosts agreed that while the shock phase is likely over, a moderate recession is the base case given the severity of the confidence shock and the potential for corporate layoffs if tariffs persist.

### Consumer and Business Sentiment [00:18:56]

CEO confidence fell to its weakest since the GFC, lower even than during COVID. Consumer sentiment among self-identified independents dropped below the lows seen during the 2022 inflation surge. University of Michigan respondents making unsolicited negative comments on government economic policy reached a record high. New York Fed regional manufacturer new orders hit an all-time survey low. The hosts caution that sentiment and actual economic behavior can diverge, especially if employment remains stable.

### Manufacturing, Trade, and Unintended Consequences [00:19:40]

US manufacturing employment has stagnated since the 1950s as the economy shifted to services; service exports now exceed $1 trillion versus China's roughly $400 billion. An FT survey found 80% of Americans think more manufacturing would benefit the country but 73% would not personally work in a factory. European travel to the US is falling sharply — Western European overnight visitors down 17% — representing a direct hit to the $253 billion annual travel and tourism sector. Some US manufacturers are reportedly choosing to produce overseas to avoid component import duties, the opposite of the tariff policy's intent.

### Market Data and Long-Term Investing Framework [00:34:19]

April 9th, 2025 was the 10th best single day for the S&P 500 since 1928 (up ~9.5%) and the second best ever for the Nasdaq. On that day, 98.2% of S&P 500 stocks advanced — historically a 100% win rate over the following 6 months in similar instances since 1990. Buying at a 15% month-end drawdown since 1950 yielded positive returns 83% of the time after one year, 96% after three years, and 100% after ten years. The hosts recommend adjusting allocation rather than exiting, citing Christine Benz's guidance on sequence-of-return risk for near-retirees.

### Crypto, Real Estate, and Broader Market Context [00:46:25]

Bitcoin was mentioned trading around $77,000 during the most acute stress; one respected commentator called for a return to $10,000 — illustrating the emotional pressure social media creates during selloffs. Ben expressed continued belief in Bitcoin's asymmetric upside while emphasizing its extreme volatility. The hosts noted that real estate's illiquidity, which masks volatility, makes it psychologically easier to hold than equities. Mortgage rates are back above 7%, but a softening economy could bring them lower and create buyer opportunities in markets like Southwest Florida where price cuts are appearing.
