# End of an Empire | TCAF 188

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=YsIrb6JKXag
**Duration:** 67:45

## Summary

In this April 2025 episode of The Compound and Friends (TCAF 188), macro strategist Warren Pies of 3Fourteen Research analyzes whether the simultaneous selloff in US equities, the dollar, and Treasuries signals the end of American exceptionalism or a cyclical correction. The discussion covers recession probability frameworks, investor sentiment wash-out indicators, housing market deterioration as a leading economic indicator, and the $8 trillion household wealth shock. All three speakers weigh bearish signals against potential silver linings including Fed rate cuts and possible trade deal clarity.

## Key Takeaways

- The simultaneous decline in US stocks, bonds, and the dollar in April 2025 is historically anomalous, occurring only 12 times since 2000, and suggests foreign capital repatriation rather than a domestic deleveraging event.
- Recession probability is now viewed as roughly a coin flip, with forward EPS estimates beginning to hook lower and credit spreads widening toward but not yet at recessionary levels.
- Volatility-targeting institutional funds have derisked to approximately 20% equity exposure, a signal that historically preceded strong 6-month forward returns when exposure then begins to recover.
- The US fiscal deficit running at approximately 7% of GDP is an unprecedented buffer against recession, but would balloon dangerously if a recession did occur.
- Residential construction employment remains at cycle highs but leading indicators such as D.R. Horton completed unsold inventory spikes and declining single-family housing starts suggest layoffs are coming.
- Sentiment is assessed as washed out across retail (inverse ETF flows), institutional (volatility targeting), and systematic (CTA net short) measures, but technical confirmation of a bottom is still absent.
- The $30 trillion in total foreign holdings of US assets means any sustained reversal of capital flows would pose a larger threat to US equity markets than to the Treasury market.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Context and the 'End of Empire' Question [00:04:27]

Josh Brown frames the shift from a cyclical recession debate to an existential question about American exceptionalism, driven by the simultaneous fall in the dollar, bonds, and equities. Warren Pies confirms his firm was already writing a client report titled 'End of the Empire?' and notes the S&P was down alongside the dollar and bonds — a historically rare and alarming configuration. Both hosts stress this feels different from prior corrections because the traditional risk-off safe-haven bids for dollars and Treasuries are absent.

### Dollar and Treasury Selloff: Foreign Capital Repatriation [00:07:11]

Pies identifies only 12 days since 2000 with a 40bp+ 10-year yield rise alongside a 3%+ dollar index drop. By pricing gold in yen and euros and finding it roughly flat in those terms, Pies concludes this was real money (primarily European and Japanese investors) selling US assets and repatriating, not a basis trade unwind or Chinese selling. The basis trade explanation favored by Scott Bessent is dismissed as insufficient given the scale and cross-asset nature of the moves.

### Historical Bear Market Comparisons and Recession Framework [00:21:22]

Pies presents data showing that after a 10% S&P correction without a recession, median maximum drawdown over the next year is about 7.3%, while recessionary cases produce far worse outcomes with more than half ending in negative returns. The market had erased 242 trading days of gains, shallow versus the historical bear market average of about 400 days. To fully price in a recession historically, the S&P would need to reach around 4,800. The 1998 LTCM and 2010-2011 post-GFC episodes are cited as the rare non-recessionary fast resets, but both were aided by rapid Fed intervention that is not currently forthcoming.

### Investor Sentiment and Positioning Indicators [00:37:31]

Inverse ETF volume reached 53% of total speculative ETF volume (inverse plus leveraged long), approaching the 60% threshold that has historically preceded strong forward returns. Volatility-targeting funds modeled at 20% equity exposure — one of only seven times since 1980 they have been below 25% — with a historical 6-month forward return of over 13% when exposure subsequently recovers. Wall Street strategists elevated their year-end targets sharply at end-2024 (analogous to the 2021 capitulation), and are now being forced to cut, a historically bearish contrarian signal.

### Economic Deterioration: Housing, Jobs, and Credit [00:50:13]

Payroll growth excluding government and healthcare jobs has fallen to near-flat levels consistent with past recessions. Only 57% of industries show year-over-year job growth. D.R. Horton's completed unsold home inventory has spiked and single-family housing starts hit their second-lowest level in two years in March 2025, predating tariff and wealth-shock effects. Credit card delinquencies are rising in a way Pies characterizes as a legitimate surge, concentrated in lower-income strata. The US government is running a roughly 7% of GDP deficit, historically unprecedented entering a recession, acting as a buffer but creating compounding risks if a downturn materializes.

### Foreign Holdings and Wealth Shock [00:47:06]

Foreign holders own approximately $16 trillion of US equities and $9 trillion of US Treasuries, totaling around $30 trillion in US assets. The current account structure means that eliminating the trade deficit would cut off the corresponding capital inflows into US markets. US households now hold 43% of net worth in equities — the highest ever — creating an $8 trillion wealth shock at the bear market lows, the fourth worst as a percentage of GDP in US history. Pies and Brown agree the wealth effect is now integral to the real economy given 401k dependence.

### Silver Linings and Outlook [01:03:17]

All three guests cite the likely Fed pivot as the key potential positive catalyst: Brown and Pies both argue the 4.5-5% 10-year yield is a market-mechanic distortion and expect yields to fall. Pies expects four or more Fed cuts in 2025. Batnick expects a trade deal and corporate adjustment once clarity emerges. Pies is now actively looking for a buying opportunity rather than derisking further, though technical confirmation of a bottom remains absent. Sentiment is assessed as fully washed out across multiple metrics.
