# Small Caps Are Back | Animal Spirits 448

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=677SzeIR4v4
**Duration:** 64:38

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 448 opens with discussion of geopolitical volatility driven by US threats to annex Greenland and associated European tariff threats, contextualizing market reactions against a backdrop of rising bond yields in Japan and the US. The hosts then pivot to the emerging small-cap rotation story, noting the Russell 2000's extended streak of outperformance over large-cap indices and arguing that years of underperformance have set the stage for a potential multi-year run. They review positive household balance sheet data, the great Baby Boomer real estate wealth transfer, and the significant gains in wealth and equity ownership by the bottom 50% of American households. The episode concludes with entertainment commentary on Netflix movies and various 2026 film releases.

## Key Takeaways

- The Russell 2000 recorded 11 consecutive sessions of outperforming the S&P 500, signaling a potential small-cap rotation trade in 2026.
- Bond yields rising while inflation falls suggests political instability rather than inflationary pressure is driving the move, with gold and silver reacting more strongly than Bitcoin.
- Household balance sheets are in better shape than in 1999 on an asset-to-liability basis, supported by Bank of America charge-off data showing consumer credit quality is holding up.
- Gen X and Millennials are set to inherit approximately $4.6 trillion in global real estate over the next decade, with implications for housing supply and financial advisor relationships.
- The bottom 50% of US households has seen the largest relative gains in wealth and equity ownership since 2012, representing a positive financial inclusion outcome of the 2020s.
- The NYSE announced plans for a tokenized securities platform enabling 24/7 trading and instant settlement, pending regulatory approval.
- Geopolitical volatility from tariff rhetoric and territorial ambitions is seen as manageable for markets as long as corporate earnings remain strong.

## Detailed Notes

### Geopolitical Risk and Tariff Threats [00:01:25]

Recording takes place 22 minutes before the market open with futures showing volatility, VIX at 20, and gold surging. The catalyst is the US president's stated desire to annex Greenland from Denmark, with European leaders responding negatively and facing threatened 10% tariffs rising to 25% by June 1st. The hosts draw a parallel to pre-WWI 1913, noting how world orders that seem permanent can shift dramatically, while expressing concern about abusing current US geopolitical dominance.

### Bond Yields and Currency Markets [00:08:08]

Japan's 30-year bond yield surged 26 basis points toward 4%, with the hosts noting that while zero yields were bad, vertical spikes are not good either. US 30-year Treasury yields are also rising but in a more range-bound pattern. Notably, currencies and Bitcoin are not moving in response to geopolitical instability, while gold and silver are spiking — which the hosts find surprising given Bitcoin's 'anti-system' narrative positioning.

### Small-Cap Rotation and Stock Market [00:13:24]

An Apollo chart shows unprecedented concentration in S&P 500 stocks with 3%+ weight (Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google), but the Mag Seven versus the rest ratio is breaking down with mega-cap underperforming on a relative basis. The Russell 2000 recorded 11 straight sessions of beating the S&P 500 and is significantly outperforming year-to-date, supported by strong GDP growth and corporate earnings. From the April 2025 liberation day lows, the Russell is up 53%, beating the Nasdaq 100. However, since November 2021, the Russell is only up 16% versus 57% for the S&P 500, suggesting years of underperformance may have set up a mean reversion opportunity.

### Economic Conditions and Household Balance Sheets [00:19:24]

Greg Ip at the Wall Street Journal argues Trump is likely to succeed in running the economy hot, given coordinated fiscal policy (high spending), accommodative monetary policy, and eased credit policy. Bank of America data shows consumer net charge-offs declining, and Carson Group analysis shows household balance sheets are in better shape on an asset-to-liability basis than in 1999 or 2007. The hosts note that liabilities are fixed while assets can fall in a bear market, which would change the calculus quickly.

### Real Estate Wealth Transfer and Bottom 50% Gains [00:39:23]

The share of US mortgages at 6%+ has now surpassed the share at 3% or lower, suggesting normalization in the housing market. Over the next decade, Gen X and Millennials will inherit $4.6 trillion in global real estate ($2.4 trillion in the US) from Baby Boomers. The hosts expect this to be a slow, gradual process rather than a sudden wave of selling. Citadel Securities data shows the bottom 50% of households has seen wealth rise ~1,200% since 2012 and experienced the largest relative gains in equity ownership — a positive outcome of the 2020s partially attributed to Robinhood and pandemic-era investing.

### Crypto and NYSE Tokenization News [00:35:33]

The hosts note that Coinbase had a dispute with the administration potentially impacting stablecoin yields. The NYSE announced a platform for tokenized securities enabling 24/7 trading, instant settlement, and stablecoin-based funding, pending regulatory approval. The hosts are positive on instant settlement but ambivalent about 24/7 trading.

### Entertainment and Final Thoughts [00:50:07]

Discussion covers the new Matt Damon and Ben Affleck Netflix movie 'The Rip,' which both hosts found mediocre but watchable, attributing its hollow feel to Netflix's optimization for attention retention over storytelling quality. Book recommendations include 'All the Devils Are Here' on the GFC, 'Too Big to Fail' by Andrew Ross Sorkin, and '1913: In Search of the World Before the Great War.' The hosts close by suggesting geopolitical volatility may become the new normal under the current administration, but prefer it to corporate earnings deterioration.
