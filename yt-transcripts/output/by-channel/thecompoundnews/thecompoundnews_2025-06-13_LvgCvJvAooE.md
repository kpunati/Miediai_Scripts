# The Next Big Short is the Trade War | TCAF 196

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-06-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=LvgCvJvAooE
**Duration:** 74:45

## Summary

Episode 196 of The Compound and Friends features Steve Eisman — the investor who shorted subprime mortgages before the 2008 financial crisis and was portrayed by Steve Carell in The Big Short — in conversation with Josh Brown and Michael Batnick. Eisman argues the US-China trade war is the single macro variable that matters, characterizing the outcome as binary: no trade war equals multi-year growth, trade war equals global recession. He dismisses the federal deficit as 'Wall Street virtue signaling,' expresses concern about Apple's total manufacturing dependence on China, and lays out a selective bull case for large-cap financials and payments networks under a deregulatory administration.

## Key Takeaways

- The trade war is Eisman's only macro concern; he views the outcome as binary and cannot handicap the probability.
- Eisman considers deficit anxiety to be 'nonsense' and 'virtue signaling,' arguing the global financial system's dependence on Treasuries prevents the feared consequences.
- Apple's near-total reliance on Chinese manufacturing makes it the most exposed Mag-7 name to trade war escalation, and rebuilding that ecosystem would take 5–10 years.
- Eisman favors Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as trading vehicles for an M&A/IPO recovery theme, and prefers Morgan Stanley as a long-term holding due to its higher ROE and diversification.
- Visa and Mastercard are his preferred payments names; he is skeptical that stablecoins will disintermediate their rails in traditional finance, though sees a niche in cross-border remittances.
- Regional bank consolidation is a potential theme Eisman finds interesting but does not yet own, arguing the industry needs M&A to fund technology investment and avoid JP Morgan dominance.
- Incentives trump ethics almost every time — the principle he applies from subprime to the Wells Fargo fake account scandal to how Wall Street shapes behavior.

## Detailed Notes

### Behind the Scenes on The Big Short [00:08:44]

Eisman recounts how he met Steve Carell for breakfast at a Madison Avenue diner and later had Carell meet his family. His sole creative contribution to the film was suggesting the word 'schmuck' instead of 'asshole' in a cab-hailing scene. He initially thought Carell's portrayal was angrier than he actually was, but after reading a 2010 Financial Crisis Commission interview transcript, he concluded Carell had it right. He also discusses the original subprime loan mechanics: loans were underwritten to teaser rates with full knowledge borrowers could not pay the adjusted rate, incentivizing endless refinancings that generated fees for every party in the chain.

### The Federal Deficit — Dismissing the Armageddon Narrative [00:24:46]

Eisman calls deficit concern 'Wall Street virtue signaling,' arguing none of the predicted consequences — crowding out, dollar reserve status loss, rising rates — have materialized over 40 years of predictions. His core thesis is that the entire global financial system runs on Treasuries, from the repo market to sovereign wealth fund allocations, so there is no viable alternative until a real substitute emerges. He acknowledges that a deficit heading into a recession could constrain fiscal policy response, but does not view this as a near-term alarm.

### Trade War as the Only Macro Risk [00:37:05]

Eisman frames the trade war outcome as strictly binary: without a trade war the US economy will grow for years; with one, expect a global recession. He highlights the complexity of EU negotiations — each member country effectively holds a veto on sectoral issues, making continent-wide deals extremely difficult. He draws a parallel to World War I using Barbara Tuchman's 'Guns of August,' noting that no one wanted that war but reciprocal treaty structures dragged nations into it. Despite the S&P being near all-time highs, he is not fully invested and declines to handicap the probability.

### Apple's China Problem [00:48:28]

Drawing on the book 'Apple in China' by FT reporter Patrick McGee, Eisman explains Apple's manufacturing concentration in China was never a strategic decision but evolved organically until around 2010–11. He argues Apple has no ability to maneuver between the US and China — rebuilding its Foxconn-dependent ecosystem would take 5–10 years regardless of destination. Apple's claims of Indian manufacturing are, in Eisman's view, largely reassembly of Chinese-built components. He views Apple as an Apple-specific risk rather than a systemic market risk.

### Financials Sector Outlook [00:52:35]

Eisman sees two distinct financials narratives. For large-caps, a deregulatory administration favoring M&A and IPO activity makes Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, and Morgan Stanley natural beneficiaries; he prefers Morgan Stanley for long-term ownership given its diversification and higher ROE. For regional banks, he believes consolidation is structurally necessary — JP Morgan has doubled its deposit share since 2007 partly because smaller banks cannot afford rising technology costs — but does not yet own regionals. In payments, he stays with Visa and Mastercard exclusively, citing their entrenched global rails and the recurring failure of would-be disruptors.

### Stablecoins and IPO Market [01:04:36]

Eisman views Circle as more credible than Tether due to US regulatory oversight and transparent custody, but questions how large the stablecoin market can realistically become in traditional payments given Visa's Visa Plus and Visa Direct infrastructure. He sees cross-border remittances as the most plausible growth niche. On IPOs, he is cautiously optimistic — CoreWeave is cited as a positive sign of risk appetite — but notes a trade war would immediately halt activity.
