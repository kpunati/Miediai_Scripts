# Tax the Billionaires | Animal Spirits 446

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=T4ptnSu4khI
**Duration:** 76:13

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 446 opens the new year with Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson sharing 2026 market predictions and assessing bear market odds, arguing that higher valuations are justified by structurally expanded profit margins and AI-driven productivity gains. The episode examines why international stocks — particularly the EFA — dramatically outperformed U.S. equities in 2025 despite widespread skepticism, and assesses recession risk as low. Discussions span AI hallucination rates in commercial LLMs, firsthand Waymo autonomous vehicle experiences, Disney's chronic stock underperformance relative to the S&P, a frozen U.S. housing market, a proposed California billionaire tax, and streaming viewership rankings.

## Key Takeaways

- Hosts predict a 75% probability of a 20%-plus S&P 500 gain in 2026 and roughly 80% probability of at least a 10% correction at some point during the year.
- Rising corporate profit margins — from roughly 7% in 2005 to an estimated 16% by 2027 — justify elevated forward P/E multiples and undermine valuation-bear arguments.
- International stocks (EFA) outperformed the S&P by about 14% in 2025 through a combination of dollar weakness and fundamental valuation recovery, surprising even the hosts.
- AI hallucination rates remain alarmingly high (up to ~52% for some models), limiting professional utility despite the technology's rapid progress.
- Disney has underperformed the S&P 500 on every major time frame over the past 30 years, weighed down by linear TV assets and a Disney+ streaming drag on what would otherwise be a highly profitable parks business.
- A proposed California ballot measure would impose retroactive one-time taxes of up to billions on resident billionaires; hosts argue it is politically popular but practically unworkable due to taxpayer mobility.
- The U.S. housing market remains frozen because of the lock-in effect from low-rate mortgages, with hosts suggesting only government-subsidized rate buy-downs could meaningfully restore transaction volume.

## Detailed Notes

### 2026 Market Predictions and Valuation Case [00:05:44]

Ben Carlson's baseline prediction is that conditions persist: no AI bubble pop, Mag 7 perform well but not spectacularly, no recession, housing stays frozen, and interest rates remain range-bound around 4-5%. Hosts give 75% odds for a 20%-plus S&P gain and 80% odds for at least a 10% intra-year correction. A key supporting argument is a JP Morgan chart showing forward P/E barely moved from 19x in early 2020 to 22x by end-2025 even as the 10-year Treasury more than doubled to 4.2%, yet the S&P doubled — demonstrating the market has absorbed higher rates through earnings growth.

### Profit Margins Justify Higher Multiples [00:13:25]

The hosts highlight a Duality Research chart plotting forward P/E against net profit margins, showing a strong upward relationship. S&P 500 profit margins have risen from about 7% in 2005 to near 16% estimated for 2027, driven by Mag 7 companies achieving unprecedented scale, moat expansion, and consistent ~20% earnings growth. Revenue per worker also reached a record high after stalling for 15 years, reinforcing the productivity narrative and the argument that bears citing CAPE or traditional valuation metrics are missing structural changes.

### International Stock Outperformance in 2025 [00:16:56]

The EFA outperformed the S&P 500 by roughly 14% in 2025 — the largest such gap since 1993 — with attribution analysis showing fundamental valuation improvement drove more of the gap than dollar weakness. European small-cap value outperformed significantly while U.S. small-cap value lagged, producing notable global dispersion. Hosts acknowledge this caught nearly every forecaster off guard and note that European stocks have outperformed the NASDAQ 100 over the trailing five years.

### AI: Hallucinations, Job Displacement, and LLM Utility [00:28:33]

Michael Batnick shares he is canceling a $200/month ChatGPT subscription because value uplift over the $20 tier seems insufficient, citing persistent errors on simple tasks. Data from Symblist shows AI model hallucination rates (incorrect answers as a share of all questions) remain at 45-52%, described as functionally a coin flip. Separately, Benedict Evans' annual report shows tech-sector layoffs spiked around ChatGPT's launch for macro reasons but have since fallen, suggesting AI displacement has not yet meaningfully materialized in employment data — though hosts acknowledge uncertainty about the medium-term path.

### Waymo Autonomous Vehicles [00:38:05]

Ben Carlson describes multiple Waymo rides in Phoenix, calling the experience transformative and noting the cars are Jaguar-branded, competitively priced versus Uber, and performed confidently in traffic. Hosts cite Nvidia's announcement that it aims to supply chips and software for robo-taxi fleets by 2027, and Waymo's reported 14 million trips in 2025 and 20 million lifetime trips by year-end.

### Disney Stock and Streaming [00:44:56]

Despite Disney parks being described as a near-gold mine with roughly one million visitors daily across all parks, earnings per share have gone nowhere in a decade. Disney has underperformed the S&P on every time frame from 1 to 30 years — the index is up approximately 1,800% cumulatively versus about 298% for Disney over 30 years. Hosts argue Disney Plus and linear TV assets are value destroyers; without streaming, a pure parks-and-IP-licensing model would likely command a far higher valuation. Streaming data shows Disney's share of TV viewing has been flat for three years, while Netflix commands 61% of streaming film viewing.

### Housing Market and Billionaire Tax [00:51:14]

The housing market remains frozen due to the lock-in effect: owners with low-rate mortgages are reluctant to sell, and the gap between monthly mortgage payments for buyers versus existing owners is wide. Ben suggests only government mortgage-rate buy-down programs could meaningfully restore activity. The episode also discusses a proposed California ballot measure that would retroactively impose a one-time tax on residents with over $20 billion in assets as of January 1, 2026 — approximately $1 billion per person, rising to $12+ billion for Larry Page — with hosts arguing billionaires would simply leave the state, making the measure ineffective.
