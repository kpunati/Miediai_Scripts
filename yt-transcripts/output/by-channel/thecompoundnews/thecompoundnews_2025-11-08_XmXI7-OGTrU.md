# The Two Biggest Stories of the Year: AI & Tariffs

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-11-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=XmXI7-OGTrU
**Duration:** 31:14

## Summary

In this episode of Animal Spirits: Talk Your Book, economists and financial media hosts Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson interview Ara Kharazian, economist at fintech company Ramp. Kharazian explains how Ramp's transaction-level spending data provides a uniquely granular view of AI adoption and tariff impacts across its business customers. The conversation contrasts Ramp's 44% AI adoption estimate with the government's 9% survey figure and explores why tariff effects are materializing far more slowly than most economists predicted.

## Key Takeaways

- Ramp's line-item transaction data shows roughly 44% of businesses have significant AI contracts, compared to only 9% per government surveys — a gap explained largely by the government's outdated survey question.
- AI product retention rates have climbed to 80% in 2024 from 50% in 2022, and average AI contract sizes are projected to reach around $1 million, suggesting deepening rather than fading adoption.
- Despite broad tariff announcements, only about 3% of Ramp invoices contain a tariff line item, indicating price pass-through has been far slower and more gradual than economists initially feared.
- Frictions at ports, unresolved conflicts with existing trade agreements, and companies seeking lower-tariff production alternatives have all slowed tariff transmission through the economy.
- The fastest-growing spending segment on Ramp is tech companies purchasing from other tech companies, including both AI-native providers and legacy software vendors.
- Kharazian is skeptical of pure AI-bubble narratives because companies buying AI products are renewing contracts and reporting productivity gains, unlike the dot-com era where revenue was sparse.

## Detailed Notes

### Introduction to Ramp and Its Data Set [00:01:42]

Ramp is described as the fastest fintech company to reach $1 billion in annualized revenue by automating expense reporting, receipt capture, and business card tracking. As its economist, Kharazian has access to line-item receipt data — not just vendor-level spend — allowing visibility into AI contract renewals, API usage, and spending across multiple AI providers simultaneously.

### Ramp AI Index: Adoption Rates and Government Discrepancy [00:06:13]

Ramp estimates 44% of its business customers have a significant AI contract, while the US government's biweekly survey reports only 9%. The government question — asking whether businesses used AI to 'produce goods and services' in the last two weeks — was written before 2023 and does not capture common AI uses such as coding assistance or customer service automation. Kharazian notes Ramp's estimate likely also understates adoption because it excludes free tools like Google's Gemini bundled into Workspace.

### Signs of Genuine Adoption vs. Bubble Dynamics [00:04:29]

Retention rates for AI products have risen from about 50% in 2022 to 80% in 2024, and contract sizes are growing toward an estimated $1 million average. Kharazian argues this distinguishes AI from the dot-com bubble: companies are paying and renewing rather than abandoning pilots. Enterprise adoption is growing more cautiously due to liability concerns and the complexity of integrating AI across customer-facing workflows.

### Tariff Impact: Slower Than Expected [00:23:37]

Ramp's data shows tariff line items appearing on a growing share of manufacturing and retail invoices, but the overall share remains around 3% despite broad tariff announcements — roughly double the year-ago level but far below what widespread tariffs would imply. Kharazian attributes the slow pass-through to port-level implementation frictions, conflicts with existing trade agreements, and companies shifting production to lower-tariff nations rather than absorbing costs. Price increases have thus been limited so far, though supply chain delays are a notable real disruption.

### AI and the Labor Market [00:20:33]

The discussion touches on concern about software engineer hiring trends reversing and the broader risk of AI-driven labor displacement. Kharazian takes a non-doom view, noting most US jobs require a physical component and are not immediately automatable. Early research suggests unemployment impacts are narrow and limited to specific sectors, and the overall unemployment rate has not increased meaningfully.
