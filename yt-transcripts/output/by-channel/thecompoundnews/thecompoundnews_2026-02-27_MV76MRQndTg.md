# Data Centers Are the New Fracking | TCAF 231

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=MV76MRQndTg
**Duration:** 83:36

## Summary

In TCAF episode 231, Josh Brown and Michael Batnick host Daniel Clifton and Chris Verrone of Strategas Research for a wide-ranging discussion on the political and market implications of AI and data centers. A central thesis is that data center opposition mirrors the fracking battles of the early 2010s, with local NIMBY resistance threatening capex plans but likely to be overcome through better industry communication. The episode also argues that a sustained market rotation is underway away from mega-cap tech toward equal-weight, industrials, materials, and international equities, driven by a shift from a post-Berlin Wall globalized regime to a multipolar, capex-intensive one. Additional segments cover the Anthropic-DoD AI safeguards standoff, weakness in Bitcoin and the US dollar, and the mechanics of Strategas's three thematic ETFs.

## Key Takeaways

- Public sentiment toward AI has turned sharply negative, with majorities concerned about job displacement and privacy, creating a political risk for the AI capex buildout.
- Data centers face community opposition analogous to fracking; the tech industry must proactively tout economic benefits such as property tax revenue to win local approval.
- A market regime shift is underway: the post-2008 era of mega-cap tech dominance is rotating toward equal-weight, industrials, energy, materials, and international stocks.
- Anthropic faces a tension between its 'safe AI' brand and the demands of its critical DoD customer, with both sides likely to reach a compromise given mutual dependence.
- Bitcoin is in a significant drawdown and entering a repair phase, while stablecoins are seen as a more durable long-term winner from crypto adoption.
- Year two of a presidential term historically delivers weak equity returns, but the 12 months after midterm elections have never produced a loss since 1938.
- Strategas's lobbying-intensity factor—measuring lobbying spend relative to company size—has produced strong returns by identifying companies with outsized Washington influence.

## Detailed Notes

### AI Backlash and Public Sentiment [00:13:41]

Josh Brown presents polling data showing a majority of Americans are more concerned than excited about AI, citing job displacement for recent college graduates as a key driver. Daniel Clifton notes the issue cuts into Trump's own base, creating political risk for AI capex investment. Sam Altman's public appearances are criticized as damaging to OpenAI's image.

### Data Centers as the New Fracking [00:18:25]

Daniel Clifton draws a detailed parallel between current data center opposition and the fracking battles of the early 2010s. He argues local NIMBY resistance—citing energy costs, water usage, and noise—has already caused 25 project cancellations in January. The resolution requires the tech industry to make the economic case proactively (e.g., property tax benefits), as the energy industry eventually did for fracking. Virginia, the largest data center hub, is a particular political flashpoint.

### Market Breadth and the Rotation Away from Mag7 [00:11:11]

Chris Verrone highlights that 2026 has already seen four days where the NASDAQ fell 1%+ while more stocks advanced than declined—versus only three such days in all of 2025. Equal-weight indexes, mid-caps, value, energy, and industrials are making new highs while the Nasdaq 100 is flat to down. The analogy to March 2000–March 2001 is raised but contextualized with key differences. Verrone views the rotation as a regime change from 'light' (asset-light, high-PE tech) to real assets and capex-heavy industries.

### Industrials and Capex Supercycle [00:38:38]

Clifton and Verrone argue the world is shifting from a post-Berlin Wall unipolar era (low rates, globalization, asset-light dominance) to a multipolar world demanding physical resources. The 'One Big Beautiful Bill' provides 100% expensing for capex, R&D, and factory building, incentivizing broad industrial investment beyond just AI. Clifton says companies lobbying for those provisions are outperforming the S&P by 15-20%. Verrone sees Corning and small-cap electrical equipment companies as emerging leaders.

### Anthropic vs. Department of Defense [01:00:33]

The segment covers Hegseth's ultimatum to Anthropic over AI safeguards and Anthropic's addition of Trump-aligned board member Chris Liddell. Clifton frames this as part of a broader defense-tech consolidation trend, comparing it to LHX's breakup and government partnerships. Both sides are seen as too mutually dependent to allow a permanent rupture; the dispute is expected to resolve through compromise on surveillance and content moderation terms.

### Gold, Bitcoin, and the US Dollar [01:04:59]

Verrone sees gold in a 'penalty box' for roughly six months after a parabolic move, with silver likely finished and copper making new highs. Bitcoin is characterized as in a significant drawdown with outflows from IBIT viewed as symptomatic rather than causal; historically it requires 6-12 months of repair. Clifton argues the dollar's decline simply returns it to its 2015-2019 range and could bounce as tariff rates fall; he sees a later, post-midterm leg lower as part of Trump's export strategy.

### Strategas Thematic ETFs and Lobbying Factor [01:13:55]

Clifton describes SAGP, which weights stocks by lobbying intensity relative to company size rather than raw spend, with 40% non-US exposure reflecting the global importance of Washington influence. He cites Vertex Pharmaceuticals and Eli Lilly as examples of high-lobbying compounders. Verrone's SAMM is a pure trend-following momentum fund that can go 100% cash. SAMT is the flagship macro thematic rotation fund managed by Jason Trennert's investment strategy team.
