# No One Can Figure Out 5% Treasury Rates | TCAF 173

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=sdW7uMCvSh8
**Duration:** 85:38

## Summary

Episode 173 of The Compound and Friends features Josh Brown, Michael Batnick, Julie Hyman, and Doug Boneparth covering the surge in 10-year Treasury yields toward 5% and its implications for stocks, the real estate market freeze, and uncertainty around the Federal Reserve's direction. The panel debates private equity's imminent push into 401(k) retirement plans, whether retail investors can realistically evaluate PE and private credit products, and the explosive then collapsing quantum computing stock bubble triggered by Jensen Huang's comment that useful quantum computers are 15-30 years away. Additional topics include Trump 2.0 presidential volatility, the labor market softening for office workers, return-to-office mandates, binge shopping and buy-now-pay-later trends, and the financial downfall of Adrian Peterson.

## Key Takeaways

- The 10-year Treasury yield approaching 5% is a genuine threat to equity valuations because bonds become a competing safe asset class, though Wall Street strategists see earnings—not the Fed or fiscal policy—as the primary driver of stocks.
- The term premium on Treasuries has turned positive and is described as the 'dark matter of Wall Street'—unexplained by inflation expectations alone—potentially reflecting broader risk concerns about US fiscal deficits.
- Private equity firms are preparing a lobbying push to gain access to 401(k) retirement savings, with panelists broadly supportive of the concept but concerned about investor education, fee transparency, and IRR literacy.
- Jensen Huang's offhand remark that useful quantum computers are 15-30 years away triggered single-day declines of 46-50% in several quantum computing micro-cap stocks, illustrating the fragility of thematic mini-bubbles.
- The real estate market remains deeply frozen because Fed rate cuts did not translate into lower mortgage rates; the 10-year actually rose 100 basis points after the first cut.
- Return-to-office mandates at large banks like JP Morgan may be as much about passive workforce reduction through attrition as about genuine productivity goals.
- Consumer spending as a share of GDP continues to rise, fueled by frictionless mobile commerce, social media shopping, and the growth of buy-now-pay-later products.

## Detailed Notes

### Rising 10-Year Treasury Yields and Market Impact [00:08:31]

Julie Hyman highlights that the 10-year Treasury yield rose roughly 50 basis points since mid-December 2024, with the 20-year already hitting 5%. The panel notes that yields rising alongside inflation concerns—rather than optimism about growth—is more threatening to equities. The term premium has turned meaningfully positive, described by Bloomberg as the 'dark matter of Wall Street' because experts cannot agree on how to measure or explain it. Bond vigilantism is mentioned as a nascent concern by John Hilsenrath, though panelists are skeptical it has fully arrived.

### Bull Market Year Three and Concentration Risk [00:19:20]

Charts from Todd Sohn show that in 2023 and 2024, roughly 69% of S&P 500 returns came from only 10 stocks—a record level of concentration. The panelists debate whether broadening is necessary to sustain the rally; the consensus is that it is not required as long as Magnificent Seven earnings growth continues, though a flat year after back-to-back 20%+ gains would be considered healthy. The CAPE ratio is noted to be around 37, making extreme valuations easy to envision reversing over a 10-year horizon.

### Presidential Volatility and Trump 2.0 Outlook [00:28:02]

Julie Hyman presents data showing VIX averaged 17.7 under Trump 1.0 versus 19.7 under Biden, and 2017 was the calmest year in market history despite a noisy news cycle. The panelists caution against reacting to headlines about Greenland, Panama Canal, or other political theater, but expect volatility to materialize if and when immigration enforcement roundups occur or tariffs on China are implemented around inauguration day.

### Labor Market Softening and Return-to-Office [00:32:19]

More than 1.6 million unemployed Americans have been job hunting for at least 6 months, a significant increase from end-of-2022 levels. The panel attributes part of the slowdown to AI-driven attrition rather than mass layoffs—companies like Klarna stopped recruiting altogether. JP Morgan's plan to mandate 300,000 employees back to the office 5 days a week is analyzed as primarily a financial decision to shrink headcount through voluntary attrition rather than a genuine productivity initiative. Government workforce cuts under DOGE are flagged as a potential additional wild card for near-term labor market disruption.

### Private Equity Access to 401(k) Plans [00:49:31]

The panel discusses the PE industry's lobbying effort to access the 401(k) market, with the conceptual argument that the illiquidity premium on PE matches well with the long-term, illiquid nature of retirement accounts. However, panelists raise concerns about the complexity of IRR metrics, opaque fee structures, and the lack of investor financial literacy around private credit. BlackRock is identified as the likely winner if a single-ticker bundled product combining public fixed income and private credit can be created. Yahoo Finance's ownership by a PE entity is disclosed as a potential conflict.

### Quantum Computing Stock Bubble [01:03:22]

Alphabet's quantum computing announcement sparked a brief bubble in micro-cap quantum stocks. Jensen Huang's subsequent comment that useful quantum computers are 15-30 years away caused Rigetti Computing, IONQ, D-Wave Quantum (QBTS), and Quantum Computing Inc. (QUBT) to each fall 46-50% in a single day. The episode treats this as an example of thematic mini-bubbles in the current casino-like market environment.

### Consumer Spending and Binge Shopping [00:58:56]

Americans are projected to spend $5.28 trillion at retail in 2024. The panel identifies frictionless mobile commerce (Amazon, Instagram, YouTube, TikTok) and affiliate influencer marketing as structural drivers. Buy-now-pay-later usage hit an all-time high during the holiday season, with Cyber Monday its biggest day on record. Doug Boneparth warns that BNPL reinforces bad spending behaviors and debt accumulation, though others note default rates remain low in the current economic environment.
