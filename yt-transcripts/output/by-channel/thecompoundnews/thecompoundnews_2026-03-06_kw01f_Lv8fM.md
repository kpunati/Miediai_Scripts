# True or False - Private Credit Is This Generation’s Subprime | TCAF 232

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-03-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=kw01f_Lv8fM
**Duration:** 75:43

## Summary

Episode 232 of The Compound and Friends brings together hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick with guest Garrett Baldwin, a research economist and author of 'Me and the Money Printer,' to analyze a volatile market week shaped by geopolitical events and ongoing stress in private credit. The conversation covers why U.S. equity markets have proven resilient to geopolitical shocks, the unusual dynamics of 2026's range-bound market where mega-cap tech lags while most large-cap stocks advance, and whether private credit represents a systemic risk akin to subprime mortgages in 2006-2007. Baldwin presents a liquidity-cycle framework drawn from CrossBorder Capital's work to explain market behavior and outlines specific signals he watches for inflection points.

## Key Takeaways

- U.S. energy independence is a primary reason equities have not reacted to Middle East geopolitical events as severely as energy-importing economies like South Korea or Europe.
- Passive investing now represents roughly 50% of equity market participation, creating a persistent price-insensitive buyer that cushions downturns compared to prior decades.
- Private credit is being priced at GFC-level default assumptions despite few actual defaults materializing yet, reflecting opacity and refinancing risk rather than confirmed credit deterioration.
- Mega-cap tech (Mag 7) has been bleeding while more than half of large-cap stocks are up 5% or more over three months, a breadth divergence not seen since 1992.
- Baldwin's liquidity-momentum framework signals market stress when more stocks are breaking down than breaking out, and the model turned negative on January 28, 2026.
- Investors should clearly identify themselves as traders or long-term investors, as the current algo-driven, round-trip intraday market punishes hybrid strategies.
- CrowdStrike is characterized as a network-effects business rather than pure SaaS, where each additional customer exponentially improves threat intelligence for all users under its umbrella.

## Detailed Notes

### Geopolitical Events and Market Resilience [00:02:09]

The hosts ask Baldwin why futures fell only about 1.2% after a weekend of Middle East missile strikes. He attributes market resilience primarily to U.S. energy independence — the U.S. is now a net energy exporter — contrasting sharply with energy-importing nations like South Korea and European markets that were hit harder. Baldwin warns that the situation remains one headline away from a commodity rally comparable to the Russia-Ukraine run-up.

### Weird Market Dynamics: Breadth Divergence and Passive Flows [00:14:30]

Despite the S&P 500 having a negative three-month return, more than half of large-cap stocks are up 5% or more and nearly 40% are up 10% or more — a divergence last seen in 1992. The hosts attribute this to rotation out of mega-cap AI names into energy, commodities, and consumer defensives. They argue passive investing (now ~50% of equity ownership vs. ~5% in the 1990s) creates a persistent price-insensitive buyer that prevents large sustained drawdowns and has fundamentally changed how quickly markets recover.

### Private Credit: Subprime Parallel and Systemic Risk [00:36:59]

The central debate rates the claim 'private credit is this generation's subprime' at a 7 out of 10. Baldwin argues private credit is the shadow banking system — largely unregulated loans originated by banks and handed off to Apollo, Ares, Blackstone, and similar firms — and that refinancing challenges, combined with U.S. Treasury crowding out capital, make it the most likely source of the next credit crisis. Brown counters that illiquidity in private credit structures may actually slow any blow-up to a 5%-per-quarter bleed rather than a sudden collapse, and notes that publicly traded BDCs serve as better real-time stress indicators than the higher-quality HYG/JNK ETF proxies.

### Liquidity Framework and Market Signals [01:06:41]

Baldwin explains his investment framework built on Michael Howell's CrossBorder Capital global liquidity index, which tracks approximately $185 trillion in global liquidity used primarily for refinancing rather than new growth. He layers this with a momentum indicator (stocks breaking out minus breaking down) and insider buying data scraped from EDGAR. The model went negative on January 28, 2026, and has remained negative despite the market being near all-time highs — an unusual combination. Baldwin watches SOFR and Federal Reserve SOMA operations as real-time liquidity gauges.

### CrowdStrike vs. Blackstone Minerals Trade Debate [00:58:14]

Baldwin contrasts CrowdStrike (high-multiple, momentum-driven) with Blackstone Minerals (BSM), a royalty-based energy company paying roughly 8% that owns rights to fuels powering AI data centers. Batnick describes trading CrowdStrike for a quick 12% gain on a momentum reversal, while Brown makes a structural bull case: CrowdStrike is a network-effects business where each new customer strengthens threat intelligence for all others, making churn nearly impossible and justifying a premium multiple relative to ARR growth.
