# The Perfect Price of Oil | Animal Spirits 456

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-03-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=T3ZQ52IqRac
**Duration:** 79:44

## Summary

In episode 456 of Animal Spirits, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson explore the paradox of low oil prices despite significant Middle East supply disruption, arguing that markets are not so much complacent as forward-looking. They debate whether AI represents a bubble, concluding it likely does not given hyperscaler capex commitments and real-world agentic use cases emerging at the SMB level. The episode also examines growing stress in private credit funds, improving housing affordability driven by wage growth rather than price declines, and the adequacy of American retirement savings as described by economist Allison Schrager.

## Key Takeaways

- Oil markets appear to be pricing in a near-term resolution to Middle East conflict rather than a prolonged supply disruption, reflecting forward-looking market behavior rather than complacency.
- Energy stocks have massively outperformed since COVID lows despite comprising only a small slice of the S&P 500, creating career risk for portfolio managers who underweighted the sector.
- Ben Thompson reversed his AI-bubble view, arguing that hyperscaler capex demand and the high compute cost per agentic interaction mean the business model does not require mass consumer adoption to be viable.
- Private credit redemption pressures at funds like Cliffwater highlight an asset-liability mismatch, with advisors caught between client anxiety and the long-term nature of the underlying loans.
- Housing affordability improved to a four-year best in February as wage growth outpaced stagnant home prices, suggesting prices declining may not be necessary for affordability recovery.
- Allison Schrager argues there is no retirement crisis: median net worth of Americans 65 and older has more than doubled in inflation-adjusted terms since the early 1990s.
- AI skepticism among Americans (only 17% view it positively per Pew) contrasts sharply with Chinese sentiment at 83%, with hosts noting a political backlash risk.

## Detailed Notes

### Oil Prices and Market Complacency [00:04:44]

Hosts discuss why oil has not spiked despite a Middle East conflict affecting 20% of global supply. Michael notes the Billy Bob Thornton clip from Landman — $78/barrel described as 'perfect' because it sustains exploration without pinching demand. Ben argues markets are not whistling past the graveyard; historically markets overreact rather than underreact, so the burden of proof lies with those claiming complacency.

### Stock Market Leadership and Energy Sector [00:13:45]

S&P 500 geopolitical event chart shows average 14%+ returns one year after major events, suggesting institutional awareness of historical patterns. Energy stocks have outperformed the S&P by over 200% from COVID lows and have destroyed tech returns over the same period, despite representing only 3.7% of the index. Michael raises career risk concerns for active managers who were forced into Mag 7 but now face a broadening market. Retail oil trading volumes surged over 1,100% month-over-month on WeBull amid speculation.

### AI: Bubble or Not? [00:27:50]

Ben Thompson reversed his AI bubble view, citing the economics of agentic compute: a $200/month Claude subscription can consume $5,000 in compute, meaning demand does not need to be mass-market. Real-world SMB use case (AI scheduling agent for upholstery company) cited as evidence of genuine value creation. Hosts note Taiwan's GDP surged 23.6% annualized in Q4 2025, driven almost entirely by TSMC's AI chip production. AI political backlash risk flagged: only 17% of Americans view AI positively versus 83% in China.

### Real Estate and Housing Affordability [00:45:18]

February affordability hit best level in four years at 27.4% of median income for median-priced home, driven by wage growth rather than price declines. Existing home sales running at 4.1 million annually versus long-term average of 5.2 million. OpenDoor's 4.99% mortgage offer discussed as potentially a promotional subsidy strategy rather than sustainable business.

### Private Credit Stress [00:49:22]

Cliffwater's $33 billion private credit fund received 14% redemption requests but only fulfilled 4%. Hosts debate advisor liability when clients redeem illiquid long-term vehicles prematurely. Key structural risk: approximately 20% of private credit loans are to SaaS companies facing AI disruption, unlike HYG where tech is only 9%. Ben argues maturity walls historically have not triggered crises; Michael notes no credit cycle stress has yet materialized, meaning distress signals may be premature. 2028 software loan maturity wall flagged as a watch item.

### Personal Finance and Retirement [01:04:08]

DoorDash usage data shows heaviest consumers are millennials aged 30-44 earning under $50K, raising questions about financial decision-making. Allison Schrager argues retirement crisis is overstated: median net worth for Americans 65+ has more than doubled in inflation-adjusted terms to roughly $450K, supplemented by home equity and Social Security. Hosts note IRS free-file program was shut down, correcting an earlier on-air misstatement.
