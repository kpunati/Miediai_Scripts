# The Week We All Found Out | TCAF 203

**Channel:** The Compound

**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=EQaubA4Xg6Q

## Summary

Episode 203 of The Compound and Friends brings together Josh Brown, Michael Batnick, Sam Ro (Ticker newsletter), and Luke Kawa (Sherwood News) for a wide-ranging discussion on the dominant market narrative of August 2025: AI capex is eating the US economy. The panel analyzes Tim Cook's White House visit and Apple's investment pledge, debates the merits and risks of allowing private equity and crypto into 401(k) plans via executive order, and examines charts showing AI infrastructure spending crowding out consumer spending as the marginal driver of both GDP growth and S&P 500 earnings. The episode also addresses the firing of the BLS commissioner and potential politicization of government economic data, closing with a tribute to the Odd Lots podcast and Future Proof conference announcements.

## Key Takeaways

- AI capital expenditure by hyperscalers (Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle) has become the primary driver of US economic growth and S&P 500 earnings expansion, even as consumer spending decelerates to its slowest pace since August 2020.
- Trump's executive order opening 401(k) plans to private equity, real estate, and crypto is seen as a significant potential exit liquidity mechanism for institutional sellers rather than a straightforward win for retail investors.
- The hosts argue crypto in retirement accounts is particularly risky because the implicit endorsement of a 401(k) menu could mislead participants into treating speculative assets as safe.
- Firing the BLS commissioner raises concerns about politicization of economic data; JP Morgan warned that the $2.1 trillion TIPS market depends on trust in CPI construction, making data integrity as important as the employment figures themselves.
- The AI capex-versus-consumer-spending divergence is characterized as a K-shaped dynamic inside the stock market: the S&P 493 shows little margin expansion while hyperscalers drive nearly all earnings growth.
- Historical job creation data shows technology-driven disruption has consistently created more jobs than it destroys over long horizons, but the near-term displacement of entry-level positions by AI is described as already visible in recent hiring data.
- Apple's $100 billion US investment pledge is viewed skeptically by the panel as largely symbolic, consistent with a pattern of large headline commitments that rarely materialize fully.

## Detailed Notes

### Apple, Tim Cook, and the White House Visit [00:11:33]

Tim Cook visited the White House and announced a $100 billion US investment, presenting Trump with a 24-karat gold-based plaque featuring Kentucky-made Corning glass. The panel views this as savvy political management rather than a genuine manufacturing shift, noting that Apple's total operating expenses for the last nine months were only $46 billion, raising questions about where the hundred billion originates. Luke Kawa draws a parallel to Jensen Huang's successful public pressure campaign that secured H100 export approvals to China without a comparable financial tithe.

### Private Equity and Crypto in 401(k)s [00:18:27]

Trump's executive order opens 401(k) plans to private equity, real estate, and cryptocurrency, unlocking access to an estimated $12.5 trillion in retirement savings. Josh Brown argues the outcome is unlikely to be catastrophic because guardrails like target date funds will constrain allocations, and fee compression is already occurring as seen with Vanguard's HarborVest partnership. Luke Kawa counters that private equity in retirement accounts functions primarily as exit liquidity for institutional sellers, echoing how the democratization of hedge funds a generation ago failed to deliver top-performing strategies to retail investors. The panel agrees crypto carries the unique risk that its presence on a 401(k) menu will be misread as a safety endorsement.

### AI Capex Dominating Economic and Market Growth [00:27:26]

Luke Kawa presents charts showing S&P 500 capital expenditures accelerating year-over-year as consumer spending decelerates to its slowest six-month pace since August 2020. Information processing equipment and software spending is approximately 6% of GDP yet is contributing more to growth than the 70%-of-GDP consumer sector. The panel discusses whether this divergence is sustainable, with the consensus that a prolonged consumer retrenchment would eventually weigh on AI spending itself, as hyperscalers' ultimate customers are enterprises and consumers. A second chart shows hyperscaler forward capex estimates briefly collapsed after the April tariff shock and then snapped back, indicating markets treat AI spending as economically sensitive but expect it to persist.

### AI as a Bubble? Historical Infrastructure Comparisons [00:49:53]

Paul Kasriel's analysis places AI data center capex at roughly 1.2% of US GDP, far below the 6% peak of railroad construction in the 1880s or the telecom fiber boom. One key distinction raised is durability: railroad tracks and dark fiber remained usable for decades, whereas GPU generations cycle rapidly, creating ongoing replacement demand that could sustain spending but also represents a structural difference in the economic footprint of the buildout.

### BLS Commissioner Firing and Data Integrity [00:57:21]

The firing of the BLS commissioner generates concern about politicization of government economic statistics. A JP Morgan note by Michael Feroli is read on air, warning that the $2.1 trillion TIPS market rests on trust in BLS CPI methodology and that data integrity is as important as the employment figures. The panel notes that declining survey response rates and funding cuts have already degraded data quality, and that politically motivated replacements would compound the problem rather than solve it.

### Odd Lots, Future Proof, and Closing Segment [01:02:18]

The hosts celebrate the New York Times Sunday Business section profile of Odd Lots podcast hosts Joe Weisenthal and Tracy Alloway, with Sam Ro and Josh Brown sharing personal anecdotes about their mentorship. The episode closes with the announcement that Bush and Blues Traveler will headline the Future Proof conference in September.
