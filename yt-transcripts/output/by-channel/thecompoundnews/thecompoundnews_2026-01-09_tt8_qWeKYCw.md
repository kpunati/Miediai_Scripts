# It's not 1929, but it might be - Andrew Ross Sorkin | TCAF 224

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-01-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=tt8_qWeKYCw
**Duration:** 66:15

## Summary

Andrew Ross Sorkin, author of '1929: Inside the Greatest Crash in Wall Street History,' joins The Compound and Friends to argue that a true repeat of 1929 is structurally impossible given the SEC, FDIC, modern leverage limits, and the modern policy playbook of flooding the system with money during crises. The conversation explores the dangerous allure of pattern recognition — comparing today's AI boom to 1929 or 1999 — while acknowledging that genuine excesses may now reside in private markets rather than public equities. Sorkin also reflects on his seven-year research process, his craft as an interviewer, and his most memorable on-air moments.

## Key Takeaways

- The structural safeguards erected after 1929 — SEC, FDIC, capital requirements, modern margin rules, and instant price discovery — make a true 1929-style cascade essentially impossible today.
- The modern policy playbook (learned from Bernanke's Great Depression research) is to inject massive liquidity in a crisis; the only scenario that breaks this is a bond market revolt triggered by runaway sovereign debt.
- Paul Tudor Jones privately characterizes the current market as September 1999, but the hosts note that the NASDAQ still tripled after that and then fell 85% — invoking 1999 is not a benign comparison.
- The 2020-2021 SPAC and meme-stock era was closer to 1929-style speculation than today; the excess has largely been cleared out in public markets, but private market valuations remain a key risk.
- Pattern recognition is hardwired into human cognition but routinely misfires in markets — the tariff panic of 2025 looked like 1930 Smoot-Hawley until Trump reversed course, invalidating the pattern.
- Sorkin argues the best interviews come from genuine curiosity, radical preparation, and listening — not from a scripted list of questions — citing his Elon Musk Dealbook interview as the most instructive example.
- Tokenization of private equity and venture capital into semi-liquid instruments is the next looming risk, as price discovery on conversion would likely impose immediate 20-30% haircuts.

## Detailed Notes

### Is It 1929? Key Structural Differences [00:00:00]

Sorkin outlines why today cannot replicate 1929: no SEC or insider-trading rules existed then, no FDIC meant bank runs eliminated 9,000 institutions, and 10:1 margin was normal. The stock ticker technology ran 4-7 hours behind real prices, amplifying panic selling. He stresses that 1929 was only the first domino — bad policy choices turned a crash into a 15-year depression, which was not pre-ordained.

### The One Plausible Path to a Modern Crisis [00:00:00]

The modern playbook — spending massively to arrest crises (as Bernanke applied in 2008 and 2020) — could eventually hit a wall if the bond market refuses to absorb more US debt. At that point, austerity spirals become possible. The hosts note the US largely owes the debt to itself and that 60% of Americans rely on the stock market for retirement, creating a massive political incentive to always bail out equities.

### Pattern Recognition Pitfalls and the 1999 vs. 1929 Debate [00:00:00]

Hosts explain the evolutionary basis for pattern recognition and why it misfires in markets. Sorkin notes the phrase 'democratization of finance' appeared in both the 1920s and today. Paul Tudor Jones called the current environment September 1999, but the hosts counter that 2021 — not today — was the true speculative mania, with 1,000 IPOs on each exchange, ARK ETFs up 100%, and celebrity SPACs. The 2022 correction in tech names (Meta and Nvidia each fell ~75%) may have already cleared that excess.

### Private Markets as the Next Risk [00:00:00]

Both hosts and Sorkin agree that genuine valuation excess now lives in private equity and venture capital, not public markets. Sorkin flags the coming tokenization of private assets into semi-liquid retail vehicles: when these instruments meet true price discovery, he expects immediate 20-30% haircuts — which he calls the healthiest possible outcome. He cites a recent private-to-public vehicle merger where the market instantly priced shares at $0.70 on the dollar.

### The SPAC Retrospective [00:00:00]

Sorkin recalls warning audiences about SPACs but being told he was being paternalistic. He clarifies that SPACs themselves were safe (guaranteed $10 floor) — the risk came upon conversion to operating companies, when nearly every SPAC saw a massive drawdown. Celebrity SPACs were the new element in 2020-21; the structure itself had been used in the mid-2000s to bring Chinese companies public.

### The Art of the Interview [00:00:00]

Sorkin attributes his versatility to genuine curiosity about people regardless of field. He describes over-preparing but then listening deeply rather than running through a list — a lesson he learned at 15 when interviewing NBA Commissioner David Stern and realizing on playback he had never followed up on key answers. His Elon Musk Dealbook interview is cited as his best: after Musk made incendiary remarks about advertisers, Sorkin navigated back to productive territory using prepared contingency pivots.
