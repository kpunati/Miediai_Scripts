# A National Housing Emergency | Animal Spirits 428

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-09-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qGCDacDhLko
**Duration:** 67:46

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 428 (September 3, 2025) features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson discussing a broad range of market and economic themes as summer ends. Key topics include whether current US equity valuations resemble the dot-com bubble, the surprising strength of junk bonds and credit markets despite rising defaults, and how consumer sentiment has become distorted by social media and the pandemic. The hosts also dig into housing policy, private equity underperformance, AI-driven job displacement at entry-level roles, and Affirm's strong earnings results.

## Key Takeaways

- Hosts argue the current market is not like the dot-com bubble because today's leading AI companies are profitable mature businesses, unlike dot-com-era firms.
- Junk bond spreads are near historically tight levels and credit markets show little sign of stress despite a modest uptick in defaults.
- Consumer sentiment surveys have become unreliable after the pandemic due to social media distortion; actual spending data tells a different story than sentiment readings.
- The US housing shortage of 3-5 million units cannot be solved by lower mortgage rates alone; hosts argue government-backed builder financing similar to post-WWII programs is needed.
- S&P 500 has outperformed private equity over the past decade, and hosts question the reliability of self-reported private equity index returns.
- AI is displacing entry-level college-educated workers (software developers, customer service) before affecting lower-education roles, which is counterintuitive.
- Seven-year auto loans now account for over a fifth of new vehicle financing, driven by consumers anchoring on monthly payment amounts rather than total cost.

## Detailed Notes

### Stock Market Valuations and the Dot-Com Comparison [00:06:38]

A Wall Street Journal article noted that the S&P 500 price-to-sales ratio now exceeds dot-com bubble levels, though the hosts push back hard, noting that price-to-earnings is far below those levels. They emphasize that today's top AI companies like Nvidia are highly profitable, unlike 2000-era firms. Nvidia's Jensen Huang estimated $3-4 trillion in data center AI infrastructure spend by end of decade.

### Credit Markets and Junk Bonds [00:14:42]

Junk bond yields spiked above 8% earlier in the year but have since fallen to roughly 6.5%, with spreads tightening and investor flows into high-yield funds accelerating. Private credit managed by business development companies grew approximately 33% over 12 months. The hosts note the bond market shows little systemic worry despite rising defaults.

### Consumer Sentiment and Economic Vibes [00:23:12]

The hosts discuss how traditional consumer sentiment readings have diverged sharply from economic fundamentals since the pandemic, attributing the gap to social media influence and pandemic-era brain rewiring. A Wall Street Journal poll found only 25% of Americans think people like them have a good chance of improving their standard of living, a record low since 1987. Meanwhile, United Airlines reported 5.6% premium cabin revenue growth alongside negative economy cabin results, consistent with a K-shaped spending environment.

### AI and Employment Effects [00:32:20]

A Stanford paper titled 'Canaries in the Coal Mine' found steep employment drops for young workers aged 22-25 in AI-exposed jobs such as software development and customer service. Counterintuitively, unemployment is falling among workers with less than a bachelor's degree, possibly due to immigration enforcement affecting that labor pool. The hosts highlight this as the key near-term AI risk rather than broad workforce displacement.

### Housing Crisis and Policy Options [00:37:10]

Reports surfaced that the Trump administration may declare a national housing emergency in fall 2025. The hosts note the US is 3-5 million housing units short due to underbuilding over the past decade. They argue that simply lowering mortgage rates would boost demand and prices without fixing the supply gap, and that what is needed is government-backed construction financing modeled on post-WWII housing programs that removed risk from home builders.

### Private Equity vs. S&P 500 and Affirm Earnings [00:39:17]

A Wall Street Journal piece on Yale's endowment model showed private equity 10-year annualized returns around 9% while the S&P 500 significantly outperformed. Hosts are skeptical of private equity index figures due to self-reporting bias. On Affirm, gross merchandise volume rose 43% year-over-year to $10.4 billion, with the company posting its first GAAP operating income quarter; delinquency data showed no consumer stress signal.

### Personal Finance: Auto Loans and Lifestyle Topics [00:46:30]

Seven-year auto loans now represent 22% of new vehicle financing, with average car prices up 28% in five years. Hosts frame this as a behavioral finance issue where consumers optimize for monthly payment rather than total cost. Separately, the hosts discuss the 996 work schedule trend among young San Francisco professionals and recommend the Netflix documentary 'Unknown Number: The High School Catfish' and Mission Impossible: Final Reckoning (with mixed enthusiasm).
