# Did Trump Blink? | Animal Spirits 405

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=I4J1RM5nYu0
**Duration:** 68:57

## Summary

In Animal Spirits episode 405, Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson react to the stock market's sharp rebound after Trump signaled tariff flexibility, debating whether the correction is truly over and cautioning against panic selling. They examine international stock outperformance — Europe up 18% YTD vs. the US — and contrast household equity ownership rates in the US (34% of financial assets) versus Europe (10%). Jerome Powell's steady, data-dependent Fed stance is reviewed alongside housing market stress, Klarna's IPO filing, and Gen Z economic challenges driven by COVID disruption, social media, and housing costs.

## Key Takeaways

- Trump's signal of tariff flexibility drove a sharp single-day rally, but hosts caution that calling the correction over is premature.
- Europe's 18% YTD outperformance over US equities is the largest since at least 2006, validating the case for international diversification.
- US households hold 34% of financial assets in equities versus only 10% in Europe, creating long-term potential for foreign equity adoption.
- Jerome Powell signaled patience, noting hard economic data remains solid despite soft sentiment surveys, and the market rallied sharply on his remarks.
- The housing market is in a de facto recession for homebuilders, with KB Home and Lennar down 40% and 35% respectively from peaks.
- Klarna's average customer balance of $87 and 40-day average loan duration suggest BNPL risk is more limited than feared, though recession dynamics are untested.
- Gen Z faces a uniquely difficult combination of post-COVID disruption, social media anxiety, and housing unaffordability that hosts view as a legitimate structural grievance.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Rebound and Trump Tariff Blink [00:12:37]

Hosts discuss the sharp single-day market rally after Trump signaled flexibility on tariffs. Key sector moves included XLY up 3.8%, IWM best day since the election, and VIX falling to 17.5. They caution against reading the bounce as the all-clear, noting the S&P was still down ~2% YTD through that Monday.

### Wealth Effect and Household Equity Exposure [00:15:43]

Citing Mike Bird of The Economist, hosts note US households held $38 trillion in equities with stock wealth at 170% of disposable income. They debate whether the wealth effect is direct (asset liquidation drives spending) or psychological/circular, ultimately concluding Vibes play a large role and that the top 10% of households, who own 90% of stocks, are most exposed.

### International Stocks and Diversification [00:18:49]

Europe is outperforming the US by 18% YTD — the widest margin since VGK launched in 2006. Hosts cite CTA positioning (long $52B Europe, short $34B US) as a contributing factor to the reversal. They invoke Peter Bernstein's framing of diversification as an offensive strategy, emphasizing you don't know where the next winner comes from.

### US vs. European Household Stock Ownership [00:21:57]

Goldman Sachs data shows US stocks as 34% of household financial assets versus ~10% in Europe and the UK — a level that has barely moved since 1990. Hosts view rising European equity adoption as a long-term structural tailwind, while also noting foreign investors now own 18% of the US equity market.

### 100-Baggers and Market Structure [00:28:10]

Bespoke data shows 19 Russell 1000 stocks became 100-baggers over the past 25 years, with Monster Beverage as the top performer (1000x) and Nvidia second. Hosts note surprisingly few pure-tech names on the list and flag a filed 2x Animal Spirits ETF (holding the five fastest-growing leveraged single-stock ETFs) as a potential flash-crash risk.

### Federal Reserve and Jerome Powell [00:35:29]

Powell signaled patience at the FOMC presser, emphasizing hard data remains solid while soft sentiment data is watched carefully. Hosts note the market rallied strongly on his remarks, a contrast to 2022. One host warns that Fed independence may erode as Trump pressures rate cuts, and that Powell may be the last politically independent Fed chair for some time.

### Housing Market Stress [00:43:57]

Mortgage originations to borrowers with credit scores below 740 have collapsed to their lowest share since 1965, a direct legacy of the GFC. Homebuilders KB Home and Lennar are down 40% and 35% from peaks respectively, and KB Home lowered fiscal 2025 revenue guidance citing declining consumer confidence. The 30-year mortgage rate remains near 6.8%.

### Klarna IPO and BNPL Sector [00:49:11]

Klarna filed for IPO amid meme-driven skepticism about BNPL viability. Hosts cite Mark Rubenstein's analysis: average loan duration is 40 days (vs 2.9 years for personal bank loans), average customer balance is $87 (vs $673 average credit card balance), and 99% of consumer loans are paid on time. They acknowledge recession behavior for the sector is untested.

### Gen Z and the Shrinking Middle Class [00:53:24]

FT data shows declining Gen Z confidence across housing, government, judiciary, and life expectations, alongside rising stress. Hosts attribute this to three unique structural factors: COVID disruption during formative years, social media anxiety, and housing unaffordability. A separate chart from Chris Freeman shows the middle class shrank from 55% to 39% since the 1960s, primarily because households moved up into the upper-income bracket.
