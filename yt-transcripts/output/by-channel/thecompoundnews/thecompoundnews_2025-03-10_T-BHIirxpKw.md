# How to Survive the Growth Stock Massacre | WDWL

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-03-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=T-BHIirxpKw
**Duration:** 33:45

## Summary

Josh Brown and Nick Colas of DataTrek Research dissect the February-March 2025 US equity pullback, attributing it primarily to excessive institutional positioning, a sharp expected dip in S&P 500 Q1 earnings due to tariff-driven guidance cuts, and elevated forward valuations around 21x. They contrast this with emerging bright spots in non-US markets — surging Chinese internet stocks and European defense equities — and provide a framework for navigating volatility using VIX thresholds, minimum-volatility ETFs, and historical drawdown data showing that genuine 10%-plus annual declines are rare and require severe catalysts.

## Key Takeaways

- Institutional investors entered 2025 near peak bullishness per the State Street risk appetite index, setting up a vulnerable positioning backdrop when tariff headwinds materialized.
- S&P 500 Q1 2025 earnings face an 8% sequential drop versus Q4 2024, making the current 21x forward P/E multiple difficult to justify given back-half earnings uncertainty.
- Chinese internet stocks (KWEB) and European defense equities have dramatically outperformed US large-cap tech year-to-date, driven by AI policy shifts in China and German fiscal rearmament.
- Consumer staples have already rotated to outperform technology over the prior year, making a late defensive rotation into staples or dividend ETFs potentially ill-timed.
- The VIX reaching 27+ (one standard deviation above mean) has been a statistically useful buy signal, delivering positive S&P returns in the subsequent month in each instance since 2023.
- USMV is preferred over SPLV for minimum-volatility exposure because its sector weights remain closer to the broad S&P 500, reducing regret risk in a tech-led recovery.
- Historically, S&P 500 annual declines exceeding 10% have occurred only 12 times since 1928, almost exclusively during recessions, world wars, or major policy shocks.

## Detailed Notes

### Institutional Positioning and the Setup for a Pullback [00:02:17]

Nick Colas uses the State Street institutional investor risk appetite index to show that large portfolio managers were at their most bullish in January 2025, with very low cash balances and heavy US equity overweights. Prior peaks in this index in July 2023 and July 2024 each preceded roughly 8% S&P pullbacks, making the current decline consistent with historical over-enthusiasm patterns.

### Earnings Air Pocket and Forward Valuation Pressure [00:05:25]

S&P 500 sequential earnings are expected to drop about 8% from Q4 to Q1 2025, driven by guidance cuts in consumer discretionary, materials, and energy due to tariff concerns, before rebounding 21% through year-end per street estimates. At 21x forward earnings versus a 10-year average of 18.4x, the multiple offers limited cushion, and Colas identifies 18-20x as a fair range if uncertainty persists.

### Non-US Market Outperformance [00:10:43]

Three catalysts are driving non-US equity strength: China's softening stance toward tech entrepreneurs and DeepSeek validating domestic AI capability; Germany's historic fiscal pivot toward defense and infrastructure spending ahead of a parliamentary deadline in late March 2025; and the general cheapness of non-US equities after years of US outperformance. KWEB is up over 26% year-to-date and a European defense ETF (EUA) is up 36%, including Rheinmetall, Airbus, and Rolls-Royce.

### US Sector Rotation: Staples vs. Tech [00:17:04]

A chart of consumer staples minus technology relative trailing returns shows staples have gone from their worst decade-long underperformance in late 2023 to actually outperforming tech over the prior year. Colas argues this rotation has already largely occurred, making a defensive pivot into dividend-paying staples (as advocated in a Wall Street Journal weekend article) feel late; investors caught between expensive staples and falling tech may simply sell the broad index.

### When and Why Bad Things Happen to Good Markets [00:21:14]

Since 1928, the S&P 500 has declined more than 10% on a total-return basis in only 12 calendar years — 8 during recessions, 3 during major geopolitical shocks (WWII, Gulf War II lead-up), and 1 from a Fed rate shock (2022). The key message is that genuine annual double-digit losses require severe macroeconomic or policy catalysts, and the current tariff uncertainty, while uncomfortable, has not yet met that threshold.

### Minimum Volatility ETFs: USMV vs. SPLV [00:22:17]

Both USMV (iShares) and SPLV (Invesco) are outperforming the S&P 500 year-to-date. USMV holds about 25% tech and tracks broader S&P sector weights, while SPLV owns the 100 lowest-volatility stocks and has only 4.6% tech with heavy utility and staples exposure. Colas prefers USMV to minimize sector drift and reduce upside regret if tech rallies, while acknowledging SPLV's longer track record.

### VIX Playbook and Entry Signals [00:27:34]

DataTrek's VIX playbook uses a 27.3 threshold (one standard deviation above the mean since 2023) as a buy signal. Three prior VIX spikes to or above this level — SVB failure (26.5), the yen-carry unwind flash crash (38.6), and the December 2024 hawkish Fed meeting (27.6) — all delivered positive S&P 500 returns over the subsequent month. Colas advises waiting for a 27-handle close before adding equity exposure in the current environment.
