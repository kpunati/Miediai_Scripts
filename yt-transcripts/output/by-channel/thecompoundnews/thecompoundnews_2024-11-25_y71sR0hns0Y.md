# What if This Is the Minsky Moment Right Now?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-11-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=y71sR0hns0Y
**Duration:** 41:47

## Summary

Josh Brown hosts DataTrek Research co-founders Nick Colas and Jessica Rabe for a wide-ranging discussion on market conditions as of late November 2024. The conversation covers record-low corporate bond spreads as an indicator of extraordinary investor confidence, a structured examination of whether current speculative conditions qualify as a Minsky moment, and detailed sector-level valuation analysis showing most S&P 500 sectors trading at premiums to their 10-year average forward PE multiples. The guests also assess the historically strong U.S. labor market and conclude with a comparative analysis demonstrating that today's leading tech companies have fundamentally superior financial metrics — particularly return on equity — compared to 1990s market leaders, justifying higher valuations.

## Key Takeaways

- Corporate bond spreads for both investment grade and high yield are near multi-decade lows, signaling extreme optimism but not yet a confirmed catalyst for a Minsky-type unwind.
- The market appears to be in Minsky's 'speculative borrowing' phase rather than the more extreme 'Ponzi' phase, with the absence of a clear catalyst making timing any reversal difficult.
- S&P 500 forward PE of 22x sits 3.9 points above its 10-year average; technology leads multiple expansion at 29.2x, up 38% from its 10-year average, yet elevated rates have not reversed the trend.
- The U.S. labor market remains historically tight with 4.1% unemployment — lower than the entire 2000s expansion — and wage growth of 4.6% above the peaks of the prior two cycles.
- Compared to the top S&P companies of 1995, today's leaders (Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft) deliver three times the net margins and nearly three times the ROE, making historical PE comparisons structurally misleading.
- Small- and mid-cap indices trade at notably lower PE multiples (15-16x) versus large caps, representing relative value even as large cap valuations appear stretched.
- Fed patience on rate cuts is well-grounded in ongoing labor market strength, and futures markets are pricing in only two to three cuts over the next 13 months.

## Detailed Notes

### Corporate Bond Spreads at Historic Lows [00:03:23]

Investment grade bond spreads are at levels not seen since the mid-1990s; high yield spreads are similarly near record lows. Nick Colas notes that corporate bond investors — a risk-averse cohort — are signaling 'nothing but blue skies' for corporate profits over average bond durations of eight to nine years. The hosts estimate about 70% of this optimism is standalone latent confidence rather than a derivative of equity market levels.

### Minsky Moment Risk Assessment [00:09:40]

Josh Brown outlines Minsky's three-stage lending model — hedge, speculative, and Ponzi borrowing — and asks whether current conditions qualify. Colas argues the market appears to be in the speculative phase but that a genuine Minsky moment requires a specific catalyst (e.g., an oil shock or credit crisis); he sees no such catalyst presently. MicroStrategy's 600%+ year-to-date gain and its position as the most-traded U.S. stock for a day is flagged as a speculative signal, though Colas notes there were 'a hundred MicroStrategies' in 1998-1999, suggesting one alone is insufficient for a systemic turning point.

### S&P 500 Valuations and Sector Analysis [00:15:57]

Jessica Rabe presents FactSet data showing the S&P 500 at 22x forward earnings versus an 18.1x 10-year average, a 22% premium. Technology trades at 29.2x, up 8.1 points from its 10-year average. Almost every sector trades at a 1-5 point premium to its 10-year average PE, with energy and real estate as the only exceptions. Rabe attributes the premium not to low interest rates (the 10-year yield is 4.3% versus a 2.45% 10-year average) but to investor confidence in superior U.S. corporate earnings power and global capital flows preferring U.S. large caps.

### U.S. Labor Market Strength [00:25:27]

Initial claims at 213,000 are near multi-year lows and comparable to 1970 absolute levels despite a doubled labor force. The unemployment rate of 4.1% is below every reading during the entire 2000s expansion. The Atlanta Fed wage tracker shows a 4.6% three-month median wage growth, above the peaks of the last two cycles. The ratio of job openings to unemployed workers remains one standard deviation above its long-run average, and the labor shortage is noted to predate the pandemic, beginning around 2018.

### Comparative Quality Analysis: 1995 vs. 2024 Market Leaders [00:33:57]

Nick Colas presents a DuPont analysis comparing the top three S&P companies in 1995 (GE, Exxon Mobil, Coca-Cola, ~31% ROE, ~10% net margins) to 2024 leaders (Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft, ~89% ROE, ~36% net margins). He argues that markets price ROE, and the structurally superior returns of today's leaders — anchored in global scalability, moats, and high margins — justify materially higher PE multiples, making CAPE-style historical comparisons misleading. Competitive threats (overseas tech rivals, disruptive startups) are discussed but assessed as unlikely to materialize within a decade.
