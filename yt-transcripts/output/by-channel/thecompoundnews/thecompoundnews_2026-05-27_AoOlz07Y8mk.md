# A Fire Alarm For Interest Rates | Animal Spirits 466

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AoOlz07Y8mk
**Duration:** 60:28

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 466 opens with discussion of the SpaceX IPO filing, examining its large net loss relative to revenue and how Nasdaq's modified float-based index inclusion rules could create unusual buying pressure. The hosts then debate whether rising long-term bond yields represent a genuine fire alarm or a normalization toward a higher-growth, higher-inflation equilibrium. Additional segments cover historically low consumer sentiment driven by social media amplification, Jeff Bezos's proposal to zero out taxes on the bottom half of earners, the thesis that AI has captured the talent and mindshare that crypto once sought, and Michael Batnick's contrarian buy of Floor & Decor as a long-horizon bet on the coming home renovation cycle.

## Key Takeaways

- SpaceX's declining average revenue per user is explained by customer mix shift toward lower-paying residential and mobile users, not fundamental business deterioration.
- Nasdaq's three-times-float cap for SpaceX's index inclusion limits forced passive buying pressure relative to its $1.75 trillion headline valuation.
- The yield curve returning to a normal upward slope (long rates above short rates) is a sign of normalization, not crisis, according to the hosts.
- Consumer sentiment readings are structurally broken as a reliable economic indicator due to social media amplification of negativity, making them unreliable for investment decisions.
- Crypto appears to have missed its consumer adoption moment as AI talent, capital, and attention have largely displaced it.
- Floor & Decor is positioned as a multi-decade renovation-cycle play given the aging U.S. housing stock and the lock-in effect of elevated mortgage rates.
- Eli Lilly's Retatrutide Phase 3 results showing ~28% body weight loss represent bariatric-surgery-level outcomes and could have major healthcare cost implications.

## Detailed Notes

### SpaceX IPO and Nasdaq Index Inclusion [00:05:40]

Paul Kedrosky flagged that massive equity supply from OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX IPOs creates a 'money has to come from somewhere' math problem, though the hosts push back on the idea of a top signal. Om Malik's analysis explains SpaceX Starlink's ARPU decline as a customer mix shift from high-paying maritime and aviation terminals toward lower-paying residential consumers, not business degradation. Nasdaq modified its rules to allow 15-day fast-track index inclusion for SpaceX but capped its index weight at three times its public float to protect against inflated insider-share market caps.

### Bond Yields: Fire Alarm or Normalization? [00:17:27]

Investor John Arnold described rising 30-year bond yields across major economies as a fire alarm being ignored. The hosts argue the normalization of the yield curve (long rates above short rates) is healthy and expected, not recessionary. Warren Pies data showing 18% S&P 500 forward sales growth corresponds to roughly 6% annualized nominal GDP growth, suggesting the bond market may simply be pricing in a higher-growth, higher-inflation regime than the 2010s. The hosts say they would only become alarmed if the 10-year yield broke out sustainably above 5%.

### Consumer Sentiment and the Bezos Tax Proposal [00:27:31]

University of Michigan consumer sentiment is at its lowest level since 1950, which the hosts attribute primarily to social media amplification rather than actual economic conditions, calling the metric 'broken' as a standalone indicator. The Bezos proposal to zero out federal income taxes for the bottom 50% of earners—who represent only 3% of total tax revenue—is discussed as appealing in isolation but potentially dangerous for class dynamics and social cohesion. The hosts note the irony that many who express the most economic pessimism online have benefited significantly from the simultaneous boom in stock and home prices.

### AI vs. Crypto, and Tech Observations [00:36:30]

The hosts debate whether crypto missed its consumer adoption moment, noting that talent and capital flows have clearly shifted toward AI. Benedict Evans data shows OpenAI at 900 million weekly users with only 5% paying, suggesting massive early-stage adoption. Jeff Bezos's CNBC interview outlined an optimistic AI thesis: productivity gains leading to deflation, labor shortages, and more businesses and jobs—a counterweight to prevailing AI-doom sentiment.

### Housing Market and Floor & Decor Thesis [00:41:07]

Michael Batnick disclosed buying Floor & Decor (FND) as a contrarian bet on a housing renovation cycle, noting the stock is in a 65% drawdown. The Wall Street Journal data shows most U.S. homes are 55 years old or older, and Redfin data indicates two-thirds of baby boomers have lived in their homes 15+ years, creating pent-up renovation demand. The hosts debunked a Torsten Slok chart showing renovation spending surging—the rise was an artifact of construction spending falling in the denominator rather than renovation dollars actually increasing.

### Recommendations and Entertainment [00:56:38]

Michael highlights a positive experience with Function Health's 96-panel blood test and cancer screening at roughly $1,400-$1,500, noting his biological age came back at 30.8. Ben recommends the Netflix film Remarkably Bright Creatures. The hosts note IMAX may be fielding acquisition interest and discuss vertical video streaming (Reels) now generating more revenue than Netflix, illustrating the scale hidden inside big tech platforms.
