# The Biggest Investing Lessons of 2024

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2024-12-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=LLAMZN7Yn74
**Duration:** 41:31

## Summary

Nick Colas of DataTrek Research joins Josh Brown for a year-end review of the major investing lessons from 2024. The conversation covers quarterly analysis of how 10-year Treasury rates shaped equity returns across asset classes, a multi-timeframe comparison of US versus international equity performance, and a breakdown of the S&P financial sector's leadership role for the year. Colas also rebuts the dot-com bubble comparison using indexed NASDAQ price charts and argues that superior corporate return on equity fundamentally explains sustained US equity outperformance.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500 worked in all four quarters of 2024 regardless of rate direction, while rest-of-world equities only outperformed in Q3 when rates actually fell, confirming global investors use rate declines as the green light for international allocation.
- US market dominance is not solely a tech story: over the last 10 years every S&P sector except energy outperformed MSCI EAFE, MSCI EM, and ACWX on a compound annual basis.
- Financials were the top-performing S&P 500 sector in 2024 with a 33% average return and a 29% median stock return, driven by rate normalization and deregulation expectations after years of underperformance.
- The current NASDAQ rally is not analogous to the dot-com bubble: the index is up about 200% over five years versus 570% peak-to-start in the late 1990s, and year-over-year returns are near average historical levels.
- The top five S&P 500 companies average a 62% return on equity versus 39% for top European names and 13% for top EM names, which Colas argues is the fundamental driver of US equity premium, not just reserve currency status.
- Colas attributes roughly 80% of US outperformance to superior corporate capital allocation culture and only 20% to reserve currency / deficit spending advantages, and sees no structural catalyst to close the ROE gap.

## Detailed Notes

### Treasury Yields and Quarterly Equity Returns [00:03:19]

Colas presents a chart showing 10-year yields against S&P, Russell 2000, and ACWX quarterly returns. In Q1 and Q2 rates were rising or elevated and investors crowded into US large caps; rest-of-world and small caps underperformed. In Q3, rates fell and the risk-on trade returned with Russell and ACWX outperforming the S&P. In Q4 rates rose again but a strong dollar hurt non-US returns while the Trump trade supported US small caps, leaving full-year results of S&P +27%, Russell +16%, ACWX +7%.

### US vs International Equity Performance [00:07:32]

Over the past decade the US has gained 14 percentage points of global equity market share, rising from 50% to roughly 66% of the global equity index, while every country except China and Taiwan has lost share. Colas shows that on a one-, five-, and ten-year CAGR basis every US large-cap sector except energy has outperformed MSCI Europe, Japan, and EM averages, disproving the argument that US outperformance is solely attributable to tech. Colas attributes 80% of the gap to superior US corporate governance and capital allocation and 20% to reserve currency effects.

### S&P Financials Leadership and Broad Market Rally [00:20:16]

Financials were the top-performing S&P sector in 2024, led by KKR (+87%), Synchrony Financial (+76%), Goldman Sachs (+51%), and others including American Express, Progressive, PayPal, and Brown & Brown. Colas notes financials are now the second-largest S&P sector at 13.3% and that 66% of financial stocks sit above their 200-day moving average, suggesting sustainable leadership rather than a mean-reversion bounce. The overall market rally was notably broad in 2024: all 11 sectors were positive, industrials were up 21%, and the Mag-7's contribution to index returns fell from 83% in 2023 to 68%.

### NASDAQ vs Dot-Com Bubble and ROE Analysis [00:29:41]

Colas overlays the 1995-2000 NASDAQ bubble trajectory against 2019-present, showing the current rally is up roughly 200% from the starting point versus 570% at the 2000 peak; year-over-year NASDAQ returns at 35% are well below the near-100% gains seen in 1999-2000. A 'double in 12 months is a bubble' rule has not been triggered. On ROE, the US top five companies average 62% (Apple 165%, Nvidia 67%, Microsoft 33%, Amazon 15%, Alphabet 26%) versus 39% for top MSCI EAFE names and 13% for top MSCI EM names, with Colas arguing this fundamental quality gap is persistent and unlikely to close without significant structural reform in Europe and emerging markets.
