# Ben's Bearish | Animal Spirits 406

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=l6ctyPRfBvE
**Duration:** 62:01

## Summary

Episode 406 of Animal Spirits features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson recording the day before Trump's 'Liberation Day' tariff announcement, with Ben sharing his most bearish market outlook in 15 years. The conversation covers the binary nature of tariff outcomes — either a genuine economic disruption or an elaborate negotiating tactic — alongside the growing stress on consumers evidenced by auto loan delinquencies and repossessions. They also examine structural issues including mag-seven stock drawdowns, the push of private equity into 401k plans, the debate over options-based buffered ETF strategies, and an increasingly divergent housing market between hot Midwest markets and cooling Sun Belt cities.

## Key Takeaways

- Ben Carlson expresses his most bearish sentiment in 15 years, while Michael Batnick notes the S&P is only down ~5-6% year-to-date despite significant uncertainty.
- The tariff situation presents a binary outcome: either a genuine economic policy causing lasting damage, or a negotiating tactic that resolves quickly — and no one will admit they were uncertain afterward.
- Auto tariffs of 25% could add $5,000–$15,000 to new vehicle prices, likely leading consumers to extend loan terms rather than reduce purchases, increasing household debt.
- AQR research finds options-based buffered ETF strategies mathematically underperform passive equities, but the hosts argue the psychological benefit of keeping investors invested may justify them.
- Private equity's push into 401k plans is driven by institutional investors being 'tapped out,' raising concerns that average retirement savers won't understand illiquid investments.
- The housing market is diverging sharply: competitive bidding wars in places like Northern Virginia contrast with 12+ months of supply in Miami due to rising insurance costs and prior overbuilding.
- Getting out of markets during downturns is easy, but identifying the bottom to get back in is historically very difficult — every major market low since the 1980s was non-obvious in real time.

## Detailed Notes

### Tariffs and Liberation Day [00:01:24]

Recorded April 1st, the day before Trump's Liberation Day announcement. The hosts frame the situation as binary: tariffs are either real policy causing lasting economic damage, or a negotiating tactic for deal-making. Both agree that whichever outcome occurs, observers will claim it was obvious in hindsight. Michael is skeptical tariffs are real; Ben is increasingly leaning toward believing they are, noting how long the situation has continued.

### Auto Tariffs and Consumer Stress [00:03:54]

25% tariffs on imported autos were announced, estimated by Neil Dutta to represent a quarter-point GDP drag. Car repossessions hit their highest level since 2009 in 2024. Dan Ives estimated $5,000–$15,000 in price increases per vehicle. Ben predicts consumers will extend loan terms rather than downsize vehicle preferences, deepening household debt rather than changing behavior.

### Economic Uncertainty and Corporate Sentiment [00:08:23]

Dallas Fed energy survey quotes show executives frustrated by conflicting administration signals, noting that '$70 per barrel is the new $50 per barrel' and that current uncertainty is freezing corporate planning. Torsten Slok's data shows household and corporate balance sheets are in relatively good shape compared to the government, suggesting any recession would likely be shallow. Ben shares his most bearish sentiment in 15 years, comparing current policy risks to the Great Depression era of self-inflicted economic wounds.

### Stock Market Assessment and Mag-Seven [00:18:30]

Despite massive headline uncertainty, the S&P 500 is only down 5-6% year-to-date, with the S&P 493 (ex-mag-seven) essentially flat. All mag-seven stocks except Apple are in bear market territory, with Tesla down ~50% and Nvidia down ~30%. The hosts note the market appears to be pricing in the negotiating-tactic outcome, and warn that a hard Liberation Day announcement could create a sharp air-pocket decline.

### Options-Based Strategies and Buffered ETFs [00:23:47]

AQR (Cliff Asness and Daniel Villalon) published a paper showing that 99 options-strategy funds with histories back to January 2020 did not outperform passive US equities nor provide meaningfully better drawdown protection. The category has grown to $234 billion from near zero. The hosts acknowledge the math but argue that keeping investors invested through volatile periods justifies the psychological trade-off. BlackRock projects the category will triple to $650 billion by 2030.

### Private Equity in 401ks and Household Wealth [00:29:46]

Bloomberg reported that the bottom 50% of US households hold only 2.5% of wealth, with nearly half of that concentrated in real estate. The hosts add historical context, noting the bottom 50% held just 0.6% of wealth at the post-GFC trough in 2011. Private equity firms are targeting the $12 trillion 401k market after institutional investors became 'tapped out.' Both hosts are skeptical the average 401k participant will understand illiquid private equity investments, and see the push as liquidity-driven by asset managers rather than investor-benefit-driven.

### Housing Market Divergence [00:46:27]

The median monthly mortgage payment has risen from $1,600 in early 2022 to $2,800 at current rates and prices. A stark regional divide is emerging: Northern Virginia saw 50 prospective buyers and 7 over-asking offers on one listing in a weekend, while Miami has over 12 months of housing supply versus a national average of 3.5 months. Florida, Georgia, Texas, and South Carolina are seeing prolonged days-on-market, while Midwest markets remain competitive — creating attractive buyer opportunities in Sun Belt markets.
