# Most Stocks Suck | Animal Spirits 414

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-05-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cxjMfvIn1x8
**Duration:** 67:12

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 414 features Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson debating whether rising long-term bond yields signal a crisis or simply a return to historical norms, with the hosts leaning toward normalization. They examine Morgan Stanley research showing that most individual stocks suffer severe permanent drawdowns, reinforcing their conviction in index fund investing. The discussion covers private equity's push into retail and 401(k) channels, the frozen housing market, AI venture capital trends, and listener feedback on Social Security claiming strategies. The episode also touches on cultural themes including the psychology of wealth display and entertainment recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- Bond yields in the 4-5% range are likely normalization relative to the artificially low rates of the 2010s, not a signal of imminent crisis.
- Research on 6,500 stocks from 1985-2024 shows the median drawdown is 85% and only 46% of affected stocks ever recover their peak, validating index fund strategies over individual stock picking.
- The probability of experiencing a bear market increases with holding period, reaching 100% over any 15-year rolling window since 1950, a fact advisers should communicate alongside long-term return data.
- April outflows from money market funds largely go to tax payments rather than equity purchases, explaining seasonal stock market softness around tax deadlines.
- Private equity is expanding toward retail investors and 401(k) plans, raising concerns about illiquid or underperforming assets being sold to less-sophisticated buyers.
- The US housing market is at the slowest April sales pace since 2009, driven by 7% mortgage rates and a lack of starter homes — only 22% of new construction is under 1,500 square feet versus 92% in 1950.
- IMAX captured roughly 20% of the domestic opening weekend box office for Mission Impossible from approximately 1% of theaters, demonstrating premium theater resilience.

## Detailed Notes

### Bond Yield Normalization Debate [00:02:59]

Hosts argue that scary bond market headlines overstate the risk; the 30-year yield has traded in a 4-5% range for two years. Matthew Klein's analysis of Japan's post-pandemic yield adjustment is cited as evidence that current US yields align with higher growth and inflation. The net fiscal impact of the pending tax bill is described as roughly contractionary when tariffs are included, partially offsetting deficit expansion fears.

### Retail Investor Behavior and Financial Education [00:09:41]

A listener email challenges the notion that retail investors are more disciplined, noting that recent drawdowns (COVID crash, tariff tantrum) were brief and rewarded immediate buyers — a different test than the dot-com bust or GFC. Hosts acknowledge the counterpoint but suggest improving automatic contribution flows may itself be shortening drawdown duration. Money market outflows in April are attributed primarily to tax payments, not equity purchases, based on Treasury General Account data.

### Stock Drawdown Research and Index Fund Case [00:33:26]

A Morgan Stanley report by Michael Mauboussin examining 6,500 stocks from 1985-2024 finds the median drawdown is 85% over 2.5 years, with only 46% of stocks recovering their peak. The average recovery is 340%, but this is heavily skewed by outliers like Nvidia and Netflix. Ben Carlson discloses he has been trimming individual stock positions and redirecting proceeds to index funds, calling it less 'brain damage.'

### Housing Market and Real Estate Trends [00:37:50]

Existing home sales are at the slowest April pace since 2009 despite prices not falling, as 7% mortgage rates lock out buyers without compelling sellers to lower prices. The median age of homes sold has risen from 27 years in 2012 to 36 years, reflecting a construction gap in the 2010s. Only 22% of new homes are 1,500 square feet or less, down from 92% in 1950, eliminating the entry-level starter home segment.

### Private Equity and Retail Access [00:42:23]

Thoma Bravo co-founder warns that unsold private equity assets could end up in retail-targeted funds, effectively making retail investors bag holders for positions institutions would not absorb. Hosts note that 87% of US companies with over $100 million in revenue are now private, lending legitimacy to the access argument, though they caution about wide range of outcomes and opaque valuations. The recommended path for retail exposure is via target-date funds with a modest fixed allocation to alternatives.

### AI Venture Capital and Technology Commentary [00:27:09]

CB Insights data shows AI now drives one in five venture deals, up from one in ten in 2022. OpenAI raised $40 billion at a $300 billion valuation. Eight early-stage AI companies set a record with $100 million-plus mega rounds in a single quarter. Nvidia has participated in 49 equity deals since 2020, matching Google's total.

### Social Security and Retirement Planning [00:50:45]

Many listeners report taking Social Security early based on personal break-even calculations, typically citing a break-even age of 77-80. Hosts present IMF data suggesting the average 70-year-old in 2022 has the cognitive ability of a 53-year-old in 2000, supporting the strategy of working longer to delay claiming and reduce longevity risk.

### Entertainment: IMAX, Mission Impossible, and Streaming [00:53:41]

IMAX achieved a record Memorial Day opening with $31 million from Mission Impossible, capturing roughly 20% of domestic box office from about 1% of theaters. Total domestic holiday weekend box office hit $326.7 million, exceeding the prior record of $314 million set in 2013. YouTube's share of TV viewing grew by 3%, now exceeding all other streamers combined.
