# Why Trend Following Works in the Stock Market | TCAF 197

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-06-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=jEgy5KQI12o
**Duration:** 73:40

## Summary

In this episode of The Compound and Friends, hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick sit down with Cole Wilcox of Longboard Asset Management to explore trend following applied specifically to individual equities — a niche Wilcox has pioneered since publishing his landmark 2005 paper 'Does Trend Following Work on Stocks?' Wilcox argues that a rules-based, systematic approach removes the behavioral biases that devastate discretionary investors, particularly around selling winners too early and failing to re-enter after a drawdown. The conversation covers the mechanics of trend following — moving averages, average true range, position sizing, and stop-loss calibration — as well as why Longboard's stock-only trend strategy is highly uncorrelated to traditional multi-asset CTA funds. A recurring theme is the behavioral and psychological advantage of systematic investing: investors are more likely to stay the course when they understand what they own and can tolerate the ride.

## Key Takeaways

- Trend following on individual stocks is distinct from multi-asset CTA trend following and offers substantially higher returns with low correlation to that category.
- Average true range is a critical but underappreciated tool for calibrating position sizing and stop-loss levels to a stock's actual volatility profile.
- The primary benefit of trend following is not return maximization but drawdown management and behavioral reinforcement — helping investors stay invested through cycles.
- Most individual stocks are long-term losers; systematic trend following helps capture the rare outsized winners while aggressively cutting losers.
- Multi-asset trend strategies suffer from persistent FOMO-driven redemptions when the S&P 500 outperforms; stock-only trend strategies mitigate this by keeping investors in the asset class they care about.
- Investing by analogy to past market cycles is a significant risk, as investor demographics, valuation regimes, and information speed have fundamentally changed.

## Detailed Notes

### Formative Market Experiences and Systematic Investing Origins [00:00:00]

Wilcox and Brown share how living through the 1997-2002 boom-bust cycle shaped their risk thinking. Wilcox argues that personal experience creates powerful biases — useful for understanding risk, but dangerous when used to reason by analogy. This led Wilcox to focus on systematic strategies that encode historical data rather than subjective memory, separating the math from the opinion.

### Trend Following Philosophy and the Case Against Multi-Asset CTA [00:00:00]

Wilcox frames trend following as a capital preservation tool designed to remove the 'double zero' of permanent capital loss. He distinguishes his stock-specific approach from traditional multi-asset CTA strategies, which are long-short across commodities, currencies, and indexes. His fund concentrates on equities because capitalism reliably produces identifiable mega-trends in individual companies, which he argues is a clearer fundamental rationale than trading corn or sugar.

### Mechanics: Moving Averages, ATR, and Stop-Loss Calibration [00:00:00]

Wilcox explains that simple moving averages (200-day or 200-week) are sufficient trend signals; complexity adds noise, not precision. Average true range is used to equalize risk across positions with different volatility profiles — a stock with twice the ATR gets half the position size. For stocks trading far above their moving average (e.g., Nvidia at 100% above its 200-day), position sizing can be reduced rather than moving the stop closer, preserving the integrity of the system.

### Behavioral Advantages and Client Retention [00:00:00]

The psychological case for trend following centers on reducing drawdowns so investors do not abandon their strategy at the worst moment. Wilcox notes that multi-asset trend funds experienced severe redemptions during the S&P's strong run because clients experienced FOMO relative to a benchmark they care about. His stock-only fund has much higher retention because clients understand what they own and the strategy's performance is more legible.

### The 2005 Paper and the State of Stock Trend Following [00:00:00]

Wilcox published 'Does Trend Following Work on Stocks?' in 2005 after observing that no multi-asset CTA was applying trend methodology to individual securities. The paper's three conclusions were: (1) comparable risk-adjusted returns to passive equity investing with substantially less drawdown; (2) very low correlation to multi-asset trend funds; (3) a compelling behavioral improvement for investors. Twenty years later, Longboard remains effectively the only public fund doing this.

### Future Opportunities: ETF Share Classes and Sector Trend Funds [00:00:00]

The hosts and Wilcox discuss two emerging opportunities: (1) the potential SEC approval allowing mutual funds to offer ETF share classes (similar to Vanguard's long-standing exemption), which Wilcox says would lower costs and expand distribution without new operational overhead; (2) the concept of sector-specific trend following funds (e.g., trend-following semiconductors), which does not yet exist and could be a significant product opportunity.
