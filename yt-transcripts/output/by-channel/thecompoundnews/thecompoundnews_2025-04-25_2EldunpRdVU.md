# The Worst Is Still to Come | TCAF 189

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-04-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=2EldunpRdVU
**Duration:** 75:34

## Summary

In this 75-minute interview, Rebecca Patterson joins Josh Brown and Michael Batnick to assess the economic fallout from the US-China trade war underway in April 2025. Patterson argues that even a partial rollback of tariffs is too late to avoid a significant growth slowdown, and assigns a 75% probability to a shallow recession later in the year. The conversation covers the erosion of the US brand in global capital markets, the Fed's constrained policy options, and a constructive multi-year case for gold driven by central bank reserve diversification away from US Treasuries.

## Key Takeaways

- Patterson's base case is at least a shallow US recession in 2025, with a 75% probability, driven by the inflationary and demand-destroying effects of sustained tariffs on China.
- A potential 4-percentage-point reallocation away from US assets by foreign investors could represent roughly $880 billion in outflows, which Patterson describes as a prolonged capital drought rather than a sudden crisis.
- Central banks have been quietly diversifying reserves into gold for three years, and the 2022 freezing of Russian assets accelerated that shift; Patterson sees gold reaching $5,000 per ounce.
- The Fed is constrained: cutting prematurely risks unanchoring inflation expectations and spiking the 10-year yield, while holding rates risks deepening a trade-war-induced recession.
- Stocks historically bottom before earnings trough, but the hosts and Patterson agree that as of recording date the market has not yet priced in the full potential recession scenario.
- Small US businesses, which lack lobbyists for tariff carveouts, face disproportionate harm; US agriculture risks permanently ceding market share to Brazil in Chinese soy imports, as occurred after the first trade war.
- The US brand as a safe, reliable financial partner has been structurally damaged, and institutional allocation shifts will play out over months to years given slow-moving investment committee processes.

## Detailed Notes

### Trade War Backdrop and Market Volatility [00:05:54]

Patterson, fresh from the IMF/World Bank spring meetings in DC, describes a week of whiplash headlines: WSJ reports of potential China tariff cuts were quickly denied by Bessent, while Navarro-wing statements drove intraday selloffs. She observes that White House factions—Bessent moderates vs. Navarro-Lutnik hawks—are competing for messaging dominance, and whoever has the president's ear that day drives market direction.

### Recession Probability and Economic Fragility [00:12:06]

Patterson assigns a 75% probability to at least a shallow US recession, contingent on China tariffs remaining high for several months. She acknowledges the economy entered the trade war from a position of strength—solid labor market, low leverage, high household net worth—but warns that front-loaded consumer spending ahead of tariffs could create a Wile E. Coyote moment once that pull-forward demand exhausts. Chipotle's earnings miss on revenue and rising input costs are cited as a real-time example of tariff pass-through.

### Foreign Capital Flows and US Brand Erosion [00:44:58]

Patterson argues the US has enjoyed 20 years of capital inflows because of exceptional growth and brand credibility; foreign investors now hold roughly $22 trillion in US stocks and bonds. Even a modest 4-percentage-point reallocation represents an estimated $880 billion leaving US markets, and institutional processes mean this will unfold over months, not days, creating a persistent headwind. Ken Griffin's analogy of the US Treasury as a premium brand being devalued is highlighted as capturing the long-term risk.

### Federal Reserve Dilemma [00:31:03]

Patterson supports Powell holding rates steady: inflation remains above target, unemployment is low, and premature cuts risk unanchoring inflation expectations in a tariff-driven price environment, which would spike the 10-year yield. She expects the Fed to begin telegraphing cuts only after jobless claims visibly rise, likely in the summer. The market's implied path of roughly three cuts by end-2026 is viewed as reasonable given a potential shallow recession.

### Gold and Reserve Diversification [00:58:23]

Patterson explains that gold's multi-year rally began in earnest when the US froze Russian assets in 2022, prompting other central banks to accelerate reserve diversification into physical gold. She ran the JP Morgan gold trading desk in 2008-2009 and sees gold as performing best at tail events—recessions with falling yields, or unanchored inflation. Given dollar weakness and ongoing central bank buying, she believes gold can reach $5,000 per ounce, while noting that a rapid trade-war resolution could erase the fear premium and send gold down $500 or more. For retail investors, she recommends GLD (physically backed ETF) over physical gold bars for liquidity and no premium.

### Structural Damage to US Agriculture and Small Business [00:18:44]

Patterson notes that during Trump's first trade war, the US permanently lost its position as the dominant soy exporter to China—Brazil now holds that role. With less fiscal space to bail out farmers this time, the structural damage could be worse. Small businesses (representing roughly 85% of US businesses) lack the lobbying access large companies use to obtain tariff carveouts, leaving them disproportionately exposed.
