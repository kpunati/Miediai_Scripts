# What if It's Still Early? | TCAF 244

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-05-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=LgmzAXMBbu4
**Duration:** 67:22

## Summary

In episode 244 of The Compound and Friends, hosts Josh Brown and Michael Batnick are joined by Denise Chisholm, Director of Quantitative Market Strategy at Fidelity Investments, to assess whether the current AI-driven CapEx boom constitutes a bubble or a durable investment cycle. Chisholm presents quantitative evidence that aggregate corporate CapEx relative to free cash flow remains well below the levels seen at the 2000 tech peak, and that earnings growth — not multiple expansion — is driving market gains. The conversation covers semiconductor valuations, the broadening of earnings recovery to the S&P 493, inflation and Fed policy, and the implications of a potential mega-IPO wave from SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic.

## Key Takeaways

- Aggregate corporate CapEx relative to free cash flow is still below 1x even including hyperscalers, far below the 3.5-4x seen at the 2000 tech bubble peak.
- Semiconductor earnings growth has outpaced price appreciation over the last two years, making the sector's valuation less stretched than headline returns suggest.
- The S&P 493 (ex-Mag 7) is only about 4 months into an earnings recovery after roughly 3 years of earnings contraction, suggesting the bull market may be earlier in its cycle than commonly assumed.
- Persistent fear and multiple compression in equities — despite strong earnings — is historically a bullish setup, as the market climbs a wall of worry.
- Oil price spikes historically make the Fed less likely to hike, not more, because oil acts like a consumer tax rather than a driver of broad-based core inflation; core CPI ex-shelter was 2.3% last month.
- The anticipated IPO wave from SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic could require roughly $170 billion in equity absorption, comparable to the entire 1998-2000 IPO boom in nominal terms.
- Small and equal-weight earnings are finally inflecting positively after a recessionary earnings period, supported by improving unit labor costs and a manufacturing recovery.

## Detailed Notes

### AI CapEx: Bubble or Durable Cycle? [00:09:12]

Chisholm argues that CapEx cycles historically generate growth rather than create bubbles. At the 2000 peak, corporate America spent 3.5-4x its free cash flow on CapEx; today, even including hyperscalers, the aggregate technology sector remains under 1x free cash flow. All 11 GICS sectors are now accelerating CapEx relative to sales, but not at levels that historically signal a bubble.

### Semiconductor Valuations and the Bull Case [00:26:24]

The SOX index is 69% above its 200-day moving average, an extreme not seen in 25 years of data. However, Chisholm notes semiconductor price appreciation has actually lagged semiconductor earnings growth over the past two years — an unusual pattern. Historically, when any industry is up 70-100% over a year, it goes on to outperform 65% of the time over the next 12 months, particularly when starting valuations are in the bottom quartile.

### S&P 493 Earnings Broadening [00:39:47]

Equal-weighted earnings growth was contractionary for approximately 3 years, making 2022-2024 effectively a corporate earnings recession outside the Mag 7. The recovery in the S&P 493 is only about 4 months old, according to Chisholm. The Mag 7 is up 6.6% year-to-date while the ex-Mag 7 is up roughly 10%, marking an important leadership rotation.

### Inflation, Oil, and Fed Policy [00:45:44]

The equity market sweet spot for inflation is 3-4% — not sub-2%, as lower rates often reflect weaker growth. Core CPI ex-shelter is approximately 2.3%, with shelter acting as a supply shock that the Fed should not double down on by hiking. Historically, oil price spikes of 10-60% actually reduce the probability of a Fed hike, with pass-through to core inflation happening less than 50% of the time since the 1980s. Strong durable goods orders (top quartile) raise the odds of a hike to roughly 65%, but small hikes in growth environments are historically correlated with above-baseline equity returns.

### IPO Wave: SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic [00:55:32]

The three anticipated mega-IPOs could require roughly $170 billion in new equity issuance, comparable in nominal terms to the entire 1998-2000 IPO boom. Chisholm acknowledges short-term absorption and rebalancing challenges but argues that over a one-year horizon, earnings growth will be the ultimate determinant of market performance. Anthropic's Series H round at $965 billion post-money valuation and $65 billion raise was announced just before the episode recorded.
