# 88% of Americans Want Congress to Stop Trading Stocks, Will They Listen?

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-10-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Hq_rnkW5eLA
**Duration:** 20:12

## Summary

Josh Brown sits down with Representative Greg Landsman (D-OH) to discuss the TRUST Act, which would require Congress members and their spouses to divest individual stocks or place holdings in qualified blind trusts. Landsman describes his own decision to sell all personal stocks and absorb a $150,000 capital gains tax as a demonstration of principle, while noting that few colleagues have followed. The interview covers the enforcement failures of the existing STOCK Act, strong bipartisan public support for reform, the role of corporate PAC money in entrenching the status quo, and the prospect of using a discharge petition to force a floor vote on the bill.

## Key Takeaways

- The TRUST Act would require Congress members and spouses to divest individual stocks or place them in qualified blind trusts to eliminate conflicts of interest.
- Public polling consistently shows ~86-88% bipartisan support across party lines for banning congressional stock trading.
- The existing STOCK Act (2012) only requires disclosure, carries a $200 penalty for non-compliance, and has never resulted in a prosecution — effectively toothless.
- Landsman voluntarily sold all stocks and paid a $150,000 capital gains tax before the law passed, while the proposed updated bill includes a capital gains carveout to incentivize compliance.
- A discharge petition requiring 218 signatures is being considered as a mechanism to force a floor vote if congressional leadership refuses to bring the bill forward.
- The conflict-of-interest problem extends beyond information asymmetry to include whether legislators are making policy decisions that benefit their personal portfolios.

## Detailed Notes

### Overview of the TRUST Act and Congressional Trading Problem [00:00:14]

Host Josh Brown introduces the TRUST Act (Transparent Representation Upholding Service and Trust), which would require Congress members and spouses to divest or place individual stock holdings in blind trusts. Landsman argues the core issue is fairness — the perception that legislators have an informational or legislative advantage that benefits their investments, even if unintentional.

### Weaknesses of the Existing STOCK Act [00:06:35]

The STOCK Act (2012) requires trades to be disclosed but imposes only a $200 fine for late reporting and has never resulted in a prosecution. Landsman and Brown note that some members publicly revel in their trading activity, treating transparency requirements as a feature rather than a deterrent, which underscores the law's ineffectiveness.

### Landsman's Personal Decision to Divest [00:08:48]

After his first financial disclosure listed stocks managed by a broker, Landsman decided to sell everything and reinvest in a broadly diversified mutual fund portfolio. This resulted in a $150,000 capital gains tax bill. The proposed TRUST Act includes a capital gains carveout to encourage others to do the same, a provision Landsman accepts despite having already paid out of pocket.

### Polling Data and Path to Legislation [00:12:49]

Polling shows ~86% of Americans across party lines support a ban on congressional stock trading, one of the most bipartisan issues in recent polling. Landsman identifies the path forward as either leadership support or a discharge petition (218 signatures to force a floor vote), noting he previously used a discharge petition successfully to pass the Social Security Fairness Act, which restored $20 billion in benefits to retired public employees.
