# Homebuyer's Remorse | Animal Spirits 458

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-04-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=knlU1QtKsGQ
**Duration:** 77:13

## Summary

Animal Spirits episode 458, hosted by Michael Batnick and Ben Carlson of Ritholtz Wealth Management, opens with an argument that the April 2026 stock market correction is orderly and not signaling recession, citing stable business data from Arthur J. Gallagher and Paychex. The hosts then examine surging US wealth concentration, unaffordable housing costs, and a long email from a homeowner cataloguing expensive repairs. The episode closes with segments on AI's potential to worsen inequality, structural risks in private credit interval funds facing redemption pressure, crypto market stagnation, and entertainment recommendations.

## Key Takeaways

- The sell-off through early April 2026 was characterized by no single daily decline exceeding 1.7%, leading Batnick to argue the burden of proof remains on recession bulls.
- Real-time business data from Paychex (700,000+ SMBs) and Arthur J. Gallagher showed no signs of economic slowdown as of mid-March 2026.
- US household wealth is increasingly concentrated at the very top, with 430,000 households holding $30M+ as of 2022, a figure likely substantially higher given subsequent market gains.
- Homeownership costs have surprised many buyers: one listener detailed over $40,000 in unplanned repair and renovation expenses within two years of purchasing a Long Island home.
- Private credit interval funds face a structural redemption risk because investors can redeem at NAV while publicly traded BDCs trade at steep discounts, creating arbitrage incentive to exit.
- VC funding in enterprise software is now extremely concentrated, with the top 20 deals representing 44% of all funding in 2025, dominated by AI companies.
- Carlson predicts AI will worsen wealth inequality by allowing businesses to generate more revenue with fewer employees, potentially setting the stage for a socialist political movement within 12 years.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Correction Assessment [00:03:26]

Batnick argues the correction is orderly: no day has declined more than 1.7% and the VIX has not spiked. He frames it as a courtroom analogy where the stock market must be proven guilty beyond reasonable doubt using inflation, interest rates, and earnings data. The S&P was down roughly 9% from highs, the NASDAQ 100 down 12%, Russell 2000 down 11%, while software stocks were down 34% and Bitcoin near a 50% drawdown.

### Earnings vs. Valuations Divergence [00:08:39]

Micron reported record DRAM revenue up 207% year-over-year yet its stock fell 30%, illustrating that strong earnings can coexist with multiple compression. Duality Research data showed every S&P sector has positive earnings growth in 2026, but technology stocks are down 12% despite 18% earnings growth, implying roughly 25% multiple compression. The hosts discuss terminal value concerns for AI-disrupted software companies like Adobe and Duolingo.

### Wealth Concentration and Inequality [00:33:58]

A WSJ report showed 430,000 US households worth $30M+ and 74,000 worth $100M+ as of 2022; the top 0.1% has seen 13-fold real wealth growth over 50 years. The hosts connect this to Delta Airlines reporting 8 of its 10 best sales days in history in Q1 2026, with bookings up 25% year-over-year, illustrating the bifurcated consumer. Carlson argues AI will accelerate concentration by letting founders retain more equity with fewer employees.

### Housing Affordability and Homeowner Costs [00:52:36]

Torsten Slok data shows the average monthly mortgage payment on a new 30-year mortgage was remarkably stable from 2000 to 2020, then spiked dramatically. A listener email detailed closing on a Long Island home in June 2024 and subsequently incurring a new water heater ($2,500), roof replacement ($12,000), central AC replacement ($14,000), and front porch repair ($10,000+) within two years. Hosts use the Bill Levitt quote from The Prize to connect home ownership to political stability.

### Private Credit Redemption Risk [00:59:36]

Blue Owl's CEO acknowledged the industry failed to adequately communicate illiquidity risks to advisors and clients. The hosts explain an arbitrage dynamic: investors can redeem private BDC interval funds at NAV while publicly traded BDCs trade at a 25% discount, incentivizing redemptions. Carlson notes the risk compounds if underlying assets are worth less than NAV but cannot be marked down due to leverage constraints. They criticize new US Department of Labor proposals to allow alternative assets in 401(k) plans.

### AI Labor Replacement and VC Concentration [00:46:27]

Torsten Slok data showed no meaningful unemployment spike in the Philippines or India despite AI expansion, leading the hosts to debate when AI labor displacement would become visible in data. A Redpoint 2026 market update showed the top 20 VC deals accounted for 44% of enterprise software funding in 2025, up from 7-8% in 2022, with OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI absorbing the bulk.

### Crypto and Active Management [00:49:57]

Total crypto market cap is roughly flat over five years despite significant protocol inflation, with hosts concluding that only Bitcoin and stablecoins have meaningfully emerged. Separately, 2025 was described as a historically bad year for active stock pickers with 79% underperforming the index; the SPIVA long-term data shows 90-95% of active managers underperform across market cap categories over 10-20 year horizons.
