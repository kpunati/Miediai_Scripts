# Dan Ives: Still the First Inning for AI | TCAF 176

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2025-01-31
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=CQCA0iLGOxY
**Duration:** 76:57

## Summary

In this 77-minute interview on The Compound and Friends (TCAF 176), Wedbush analyst Dan Ives argues that DeepSeek's emergence is net bullish for AI infrastructure, not a threat to capex spending, framing it as a competitive 'sputnik moment' that accelerates the AI arms race. Ives maintains that $2 trillion in locked-in AI capex over three years represents only the first inning of a multi-decade tech buildout, with software, energy, and robotics as the biggest second-order beneficiaries. The conversation covers Magnificent 7 earnings expectations, Tesla's autonomous and Optimus robot thesis, and specific stock ideas including Palantir, Uber, Serve Robotics, and MongoDB.

## Key Takeaways

- DeepSeek is characterized as the 'Temu of AI' — a cheaper Chinese model that will not reduce GPU demand but signals a bipolar AI race that ultimately accelerates spending.
- Enterprise AI capex commitments from major tech companies are described as already 'cemented' through mid-2026 and unlikely to be reversed by any single competitive development.
- Apple is reframed as the 'toll booth' of consumer AI because all leading models — including DeepSeek — must go through the App Store, generating an estimated $10-15 billion in incremental services revenue.
- Tesla's near-term auto earnings are described as poor, but the bull case rests entirely on Cybercab autonomous ride-hailing and Optimus humanoid robots, which Ives values at over $1 trillion for autonomous alone.
- Nvidia's valuation is defended on the basis that earnings have grown so rapidly that its P/E has actually compressed from 33x in 2020 to 28x today despite a 2,000% price gain.
- The best bear case for the AI trade, per Ives, is not valuation but the absence of killer consumer apps — enterprises are spending, but mass consumer AI use cases have yet to materialize.
- Uber is viewed as an eventual Tesla autonomous partner rather than a competitor, with the stock seen as a play on a future 'super app' combining rides, flights, and hotels.

## Detailed Notes

### DeepSeek Context and Initial Reaction [00:00:00]

Ives describes a 'White Knuckle weekend' fielding 50-60 calls from institutional investors worried about Nvidia GPU demand after DeepSeek's R1 model went viral. He frames DeepSeek as a geopolitical signal from Beijing timed with TikTok's brief outage, not a fundamental challenge to the AI buildout. He compares it to Sputnik — alarming in the moment but ultimately bullish because it forces greater investment.

### Why DeepSeek is Bullish for AI Infrastructure [00:07:28]

Ives argues that cheaper models lower the cost of AI adoption, bringing more enterprises into the ecosystem rather than reducing GPU needs — an application of Jevons Paradox. He states demand-to-supply for Nvidia chips is 15-to-1 and that only 4% of U.S. enterprises have gone down the AI path so far, implying enormous runway. Tech company capex plans involving Meta's $65 billion and Microsoft's $80 billion are described as already locked in.

### Magnificent 7 Earnings and Valuations [00:28:29]

Ives reviews a chart showing Mag 7 forward P/E vs. expected earnings growth; highlights Nvidia at 28x with 51% growth as 'appropriately priced,' Tesla at 116x on 34% growth as the outlier, and the group aggregate at 23% growth on 41x forward P/E. He argues the street is underestimating numbers and that multiples look overstated because earnings estimates will rise, similar to Nvidia's trajectory where P/E compressed even as the stock gained 2,000%.

### Apple as Consumer AI Toll Booth [00:19:04]

Ives makes the case that Apple benefits regardless of which LLM wins because all apps must go through its App Store, estimating $10-15 billion in incremental annual services revenue. He acknowledges Apple's 'first round loss' on AI hardware investment but draws historical parallels to AirPods and services revenue that exceeded analyst expectations. The upcoming generative AI app store, with 800-1,000 apps in development on Apple Intelligence, is cited as the medium-term catalyst.

### Tesla Autonomous and Optimus Thesis [00:51:56]

Ives recounts Musk's earnings call statements projecting Tesla could be worth more than the next five companies combined, driven by autonomous vehicles and humanoid robots. Despite weak Q4 deliveries and earnings, Ives values Tesla's autonomous business alone at over $1 trillion and expects Cybercab deployment in Austin to begin in 2025. He also discusses Optimus robots, predicting 2026-2027 for meaningful consumer availability, and suggests GM/Ford OEMs will eventually license Tesla's FSD technology.

### Lightning Round: Other AI Names [01:03:32]

Ives highlights Palantir, Salesforce, ServiceNow, Oracle, MongoDB, and Snowflake as software beneficiaries. Serve Robotics is discussed as a pure-play autonomous delivery company with an Uber partnership deploying 2,000 units; despite minimal revenue, Ives views it as compelling on a technology basis. Uber itself is seen as a long-term Tesla autonomous partner and potential super-app candidate, particularly if it acquires Expedia.
