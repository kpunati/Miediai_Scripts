# The Baddest Hedge Fund in the World | TCAF 229

**Channel:** The Compound
**Published:** 2026-02-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=i9lKz8fnmq4
**Duration:** 66:33

## Summary

Episode 229 of The Compound and Friends features Nathaniel Horwitz and Sam Koppelman of Hunterbrook, a media-plus-hedge-fund hybrid that funds investigative journalism through disclosed trading positions. The guests explain their model of monetizing accurate information rather than eyeballs, describe their editorial and investment process, and walk through major case studies including a bullish reversal on Evolve Technologies and a short thesis on Sable Offshore. The conversation also covers the current AI-driven market volatility, where dozens of stocks are being sold indiscriminately at near all-time highs, and how that environment creates opportunities for information-driven investors.

## Key Takeaways

- Hunterbrook's model funds journalism by taking disclosed long or short positions based on its own reporting, distinguishing itself from both traditional hedge funds and legacy media.
- The firm raised $10 million in seed capital and subsequently launched a $100 million fund, reaching profitability in its second year.
- Evolve Technologies was a contrarian long: despite SEC/FTC scrutiny and suspended financials, Hunterbrook's ground-level reporting found strong customer loyalty and quality technology, and the stock subsequently rose from ~$2 to ~$8.50.
- Sable Offshore became a major investigative target when Phil Mickelson's group chat messages and a leaked CEO audio call suggested undisclosed capital needs and potential securities violations; the SEC and SDNY subsequently issued subpoenas.
- Sphere Entertainment was an early long thesis based on proprietary sales data showing the Wizard of Oz IP experiment worked, though Bloomberg ultimately published the key story first.
- Hosts discuss a market pattern where hundreds of S&P 500 stocks are falling 7%+ in single days at near all-time highs, a pattern historically associated with the dot-com era.
- Hunterbrook argues that AI commoditizes intelligence processing but increases the value of original, ground-level information gathering — their core competitive advantage.

## Detailed Notes

### Market Context: AI Disruption and Sector Selloffs [00:08:48]

The hosts and guests discuss how AI uncertainty is causing indiscriminate selling in sectors like commercial real estate (CBRE, JLL, Cushman & Wakefield) and software. Michael Batnick notes that at near all-time highs, over 115 S&P 500 stocks are falling 7%+ in single days — a pattern last seen before the dot-com bust. The equal-weight index reached an all-time high in the same period, underscoring the bifurcation between 'disruptible' and 'non-disruptible' businesses.

### Hunterbrook's Business Model and Origins [00:17:58]

Hunterbrook operates as a media company with three revenue streams: advertising, a disclosed investment fund (Hunterbrook Capital), and a legal business that converts investigative stories into lawsuits. Horwitz and Koppelman met at the Harvard Crimson and were motivated by the observation that the investment industry free-rides on journalism without compensating it. The firm raised $10 million in seed funding from Emerson Collective, RA Capital (Peter Kolchinsky), and General Catalyst (David Fialkow), then launched a $100 million fund and became profitable in year two.

### Case Study: Evolve Technologies (Bullish Reversal) [00:44:14]

Evolve makes walk-through security scanners deployed in schools, stadiums, and transit. Hunterbrook initially suspected fraud after the CFO was fired, financials were suspended, and the stock fell ~45% on NASDAQ delisting risk. Ground-level reporting revealed customers — including school superintendents — were deeply loyal to the technology. Hunterbrook published a bullish story disclosing a long position; Evolve restated financials with a minor $4 million accounting error, settled regulatory actions, and the stock rose from roughly $2 to $8.50.

### Case Study: Sable Offshore and Phil Mickelson [00:52:31]

Sable Offshore holds rights to restart an oil pipeline near Santa Barbara that caused one of the largest oil spills in US history; CEO Jim Flores obtained a large loan from ExxonMobil to fund the restart. Hunterbrook reported repeatedly on missed regulatory timelines, then obtained Phil Mickelson's group chat messages discussing an imminent company announcement and a leaked CEO audio call admitting an undisclosed capital need. The stock fell from $30 to roughly $10, and Sable subsequently disclosed SEC and SDNY subpoenas tied to Hunterbrook's reporting.

### Sphere Entertainment and Sourcing Intelligence [01:01:07]

Hunterbrook built a long position in Sphere Entertainment after a newsroom source developed a method to estimate real-time ticket sales from public online data, showing the Wizard of Oz IP residency was highly profitable on non-concert nights. Horwitz admits personal bias against James Dolan delayed prioritizing the story, and Bloomberg ultimately published first. The episode closes with an open call for story tips at ideas@hntbr.com and the newsletter at newsletter.hntbr.com.
