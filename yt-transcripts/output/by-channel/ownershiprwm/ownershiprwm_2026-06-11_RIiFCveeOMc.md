# Long-Term Care Costs $115K a Year - Is Insurance the Answer?

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-06-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=RIiFCveeOMc
**Duration:** 40:57

## Summary

This episode of Ownership by Ritholtz features host Nick Sapienza in conversation with senior adviser Jonathan Novy about long-term care planning for high-net-worth retirees. The discussion uses a case study of a couple in their early 60s with $2.5 million to illustrate why long-term care is a high-probability, high-impact event rather than a low-probability one like early death. Novy outlines the three main insurance structures available (traditional standalone, asset-based hybrid, and permanent life with LTC rider), explains self-funding approaches, and stresses that the financial plan must come before any product recommendation. The conversation also covers the emotional and family-coordination dimensions of care planning that are often overlooked.

## Key Takeaways

- Long-term care is a high-probability, high-impact event; over 60% of couples will live past 90 and most will need some form of custodial care.
- The biggest financial risk is when one spouse needs expensive care on what was a two-person budget, potentially consuming the entire household income.
- Whether to insure depends on withdrawal rate and spending patterns, not simply on net worth; a $2.5M household could be better or worse positioned than a wealthier one.
- Three insurance structures exist: traditional (use-it-or-lose-it, cheapest per benefit), hybrid asset-based contracts, and permanent life insurance with an LTC rider (most expensive but guaranteed payout).
- Medicaid is a last resort with a 5-year asset look-back; it covers custodial care Medicare does not, but quality of care is often significantly lower.
- Home equity and reverse mortgages are legitimate funding sources that should be included in the overall care-cost plan.
- Starting the care conversation early—ideally prompted by a parent's care experience—is more valuable than waiting until a crisis forces reactive decisions.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Long-Term Care Is Different from Other Insurance Risks [00:02:29]

Novy distinguishes long-term care from life insurance: life insurance covers a low-probability event (premature death), while custodial care is a high-probability, high-impact event for most couples. Because the likelihood of needing care is so high, traditional insurance logic breaks down and a holistic financial-plan approach is essential before recommending any product.

### Cost of Care and Financial Impact on Household Budgets [00:04:44]

A private nursing-home room runs roughly $11,000 per month or over $120,000 per year; memory care is higher. Care costs are growing at roughly 3–5% per year, meaning a 10–20 year projection could double today's figures. The primary budget risk is when one spouse needs full-time care and the fixed budget meant for two people is entirely consumed by that care.

### Three Types of Long-Term Care Insurance [00:20:00]

Traditional standalone policies are the cheapest per benefit dollar but are use-it-or-lose-it and carriers have historically mispriced them, leading to steep premium increases over time. Asset-based hybrid contracts are funded in shorter windows and carry a nominal death benefit with an inflation-growing LTC benefit pool. Permanent life insurance (universal life, whole life) with an LTC rider is the most expensive because the insurer will pay regardless, but it eliminates sunk-cost anxiety. Existing whole life cash value can be 1035-exchanged into a hybrid or LTC-enabled contract without new premiums.

### Self-Funding, Home Equity, and Medicaid [00:15:43]

Self-insuring clients may compartmentalize assets into longevity annuities or HSAs, though Kitces research suggests bucketing versus rebalancing produces equivalent outcomes. Home equity and reverse mortgages are valid resources, especially when care is received at home. Medicaid covers custodial care but carries a 5-year look-back and is most relevant to households without significant investable assets; quality of Medicaid-funded facilities is typically much lower than private-pay options.

### Timing, Underwriting, and Practical Guidance [00:33:54]

Mid-to-late 50s or early 60s is the natural window to consider coverage, as disability insurance becomes less relevant and retirement approaches. Underwriting is morbidity-based and some conditions (e.g., diabetes) can still qualify. Shared-care contracts allow a combined benefit pool for both spouses. Novy emphasizes that the decision of how much to insure—50% or 100% of projected costs—should be driven by the household's withdrawal rate and spending patterns, not net worth alone.
