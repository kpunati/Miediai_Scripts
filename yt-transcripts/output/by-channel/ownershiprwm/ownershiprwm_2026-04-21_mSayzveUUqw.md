# Every Early Retiree Faces This Dilemma

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-04-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=mSayzveUUqw
**Duration:** 1:32

## Summary

Examines the dilemma early retirees face when they qualify for ACA health-insurance subsidies but also want to reduce future taxes through Roth conversions, since conversions raise MAGI and can disqualify the subsidy. Presents three approaches: preserve the subsidy and accept larger future RMDs, convert aggressively and forfeit the subsidy, or do smaller annual conversions that thread the needle. Frames the decision around the true cost of a conversion, including lost subsidy value, rather than the tax bill alone.

## Key Takeaways

- ACA subsidies are a short-term saving while taxes are a lifetime cost, so the two goals must be traded off against each other.
- Preserving the subsidy can save roughly $72,000 in premiums over five years but leaves a large IRA that grows into six-figure required minimum distributions.
- Aggressive Roth conversions raise MAGI above the subsidy threshold, so the true cost includes both the conversion tax and the lost subsidy (about $25,000 in the example).
- The real cost of a conversion should be evaluated including lost subsidy value, not the tax bill in isolation.
- Smaller annual conversions (around $30,000) can meaningfully reduce future taxes at a low rate while keeping subsidy eligibility intact.

## Detailed Notes

### The subsidy-vs-taxes trade-off and three paths [00:00:00]

Frames ACA subsidies as a short-term problem and taxes as a lifetime problem. Path one preserves the subsidy (about $72k in premium savings over five years) but leaves a roughly $1.6M IRA that could grow to about $3M by age 75 and force six-figure RMDs.

### Aggressive conversion vs threading the needle [00:00:58]

Converting $80k on top of $80k of MAGI pushes MAGI to $160k, eliminating the subsidy and raising premiums back to about $22k, making the real conversion cost roughly $25k. Alternatively, smaller conversions of about $30k per year reduce taxes at a low rate while keeping subsidy eligibility.
