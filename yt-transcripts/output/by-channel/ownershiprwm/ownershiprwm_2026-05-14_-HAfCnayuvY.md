# $3M in an IRA? Here’s What the IRS Takes When RMDs Hit

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-05-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=-HAfCnayuvY
**Duration:** 16:44

## Summary

This video walks through the full tax picture for a married couple retiring at 65 with $3M in traditional IRAs, tracing every dollar from RMD calculation through Social Security taxation, IRMAA surcharges, the new senior deduction phase-out, and NIIT once RMDs begin at age 75. The presenter shows that the real problem is not account depletion—cumulative RMD withdrawals can exceed twice the original principal while the account still holds $3M—but rather the escalating tax bill as income crosses multiple Medicare and tax thresholds. Two actionable tools are presented: systematic Roth conversions during the 10-year pre-RMD window, and qualified charitable distributions for charitably inclined retirees aged 70.5 or older. A technical appendix covers provisional income rules, the tax torpedo, NIIT, Roth five-year rules, IRMAA lookback, and estate implications.

## Key Takeaways

- RMDs on a $3M IRA at age 75 produce roughly $198,000 of ordinary income annually, which cascades through Social Security taxation, IRMAA tiers, and potential NIIT exposure.
- Delaying the first RMD into the following year to take two RMDs in one year dramatically amplifies the tax spike and is almost always the wrong move.
- The 10-year gap between age 65 retirement and age 75 RMD onset is the most valuable Roth conversion window because income is low and the taxpayer controls the marginal rate.
- Qualified charitable distributions satisfy RMDs tax-free and reduce AGI, potentially keeping the couple below IRMAA tiers and limiting NIIT exposure—making them more powerful than a standard charitable deduction for most retirees.
- The new $6,000-per-person senior deduction phases out almost entirely at this income level, reducing its practical benefit to roughly $6,300 of the original $12,000.
- Over a 20-year retirement, IRMAA surcharges alone can cost tens of thousands of dollars as RMDs push income higher each year.

## Detailed Notes

### RMD Mechanics and Projected Balance [00:00:58]

Under Secure Act 2.0, anyone born after 1960 has an RMD starting age of 75. At 5% annual growth, the couple's $3M IRA reaches approximately $4.8M by age 75, producing an RMD of about $198,000 (balance divided by the IRS life expectancy factor of 24.6). Delaying the first RMD to double up in one year is strongly discouraged.

### Social Security Taxation and the Tax Torpedo [00:03:09]

Provisional income—AGI plus tax-exempt interest plus half of Social Security—determines what portion of benefits is taxable. With a $200,000 RMD, the couple's provisional income reaches about $225,000, well above the 85% cap threshold of $44,000, resulting in $45,900 of taxable Social Security. During the pre-RMD phase-in zone, the effective marginal rate can reach 40–50% inside what appears to be a 22% bracket (the 'tax torpedo').

### Stacked Taxes: IRMAA, NIIT, and Bracket Creep [00:06:37]

The first IRMAA tier for joint filers triggers around $218,000 MAGI. The couple crosses it in year one and reaches higher tiers by age 80–85. The 3.8% net investment income tax applies above $250,000 MAGI—just above year-one income but crossed within a year or two. The senior deduction phase-out between $150,000 and $250,000 MAGI eliminates most of the $12,000 benefit at this income level.

### Roth Conversions: IV Drip vs. Horse Pill [00:07:35]

Converting roughly $90,000 per year for 10 years moves about $900,000 into a Roth at 22%, versus letting that money compound into larger RMDs taxed at 24% or higher. The math only works if conversion taxes are paid from outside the IRA; drawing taxes from the conversion itself breaks the strategy. Total upfront conversion taxes over the ladder are approximately $200,000.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions [00:10:22]

Taxpayers aged 70.5 or older can transfer funds directly from an IRA to charity—satisfying the RMD tax-free. The 2026 annual QCD limit is per person, so a couple can each use the full limit. Because QCDs reduce AGI rather than just providing an itemized deduction, they offer ancillary benefits: less taxable Social Security, potential IRMAA tier avoidance, reduced NIIT exposure, and preservation of the senior deduction.
