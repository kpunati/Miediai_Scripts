# The Rollover Mistake That Could Cost You $100,000

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2025-10-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ZkDVxafhFqE
**Duration:** 14:55

## Summary

This video provides a detailed walkthrough of the Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) tax strategy for retirees who hold appreciated company stock in their 401k. Nick Sapienza of Ritholtz Wealth Management explains that NUA allows company stock to be transferred to a taxable account where only the cost basis is taxed as ordinary income, while all gains are taxed at the lower long-term capital gains rate upon eventual sale. The video uses numerical examples to show scenarios where NUA saves significant taxes versus where it backfires due to high basis or company risk. IRS qualification rules, methods to reduce the upfront tax bill, hidden costs including IRMAA surcharges, and a concise decision checklist are all covered.

## Key Takeaways

- NUA allows retirees to pay income tax only on their original cost basis of company stock, with all growth taxed at long-term capital gains rates—often 15%—rather than ordinary income rates.
- NUA works best when the cost basis is small relative to total gains; the strategy likely does not make sense if cost basis exceeds 3.5 times the gain.
- Concentration risk is a major hazard of NUA: if the company stock declines sharply after the strategy is executed, the upfront tax cost becomes a permanent loss with no offsetting gain.
- The entire 401k must be distributed in a single tax year for NUA to qualify, which can trigger IRMAA Medicare surcharges, phase-outs, and bracket jumps.
- Strategies to manage the upfront tax cost include cherry-picking the most appreciated lots, retiring in a low-income year, using tax loss harvesting, and applying after-tax 401k balances to offset cost basis.
- NUA gains are not subject to the 3.8% net investment income tax, providing an additional hidden benefit over IRA withdrawals.

## Detailed Notes

### What NUA Is and How It Works [00:01:06]

NUA is an IRS rule that gives company stock in a 401k special tax treatment upon distribution. Instead of rolling everything into an IRA where all withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income, company stock is transferred to a taxable brokerage account. The cost basis is taxed as ordinary income immediately, while all appreciation above that basis is taxed at long-term capital gains rates—typically 15%—when eventually sold.

### When NUA Wins vs. When It Backfires [00:02:29]

A $1M Nvidia stock example with $200K cost basis shows NUA saving approximately $120K in taxes versus a standard IRA rollover. By contrast, a $500K stock position with $400K cost basis produces minimal savings and high upfront tax, making the standard rollover preferable. Additionally, catastrophic company stock declines—exemplified by GE's ~80% drawdown—mean the upfront tax cost becomes a pure loss. JP Morgan data indicates 40% of Russell 3000 companies since 1980 have suffered catastrophic declines from which they never recover.

### Strategies to Reduce the Upfront Tax Hit [00:07:53]

Five tax management techniques are presented: (1) timing the distribution to a low-income year; (2) cherry-picking only the most appreciated lots for NUA while rolling the rest; (3) offsetting with tax loss harvesting or custom indexing; (4) charitable giving via donor advised funds or charitable trusts; and (5) applying an after-tax or tax-paid 401k balance to the cost basis, which can eliminate the ordinary income tax on the cost basis entirely.

### IRS Rules, Hidden Costs, and Inheritance Quirks [00:09:26]

NUA requires a triggering event: job departure, age 59.5, death, or disability. The entire 401k must be distributed in one tax year as a lump sum. Post-distribution gains on the transferred stock restart a new capital gains clock. Heirs do not receive a step-up in basis on the NUA gain, unlike regular inherited stock. Hidden costs include IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges from the one-year income spike, phase-outs on QBI and other deductions, and sequence risk if the stock is volatile.

### NUA Decision Checklist [00:13:02]

NUA generally makes sense when four conditions are met: low cost basis relative to total value, a large gain, a manageable tax bracket in the distribution year, and a desire for flexibility in how retirement taxes are paid. NUA typically does not make sense with a small stock position, a tax bracket equal to or lower than prevailing capital gains rates, or a cost basis more than 3.5 times the gain.
