# How to Invest $2 Million

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-01-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3BeEAyK5Tbc
**Duration:** 13:39

## Summary

Nick Sapienza of Ritholtz Wealth Management presents a detailed framework for investing a $2 million retirement portfolio using a hypothetical couple, Jim and Jane. The process follows a purpose-plan-portfolio sequence: establish goals and spending needs first, then structure the portfolio to serve those goals. The framework uses three layers — a cash buffer for the current year, a bond ladder covering five years of spending, and an equity portfolio for long-term growth — with rules-based stock investing blending index funds, active management in less efficient markets, and trend-following tactical overlays to manage behavioral risk.

## Key Takeaways

- The retirement portfolio must be restructured from the accumulation phase; the same portfolio that built wealth may not be appropriate for drawing it down.
- A three-bucket approach — cash for year one, bond ladder for years two through five, equities for everything beyond — addresses sequence of return risk without sacrificing growth.
- Rules-based investing (fixed rebalancing bands, concentration limits, no emotional trading) protects against the behavioral errors that cause most retirement portfolio failures.
- Diversifying across US stocks, international stocks, and different cap sizes prevents being trapped on the wrong side of cyclical leadership shifts.
- Trend-following tactical strategies act as a pressure release valve during deep drawdowns, helping investors stay the course even if they occasionally generate false signals (whiplash).

## Detailed Notes

### Portfolio Transition at Retirement [00:00:04]

Sapienza introduces Jim and Jane, a couple with $2 million ($1.5 million in IRAs, $500,000 in taxable accounts) retiring at 62 with $100,000 in annual spending needs. He argues that the portfolio which built their wealth needs to be restructured around the primary goal of generating reliable income while preserving purchasing power.

### Three Ways a Portfolio Can Fail [00:03:11]

Sapienza applies Charlie Munger's inversion principle by first identifying failure modes: concentrating too much risk, locking money in illiquid alternatives, chasing performance and panic selling, and simply overspending. The portfolio is then structured specifically to avoid these failure modes.

### The Bucket System and Bond Ladder [00:04:13]

Bucket one is cash held in money market funds and short-term treasuries covering one year of spending. Bucket two is a bond ladder covering years two through five, with each year's bonds maturing sequentially. Bucket three is equities, which replenishes the bond ladder over time. Five years of spending at $100,000 inflated annually totals roughly $583,000, leaving about 70% in equities.

### Rules-Based Stock Investing [00:07:46]

Sapienza establishes explicit portfolio rules: maintain the stock-to-bond ratio without emotion, rebalance when drift exceeds 10%, no single company exceeds 10% of the portfolio, and always hold 6-12 months of cash. The approach blends index funds in efficient US large-cap markets, active management in less efficient international and small-cap markets, and tactical trend-following strategies as a behavioral buffer during severe downturns.

### Bringing It Together [00:12:11]

Sapienza summarizes that the best retirement plan is not the mathematically optimal one but the one the investor can actually stick with. The layered structure — cash, bond ladder, diversified equities with tactical overlay — ensures that spending needs are met without forced selling, that the portfolio grows to pace inflation, and that behavioral anchors prevent panic in volatile markets.
