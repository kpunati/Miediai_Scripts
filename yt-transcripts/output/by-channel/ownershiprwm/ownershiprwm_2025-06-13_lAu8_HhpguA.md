# They Can’t Afford to Retire - Even with Three Million Dollars

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2025-06-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lAu8_HhpguA
**Duration:** 14:05

## Summary

Nick Sapienza of Ritholtz Wealth Management walks through the case of Rich (defense contractor, ~$400K income) and Rachel (hospital administrator, ~$200K income), a 63-year-old couple with $3M split across traditional IRAs, Roth IRA, and taxable brokerage. Their $240K/year net spending goal required a 10% portfolio withdrawal rate, which was unsustainable over a 30-year horizon. The video demonstrates how a detailed spending audit, bucket strategy, staggered Social Security filing, and existing tax diversification resolved the shortfall without eliminating what they most valued.

## Key Takeaways

- A $3M portfolio at a 10% withdrawal rate is unsustainable; aligning spending with genuine values rather than habitual lifestyle can close the gap.
- A bucket strategy (2 years cash, 5 years bonds, remainder equities) mitigates sequence-of-returns risk and bridges the gap to Social Security.
- Staggered Social Security filing balances short-term income needs with maximizing the survivor benefit and long-term portfolio durability.
- Tax diversification across traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and taxable brokerage gives flexibility to manage tax brackets in retirement.
- The financial plan is a map; strategies like spending adjustments, rebalancing, and Social Security timing are the tools to navigate real-world terrain.
- Retirement planning success requires distinguishing between goals (what to protect), plans (how to generate income), and strategies (tactical levers).

## Detailed Notes

### The Problem: High Savings, Unsustainable Spending [00:00:04]

Rich and Rachel had $3M ($1.8M traditional IRA, $500K Roth, $700K taxable brokerage) and planned to claim Social Security at 67 for a combined ~$92K/year. Their $240K/year net spending target required ~$300K gross, a 10% withdrawal rate. At a more sustainable 5% rate, the portfolio supports only ~$150K. Their lifestyle had outpaced their savings, and cutting back felt like failure.

### Spending Audit and Values Clarification [00:03:13]

A structured spending audit separated fixed expenses (~$5K/month) from discretionary items (over $15K/month). Travel was the dominant discretionary cost with 5-6 trips per year, including spontaneous weekend getaways. By shifting from reactive to intentional spending, they eliminated ~$35K/year of lifestyle creep while preserving non-negotiables like annual family trips. New net spending target: $25K/month.

### Income Strategy: Buckets and Staggered Social Security [00:07:01]

Two years of expenses in cash/money market (~4%), five years in bonds, and the remainder in equities. This seven-year bridge aligns with the delay until both are on Social Security. Rachel files at 67 (~$46K/year), Rich delays to 70 (~$57K/year) accumulating delayed credits, maximizing the survivor benefit if Rich predeceases. Delaying both to 70 would risk withdrawal rates of 8-9%, which in a down market could be damaging.

### Tax Strategy and Behavioral Reframe [00:10:46]

Rich and Rachel were already tax diversified via mega backdoor Roth contributions alongside traditional 401k contributions and a taxable brokerage. The plan optimizes tax-efficient withdrawal sequencing. The key behavioral shift was from 'spending freely' to 'spending with intention', aligning expenditure with what they truly valued rather than habitual patterns.
