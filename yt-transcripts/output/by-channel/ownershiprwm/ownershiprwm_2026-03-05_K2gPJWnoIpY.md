# Can They REALLY Spend $150K a Year in Retirement? (The Math Says…)

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz

## Summary

This episode of Ownership by Ritholtz Wealth walks through a detailed retirement planning case study for a hypothetical couple, John (64) and Lisa (62), who hold $3.25M in assets and want to spend $150,000 per year in retirement. Advisor Nick Sapienza models their full financial plan, stress-tests it using Monte Carlo simulation and historical scenarios including the 2008 financial crisis, and explores how a $450K lake house purchase affects plan durability. The video concludes with a tax analysis showing that a disciplined Roth conversion strategy could save the couple hundreds of thousands in lifetime taxes and significantly increase their estate value.

## Key Takeaways

- A 95% Monte Carlo probability of success does not mean the plan is perfect — it signals the couple is overfunded and could actually spend more.
- Delaying Social Security to age 70 for both spouses substantially lowers the portfolio withdrawal rate once benefits begin, improving long-term plan durability.
- The couple can afford the $450K lake house in cash while still maintaining an 88% probability of success, provided they begin planning for it five years in advance.
- Roth conversions targeting the 22% bracket can save an estimated $355,000 in lifetime taxes and boost net legacy by $757,000 compared to doing nothing.
- Without proactive Roth conversions, RMDs beginning at age 75 would force John and Lisa into the 24% bracket and trigger IRMAA Medicare surcharges for the rest of their lives.
- Spending guardrails — triggered by portfolio balance thresholds — historically required only a 4% spending cut even in a 2007-2009 global financial crisis scenario.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Profile and Planning Timeline [00:00:43]

John (64) and Lisa (62) are six months from retirement with $3.3M in investment accounts (taxable: $850K, Roth IRA: $350K, pre-tax IRA/401k: ~$2M) and a $900K paid-off primary residence. John has a $12K/year pension with a 100% survivor benefit. Their plan targets $150K/year in inflation-adjusted spending plus $15K/year for travel, with Social Security deferred to age 70 for both spouses.

### Monte Carlo Stress Test and Probability of Success [00:09:20]

Using conservative assumptions (6.5% return, 2.5% inflation, 90-year life expectancy), the baseline plan achieves a 95% Monte Carlo probability of success without the lake house. Adding the $450K lake house purchase drops this to 88%, still indicating an overfunded plan with less than a 12% chance of needing significant lifestyle changes. The median outcome leaves approximately $8M to beneficiaries.

### How the Portfolio Weathers Market Crashes [00:20:27]

Backtesting against the 2007-2009 global financial crisis shows that spending guardrails would have required only a $200/month cut, and the couple would have recovered to hit upper guardrails and receive income increases by June 2016. A 38% portfolio decline would trigger only a 4% spending reduction thanks to five years of fixed-income cash buffer that prevents forced selling during downturns.

### Roth Conversion Strategy and Tax Impact [00:24:43]

The advisor models $75,000-$138,000 in annual Roth conversions capped at the 22% bracket. Without conversions, John's IRA could grow from $2M to $4M by age 75, pushing RMDs into the 24% bracket with compounding IRMAA surcharges. Executing conversions saves roughly $355,000 in lifetime taxes and increases net legacy by $757,000. The strategy also prevents the beneficiary problem of heirs inheriting a large pre-tax IRA subject to a 10-year depletion rule during their peak earning years.
