# The Retirement Tax Trap - How ‘Maxing Out’ Your 401(k) Backfires

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-03-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=362hWe2NzQ4
**Duration:** 15:58

## Summary

Nick Sapienza of Ritholtz Wealth Management argues that blindly maximizing pre-tax 401(k) contributions creates a tax trap in retirement by concentrating all wealth in taxable accounts, eliminating flexibility to manage brackets, and producing large forced Required Minimum Distributions. The solution is a three-bucket structure (pre-tax, Roth, taxable brokerage) built during accumulation using a suggested 50/20/30 allocation across contribution types. The video then details two additional phases: the Roth conversion window between retirement and RMD onset (ages roughly 62–73), during which the pre-tax IRA should be systematically reduced, and the ongoing annual withdrawal sequencing to avoid tax spikes and IRMAA surcharges in later retirement. Estate planning benefits for heirs under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule are also addressed.

## Key Takeaways

- Having all retirement assets in a tax-deferred account eliminates bracket control, forces high-tax RMDs, and may push Medicare premiums higher — a structure problem not solvable after retirement.
- A taxable brokerage account is the only account with no age or penalty restrictions, making it the most flexible bucket and essential for funding Roth conversion taxes without pulling more from the IRA.
- The 50/20/30 rule suggests allocating retirement savings roughly 50% to pre-tax 401(k), 20% to Roth, and 30% to a taxable brokerage account to achieve three-bucket diversification.
- The Roth conversion window — from retirement until RMDs begin around age 75 — is the cheapest opportunity to convert IRA dollars, especially when Social Security is delayed and no earned income exists.
- Converting IRA assets to Roth before large RMDs begin can reduce annual forced withdrawals from $170,000 to $75,000, saving hundreds of thousands in lifetime taxes.
- Roth assets inherited by children under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule generate significantly less tax than inherited traditional IRA assets withdrawn during peak earning years.

## Detailed Notes

### The Tax Trap: Why Maxing the 401(k) Alone Backfires [00:00:03]

Concentrating retirement assets in a single pre-tax bucket means every withdrawal creates ordinary income, the tax tail controls all planning decisions, and large RMDs beginning around age 75 can push income into high brackets and trigger IRMAA surcharges. Nick contrasts this with a structured couple who saved $350,000 in taxes by maintaining all three buckets. Nearly $27 trillion in tax-deferred accounts nationally illustrates the scale of the problem.

### Phase 1 — Build the Right Structure During Accumulation [00:02:53]

The 50/20/30 savings allocation places roughly half of contributions in a pre-tax 401(k), about $7,500 in a Roth IRA (annual limit), and the remainder in a taxable brokerage account. For a 40-year-old earning $150,000 saving $30,000 per year at 7%, this produces approximately $900,000 in pre-tax, $500,000 in Roth, and $600,000 in taxable after 25 years. The mega backdoor Roth — up to roughly $46,500 in after-tax 401(k) contributions converted to Roth — can dramatically accelerate the Roth bucket for those whose plans allow it.

### Phase 2 — Work the Conversion Window (Ages ~62–73) [00:08:44]

With no earned income, no Social Security (delayed), and no RMDs, the early retirement years offer the lowest tax rates available. In a case example, John retired at 64 with $2 million in an IRA. Without conversions, it grows to $4.2 million by 75 with a first RMD of $170,000. By converting $100,000–$150,000 per year and funding conversion taxes from the taxable account, the IRA is halved and the RMD drops to $75,000 — a reduction of nearly $100,000 per year in forced taxable income. Delaying Social Security simultaneously keeps MAGI low and extends the conversion window.

### Phase 3 — Annual Withdrawal Sequencing in Retirement [00:13:59]

Each year, project all income sources (Social Security, RMDs, pensions), identify the remaining room within the target tax bracket after the standard deduction, fill that room from the traditional IRA, and cover remaining expenses from Roth and taxable accounts. A 10–20k buffer within the bracket accommodates unexpected dividends or income. The taxable account will typically be depleted within the first 10–15 years of retirement; the Roth and pre-tax money persist longer. The strategy extends to estate planning: Roth assets inherited under the SECURE Act's 10-year rule are far less tax-burdensome than traditional IRA assets inherited during heirs' peak earning years.
