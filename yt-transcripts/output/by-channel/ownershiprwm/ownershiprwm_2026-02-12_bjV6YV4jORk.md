# Are Roth Conversions REALLY Worth It? (When They Pay Off & When They Backfire)

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2026-02-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=bjV6YV4jORk
**Duration:** 35:34

## Summary

Nick Sapienza and Ritholtz Wealth tax expert Bill Sweet provide a structured framework for evaluating Roth conversions, focusing on two primary objectives: reducing lifetime taxes through tax arbitrage and increasing future tax flexibility. They walk through the mechanics of the traditional-Roth-brokerage bucket system, the optimal tax bracket windows for conversion (especially the 12%-to-22% jump), and the compounding value of an extended time horizon. The discussion covers both scenarios where conversions clearly benefit clients and scenarios where they produce neutral or negative outcomes, including IRMAA traps, ACA subsidy cliffs, state tax implications, and the five-year rule.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth conversions are most valuable when current tax rates are materially lower than projected future rates, particularly before RMDs force higher-bracket distributions.
- Paying conversion taxes from non-qualified (taxable/cash) funds is essential; using IRA funds to pay the tax significantly reduces the long-term benefit.
- IRMAA Medicare surcharges kick in two years after the tax year of conversion and are a commonly overlooked cost that must be factored into the break-even analysis.
- The ideal Roth conversion candidate is approximately 60 years old, in a low-income year, with a long life expectancy, a married filing jointly status, and assets intended for multi-generational transfer.
- Tax-free states like Texas and Florida make doing conversions while still in a high-tax state like California counterproductive; timing a move and delaying conversions until after relocation saves additional tax.
- Pairing a Roth conversion in a high-income year with a large charitable contribution or donor advised fund can offset part of the tax cost.
- Roth conversions are a bad idea for clients already in the 35-37% bracket who expect lower income in retirement, or those with a short time horizon under roughly 17-20 years.

## Detailed Notes

### The Case for Roth Conversions: Tax and Time Arbitrage [00:00:03]

Roth conversions serve two goals: reducing lifetime taxes and gaining control over when taxes are paid. The Roth account allows tax-free growth and distributions, making a large Roth balance the most flexible asset in retirement. A conversion on a long enough timeline always pays off, but the definition of 'long enough' is crucial.

### Optimal Tax Bracket Windows and the RMD Factor [00:07:34]

The window between retirement and the RMD start age (currently 73, potentially 75) is the prime conversion period. The most actionable bracket jump is from 12% to 22%. Projections should model what marginal rates will look like in 2035-2040 when RMDs compound with Social Security to create a 'tax torpedo.' Tax brackets themselves inflate over time, adding another dimension to the analysis.

### Hidden Costs: IRMAA, State Taxes, Net Investment Income Tax [00:12:47]

IRMAA surcharges apply two years after the conversion year and are a frequent client surprise. State tax treatment varies widely: Illinois exempts conversions at retirement age while New York and California tax them as ordinary income. The net investment income tax adds roughly 4% above $200,000 single / $250,000 joint and is not inflation-indexed, making it increasingly painful over time.

### When Roth Conversions Backfire or Are Neutral [00:26:10]

Conversions are counterproductive for clients currently in the 35-37% bracket expecting lower future income, those planning to move to income-tax-free states, those with a time horizon under 17-20 years, or those whose IRA is destined for charitable bequest. The five-year rule is a relevant constraint for anyone under 59.5; above that age and with any Roth open for five years, distributions of converted amounts are penalty-free.

### The Ideal Roth Conversion Candidate and Decision Checklist [00:32:26]

The archetype is approximately age 60, recently retired or in a low-income year, married filing jointly, with a long life expectancy, no state income tax burden, cash outside of IRAs to pay the tax, and legacy goals for heirs. Frontloading conversions at ages 60-62 before the IRMAA two-year lookback window starts at 63 is a recommended tactical approach. The episode also highlights pairing conversions with QCDs (up to $108,000/year per taxpayer at age 70.5+) and donor advised fund contributions as complementary strategies.
