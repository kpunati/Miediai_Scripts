# The Netflix Employee Stock Plan is One of the Riskiest We've Ever Seen

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2025-03-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lc2L9_T4VJY
**Duration:** 19:38

## Summary

This episode of the Ownership Channel examines Netflix's distinctive employee stock compensation structure, in which employees use salary deferrals to purchase non-qualified stock options (NSOs) at 40% of the current market price rather than receiving traditional RSU grants. Joey Fishman of Ritholtz Wealth Management explains the mechanics — including 2.5x effective leverage, immediate vesting, 10-year option expiration, and monthly purchase windows — and identifies the key risks: the need for a 67% stock gain just to break even, high volatility (50% annualized), potential for positions to expire worthless, and heavy state tax burdens for California residents. The episode also covers executive compensation rules, NSO vs. ISO tax treatment, and alternative planning strategies for employees weighing the plan's costs and benefits.

## Key Takeaways

- Netflix employees contribute salary deferrals to buy NSOs at 40% of market price, resulting in 2.5x leverage that amplifies both gains and losses.
- NSOs vest immediately upon purchase, with no waiting period, but expire after 10 years if the stock has not appreciated sufficiently.
- The break-even threshold requires the stock to rally 67% above the purchase-date price before the employee sees any profit.
- Executive officers face a minimum stock ownership requirement of three times annual salary, with any shares used as collateral excluded from that calculation.
- NSOs are taxed at ordinary income rates on exercise (not on vest), plus capital gains on any subsequent appreciation, unlike ISOs which can qualify for long-term capital gains treatment.
- Due to Netflix's 50% annualized volatility versus the S&P 500's 18%, Netflix needs to return approximately 18.5% annually just to match a 7.5% S&P 500 return on a risk-adjusted basis.
- Employees in high-tax states like California may retain only $250,000-$300,000 from an NSO position that shows $894,000 in pre-tax value after taxes.

## Detailed Notes

### Netflix Stock Performance and Employee Metrics [00:00:10]

Netflix has grown from a $340 million IPO valuation to over $380 billion, producing a $1-to-$1,270 return for IPO investors. Its 10-year annualized return of 30% exceeds all other FAANG/Mag 7 peers. The company has about 14,000 employees and generates roughly $2.8 million in revenue per employee, second only to Apple.

### How the Netflix NSO Plan Works [00:04:25]

Rather than awarding RSUs, Netflix sets baseline salaries above market and offers employees the option to invest a portion of their salary to buy NSOs at 40% of the current stock price. This creates 2.5x leverage with immediate vesting and a 10-year expiration window. Employees must commit annually to contribution levels and cannot increase them if the stock drops.

### Risk Characteristics and Break-Even Analysis [00:06:32]

The 40% purchase discount means the stock must rise 67% to break even. With Netflix's 50% annualized volatility, positions purchased in high-price periods can expire worthless after 10 years. A chart of 60 months of $2,000/month NSO purchases shows recent lots are near zero while 2022 lots are worth significantly more due to the stock's recovery.

### Executive Rules and NSO Tax Treatment [00:10:43]

Netflix's two co-CEOs must hold a minimum $3 million in company stock and are subject to a three-year vesting schedule. Executive officers must hold at least three times their annual salary in Netflix equity, with shares pledged as collateral excluded from the calculation. Unlike ISOs, NSOs are taxed at ordinary income rates at exercise and again on capital gains at sale, creating two taxable events.

### Planning Considerations for Netflix Employees [00:17:00]

Fishman recommends weighing NSO purchases against alternative uses of salary such as paying down the mortgage, funding a 529, or contributing to the Mega Roth feature in Netflix's 401k, especially for employees in high-tax states. The high volatility and opportunity cost relative to the S&P 500 mean Netflix must return approximately 18.5% annually to justify the concentrated single-stock risk.
