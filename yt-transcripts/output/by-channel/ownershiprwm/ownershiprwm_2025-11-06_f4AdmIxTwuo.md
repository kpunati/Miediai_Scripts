# Why Rich People Invest in Real Estate

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2025-11-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=f4AdmIxTwuo
**Duration:** 47:50

## Summary

John Chaffetz, co-founder of Seattle-based Timberlane Partners, joins Josh Brown to discuss why real estate—particularly institutional-quality multifamily housing—is a cornerstone of wealthy families' portfolios. The conversation covers the mechanics of cap rates, the regional supply-demand cycle driving rent growth in the Pacific Northwest, Colorado, and Utah, and the structural advantages of locally focused private real estate funds over both mega-institutional platforms and amateur landlords. Chaffetz argues that the current market presents a rare buying window as institutional capital remains sidelined and supply pipelines have contracted.

## Key Takeaways

- Wealthy investors trade liquidity for meaningfully higher returns in private real estate, insulated from mark-to-market volatility.
- Real estate serves as an inflation hedge because rising construction costs force rents higher over time.
- Supply-constrained markets like Seattle, Denver, and Salt Lake City—where geography and regulation limit new construction—provide more durable rent growth than Sun Belt markets where building is easy.
- The development-to-delivery cycle of four or more years creates predictable windows where supply shortfalls allow aggressive rent growth before new units arrive.
- Cap rates function like yield on an asset's purchase price and move in relation to the 10-year Treasury, but anticipated rent growth can compress cap rates even when interest rates are high.
- Institutional-quality local expertise—tight underwriting combined with the ability to act quickly on a single market—outperforms both mega-funds constrained by scale and hobbyist investors lacking professional management.
- Timberlane places funds on Schwab, Fidelity, and Pershing custodial platforms to allow wealth management firms to allocate on behalf of clients without bespoke arrangements.

## Detailed Notes

### Timberlane's Investment Strategy and Target Investor [00:02:11]

Timberlane focuses on value-add multifamily acquisition and development in Washington, Colorado, and Utah, targeting 200-plus-unit institutional-scale properties. The firm serves ultra-high-net-worth individuals and the wealth management groups advising them, positioning itself as offering the rigor of a large institution with the nimbleness to move quickly on dislocations.

### Why Wealthy Families Prioritize Real Estate [00:20:38]

Chaffetz explains that illiquid real estate allows investors with sufficient capital to trade liquidity for meaningfully higher returns than public markets. Quarterly income distributions reduce the psychological pressure of mark-to-market swings, and rents have historically kept pace with or exceeded inflation, making the asset class a perceived inflation hedge.

### Regional Supply-Demand Dynamics and the Current Opportunity [00:13:44]

A post-COVID construction boom delivered excess units across many U.S. markets simultaneously. Seattle absorbed its oversupply first and is now experiencing falling vacancy and accelerating rent growth, while markets like Austin still face a glut. Chaffetz argues that the multi-year development pipeline means today's supply shortage cannot be quickly corrected, creating a window of strong rent growth for current buyers.

### Why Supply-Constrained Markets Outperform [00:28:42]

Geographic barriers—mountains, water, difficult winters—and regulatory entitlement processes in markets like Seattle, Denver, and Salt Lake City limit new supply responses, preventing the boom-bust cycles seen in easier-to-build markets like Phoenix or Houston. Chaffetz contrasts this with Texas, where rapid supply additions quickly cap rent appreciation.

### Cap Rate Mechanics and Interest Rate Sensitivity [00:40:23]

A cap rate is the ratio of a property's trailing net operating income to its purchase price, analogous to a dividend yield. Cap rates tend to move with the 10-year Treasury because real estate strategies use leverage and must offer a spread over the risk-free rate. However, anticipated rent growth can compress cap rates independent of interest rates, similar to a high-multiple growth stock.

### Fund Structure and Investor Access [00:44:28]

Timberlane raises new funds approximately every two years, with acquisition funds structured as 10-year vehicles generating liquidity around years six or seven. Development funds run five to six years. The firm has registered on the Schwab, Fidelity, and Pershing platforms to streamline access for wealth management firms managing ultra-high-net-worth clients.
