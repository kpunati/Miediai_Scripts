# How Should Retirees Invest With Markets At All-Tme Highs?

**Channel:** Ownership by Ritholtz 
**Published:** 2025-07-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Iw771kzuux4
**Duration:** 15:41

## Summary

Nick Sapienza at Ritholtz Wealth Management presents Steve and Maria — a couple in their early 60s with $2.6M largely in a single former-employer stock — as a case study for understanding sequence-of-returns risk when nearing retirement. Three portfolio options are compared: maintaining concentration in the single stock (highest volatility, highest depletion risk), shifting to S&P 500 only (which itself experienced a 13-year period of near-zero real returns from 2000-2013), and adopting a diversified portfolio with a cash buffer and dynamic withdrawal strategy. Back-tested data from team members shows a dynamic approach generates $1.1M in additional value over 20 years versus static proportional withdrawal. The video closes with a checklist for sequence-risk-resilient retirement portfolios and discusses the asymmetric upside risk of retiring into a bull market.

## Key Takeaways

- Concentrated single-stock positions in retirement amplify sequence risk because high volatility means the portfolio needs much higher returns just to sustain the same withdrawal rate.
- The S&P 500 is itself a concentrated bet on US large-cap growth and experienced 13 years of near-zero real returns from 2000-2013, making it insufficient as a standalone retirement portfolio.
- A diversified portfolio with cash, bonds, and a globally diversified stock allocation is built for durability, not maximum returns, accepting lower upside to avoid catastrophic drawdowns during withdrawals.
- A dynamic withdrawal strategy — drawing from bonds when equities are down year-to-date — added $1.1M in value over 20 years in back-testing versus static proportional withdrawal.
- Sequence risk cuts both ways: good early returns can result in far more wealth than expected, so smart plans use guardrails to ratchet spending up as well as down.
- The most important investment quality in retirement is temperament and the ability to stick with the plan, not maximizing returns.

## Detailed Notes

### Defining Sequence Risk and Secular Bear Markets [00:00:03]

Sequence risk is the risk that early-retirement withdrawals during a period of poor returns cause permanent portfolio damage even if long-term average returns are adequate. A secular bear market — defined as 10 or more years of below-inflation returns — has occurred roughly four times since 1900, with the most recent running from 2000 to 2013.

### Case Study: Steve and Maria's Concentrated Stock Position [00:00:56]

Steve and Maria have $2.6M, no debt, and want $130,000/year. A large portion is in a single former employer stock that has performed well. The fear of selling a winning position is common, but concentration multiplies sequence risk in retirement because withdrawals can no longer be offset by new contributions.

### Option 1: Staying Concentrated vs. Option 2: S&P 500 Only [00:03:11]

A single-stock portfolio requires significantly higher returns or a much larger cushion to safely support the same withdrawal rate as a diversified portfolio. Nvidia, cited as a fear-of-missing-out example, dropped over 60% in 2022 while taking over 18 months to recover. The S&P 500-only option, while more diversified, is still a concentrated bet on US large-cap; from 2000 to 2013 it returned near zero after inflation, representing a devastating scenario for anyone withdrawing 4-5% annually.

### Option 3: Diversified Portfolio with Dynamic Withdrawal [00:08:24]

The recommended structure allocates 5% to cash (one year of spending), 30% to high-quality bonds (2-5 years of spending), and 65% to a diversified global stock portfolio. A dynamic withdrawal rule switches from proportional drawing to bonds-only when the S&P 500 is down year-to-date. Back-testing this approach with $2.6M, withdrawing $130,000/year over 20 years, showed a 7.16% annualized return and $9.6M ending balance versus 6.5% and $8.5M with static withdrawal — a $1.1M difference with no additional risk.

### The Upside of Sequence Risk and Adaptive Spending [00:12:05]

Michael Kitces notes that following the 4% rule, retirees are equally likely to end with six times their starting balance as to run out. Smart plans therefore include guardrails to increase spending and ratchets to permanently raise withdrawals if wealth grows significantly, preventing the risk of underspending and unnecessarily leaving large sums behind.
