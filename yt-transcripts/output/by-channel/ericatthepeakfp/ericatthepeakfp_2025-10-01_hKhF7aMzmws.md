# Why Retirees With Under $5M Should Avoid Roth Conversions

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-10-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=hKhF7aMzmws
**Duration:** 13:20

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP at Peak Financial Planning, makes a detailed case that Roth conversions are commonly oversold by financial planning software and advisors because the projections assume lifespans that the majority of retirees will not achieve. Running three case studies ($1M, $3M, $5M) in Income Labs, he shows that reducing the planning horizon from 30 to 25 years eliminates most or all of the projected Roth conversion benefit. Social Security actuarial data show only a one-third chance a married couple has any survivor reach age 92—the break-even point in the modeled plans—meaning two-thirds of the time the bet fails. He identifies four hidden risks: probability uncertainty, heir tax bracket uncertainty, time value of money not captured in software, and upfront withdrawal rate risk from funding conversions in early retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- Financial planning software presents Roth conversion benefits as near-certain when they are actually longevity bets that require living to age 92 to break even in the scenarios modeled.
- Reducing a 30-year plan to 25 years turns a six- or seven-figure projected Roth conversion win into near zero across all three wealth tiers tested.
- Social Security life tables show a single 62-year-old man has only a 14% chance of reaching 92; for couples, there is a 33% chance at least one spouse does—a one-in-three bet.
- Four hidden risks in standard Roth conversion analysis are: longevity gamble, heir tax bracket uncertainty, time value of money not applied to the benefit calculation, and upfront sequence-of-returns exposure.
- For households under $2 million, Roth conversions make sense mainly when guaranteed retirement income will exceed pre-retirement income, creating a higher future tax bracket.
- Households with $2–4 million should only convert if they have after-tax funds to cover the conversion cost, have a pre-Social Security income gap, or face widow penalty or IRMAA/RMD risk.
- Households over $4 million more frequently have cases where conversions make sense, primarily for legacy planning rather than maximizing lifetime spending.

## Detailed Notes

### Three Case Studies: 30-Year vs 25-Year Horizon [00:01:03]

All three households—$1M, $3M, $5M—are modeled as 62-year-old fully retired couples with Social Security as the only guaranteed income starting at 67. At 30 years: $1M plan recommends 12% bracket conversions saving $92K; $3M plan recommends 24% bracket with $646K in tax savings; $5M plan recommends 32% bracket with $1.2M benefit. Shortening to 25 years: $1M drops to trivial conversions; $3M drops recommended spend to $76K; $5M drops to $231K with minimal projected benefit.

### Survival Probability Analysis [00:05:02]

The Software's 30-year horizon assumes survival to age 92 for a couple starting at 62. Social Security actuarial tables show only a 14% chance for a single man, 23% for a single woman, and 33% for a married couple that at least one spouse reaches 92. This means two-thirds of couples will fail to live long enough for the Roth conversion to pay off, making the standard software recommendation statistically unfavorable.

### Four Hidden Risks [00:07:30]

Risk one: Roth conversions are a longevity gamble with no guaranteed return, yet software displays them as certain benefits. Risk two: heirs may not be in a lower tax bracket than the converting retiree, making the estate planning rationale uncertain. Risk three: financial software does not apply a time value of money discount to the tax savings occurring late in retirement, overstating the real benefit. Risk four: funding conversions requires large early-retirement withdrawals, increasing withdrawal rate and sequence-of-returns exposure during the most vulnerable period of retirement.

### Decision Checklist by Wealth Tier [00:10:37]

Under $2M: convert only if guaranteed retirement income will exceed pre-retirement income. $2M–$4M: convert if after-tax funds cover the cost, a pre-Social Security income gap exists, widow penalty risk applies, or IRMAA or RMD pressure is anticipated. Over $4M: conversions more frequently make financial sense, primarily for legacy planning; Roth contributions for younger investors almost always make sense due to the long compounding horizon.
