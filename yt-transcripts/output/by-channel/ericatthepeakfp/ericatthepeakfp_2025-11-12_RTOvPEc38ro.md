# Once They Learn This, Retirees Stop Worrying About Taxes

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-11-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=RTOvPEc38ro

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, demonstrates how a 62-year-old single retiree with $2 million in assets can legally reduce federal income taxes from $40,000 per year to zero by applying four coordinated tax strategies. The video distinguishes between spending money and taxable income, showing that the right withdrawal sequence across IRA, brokerage, and Roth accounts can keep taxable income within fully sheltered brackets. A third strategy of annual tax gain harvesting progressively resets the brokerage account's cost basis, and a fourth strategy deploys the Roth IRA as invisible income to avoid IRMAA Medicare surcharges and ACA premium penalties. The compounding effect of saving $40,000 per year in taxes extends portfolio longevity meaningfully.

## Key Takeaways

- Taxable income and spending money are not the same: strategic sourcing of funds allows $168,000 in spending on only $64,000 of taxable income.
- The correct withdrawal sequence is: IRA up to the standard deduction limit, then brokerage gains up to the 0% capital gains threshold, then Roth for any remaining need.
- Annual tax gain harvesting within the 0% capital gains bracket resets brokerage cost basis over time, progressively eliminating a latent tax liability.
- Roth IRA withdrawals are completely invisible to the IRS, Medicare, and ACA systems, making them a strategic tool for avoiding IRMAA surcharges and subsidy cliffs.
- IRMAA surcharges are triggered by income from two years prior, so income management during ages 63-64 directly determines Medicare premium costs at 65.
- Staying below 400% of the federal poverty limit using the invisible income hack qualifies retirees for ACA subsidies during the pre-Medicare bridge period.
- These strategies are cumulative: each individual hack amplifies the others when applied as a system rather than in isolation.

## Detailed Notes

### Hack 1: The Income Illusion [00:01:03]

Not all money accessed in retirement counts as taxable income. When selling investments from a brokerage account, only the gains portion is taxable; the return of original principal is not. This distinction allows a retiree to access $168,000 in spending money while reporting only around $64,000 in taxable income by sourcing funds strategically across account types.

### Hack 2: Strategic Withdrawal Sequencing [00:03:04]

The optimal bucket-filling order is: pull from the IRA exactly equal to the standard deduction ($15,750 for single filers), then sell brokerage investments to realize exactly $48,350 in long-term capital gains taxed at 0%, then pull any remaining need from the Roth IRA tax-free and income-free. Every dollar pulled from the IRA beyond the standard deduction is taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 24% for this income level, making sequencing critical.

### Hack 3: Tax Gain Harvesting and Cost Basis Reset [00:06:33]

Rich retirees realize capital gains up to the 0% bracket every year regardless of spending needs, then reinvest the proceeds into a different (non-wash sale) investment to reset the cost basis higher. Over time this eliminates accumulated unrealized gains and reduces future tax liability. The strategy works whether the retiree needs the money for spending or simply wants to reset basis for tax efficiency.

### Hack 4: Roth as Invisible Income [00:09:41]

Roth IRA withdrawals do not appear on tax returns and are invisible to Medicare and ACA income calculations. This makes the Roth a release valve for accessing extra cash without triggering IRMAA surcharges (which can add $6,000-$8,000 in annual Medicare premiums) or losing ACA subsidies during the pre-Medicare bridge period from 62 to 65. Keeping income below 400% of the federal poverty limit preserves ACA subsidy eligibility.

### System Integration and Long-Term Impact [00:13:27]

When all four hacks are applied together, the retiree in the example achieves $168,000 in spending, $64,100 in taxable income, and a $0 federal tax bill, representing $40,000 annual savings versus the naive IRA-only withdrawal approach. Over five years this totals $200,000 in preserved wealth, and the compounding effect of keeping that money invested can meaningfully extend portfolio longevity into the 80s and 90s.
