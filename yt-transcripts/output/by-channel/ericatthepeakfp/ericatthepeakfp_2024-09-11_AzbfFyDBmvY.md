# How Much Should I Pay My Advisor? 1% vs Fee Only

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-09-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AzbfFyDBmvY
**Duration:** 13:49

## Summary

This video by Eric Amzalag, CFP of Peak Financial Planning, walks through the four main fee structures used by financial advisers: commission-based, hourly, flat-fee, and AUM percentage. The host argues that commission and hourly models create misaligned incentives between client and adviser, while flat-fee and optionally AUM models better serve clients. The video concludes with a case for the flat-fee-plus-optional-AUM hybrid model that Peak Financial Planning uses.

## Key Takeaways

- Commission-based advisers are incentivized to push specific products and should never also be responsible for building a financial plan.
- Hourly fee models create opposing financial incentives: clients want to minimize hours while advisers want to maximize them.
- Flat-fee arrangements incentivize adviser specialization and encourage clients to provide thorough context without fear of extra charges.
- AUM fees can be worthwhile when structured as a comprehensive concierge service including planning, tax, investment, and estate work.
- A flat-fee financial plan can serve as insurance for a 'financial passenger' spouse who might otherwise be left managing a complex inherited system.
- The right fee structure depends on the client's financial proficiency, time preference, and willingness to trust a professional.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Everyone Has a Financial Adviser [00:01:04]

Eric argues that everyone has a financial adviser whether paid or not—you are your own adviser if self-managing, or an internet commentator if consuming free content. Each scenario carries costs, whether explicit (fees) or implicit (mistakes, time, opportunity cost).

### Commission-Based Fees: Why They Fail [00:03:09]

Commission structures incentivize advisers to sell products rather than give holistic advice. Eric distinguishes this from buying a product you already know you need (e.g., life insurance or annuities), where commission is acceptable, but argues the product seller must never also be the plan builder.

### Hourly Fees: Opposing Incentives [00:04:12]

Hourly billing creates bad dynamics: clients withhold information to reduce hours while advisers may pad time. This mirrors problems common in CPA billing where opaque post-completion invoices damage trust even when the fee was fair.

### Flat-Fee Arrangements: Aligned Incentives [00:07:18]

Flat-fee models give clients an incentive to share full context (no extra cost for more time) and encourage advisers to specialize, improving advice quality. The key risk is ensuring the adviser's area of specialization matches what the client actually needs.

### AUM Fees and the Concierge Model [00:08:21]

A 1% AUM fee on $1M equals $10,000/year and is justified only if paired with comprehensive services including planning, investment management, tax planning, and estate planning. The scaling fee ideally discourages advisers from taking on too many clients.

### Peak Financial Planning's Hybrid Approach [00:10:25]

Eric's firm uses an annual flat fee for financial planning (ensuring unbiased advice and predictable cost) with an optional AUM relationship for clients who want to delegate portfolio management. This model also provides a 'financial passenger' spouse with continuity should the primary financial decision-maker pass away.
