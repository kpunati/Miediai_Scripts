# Peak FP Podcast: Retirement Planning Tools Will Ruin Your Retirement

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-07-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qikvPTsdASQ
**Duration:** 16:28

## Summary

Eric Amzalag critically examines probability-of-success retirement planning tools, arguing that while they serve a useful purpose as a baseline, their limitations are systematically underdisclosed. The core analogy compares these tools to a Google Maps app that shows a destination but provides no turn-by-turn directions, real-time traffic, or rerouting — the parts that make navigation actually useful. The episode outlines four supplemental tools Peak Financial Planning uses: Income Labs for dynamic spending guardrails, distribution rate tables to identify high-risk withdrawal years, real-time spending trackers, and advanced portfolio risk metrics.

## Key Takeaways

- Probability-of-success tools are valuable as a baseline but dangerous if used as a sole decision-making framework without supplementing tools.
- The core problem is that a single 0–100 score conceals when in retirement the risk is concentrated, what actions to take, and what to do if conditions change.
- Technology removes decision-making friction to the point where people believe they can self-diagnose complex financial decisions, analogous to misusing WebMD for medical diagnosis.
- Income Labs ties spending directly to real-time portfolio values, providing simple rules (spend this range; adjust when the portfolio crosses thresholds) that function as true contingency plans.
- Distribution rate tables show the annual withdrawal rate as a percentage of starting portfolio value, revealing which years carry the most depletion risk.
- Real-time spending tracking (e.g., EveryDollar) is described as measuring retirement's 'vital signs' because probability tools do not incorporate actual spending data.
- Retirees at 62+ with a 70%+ success score should ask themselves what specific additional metric or amount they are waiting for before asking whether better tools would let them retire sooner.

## Detailed Notes

### Three Problems with Probability-of-Success Tools [00:04:10]

Problem one: oversimplified summary scores mislead users who do not explore underlying assumptions about risk timing and spending trajectory. Problem two: heavy lifting by the tool atrophies the planner's and investor's ability to evaluate nuanced decisions — for example, choosing a Social Security claiming age is reduced to a one-line optimization output. Problem three: the tools encourage consumers to believe they can become their own financial advisors in the same way WebMD tempts patients to self-diagnose.

### The Google Maps Analogy [00:08:19]

Probability-of-success tools are analogous to a navigation app that shows a destination but omits turn-by-turn directions, real-time traffic updates, and rerouting when conditions change. These three missing elements — step-by-step action items, real-time monitoring, and contingency plans — are precisely what would allow most retirees to retire earlier and spend more with confidence.

### Four Supplemental Tools Recommended [00:11:26]

Income Labs provides dynamic spending rules tied to live portfolio values. A custom distribution rate table exports annual expected spending versus start-of-year portfolio balance to identify high-risk withdrawal years. Real-time spending trackers like EveryDollar capture actual expenditure that probability tools never incorporate. Advanced portfolio risk metrics — standard deviation, beta, market correlation, maximum drawdown — provide better retirement portfolio construction guidance than asset allocation alone.
