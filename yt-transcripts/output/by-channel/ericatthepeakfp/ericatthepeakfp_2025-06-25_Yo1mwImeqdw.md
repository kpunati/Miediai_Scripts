# Once I Show Retiree's This, They Stop Worrying About Spending...

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-06-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Yo1mwImeqdw
**Duration:** 13:40

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, addresses the paradox that the most financially prepared retirees are often the least willing to spend their savings. Drawing on EBRI research and client experience, he argues that the problem is not a lack of data but a lack of behavioral tools that give retirees a sense of control. His solution is the Retirement Confidence Dashboard, a simple three-sheet spreadsheet that tracks annual withdrawals against a spending capacity figure from guardrails software, monitors portfolio position relative to guardrails, and records a monthly self-rated spending confidence score. The tool fits into a broader four-step financial planning process covering feasibility, low-frequency decisions, emergency stress testing, and ongoing high-frequency monitoring.

## Key Takeaways

- Wealthier retirees paradoxically spend a smaller fraction of their savings, according to EBRI data, often leaving large estates unintentionally.
- The confidence gap is behavioral, not informational; more probability scores and data projections do not move retirees across the spending threshold.
- The Retirement Confidence Dashboard provides three actionable monthly tracking metrics: year-to-date withdrawals vs. capacity, portfolio position vs. guardrails, and a self-rated spending confidence score.
- Guardrails-based planning is preferable to static probability-of-success models because it provides explicit, quantified rules for when and how much to cut spending.
- Spending reductions in retirement are the one tool entirely within the retiree's control, making a system to monitor when reductions are needed essential.

## Detailed Notes

### The Spending Paralysis Problem [00:00:00]

Amzalag opens by noting that online forums are filled with well-prepared retirees who find it psychologically difficult to spend their savings. He references an EBRI 2018 study showing that those with more than $500,000 spent less than 12% of their assets in 20 years, while pensioners spent down just 4% on average. The behaviors that built wealth are the same ones that prevent enjoying it.

### Why More Data Doesn't Help [00:01:57]

Amzalag argues that probability-of-success financial plans resemble nutritionist meal calendars — technically correct but impossible to execute without a supporting system. JP Morgan's statistic that retirees are 85% more likely to die with more than they started with does not eliminate the fear of being in the unlucky minority. The real need is a system of controllable behaviors, not more forecasts.

### The Retirement Confidence Dashboard [00:05:43]

The dashboard has three simple sheets. Sheet one tracks yearly portfolio withdrawals, spending capacity from guardrails software, year-to-date withdrawals, and remaining monthly withdrawal budget. Sheet two monitors the portfolio value relative to upper and lower guardrails, so retirees always know in advance exactly when and by how much they would need to cut spending. Sheet three records a monthly 1-10 spending confidence self-rating to correlate emotions with actual portfolio metrics.

### The Four-Step Financial Planning Process [00:09:39]

Amzalag places the dashboard within a broader framework: step one is feasibility (can you retire on your desired date), step two is learning low-frequency one-time decisions like Social Security claiming and Roth conversions, step three is stress-testing for emergencies such as large surprise expenses or 30% portfolio corrections, and step four is adopting the dashboard as the ongoing high-frequency monitoring tool.
