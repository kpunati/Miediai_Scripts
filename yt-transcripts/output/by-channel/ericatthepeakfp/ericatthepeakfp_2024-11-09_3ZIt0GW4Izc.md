# How Much Can I Spend In Retirement EVERY DAY? (By Savings Bracket)

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-11-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3ZIt0GW4Izc
**Duration:** 16:17

## Summary

The video explains how to calculate daily and monthly retirement spending using the modern guardrails approach for four savings brackets: $250K, $500K, $1M, and $2M. Assumptions include Social Security at age 65 averaging $22,000 per year, a 60/40 portfolio, all pre-tax savings, and a 20% targeted risk of overspending. The presenter shares specific withdrawal amounts, upper and lower guardrail trigger points, and a practical spreadsheet system to track withdrawals throughout the year and adjust spending dynamically.

## Key Takeaways

- The modern guardrails approach sets initial withdrawal amounts and then dynamically adjusts spending based on portfolio value changes throughout retirement.
- Initial safe withdrawal rates using this method come out to approximately 5.1-5.2% across all four brackets when Social Security income is included.
- Lower savings brackets have tighter guardrails, meaning they are more sensitive to market downturns and more likely to require spending reductions.
- Higher savings brackets benefit from wider guardrails and are more likely to see spending increases over time.
- A spreadsheet tracking year-to-date withdrawals and remaining withdrawal space helps retirees manage daily and monthly spending without being overly restrictive.
- Guardrail triggers and spending adjustments can be calculated in advance so retirees are not surprised when they occur.
- The goal is to find a tracking sweet spot — not so frequent it causes anxiety, not so infrequent that problems are missed.

## Detailed Notes

### Overview and Why Daily Spending Clarity Matters [00:00:00]

Traditional annual calculations obscure how much retirees can actually spend day-to-day. The modern guardrails methodology addresses this by converting annual withdrawal tolerances into practical daily and monthly figures, anchored to the portfolio's current value.

### Modern Guardrails Inputs and Mechanics [00:01:02]

The system requires five inputs: retirement age, non-portfolio income sources and timelines, current portfolio value, investment allocation, and desired spending risk tolerance. Unlike Monte Carlo tools where you specify spending and get a probability, guardrails tells you what you can spend given a desired risk level — here set at 20% chance of overspending.

### Withdrawal Amounts by Savings Bracket [00:04:11]

Using a 60/40 portfolio, Social Security of $22K/year, and pre-tax savings only: $250K yields $14,100 initial withdrawal; $500K yields $27,300; $1M yields $54,600; $2M yields $111,000. All brackets produce approximately a 5.1-5.2% initial withdrawal rate. These amounts represent maximum tolerable spending, not required spending.

### Guardrail Trigger Points and Spending Adjustments [00:06:18]

Lower guardrails trigger spending reductions of 5% when portfolios decline: at -18% for $250K, -23% for $500K, -28% for $1M, and -31% for $2M. Upper guardrails trigger 5-10% spending increases with portfolio gains of 5-10% depending on bracket. Lower brackets have tighter rails because there is less cushion.

### Practical Spending Tracking System [00:08:23]

A spreadsheet tracks year-to-date withdrawals against the annual safe withdrawal amount, calculates remaining withdrawal space, and projects a pace in months. If calendar months remaining exceed the pace figure, spending may need to be reined in; if fewer months remain than the pace, surplus capacity can be used for gifts or Roth conversions.

### How Guardrails Are Mathematically Derived [00:14:40]

Spending capacity is the amount that, if consistently withdrawn, achieves the targeted risk of overspending or underspending. Lower guardrails are triggered when the risk of overspending reaches 75% due to portfolio decline; upper guardrails trigger when the risk of underspending reaches 100% due to portfolio growth.
