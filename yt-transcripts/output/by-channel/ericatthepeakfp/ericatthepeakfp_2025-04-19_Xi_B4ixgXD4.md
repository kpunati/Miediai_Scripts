# I Showed This Retiree Where It All Went Wrong...

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-04-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Xi_B4ixgXD4
**Duration:** 16:07

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents a cautionary case study of a couple who purchased a single life annuity in August 2022 at a non-fiduciary adviser's recommendation, unknowingly creating severe survivor-income risk for the younger wife. Using both a Monte Carlo tool (Right Capital) and a guardrails-based tool (Income Labs), he demonstrates quantitatively how the annuity's reliance on the husband's longevity made the plan far more fragile than the 66% success score suggested. He attributes the poor decision to information denial, misplaced trust, and lack of pre-crisis financial education, and proposes a time-trust-complexity decision framework to prevent similar mistakes.

## Key Takeaways

- A single life annuity that pays only during the primary annuitant's lifetime creates catastrophic survivor risk when the annuitant is older and statistically likely to die first.
- Monte Carlo scores can be misleading if they model only the most favorable life-expectancy scenario; sensitivity analysis across realistic mortality outcomes is essential.
- The survivor income ratio used by Income Labs more directly surfaces spousal financial risk than a single Monte Carlo probability score.
- Rushed decisions under market duress (information denial, misplaced trust in a non-fiduciary, lack of prior education) are the root cause of many costly retirement mistakes.
- The time-trust-complexity framework argues that complex financial decisions require more time for education, not less, and that trusting an expert to save time is the wrong substitute.
- Beginning rigorous financial planning at least five years before retirement allows the education investment to be made under low-stress conditions, so it is available when a crisis occurs.

## Detailed Notes

### The Annuity Decision and Its Consequences [00:01:03]

In August 2022 with the S&P 500 down roughly 20%, Bill (65) and Cara (58) purchased a single life annuity covering 50% of their retirement funds at their financial adviser's recommendation. The annuity pays only while Bill is alive, meaning Cara's financial security is fully dependent on Bill living to advanced age. Their previous plan showed 66% success at age 95 but collapsed to 37% if Bill dies at 85.

### Monte Carlo Analysis: Annuity vs. No Annuity [00:10:37]

Amzalag rebuilds the plan in Right Capital with and without the annuity. Without the annuity, success holds at 67% if Bill dies at 85, versus 37% in the annuity version. At Bill's death at 80, success is 64% without annuity versus 20% with it. The annuity benefits from a longer Bill lifespan and hurts disproportionately with shorter lifespans, creating an asymmetric risk profile that disadvantages the more probable outcome.

### Income Labs Survivor Income Ratio [00:13:27]

Income Labs scores the annuity scenario at 24%, worse than Monte Carlo's 37%, because it heavily weights the survivor income ratio. With the single life annuity, Cara's survivor ratio is 50%; without the annuity it rises to 80%. The modern guardrails plan without the annuity also shows a 30% lower guard rail, meaning the portfolio could absorb the entire actual portfolio decline experienced in 2022 without triggering any spending reduction.

### Three Root Causes and the Decision Framework [00:07:47]

Amzalag identifies information denial (not knowing their portfolio was only down 10% vs. the S&P's 20%), misplaced trust in a non-fiduciary adviser who collected a commission, and lack of education under duress as the three causes. His time-trust-complexity framework advises that the more complex and consequential a financial decision, the less one should defer to an expert and the more time one must invest in education beforehand, ideally well before a crisis occurs.
