# Do THIS Retiree Withdrawal Hack & Never Worry About Tax Again

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-11-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FLV2_-WQe-g
**Duration:** 16:50

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, walks through a detailed case study of Steve (age 55) and Nancy (age 54), early retirees with $2.4 million in pre-tax 401k accounts and $600,000 in a taxable brokerage account with a 95% cost basis. Conventional wisdom says to spend the taxable account first, but he demonstrates that doing the opposite—drawing $80,000 per year from Steve's 401k first via the rule of 55—saves $103,000 in the first decade and $352,000 over a lifetime by exploiting low tax brackets, maintaining ACA subsidies, and reducing future RMD exposure. The video also explains why Roth conversions are inferior in this specific scenario.

## Key Takeaways

- For retirees with a high-cost-basis taxable account and large pre-tax balances, drawing from the pre-tax account first can save hundreds of thousands in lifetime taxes.
- Maintaining modified adjusted gross income under the ACA subsidy threshold (about $84,600 for a couple) can reduce healthcare costs by up to $19,000 per year.
- Using the rule of 55 allows penalty-free 401k withdrawals before age 59.5, enabling this strategy for early retirees who just left their employer.
- Withdrawing $80,000 from a 401k at a 7% effective rate is far cheaper than the 22-24% rates that would apply during forced RMD years.
- Roth conversions are inferior in this scenario because funding both conversions and living expenses simultaneously either depletes the taxable account faster or loses ACA subsidies.
- This strategy simultaneously addresses healthcare costs, RMD reduction, and the widow's tax trap—threading three planning needles at once.

## Detailed Notes

### The Problem with Conventional Withdrawal Sequencing [00:00:49]

Conventional wisdom recommends spending taxable accounts first, then pre-tax, then Roth. For Steve and Nancy, following this path would deplete their $600,000 brokerage account in 6 years, leaving them with only pre-tax money for years 7-10 of retirement. This would cause healthcare costs to jump from zero to $24,000 per year and force them to withdraw $139,000 gross just to net $100,000 of spending—still leaving a massive pre-tax balance and all its future tax problems unresolved.

### The Pre-Tax First Strategy Mechanics [00:06:44]

Each year for 10 years, Steve withdraws $80,000 from his 401k using the rule of 55, filling the standard deduction ($31,500 tax-free), 10% bracket ($23,850), and 12% bracket ($24,650) for a $5,300 tax bill at a 7% effective rate. The $5,300 tax is paid directly from the brokerage account—not from the 401k—to avoid a taxable income doom loop. An additional $20,000 is pulled from the high-cost-basis brokerage to cover spending, totaling $105,300 withdrawn to fund a $100,000 lifestyle.

### Why Roth Conversions Are Inferior Here [00:11:33]

Performing $80,000 in Roth conversions while also needing $100,000 for living expenses requires either drawing $105,300 from the brokerage (depleting it in 5-6 years and losing ACA subsidies) or pulling all funds from the 401k, which raises MAGI, loses subsidies, and creates high front-end tax costs. The pre-tax withdrawal approach achieves similar pre-tax balance reduction more simply and cheaply.

### Candidate Profile for This Strategy [00:14:16]

Three conditions must be met: (1) a high-cost-basis after-tax account (roughly 70%+ basis with $300K-$400K+), (2) desire to qualify for ACA subsidies while under 65 and not yet receiving Social Security or a pension, and (3) a large pre-tax balance of potentially $1 million or more in a 401k or IRA that would otherwise create RMD problems.
