# 4 ROTH Retirement Traps That WILL RUIN Your Retirement

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-06-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=hyJq0mjn5fM
**Duration:** 15:16

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents four specific traps that make Roth conversions harmful rather than helpful in retirement. The analysis uses spreadsheet illustrations to show how conversion costs can raise withdrawal rates to dangerous levels, how paying taxes from pre-tax accounts compounds the conversion cost by roughly 15%, how break-even ages often extend into the early 90s for retirees starting conversions in their mid-60s, and how strategic withdrawal sequencing can preserve more wealth than forced conversions without the upfront sacrifice. The video argues that Roth conversions require rigorous, personalized analysis rather than blanket application of conventional wisdom.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth conversions bring forward future tax costs that can raise distribution rates to unsustainable levels if the retiree cannot absorb the additional withdrawal without financial stress.
- Paying Roth conversion taxes from pre-tax (tax-deferred) accounts forces a gross-up that increases the total conversion cost by the marginal tax rate percentage.
- Accumulating liquid after-tax or cash savings 5-10 years before retirement provides dry powder to fund conversion taxes more cheaply.
- A break-even age analysis often reveals that conversions begun in the mid-60s primarily benefit heirs rather than the retirees themselves, especially given average life expectancy.
- Time value of money further erodes the attractiveness of Roth conversions because early dollars spent are worth more than future tax savings.
- Strategic sequencing—drawing down pre-tax accounts while letting existing Roth accounts grow untouched—can produce higher ending portfolio values than executing conversions.

## Detailed Notes

### Trap 1: Converting When You Cannot Afford the Cost [00:00:00]

Roth conversions accelerate a future tax expense into present spending, reducing current flexibility. The spreadsheet illustration shows that adding roughly $90,000 in annual conversions raises a couple's distribution rate from approximately 5.8% to 6.6%, which may be unacceptably high for those already at elevated withdrawal levels. The recommendation is to model annual distribution rates before and after conversions to make an informed decision.

### Trap 2: Using Pre-Tax Money to Pay Conversion Taxes [00:04:09]

Paying conversion taxes from a tax-deferred account forces a gross-up: the taxpayer must withdraw more than the desired conversion amount to cover the tax, effectively paying tax on the tax. At a flat 15% rate, this adds approximately 15% to the cost of the conversion compared to using after-tax cash. The solution is to accumulate taxable savings in the 5-10 years before retirement to serve as dry powder for conversion taxes.

### Trap 3: Ignoring the Break-Even Age [00:09:20]

Roth conversions create an upfront cost that must be recouped in future tax savings; the age at which this occurs is the break-even age. For the hypothetical couple beginning conversions at ages 62-63 with total conversion costs of roughly $51,000-$52,000, the break-even is not until approximately ages 90-91. Applying a time-value-of-money discount makes the case even weaker. Retirees with longevity concerns should weigh giving up early spending flexibility for a benefit their heirs are more likely to receive.

### Trap 4: Converting When You Don't Need To [00:12:33]

Strategically sequencing withdrawals—spending down taxable and pre-tax accounts while allowing existing Roth accounts to compound untouched—can result in a higher ending Roth balance than performing conversions. The example shows that the no-conversion scenario produces roughly $3 million versus approximately $2.336 million after unnecessary conversions. The erosion of capital from upfront conversion costs outweighs the tax benefit when conversions are not truly needed.
