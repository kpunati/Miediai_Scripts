# This Chart Shows When Most Retirements Are At Risk

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-03-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=nIXnRGC9Xsw
**Duration:** 13:21

## Summary

Eric Amzalag frames the five-to-ten year window around retirement as the retirement risk zone—a period where the most consequential financial decisions are made—but argues it is better understood as the retirement opportunity zone. He cites Kitces research showing that well-prepared retirees systematically underspend and often grow their portfolios even during poor market conditions, attributing this to industry incentives that benefit from keeping retirees fearful. He proposes replacing one-time financial plans with ongoing financial systems comprising four components: a baseline financial assessment, portfolio guardrails defining a spending speed limit, a portfolio risk management framework, and a monitoring system with regular feedback loops.

## Key Takeaways

- The retirement risk zone spans both sides of the retirement date and contains the highest-stakes financial decisions of a person's life, including Social Security timing, Roth conversion decisions, and withdrawal strategy.
- Research by Michael Kitces shows affluent retirees systematically underspend relative to their income in retirement, with the problem worsening as wealth increases.
- Two-thirds of retirees using a 4% initial withdrawal rate over 30 years more than double their starting portfolio, and a retiree is more likely to end with five times principal than to deplete it.
- Financial plans are static point-in-time snapshots without feedback loops; financial systems incorporate ongoing monitoring, course-correction triggers, and real-time data.
- Portfolio guardrails define a spending speed limit and pre-define adjustment rules for when spending must be reduced or can be increased, removing the need for reactive decision-making.
- Retiring at least five years before the intended retirement date allows time to model, visualize, and implement a financial system while still in peak wealth and health.

## Detailed Notes

### The Retirement Risk Zone Defined [00:01:02]

The retirement risk zone is the transitional phase sandwiched between accumulation and decumulation. Key decisions made here—Social Security claiming, withdrawal speed, Roth conversions, Medicare coverage, portfolio reallocation—are largely irreversible and compound over decades. Amzalag argues the industry profits from retiree fear of spending and reframes the period as an opportunity zone.

### The Underspending Research [00:04:11]

Kitces' paper 'Why Most Retirees Will Never Draw Down Their Retirement Portfolio' documents that affluent retirees fail to spend even their annual portfolio income, and the wealthier the retiree, the more pronounced the underspending. Even during 2000–2008, a historically poor return period, average financial assets of wealthy retirees continued to increase—evidence that underspending is driven by psychology and industry framing, not financial necessity.

### Four Components of a Financial System [00:07:18]

Component one is a financial assessment establishing a baseline snapshot of health. Component two is portfolio guardrails that define a spending speed limit and escalation or de-escalation rules. Component three is a portfolio risk management system that monitors more than just asset allocation and rate of return, analogous to tracking more than just weight in a health checkup. Component four is a monitoring system that regularly back-tests withdrawal and guardrail measures, providing feedback loops that prevent surprises.
