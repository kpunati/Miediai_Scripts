# Pay $0 Tax For a Decade? Use This Hidden Social Security Formula...

## Summary

This video walks through a detailed case study of Tom and Susan, a 62-year-old married Texas couple with $85,000/year spending needs, showing how claiming Social Security at 62 combined with strategic account withdrawals can produce zero federal taxes for ten years. The key mechanism is the IRS combined income formula, which determines how much Social Security becomes taxable income. By carefully managing withdrawals from a taxable brokerage account with high cost basis, a pre-tax IRA, and Social Security, the couple stays under the standard deduction every year while also qualifying for ACA health insurance subsidies before Medicare.

## Key Takeaways

- The combined income formula (50% of SS benefit + other taxable income + tax-exempt interest) determines what portion of Social Security is taxable, and it operates as a graduated system, not a cliff.
- Claiming Social Security at 62 can be financially optimal when paired with a strategic withdrawal plan that exploits the standard deduction and low-income brackets.
- High cost basis in a taxable brokerage account dramatically reduces the taxable portion of withdrawals, enabling zero-tax income generation.
- ACA subsidies are calculated based on MAGI, and careful income management in pre-Medicare retirement years can unlock thousands in annual premium subsidies.
- Proactive IRA withdrawals reduce future required minimum distributions, potentially lowering IRMAA charges and future tax brackets.
- Roth withdrawals are invisible in the combined income formula, making them a valuable lever for managing taxable income.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Study Setup and Phase One (Ages 62-65) [00:00:00]

Tom and Susan are both 62, married, retired in Texas (no state income tax), with $36,000/year combined Social Security at 62, a $750,000 pre-tax IRA, a $250,000 taxable brokerage at 75% cost basis, and a $250,000 Roth IRA. Their annual spending need is $85,000. During the pre-Medicare phase, they withdraw $36,000 SS, $49,000 from the taxable brokerage (only 25% is taxable gain = $12,250), and $12,000 from the IRA. Total taxable income is $29,375, which falls below the 2026 standard deduction of $32,200, resulting in zero federal tax.

### ACA Subsidies and IRA Conversion Bonus [00:03:35]

With MAGI of $60,250 during the pre-Medicare years, Tom and Susan qualify for approximately $6,744/year in ACA health insurance subsidies, totaling roughly $20,000 over three years. The extra $12,000 IRA withdrawal above their $85,000 need can be converted to Roth at zero cost, repositioning $36,000 from pre-tax to after-tax over three years and saving an estimated $4,000-$5,000 in future taxes.

### Phase Two (Ages 65-72) and the Combined Income Formula [00:04:37]

After Medicare begins, the couple shifts to pulling $16,000 from the IRA and $33,000 from the taxable brokerage. Their combined income calculation yields the same result: $42,250, placing $5,125 of Social Security as taxable. Total taxable income of $29,375 again falls below the standard deduction, producing zero tax each year from 65 to 72. The combined income formula uses 50% of SS + other income; for this couple it consistently hits $10,250 over the $32,000 threshold, making only 50% of that overage ($5,125) taxable.

### Strategy Rationale and Roth Account Growth [00:06:58]

By the end of the ten-year period, the couple has extracted roughly $150,000 from their IRA tax-free and grown the Roth account from $250,000 to nearly $300,000 without touching it. This strategy prioritizes after-tax wealth and flexibility over maximizing total lifetime Social Security dollars, and it reduces the size of future RMDs to mitigate IRMAA risk and higher tax brackets in later retirement.
