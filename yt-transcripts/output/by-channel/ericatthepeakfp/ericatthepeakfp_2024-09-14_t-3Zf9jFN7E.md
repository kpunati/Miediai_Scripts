# It's Not Just You: Why Everyone Gave Up Retiring Early ...

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-09-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=t-3Zf9jFN7E
**Duration:** 17:30

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, presents a data-driven analysis to test whether the widely reported narrative — that rising costs are making retirement impossible — is supported by actual Bureau of Labor Statistics data from 1992 to 2023. He examines cost of living growth, income growth, budget share shifts across six spending categories, income quintile differences, and back-tested 60/40 portfolio withdrawal scenarios. His central finding is that income has outpaced spending, major expense categories have shifted only modestly, and even inflation-adjusted 6% withdrawal rates left retirees with significant wealth over the historical 30-year window. He concludes that barriers to retirement stem primarily from savings discipline failures rather than economic headwinds, and criticizes fear-based financial media narratives that serve the interests of financial product sellers.

## Key Takeaways

- BLS data shows average annual cost of living growth of 3.04% from 1992-2023, while income grew 33% more than expenditures over the same period.
- Housing's share of household budgets rose only 1.5% over 30 years; food share fell 1.5%; healthcare was the largest gainer at 2.5% — the shifts are modest, not catastrophic.
- Lower-income households (under $58k/year) saw housing and food take about 2% more of their income than higher quintiles, a meaningful but not severe divergence.
- The 60/40 portfolio outpaced cost of living by approximately 6% annually on average over 1992-2023, even including seven negative years and a near-50% drawdown in 2008-2009.
- Sustainable withdrawal rates appear materially higher than conventional wisdom suggests; even a 7% inflation-adjusted draw ran out only in the 29th year of a 30-year period in the back-test.
- 80% of Americans over 60 own their homes with median equity of $250,000, providing a substantial cost-of-living reduction once the mortgage is paid and a backup liquidity reserve.

## Detailed Notes

### Framing: Cost of Living vs. Inflation [00:00:00]

The video challenges prevalent media narratives that retirement is becoming unaffordable. The presenter distinguishes cost of living (actual BLS-tracked household spending) from inflation (a government-constructed basket), arguing BLS consumer expenditure data is a more relevant metric for retirees.

### Income vs. Spending Growth and Category Analysis [00:03:07]

From 1992 to 2022, incomes grew 177% versus expenditure growth of 144% — a 33% income advantage. Breaking down budget shares across housing, food, transportation, healthcare, apparel, and services shows only modest shifts: housing up 1.5%, food down 1.5%, transportation down 0.75%, healthcare up 2.5%, apparel and services down 3%. The data shows a minor reshuffling, not a crisis.

### Income Quintile Differences [00:05:13]

The bottom two income quintiles (households earning under $58,000) saw housing and food consume about 2% more of income than higher quintiles over the 30-year period. While directionally meaningful, the presenter characterizes this as relatively small — not large enough to be a primary driver of retirement unaffordability.

### Portfolio Return Back-Tests Against Cost of Living [00:07:20]

A standard 60/40 mutual fund portfolio returned 9.45% annually on average versus 3.04% cost of living growth from 1992 to 2023. Decade-by-decade analysis shows the spread narrowed in 2002-2011 (5.28% portfolio vs. 2.35% cost of living) but recovered in 2012-2022 (11.5% vs. 3.7%). Back-tests of 5%, 6%, and 7% fixed and inflation-adjusted withdrawal rates showed robust outcomes; only a 7% inflation-adjusted withdrawal rate exhausted funds, doing so only in year 29 of a 30-year window, and across a period including the dot-com crash and the 2008 financial crisis.

### Conclusions and Critique of Fear-Based Narratives [00:14:40]

The presenter argues retirement is achievable for disciplined savers and that delayed retirement is primarily a savings behavior problem, not an economics problem. He singles out financial industry research (citing a Northwestern Mutual report claiming Americans need $1.46 million to retire comfortably) as motivated by product sales and fee preservation. He highlights homeownership equity as a retirement 'bonus' reducing cost of living and providing backup liquidity.
