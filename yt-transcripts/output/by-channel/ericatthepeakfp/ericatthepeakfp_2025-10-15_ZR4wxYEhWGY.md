# You Need THIS Much To Retire & Spend $10k/Mo At Age 55/60/65

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-10-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ZR4wxYEhWGY
**Duration:** 14:30

## Summary

Eric Amzalag walks through three retirement age scenarios — 55, 60, and 65 — showing the specific savings required to sustain $10,000 per month after taxes in each case. A recurring theme is that asset location (splitting savings between pre-tax and taxable brokerage accounts) can reduce required savings by roughly $200,000-$300,000 at every age. The video also highlights a 'retiring too late' trap where over-savers at 65 end up doing complex RMD and tax-planning homework instead of enjoying retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- Spending $10,000 per month requires generating $133,000-$140,000 in gross income annually due to tax grossing-up.
- Retiring at 55 requires up to $3 million with all pre-tax savings, reduced to about $2.7 million with a 50/50 pre-tax and taxable brokerage split.
- The Rule of 55 allows penalty-free 401k access at 55 for those who leave a job that year, but does not apply to IRAs.
- Having taxable brokerage assets at any retirement age saves approximately $200,000-$300,000 in required portfolio size by enabling tax-efficient withdrawals.
- Retiring too late can create a complexity trap: over-accumulated pre-tax assets generate forced RMDs and years of tax planning homework.
- The right retirement age is personal, balancing financial readiness with health span and lifestyle goals, not just minimizing portfolio requirements.

## Detailed Notes

### The Two Foundational Concepts: Grossing Up and Asset Location [00:01:02]

Before looking at specific ages, Amzalag introduces two critical concepts. First, grossing up: spending $10,000 per month requires generating $133,000-$140,000 pre-tax due to taxes. Second, asset location: where savings are held (pre-tax 401k/IRA vs. taxable brokerage) significantly changes required portfolio size, potentially by $300,000 or more.

### Retiring at Age 55: The Hardest Scenario [00:02:03]

At 55, healthcare costs can reach $1,000-$2,000 per month for a couple since Medicare is 10 years away. Portfolio must sustain 35-40 years of withdrawals. Without the Rule of 55, IRA access before 59.5 triggers a 10% penalty, requiring complex 72(t) arrangements. With all pre-tax savings, the required portfolio is approximately $3 million; with 50% in a taxable brokerage, it falls to roughly $2.7 million.

### Retiring at Age 60: The Sweet Spot [00:06:54]

At 60, the 59.5 penalty barrier is gone, the healthcare gap is halved to five years before Medicare, and the portfolio does not need to stretch as far. Required savings drop to roughly $2.25 million (all pre-tax) or $2 million (50/50 split), making this a practical balance of early freedom and financial security.

### Retiring at Age 65: The Complexity Trap [00:07:42]

At 65, Medicare kicks in and Social Security is available at 67, making the financial picture simplest. Required savings fall to $1.45 million (all pre-tax) or $1.25 million (50/50 split). However, over-saving into the traditional retirement age creates an RMD problem: forced distributions starting at 73-75 can push retirees into higher tax brackets and trigger years of Roth conversion homework.

### Choosing the Right Retirement Age [00:11:19]

Amzalag provides four diagnostic questions: know your number vs. your current savings; assess whether you can use the Rule of 55 or must manage 72(t); evaluate your asset location mix; and consider what you want to do with retirement years. The decision is ultimately personal, not purely mathematical.
