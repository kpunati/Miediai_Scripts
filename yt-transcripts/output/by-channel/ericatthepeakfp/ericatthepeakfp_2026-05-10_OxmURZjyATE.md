# Do This In Your 60s And Never Worry About Taxes Again

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-05-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=OxmURZjyATE
**Duration:** 18:18

## Summary

Eric Amzalag argues that the primary source of tax anxiety in retirement is not high taxes but unexpected taxes — surprises from portfolio rebalancing gains, Medicare IRMAA thresholds, or underestimated RMDs. He introduces the 30-5-1 rule as a structured planning framework: the 30-year map identifies where future tax pressure and planning windows exist; the five-year phases establish what the current planning objective actually is (e.g., managing ACA subsidies, Roth conversion windows, or RMD management); and the one-year annual execution plan turns phase objectives into specific tax decisions. The framework prevents both the overconfidence of 30-year projections and the shortsightedness of annual tax minimization by connecting all three lenses into a coherent and adjustable strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- Tax anxiety in retirement stems primarily from surprise, not just tax levels — the 30-5-1 rule addresses the predictability problem, not just the size-of-tax-bill problem.
- Optimizing for the lowest current-year tax bill without a multi-year phase objective can silently grow pre-tax balances and future RMDs, making the problem worse.
- A 30-year tax projection is useful as a terrain map but should not be used to justify paying taxes today for benefits that only materialize in year 25 under a long list of future assumptions.
- Each five-year retirement phase has a distinct objective: ACA subsidy management before Medicare, Roth conversion windows after Medicare, RMD mitigation before 73, and income management after RMDs begin.
- Roth conversions, withdrawal sequencing, and qualified charitable distributions are all tools — the 30-5-1 framework determines whether and when each tool is appropriate, rather than applying them by default.
- The 60s are the most critical decade for tax planning because most major planning windows — pre-RMD, pre-Social Security, pre-Medicare — are still open and actionable.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Taxes Surprise Retirees [00:00:00]

Common tax surprises include unexpected capital gains from portfolio rebalancing, Medicare IRMAA premium surcharges from income spikes, and larger-than-expected RMDs from pre-tax balances that grew unmonitored. Amzalag argues that the real problem is not the existence of taxes but their unpredictability — retirees make decisions without seeing the tax consequence until after it has locked in.

### Limitations of 30-Year Projections and Annual Plans [00:03:46]

A 30-year tax projection is valuable for seeing future tax terrain but carries low confidence for decisions that only pay off in year 25 or later. Paying taxes now for benefits contingent on long chains of future assumptions is a common error. Conversely, focusing only on minimizing this year's tax bill ignores the planning window between retirement and RMDs, where pre-tax balances can be deliberately reduced at favorable rates.

### The 30-5-1 Framework Explained [00:09:08]

The 30-year map answers 'what could happen' by identifying future RMD pressure, Social Security timing, IRMAA exposure, and account balance trajectories. The five-year phases answer 'what are we trying to accomplish right now' by defining a phase-specific objective that varies across retirement (ACA subsidy protection, Roth conversion, RMD management). The one-year execution plan answers 'what do I actually do this year' using a tax-efficient worksheet to set target taxable income and evaluate specific moves like conversions, QCDs, or charitable giving.

### Applying Tools Within the Framework [00:13:38]

Roth conversions are most justified when the map shows a clear future problem (e.g., a large age gap between spouses creating widow tax risk) and the current phase provides a clean window. Withdrawal sequencing decisions depend entirely on the current phase objective: protecting ACA subsidies calls for after-tax spending, while pre-RMD conversion windows call for the opposite. QCDs are effective only after age 70.5 in the RMD phase; using them prematurely wastes the tool. Doing nothing can also be the correct plan when no action is warranted by the phase or map.
