# Every Broken Retirement Rule Explained in 14 Minutes

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-09-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=BQfr8eFCMRI
**Duration:** 14:43

## Summary

Eric Amzalag challenges four widely cited retirement rules of thumb, showing through spreadsheet examples that each can produce dangerously misleading guidance when applied without personalizing to an individual's situation. He argues that what matters is not total annual spending but the portion of spending that must come from the portfolio after accounting for Social Security and other income. The video concludes that rules of thumb can be a useful starting point but should never substitute for a personalized, scenario-based retirement plan built three to five years before the target retirement date.

## Key Takeaways

- The 4% rule describes the lowest common denominator safe withdrawal rate, not the optimal rate, and should not be treated as a universal spending cap.
- The 100-minus-age stock allocation formula can be dangerously conservative for savers who have not met their savings targets and need higher growth.
- The 25x savings rule should use required portfolio income rather than total annual spending, which can reduce the savings target dramatically once Social Security is factored in.
- Roth conversions are not universally beneficial; the break-even age may exceed median life expectancy, and the cost can dramatically raise withdrawal rates during conversion years.
- Every retiree's situation is unique, and retirement planning should begin three to five years before the target retirement date.

## Detailed Notes

### Rule 1: The 4% Rule Misapplied [00:00:00]

The 4% rule was designed to identify the maximum safe withdrawal rate in the worst historical market environments, not as a target spending amount. If a retiree's spending needs exceed 4% of their portfolio, blindly following the rule creates a spending gap that must be addressed through additional saving, working longer, or reduced spending. The video illustrates this with a couple needing 6.88% from a $500,000 portfolio.

### Rule 2: 100 Minus Age Asset Allocation [00:03:05]

Following a glide path that mechanically reduces equity exposure with age can backfire for those who have underfunded their retirement savings and need higher returns. The video demonstrates that increasing the target rate of return from 5% to 7% can reduce required monthly savings significantly, illustrating that the appropriate allocation depends on individual savings behavior, not age alone.

### Rule 3: 25x Annual Spending Target [00:07:19]

Using total annual spending as the numerator of the 25x rule overstates the required savings for most retirees because Social Security and other income sources cover a portion of spending. Substituting required portfolio income—the spending amount the portfolio must actually fund—produces a materially lower and more accurate savings target.

### Rule 4: Universal Roth Conversions [00:10:28]

A case study shows that a financial planning tool recommended Roth conversions for a couple, but the break-even ages of 84 (husband) and 79 (wife) placed the husband below a 50% survival probability of ever benefiting. Additionally, the conversions caused distribution rates to spike dramatically during conversion years, adding significant portfolio stress. The recommendation was not to convert despite the tool's suggestion.
