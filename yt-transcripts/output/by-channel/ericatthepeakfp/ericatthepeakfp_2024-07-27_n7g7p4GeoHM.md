# Why It Makes COMPLETE Sense To Claim Social Security At 62

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-07-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=n7g7p4GeoHM
**Duration:** 15:57

## Summary

Eric Amzalag challenges the conventional wisdom that delaying Social Security to age 70 is always optimal. He presents research showing most Americans retire earlier than they plan (median actual retirement age of 62), then introduces the concept of sequence-of-returns risk — the danger that early-retirement portfolio distributions combined with market losses can cause permanent portfolio damage. Using modeled scenarios, he demonstrates that claiming Social Security at 62 acts as a prophylactic against early-distribution risk by reducing portfolio withdrawal rates and compares specific ending portfolio values under adverse market conditions. The video concludes with a profile of retirees for whom early claiming makes the most sense.

## Key Takeaways

- Most Americans retire earlier than they expect, with the median actual retirement age being 62, not 65 as workers project.
- Claiming Social Security early reduces early-retirement portfolio distribution rates, which is the highest-risk period due to the retirement spending smile.
- The loss recovery rule is nonlinear: a 30% loss requires a 43% gain to recover, and distributions compound the damage exponentially.
- Early Social Security claiming can preserve significantly more portfolio value when negative market returns occur in the first years of retirement.
- Early claiming makes the most sense for single individuals, lower-earning spouses, those with sizable retirement assets, or those with shorter life expectancy.
- Building an individualized financial model is far more important than following general YouTube advice about Social Security timing.

## Detailed Notes

### When Americans Actually Retire: The Expectation-Reality Gap [00:01:02]

EBRI data shows workers expect a median retirement age of 65 while actual retirement happens at 62. One-third of workers plan to retire at or after 70, but only 6% do. Conversely, 33% retire before 60. This mismatch means income needs arise sooner than planned, making Social Security timing a critical decision.

### Life Expectancy and Mortality Studies [00:03:06]

As of 2022, CDC data shows median life expectancy of 74.8 years for men and 80.2 years for women. A study on retirement age and mortality found that retiring one year earlier than expected increases all-cause mortality risk by 9-11%, suggesting work has a protective effect. The retirement spending smile pattern means spending is highest in early retirement, then dips in middle years, then rises again for end-of-life care.

### Portfolio Distribution Rate Comparison: Claiming at 62 vs. 70 [00:06:16]

For a hypothetical retiree with $375,000 seeking $63,000/year who retires at 61, claiming at 70 creates distribution rates rising from 4.6% to 7.19% before Social Security kicks in. Claiming at 62 holds the peak distribution rate below 4.53%. The reduced distribution burden preserves $160,000 more in portfolio value over the first 9 years.

### The Loss Recovery Rule and Sequence of Returns [00:08:22]

A 10% loss requires an 11.1% gain to recover; a 30% loss requires 43%; a 50% loss requires 100%. At the historical S&P 500 inflation-adjusted return of 7.44%, recovering a 30% loss takes 5.76 years. Adding a 4% annual distribution to a 30% loss extends the extra growth needed to 8.6% above the baseline. In a modeled scenario with two years of negative returns at ages 64-65, the early-claiming portfolio ends at $934,000 versus $758,000 at age 70, a $176,000 difference.

### Who Should Consider Early Claiming [00:13:41]

Early claiming is most compelling for single individuals, the lower-earning spouse in a couple, those with substantial retirement assets, and those with shorter-than-average life expectancy. The video emphasizes that no universal rule applies and individualized modeling combined with professional advice is essential.
