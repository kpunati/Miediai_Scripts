# If You Have $1M+ In Pre-Tax, Once You Retire, DO THIS…

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-04-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=k5RQ6Fa61k4
**Duration:** 34:27

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, walks through three detailed case studies showing how pre-retirees with $1M, $2M, and $3M+ in pre-tax accounts can dramatically reduce lifetime taxes by sequencing withdrawals around ACA subsidy cliffs, Medicare IRMAA thresholds, and RMD windows. The core insight is that the timing and ordering of account withdrawals matter more than the specific accounts chosen, and that deploying after-tax brokerage or Roth assets strategically before age 65 produces highly predictable, controllable returns. For higher balances, deliberately exceeding certain IRMAA thresholds to enable supercharged Roth conversions can produce a net benefit even after surcharges.

## Key Takeaways

- Spending down after-tax brokerage accounts while on COBRA forfeits their value as an ACA subsidy lever, a common mistake conventional advice encourages.
- Roth money should be viewed as an active tool to manage MAGI, not a sacrosanct account to leave untouched until absolutely necessary.
- For those under 65, the ACA MAGI cliff at $84,600 (married filing jointly) functions as a de facto tax; exceeding it by even $1 can cost $23,000/year in lost health insurance subsidies.
- At $3M+ pre-tax, moderate Roth conversions may fail to meaningfully reduce future RMDs because portfolio growth replaces converted amounts; supercharged conversions are often required.
- Intentionally triggering IRMAA tier-1 or tier-2 surcharges can be rational if the additional Roth conversion room produces tax savings that far exceed the temporary surcharge cost.
- Social Security claiming strategy shifts from a safety net at lower wealth levels to a pure tax planning decision at $3M+, where the 65-70 conversion runway is highly valuable.
- Retirement tax planning is best understood as three sequential phases: ACA optimization (pre-65), Roth conversion window (65 to SS claiming), and RMD management (SS to RMD age).

## Detailed Notes

### Introduction: The Pre-Tax Trap and Sequencing Logic [00:00:00]

Most people with over $1M in pre-tax accounts assume the tax bill is fixed and uncontrollable. The presenter argues that the sequence and timing of withdrawals, not the choice of accounts, determines the actual tax outcome. Three case studies cover $1M, $2M, and $3M+ pre-tax balances, each illustrating a different interplay of tax systems.

### Case Study 1: $1M Pre-Tax, $50K Brokerage [00:02:30]

A 62-year-old married couple wants $95K/year in spending. Conventional advice to spend brokerage first eliminates the ACA subsidy lever. Instead, they should use COBRA the first year, then reverse-engineer IRA withdrawals to stay below the $84,600 MAGI cliff, funding the remainder from the brokerage account. Over two years, they spend ~$50K from the brokerage to secure ~$46K in ACA subsidies — an effective 90%+ controlled return.

### Case Study 2: $2M Pre-Tax, $100K Roth [00:10:35]

This couple wants $125K/year but has no brokerage account; only Roth. Conventional wisdom says never touch the Roth early. The correct strategy is to deploy ~$80K of Roth across ages 63-64 to keep MAGI under the $84,600 cliff, avoiding ~$46K in premium penalties for a ~45% controlled return. From age 65, Medicare's higher IRMAA threshold ($218K) opens up ~$63K/year of Roth conversion room, ultimately rebuilding the Roth balance six to seven times over by age 73.

### Case Study 3: $3M Pre-Tax — Optimized vs. Supercharged Conversion [00:17:49]

With $3M pre-tax, $500K brokerage, and $500K Roth, the instinct to convert immediately post-retirement is wrong because conversions blow through the ACA cliff. The optimized approach (Scenario A) delays conversions to age 65, staying under the $218K IRMAA ceiling, converting $615K over nine years — but the IRA still sits at ~$2.6M at RMD age because growth offsets conversions. The supercharged approach (Scenario B) intentionally pushes into IRMAA tier 2 ($274K-$342K), converting ~$1.1M total, reducing the IRA to ~$2M and cutting year-one RMDs nearly in half. The cost is ~$29K in IRMAA surcharges over 5 years versus ~$100K in avoided future taxes, a roughly 4:1 benefit ratio.

### Integrating Social Security and Phase-Based Framework [00:31:41]

At all three wealth levels, delaying Social Security to age 70 is recommended, though at lower balances it also serves as a portfolio triage tool if markets drop. At $3M+, delaying is purely a tax planning decision to preserve the 65-70 conversion runway. The broader framework segments retirement into three phases: ACA optimization (pre-65), Roth conversion focus (Medicare to SS claiming), and RMD management (SS to RMD onset), with each phase governed by distinct income thresholds.
