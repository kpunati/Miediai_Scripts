# Can I Realistically Spend $10,000 in Retirement?

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-06-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=yaTCVJwlY8w
**Duration:** 21:12

## Summary

Eric Amzalag of Peak Financial Planning presents a detailed case study of John and Jane Doe, a hypothetical couple aged 60-61 with $1.1 million in liquid assets and a $50,280/year pension, exploring whether they can spend $10,000 per month after taxes in early retirement. The analysis uses a four-step decision-making framework: identifying distribution rates by retirement phase, scientifically testing action items against a baseline, evaluating tradeoffs of dynamic spending strategies, and applying the chosen approach. The central finding is that the couple's risk is heavily front-loaded into the 10 years before Social Security begins at age 70, and that no dynamic spending strategy guarantees $10,000/month throughout retirement without requiring behavioral spending cuts.

## Key Takeaways

- Monte Carlo probability of success alone does not reveal where in a retirement the risk is concentrated; distribution rates by year must also be examined.
- This couple's plan has 10 years of above-average distribution rates before Social Security begins, making them highly exposed to sequence-of-returns risk.
- All three dynamic spending strategies—spending smile, floor-and-ceiling, and guardrails—improve probability of success but embed forced spending cuts that are not visible in headline probability figures.
- The retirement spending smile raises success probability to 95% but reduces total lifetime spending by $700,000 versus the inflation-adjusted baseline.
- Early retirement at 60 with $1.1 million and a $10,000/month spending target carries uncomfortably high risk unless the couple is willing to accept dynamic spending rules with real behavioral discipline.
- Beginning retirement planning at least 5 years before the target retirement date allows time to adjust expectations and improve plan outcomes.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Study Setup and Baseline Risk [00:01:01]

John (61) and Jane (60) have $425,000 in a 457b, $200,000 in a Roth 457b, and $485,000 in a joint brokerage account, totaling $1.1 million in usable assets. Jane has a pension worth $50,280/year. Both plan to stop working at age 60 and delay Social Security until age 70, leaving a 10-year gap during which nearly all spending must come from the portfolio.

### Identifying Distribution Rate Risk [00:05:11]

A grid-based spreadsheet analysis reveals the couple faces required portfolio income (distribution rates) well above 5% for 9 successive years early in retirement. The baseline plan shows a 64% Monte Carlo probability of success, but a peak distribution rate of 9.2% in the highest-risk years. Taking the average distribution rate of 3.07% would give a misleadingly low picture of their actual risk exposure.

### Testing Action Items: What Doesn't Move the Needle [00:07:16]

Testing investment allocation changes, Social Security claiming alternatives, and pension adjustments individually against the baseline reveals none of them meaningfully improve the probability of success. The couple is already in a 65/35 stock-bond allocation and holds single stocks, limiting further risk increases. This forces attention onto dynamic spending strategies.

### Comparing Four Spending Scenarios [00:12:28]

Four scenarios are compared side-by-side: inflation-adjusted baseline (64% success, 9 years above 5% distribution rate), retirement spending smile (95% success but $700K less total spending), floor-and-ceiling (93% success, spending cuts occur early which conflicts with the couple's preference), and guardrails (98% success, most restrictive, reduces years above 5% to six). None guarantees $10,000/month throughout retirement without behavioral commitment to dynamic adjustments.
