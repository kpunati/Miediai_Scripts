# 5 Years From Retirement? Here’s Exactly What To Do

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-03-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FdjhFC30EKA
**Duration:** 33:16

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, presents a structured five-year countdown framework for retirement planning, arguing that most people start the process too late and in the wrong place by focusing on tax optimization before establishing a clear life vision. The video covers a year-by-year checklist from five years before retirement down to the final 12 months, emphasizing that each year's work builds the foundation for the next. Particular emphasis is placed on estate planning at the four-year mark and spending capacity analysis at the three-year mark as often-neglected but critical steps.

## Key Takeaways

- Saving money and having a retirement plan are two completely different things; having financial fuel without a destination and route is the root cause of most retirement planning failures.
- At five years out, the priority is target-setting — clarifying what you would do if offered an early retirement package, laid off, or presented with a phased retirement option.
- At four years out, complete a net worth inventory, assess your debt situation, and most importantly begin estate planning including wills, powers of attorney, and beneficiary designations.
- At three years out, stress-test the plan: build a real spending capacity estimate, model Social Security claiming scenarios, and map the tax landscape of your accounts.
- At two years out, the workload is intentionally light: focus on cash reserve positioning and a beneficiary/estate document review update.
- In the final 12 months, the most critical priorities in order are: spending capacity confirmation, withdrawal strategy framework, and portfolio positioning — health insurance and estate planning can follow.
- Cobra is almost always the recommended bridge health insurance option for pre-65 retirees because it buys time to optimize the tax and ACA subsidy picture without a rushed decision.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Most People Start Retirement Planning Wrong [00:00:00]

Eric argues that focusing first on tax optimization, Social Security maximization, or portfolio construction is the wrong entry point. Without a clear destination (what retirement will cost and look like), financial optimization is like having 1,000 gallons of fuel with no car, route, or destination. The most avoidable and costly mistakes happen when people try to cram all retirement decisions into the final year under time pressure.

### Year Five: Set Your Target [00:03:50]

Five years out, the priority is not spreadsheets but scenario planning: what would you do if offered an early retirement package, laid off tomorrow, or presented with a phased retirement? Roughly one-third of people retire electively; the majority are forced by health, employer action, or family circumstances. Building even a rough spending estimate at this stage dramatically reduces anxiety and improves decision-making quality if a forced retirement event occurs.

### Year Four: Build Infrastructure — Net Worth, Debt, Estate Planning [00:10:54]

The four-year mark focuses on mapping total net worth by account type (pre-tax, Roth, taxable, home equity, pensions), conducting a debt audit, and beginning long-term care research. The most under-discussed item is estate planning: wills, durable and healthcare powers of attorney, beneficiary designations, and family conversations about intentions. Eric emphasizes doing this now because the final year of work and first years of retirement are too frenzied to address it well, and unexpected health events make delayed planning far more painful.

### Year Three: Stress Test the Math [00:16:22]

Three years out is when retirement planning either confirms or challenges the target set in year five. Key tasks include running a real spending capacity analysis (can a $2M portfolio sustain $120K/year after taxes and inflation?), modeling Social Security claiming scenarios across early, full, and delayed retirement ages, mapping the tax landscape of retirement accounts, and understanding Medicare basics including IRMAA bracket implications. The goal is not to finalize decisions but to understand the range of outcomes and identify any funding gaps while there is still time to close them.

### Years Two and One: Final Preparation [00:21:52]

Year two is intentionally low-intensity: build cash reserve positioning and conduct a brief beneficiary review. The final 12 months are action-heavy regardless of prior preparation: confirm spending capacity against current balances, build the actual withdrawal strategy framework, review portfolio positioning and rebalancing protocols, decide on health insurance (almost always Cobra for pre-65 retirees), model the transition-year tax projection, and work through employer logistics (resignation timing, 401k rollover options, pension details, stock options). If starting late, prioritize spending capacity first, then withdrawal strategy, then portfolio positioning.
