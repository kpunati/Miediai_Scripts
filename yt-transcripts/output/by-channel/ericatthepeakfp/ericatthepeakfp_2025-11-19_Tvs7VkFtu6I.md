# When Early Retirees Do This, They Save $20,000 On Healthcare

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-11-19
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Tvs7VkFtu6I
**Duration:** 13:27

## Summary

Eric Amzalag identifies the ACA subsidy cliff — where household income exceeding 400% of the federal poverty level (approximately $84,100 for a married couple) eliminates all premium subsidies — as a critical but underappreciated trap for early retirees. He introduces the 'flexibility ratio' (non-taxable assets divided by total portfolio) as the metric that determines how much control a retiree has over their IRS-visible income. Two case studies illustrate that identical $120,000 spending levels produce vastly different health insurance costs depending on account mix and withdrawal sequence, and that early Social Security claiming is the most common irreversible mistake that destroys subsidy eligibility.

## Key Takeaways

- ACA premiums for early retirees are based on IRS-visible income, not actual spending — the same $120,000 lifestyle can produce $3,972 or $23,400 per year in premiums depending on account mix.
- The subsidy cliff at 400% of the federal poverty level (~$84,100 for a married couple) causes premiums to jump by $1,600 per month if crossed by even $1,100 in additional income.
- The flexibility ratio — Roth money plus taxable basis plus cash divided by total assets — measures the share of the portfolio that can be withdrawn without creating IRS income.
- Five income sources that erode flexibility and push retirees over the subsidy cliff are: early Social Security, large traditional IRA balances, automatic pensions, uncontrollable dividends and interest, and wrong withdrawal sequencing.
- Claiming Social Security early (before Medicare) is the most dangerous irreversible mistake — once claimed for more than 12 months it cannot be undone, locking in elevated MAGI indefinitely.
- After Medicare at 65, the IRMAA surcharge cliff at roughly $200,000 for married couples replaces the ACA cliff, but provides far more flexibility to manage income.

## Detailed Notes

### The ACA Subsidy Cliff Explained [00:01:54]

For a married couple, income at or below $84,100 (400% of the federal poverty level) qualifies for ACA subsidies that cap premiums. Crossing even slightly above that threshold eliminates all subsidies, causing premiums to jump by roughly $1,600 per month. The difference compounds to approximately $100,000 over a five-year pre-Medicare window.

### The Flexibility Ratio Framework [00:05:24]

The flexibility ratio equals (Roth IRA balance + taxable brokerage basis + cash) divided by total financial assets. A high ratio (example: 46%) means nearly half the portfolio can be drawn without triggering IRS income. A low ratio (example: 17%) means the retiree is trapped — almost every withdrawal counts as income and pushes them over the subsidy cliff. Account type diversification — built up over the accumulation years — determines subsidy eligibility more than spending level.

### Year-by-Year Withdrawal Strategy [00:10:23]

Couple B's annual plan is to pull $60,000 from a traditional IRA (counts as income), $20,000 from Roth (no income), $20,000 from cash (no income), and realize $20,000 in long-term capital gains (counts as income), totaling $120,000 in spending at only $80,000 of IRS income. Common mistakes include pulling too much from the IRA and failing to track dividends and interest from bank accounts that count as income even when not spent.

### The Social Security Early-Claim Trap [00:08:17]

A real client couple with a 65% flexibility ratio planned to pay $412/month in premiums but claimed Social Security at 62, adding $38,000 to annual MAGI and jumping to $1,950/month in premiums. By the time they met with the advisor they had been on Social Security for a year and could no longer unclaim it, locking in $18,000/year in excess premiums for the remaining three years until Medicare.
