# I’m a Retirement Expert. Here’s Why RMDs Don’t Matter

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-10-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=RX0UICLDVtM
**Duration:** 13:28

## Summary

Eric Amzalag uses financial planning software (Income Labs) to model how RMDs affect retirees at different wealth levels. His central argument is that for households below $3 million in pre-tax savings — which is over 99% of retirees — RMDs do not materially raise effective tax rates or threaten retirement security. He cautions against unnecessary large Roth conversions that restrict early-retirement spending flexibility and against fear-driven planning based on the assumption that tax rates will inevitably rise.

## Key Takeaways

- For a couple with $1 million in pre-tax savings, effective tax rates can actually decline once RMDs begin at age 75 because Social Security reduces the need for portfolio withdrawals.
- RMDs only start to create a meaningful tax burden at roughly $3 million or more in pre-tax accounts, which represents less than 1% of US households.
- Large upfront Roth conversions can constrain spending flexibility and may result in prepaying taxes at the same bracket as later RMD taxes, producing no benefit.
- Four practical strategies to manage RMD tax impact are: blended withdrawals, asset location, micro Roth conversions in low-income years, and qualified charitable distributions after age 70.5.
- The widows penalty from RMDs is meaningful only for a fraction of a fraction of retirees who are both high-wealth and have significant age gaps between spouses.
- Financial planning charts showing taxes in nominal dollars exaggerate the future tax burden — adjusting for inflation makes the picture far less alarming.

## Detailed Notes

### RMD Impact at $1 Million Pre-Tax [00:01:16]

Modeling for Mark and Lisa with $1 million in pre-tax accounts shows their effective tax rate of 7–8% in early retirement actually drops once RMDs begin at 75 because Social Security subsidizes their spending, reducing forced portfolio draws. The scenario illustrates that nominal-dollar chart presentations systematically mislead retirees.

### RMD Impact at $3 Million Pre-Tax [00:03:49]

At $3 million in pre-tax accounts, RMDs do incrementally push effective tax rates higher, eventually reaching a terminal rate near 20% in late retirement. However, the extra tax cost at this wealth level does not reduce quality of life and is manageable with planning. This scenario affects fewer than 1% of US households.

### Who Should Worry About RMDs [00:06:15]

Three tiers are defined: households under $3 million (RMDs are a non-issue), households over $3 million (RMDs require planning around taxes and legacy), and high-wealth couples with large age gaps who face the widows penalty. Amzalag estimates the last group is a fraction of a fraction of all retirees.

### Four Strategies to Manage RMDs [00:09:55]

Blended withdrawals gradually reduce pre-tax balances before RMDs begin. Asset location places low-growth assets in pre-tax accounts and high-growth assets in Roth or taxable. Micro Roth conversions in low-income or underspending years chip away at pre-tax balances without constraining lifestyle. Qualified charitable distributions after age 70.5 satisfy RMDs without increasing taxable income.
