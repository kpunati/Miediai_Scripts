# Retirement Portfolio Lies: Why Asset Allocation Is A Scam

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-08-31
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=hFoTI_DakIM
**Duration:** 17:22

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP and RICP at Peak Financial Planning, systematically critiques standard asset allocation as a retirement tool by demonstrating that the 2022 market correction exposed four major flaws: risk obfuscation within bond funds, impulsive decision-making, hesitancy to re-enter markets after reducing risk, and a confidence-loss snowball effect. He shows that both VBTLX and TLT — considered 'safe' assets — lost more than most investors expected, while the 60/40 portfolio took 29 months to recover. As an alternative, he advocates using four risk metrics (beta, market correlation, standard deviation, max drawdown) alongside direct bond and stock ownership, buy/sell targets for bond funds, and regular back-testing of historical scenarios. The video argues that asset allocations persist because they reduce friction and allow underqualified advisors to appear competent.

## Key Takeaways

- A standard 60/40 portfolio lost approximately 21% from January to October 2022 and did not recover until May 2024, underperforming the S&P 500 which recovered by December 2023.
- Bond funds like VBTLX and TLT carry hidden credit risk and duration risk that simple stock-bond allocations do not reveal.
- Owning bonds through funds eliminates the guaranteed return of principal that is the primary reason investors accept lower bond returns.
- Dividend and value stocks like Pepsi and Kroger were up approximately 6% during the 2022 correction, yet are lumped into the same risk category as high-beta tech stocks in standard allocations.
- Peak Financial Planning uses four additional risk measures — beta, market correlation, standard deviation, and max drawdown — alongside direct bond and stock ownership to manage retirement portfolios.
- Asset allocations persist partly because they reduce decision-making complexity for consumers and allow underqualified advisors to appear knowledgeable without deeper analysis.

## Detailed Notes

### The 2022 Market Correction as a Case Study [00:00:00]

Amzalag opens with the 2022 downturn: S&P 500 down 24%, VBTLX down 15.5%, TLT down 32%, and a 60/40 portfolio losing roughly 21%. The 60/40 portfolio took 29 months to recover while the S&P 500 recovered by December 2023 and grew 11.6% by May 2024. This sets up the core argument that bonds did not function as expected hedges.

### Four Problems With Asset Allocation [00:02:07]

Problem 1: Obfuscation — bond funds include junk bonds, mortgage-backed securities, and BBB-rated debt, while VOO concentrates 35% in its top 10 holdings. Problem 2: Impulsive decision-making — investors sold equities during the downturn only to miss the faster stock recovery. Problem 3: Hesitancy to re-enter — after reducing risk investors missed the market bottom. Problem 4: Confidence-loss snowball — eroding trust leads to paralysis or emotional decisions rather than systematic adjustments.

### Interest Rate Environment and Duration Risk [00:07:19]

Amzalag explains that 20 years of low interest rates distorted bond utility. TLT fell nearly 41% from early 2022 — almost double VOO's maximum drawdown — because long-duration bonds drop in value when rates rise. Conversely, when rates are now high, long bonds offer genuine upside when rates eventually fall.

### Peak Financial Planning's Alternative Approach [00:12:32]

Three solutions are offered: (1) Use beta, market correlation, standard deviation, and max drawdown as risk measures beyond allocation percentages. (2) Own bonds directly to retain return-of-principal guarantees and use bond funds only with defined buy/sell triggers based on interest rate levels. (3) Own individual stocks for more targeted portfolio defense during sector downturns. All approaches prioritize downside protection over maximizing returns.
