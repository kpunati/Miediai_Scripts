# The Fall of the 3 Bucket Strategy (And What’s Replacing It)

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-07-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=EnTpQ6wvU9M
**Duration:** 16:54

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, a CFP and RICP, critiques the widely used three-bucket retirement strategy, demonstrating that its apparent simplicity conceals serious execution flaws: no systematic rebalancing rules, forced market-timing decisions at refill, and a dangerous illusion of risk isolation. He introduces a four-layer guardrail framework as a replacement, comprising asset sleeves (broad allocation), target weights, tolerance-band triggers for rebalancing, and a defined rebalancing frequency — paired with a risk-based modern guardrails spending plan. The video includes a live walkthrough of a downloadable spreadsheet template that automates the framework's calculations.

## Key Takeaways

- The three-bucket strategy's core flaw is that bucket refills require reactive investment decisions at precisely the worst moments during market downturns.
- When bonds and stocks fall simultaneously (as in 2022), all 'safe' buckets can be depleted, leaving no buffer and forcing asset sales at depressed prices.
- Without predefined rebalancing rules, most retirees improvise, creating ad-hoc 'Frankenstein' strategies that lack consistency.
- The four-layer framework replaces gut-feel decisions with rule-based triggers: sleeves define allocation, weights set targets, tolerance bands determine rebalancing thresholds, and frequency sets the review cadence.
- A cash or cash-like sleeve within the four-layer framework can satisfy the psychological desire for a 'bucket' without the execution flaws of a standalone bucket strategy.
- Combining the investment framework with a risk-based guardrails spending plan provides both investment discipline and income confidence during volatile markets.

## Detailed Notes

### How the Three-Bucket Strategy Works and Why It Fails [00:00:00]

The classic strategy divides savings into a cash bucket (roughly 2 years of expenses), a fixed-income bucket (roughly 5 years), and a growth bucket (7+ year horizon). While intuitive, it embeds a market-timing problem: buckets must eventually be refilled, forcing investment decisions under duress. When both bonds and equities decline simultaneously, there is no safe bucket left to draw from without locking in losses.

### The Hidden Assumptions That Cause Collapse [00:04:12]

The strategy tries to serve as both a spending plan and an investment strategy but succeeds at neither. Without explicit rebalancing rules, retirees face unanswered judgment calls — when to refill from which bucket, and whether to violate target allocations. Reactive behavior replaces structured management, and the illusion of control substitutes for a real financial system.

### The Four-Layer Guardrail Framework [00:08:29]

Layer 1 (Sleeves) establishes broad asset-class allocations. Layer 2 (Weights) assigns target percentages to each sleeve and individual holding. Layer 3 (Triggers) sets tolerance bands — for example, 20% bands around each weight — so rebalancing is initiated automatically when any holding drifts outside its band, removing emotional decision-making. Layer 4 (Rebalancing Frequency) mandates a predetermined review schedule to check whether triggers have fired.

### Pairing the Investment Framework with a Spending System [00:13:37]

The four-layer investment framework is designed to be paired with a risk-based modern guardrails spending plan, which dynamically adjusts spending based on portfolio performance without abandoning the overall retirement plan. Together, the two systems give retirees both investment discipline and spending confidence. A cash sleeve within the framework can provide the psychological comfort of a 'bucket' without the execution pitfalls of a standalone bucket strategy.
