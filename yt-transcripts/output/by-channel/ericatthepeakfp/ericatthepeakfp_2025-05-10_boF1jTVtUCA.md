# Why you WON'T Retire - Even Though You Know You COULD Retire...

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-05-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=boF1jTVtUCA
**Duration:** 14:04

## Summary

This video examines why financially prepared retirees fail to actually retire, with Eric Amzalag identifying three core psychological and systemic barriers. He critiques Monte Carlo probability-of-success scores as non-actionable tools that benefit financial services companies rather than retirees, citing research by Derek Tharp showing that a 95% success score still requires frequent downward spending adjustments. Amzalag then addresses the behavioral trap of chasing savings accumulation past the point of necessity, and the paralysis caused by seeking a perfect retirement plan before making any change.

## Key Takeaways

- Monte Carlo scores tell retirees if they might succeed but provide no actionable roadmap for how to adjust when conditions change.
- Research shows that a 95% probability-of-success plan historically required downward spending adjustments in over 96% of scenarios.
- The financial services industry profits from retirees working longer and not drawing down assets, creating a structural bias in planning tools.
- Excessive focus on accumulation creates a savings muscle that becomes psychologically difficult to reverse into a spending mode.
- Incremental retirement transitions—such as stopping contributions while still employed, then moving to part-time—are more achievable than abrupt all-or-nothing retirement.
- Dynamic strategies like modern guardrails replace static scores with actionable feedback loops.

## Detailed Notes

### Belief 1: Trusting Broken Planning Systems [00:00:39]

Amzalag argues that Monte Carlo probability-of-success scores are structurally designed to keep retirees working longer by providing a score rather than an action plan. He references Derek Tharp's research showing that a 95% success score does not guarantee high-quality outcomes in the 95% of passing scenarios.

### The Limitations of Monte Carlo Scores [00:03:14]

Tharp's research found that planning to a constant 95% probability of success would have required downward spending adjustments in over 96% of historical scenarios, with 15% of cases requiring cuts of 35% or more. Amzalag compares the score to a momentary weight reading—it only reflects status at a single instant and cannot guide ongoing decisions.

### Belief 2: Chasing the High Score [00:08:05]

The accumulation mindset creates a virtuous cycle of saving that becomes an end in itself rather than a means to retirement enjoyment. Studies cited link prioritizing extrinsic financial goals with lower well-being, higher anxiety, and lower life satisfaction, while obsessive wealth chasing causes health and relationship costs that outweigh financial gains.

### Belief 3: Pursuit of Perfection [00:10:45]

Many retirees wait for a perfect plan or perfect moment before changing their work status, resulting in indefinite deferral. Amzalag advocates for incremental steps such as using all vacation days, halting new retirement contributions, or transitioning to part-time employment as confidence-building exercises on the path to full retirement.
