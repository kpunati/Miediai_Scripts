# Once I Show Early Retirees This, Healthcare Becomes EASY

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-10-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=JeqgnJ798L0
**Duration:** 16:00

## Summary

Eric Amzalag argues that healthcare in early retirement is not inherently complicated — it is governed by one number, modified adjusted gross income (MAGI), which can be actively managed through strategic account withdrawal sequencing. Using a detailed example of a 60-year-old California couple spending $120,000 per year, the video shows how blending withdrawals from Roth, cash/after-tax, and limited pre-tax accounts can reduce MAGI to $50,000, qualify the couple for over $1,600 in monthly ACA subsidies, and slash healthcare costs from $23,400 to near zero annually. The video also explains the ACA subsidy cliff at $84,600, the impact of claiming Social Security timing on MAGI, and three mistakes that commonly destroy the strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- ACA health insurance premiums are based entirely on MAGI, not net worth, portfolio size, or total spending, which means a retiree spending $120,000 per year can still qualify for substantial subsidies.
- Withdrawals from Roth IRAs, return of principal from after-tax brokerage accounts, and cash spending do not count toward MAGI, enabling significant lifestyle spending without triggering higher premium costs.
- The ACA subsidy cliff for a married couple in 2026 is approximately $84,600 in MAGI — crossing this threshold eliminates all subsidies with no gradual phase-out, potentially costing $19,000–$23,000 per year.
- High-income retirees spending $200,000–$300,000 per year face the same unsubsidized premium as those with $85,000 in MAGI, so healthcare above the cliff is still a manageable single-digit percentage of large budgets.
- The best time to optimize MAGI is the retirement gap — between leaving work and claiming Social Security or starting a pension — when income is most controllable.
- Claiming Social Security too early adds $20,000–$30,000 in MAGI per year, potentially pushing a retiree over the subsidy cliff and eliminating thousands in annual subsidies.

## Detailed Notes

### The One Number: MAGI and How It Works [00:01:35]

MAGI is the IRS-defined income figure used to calculate ACA premium tax credits. It includes traditional IRA/401(k) withdrawals, taxable interest and dividends, capital gains, wages, and taxable Social Security, but excludes Roth IRA withdrawals, return of principal from brokerage accounts, and other after-tax spending. The insight is that spending and MAGI can diverge significantly with the right account mix.

### Worked Example: $120,000 Spending, $50,000 MAGI [00:03:27]

A California couple, age 60, blends $30,000 from a pre-tax account, $20,000 from Roth, $50,000 from after-tax cash/principal, and $20,000 from long-term gains, producing $120,000 of spending but only $50,000 of MAGI. At 236% of the federal poverty level, they qualify for an estimated $1,619/month premium tax credit, reducing a bronze plan to $0/month and a silver plan to roughly $330/month versus $1,950 unsubsidized.

### The ACA Subsidy Cliff [00:07:29]

ACA subsidies function as an all-or-nothing switch at 400% of the federal poverty level, approximately $84,600 for a married couple in 2026. One dollar over the threshold eliminates the entire subsidy, making this cliff one of the most consequential tax thresholds in retirement planning. Unsubsidized premiums are the same at $85,000 MAGI as at $500,000 MAGI.

### Three Common Mistakes and Implementation Steps [00:10:20]

Mistake 1: Having all assets in pre-tax accounts eliminates MAGI flexibility entirely. Mistake 2: Claiming Social Security too early adds $20,000–$30,000 in annual MAGI and may push income over the subsidy cliff. Mistake 3: Assuming premiums and subsidy calculations are uniform across states — Medicaid expansion, benchmark premiums, and plan availability vary by state and zip code. The strategy requires a mix of pre-tax and after-tax accounts built before retirement.
