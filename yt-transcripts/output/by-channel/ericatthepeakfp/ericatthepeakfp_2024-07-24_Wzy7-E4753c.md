# Peak FP Podcast: Why Your Retirement Investment’s are At Risk

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-07-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Wzy7-E4753c
**Duration:** 15:48

## Summary

Eric Amzalag walks through sequence-of-returns risk as a practical concern for near-retirees and early retirees, defining it as the combination of the nonlinear relationship between market losses and required recoveries and the compounding damage that distributions add to that recovery challenge. He criticizes retirement planning tools that use straight-line average returns as misleading, then presents a three-part defense strategy: first, comprehending the mechanics; second, stress-testing plans with multiple return sequences; and third, building a bottom-up investment strategy that starts with withdrawal rules (take from growth in up markets, take from cash in down markets), then constructs an asset allocation to support those rules, and finally selects specific investments — emphasizing appropriate cash instruments and diversification beyond broad index funds.

## Key Takeaways

- Sequence-of-returns risk is most damaging in the five years before or after retirement, when portfolio withdrawals interact with market declines exponentially.
- The loss recovery rule is nonlinear: larger losses require disproportionately larger gains, and the time to recovery grows quickly with loss size.
- Distributions during a downturn compound the recovery requirement; a 4% distribution on top of a 30% loss requires 8.6% extra growth just to cover the distribution.
- Monte Carlo tools using straight-line average returns are structurally misleading because actual return sequences vary widely from the average.
- The correct planning order is withdrawal strategy first, then asset allocation, then specific investment selection — not the reverse.
- Intermediate and long-term bonds behaved like risk assets in 2022, illustrating why cash-like investments must truly be low-volatility instruments.
- Greater diversification (more positions, specific funds rather than broad index funds only) enables investment-specific pruning during downturns, providing an additional hedge.

## Detailed Notes

### Layer 1: The Unequal Loss-Recovery Relationship [00:00:00]

When a portfolio loses value, the recovery percentage is calculated from the new, lower base. A $1M portfolio down 10% to $900K needs $100K of growth, which is 11.11% of $900K. This nonlinear relationship worsens at larger losses: 20% down requires 25% to recover, 30% down requires 43%, 50% down requires 100%.

### Layer 2: Distributions Compound the Damage [00:03:08]

Adding a 4% annual distribution to a market loss dramatically extends the recovery requirement. A 10% loss plus 4% distribution requires a 14.9% gain to recover; a 20% loss plus 4% requires 28.6%. The relationship is exponential, not linear, and this is the core mechanism of sequence-of-returns risk in practice.

### Proper Retirement Planning: Stress Testing Beyond Monte Carlo [00:06:15]

Monte Carlo tools default to straight-line average returns, which never occur in reality. Retirees should stress-test financial plans with multiple custom return sequences including historical bad periods. They should also model multiple spending strategies and withdrawal behaviors, not just fixed withdrawal amounts.

### Bottom-Up Investment Strategy: Withdrawal Rules First [00:07:17]

The recommended approach starts with a simple withdrawal rule: withdraw from portfolio growth in up markets to preserve cash; withdraw from cash in down markets to allow declined investments to recover. An asset allocation is then constructed to support this rule, requiring careful sizing of the cash and cash-like bucket based on individual spending needs and wealth level.

### Investment Selection: Cash Instruments and Diversification [00:10:24]

The 2022 bond market failure demonstrated that intermediate and long-term bonds do not behave as cash. The cash bucket must contain genuinely low-volatility instruments. Broader diversification — more positions, mixing funds with individual stocks — enables investment-specific pruning: selling positions that held value or gained while declining positions recover, something impossible with broad index funds like VTI.
