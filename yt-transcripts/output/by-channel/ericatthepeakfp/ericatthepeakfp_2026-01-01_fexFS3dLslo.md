# Copy This Social Security Hack & Never Worry About Tax Again

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-01-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=fexFS3dLslo
**Duration:** 14:02

## Summary

Eric Amzalag builds a case against the conventional wisdom of delaying Social Security to 70 in order to do early Roth conversions. He demonstrates through a detailed case study that most retirees deplete their after-tax savings bucket in year one of retirement, leaving them reliant on pre-tax IRA withdrawals for all spending—consuming their Roth conversion room and forcing conversions at higher brackets. By contrast, early Social Security claiming provides tax-efficient income (only 85% taxable) that reduces required IRA withdrawals, preserves after-tax reserves longer, and opens more Roth conversion capacity each year.

## Key Takeaways

- The conventional advice to delay Social Security and convert Roth early assumes a large after-tax savings bucket that most retirees do not have.
- Social Security income is only 85% taxable compared to 100% for pre-tax IRA withdrawals, making it more tax-efficient for covering spending needs.
- Receiving Social Security at 65 rather than 70 extended the after-tax savings bucket's longevity and increased Roth conversion space across each year.
- In the case study, claiming Social Security at 65 resulted in $122,000 more in legacy value compared to delaying to 70 despite giving up $21,500 per year in higher benefits.
- Roth conversion strategy cannot be designed in isolation from the spending strategy—where spending money comes from determines how much room is left for conversions.
- This strategy works best for couples with the majority of assets in pre-tax accounts and modest after-tax savings; those with large pensions or high benefits may still benefit from delay.

## Detailed Notes

### The Conventional Wisdom and Its Hidden Assumption [00:00:00]

The standard advice—delay Social Security to 70 and perform Roth conversions in the low-income window from 65 to 70—implicitly assumes retirees have enough after-tax savings to fund living expenses during that conversion window. Amzalag argues this assumption fails for the majority of retirees who have 95% of their assets in pre-tax accounts.

### The Case Study: Delaying Social Security [00:03:38]

A couple retires at 65 with $1.5 million pre-tax and $100,000 after-tax, needing $120,000 net per year. Delaying to 70 yields $71,500 in combined benefits but burns through the $100,000 after-tax bucket in year one. For years 2 through 5, all spending comes from pre-tax accounts, leaving only about $12,000 in Roth conversion space per year after accounting for spending withdrawals.

### The Case Study: Claiming Social Security Early [00:07:36]

Claiming at 65 provides $50,000 of non-portfolio income, reducing IRA withdrawals needed for spending. Because Social Security is only 85% taxable, each dollar of Social Security income displaces more taxable income than an equivalent IRA withdrawal. The after-tax bucket lasts longer, and Roth conversion space per year increases substantially. Over the retirement, this adds up to $42,000 more in tax savings and $122,000 more in legacy value compared to delaying.

### Caveats and When Delay Still Makes Sense [00:12:42]

Amzalag acknowledges the strategy is not universal. Singles with large pensions and high Social Security benefits who face less favorable tax brackets may still benefit from delay. The key principle is that Roth conversions must be integrated with the spending strategy rather than planned in isolation from it.
