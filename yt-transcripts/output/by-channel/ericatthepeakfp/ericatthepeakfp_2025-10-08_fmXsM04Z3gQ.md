# Give Me 82 Seconds, You’ll Avoid Roth Conversions Forever

## Summary

Eric Amselog delivers a concise critique of Roth conversion recommendations in a 2-minute format, identifying three major flaws in how financial planning tools handle conversions. The upfront tax bill increases early retirement expenses and withdrawal rates, amplifying sequence of returns risk when retirees are most vulnerable. Software's unrealistic longevity assumptions (requiring 30+ years to break even) don't match actual lifespans. And conversions are treated as guaranteed when they are actually risky investments. The solution is to reverse the decision-making sequence: determine safe spending first, then convert only if it genuinely increases lifetime spending capacity or reduces risk—which most plans do not achieve.

## Key Takeaways

- Three mistakes plague financial planning tools: ignoring the upfront tax bill that increases early expenses, assuming 30+ year horizons that most retirees won't achieve, and treating conversions as guarantees rather than risky investments.
- Upfront Roth conversion taxes increase withdrawal rates and amplify sequence of returns risk in the first 5 years of retirement when portfolio value is most critical to long-term success.
- Most couples will not live long enough for Roth conversions to overcome the upfront tax cost, making the recommendation a net negative despite appearing beneficial on software projections.
- Tax-free growth does not mean risk-free growth; Roth conversions are still subject to market risk and do not guarantee outperformance over traditional accounts.
- The correct framework reverses typical advice: start with safe spending capacity, then convert only if it demonstrably increases lifetime spending or reduces risk.

## Detailed Notes

### The Three Critical Mistakes in Roth Conversion Recommendations [00:00:00]

Financial planning tools ignore the upfront tax bill, which jacks up expenses in the first 5 years, raises withdrawal rates, and amplifies sequence of returns risk when portfolios are most vulnerable. They assume unrealistic longevity (30+ years needed to recover for many couples), and treat conversions as guaranteed when they are actually investment gambles with real underperformance risk.

### Upfront Tax Impact on Early Retirement Risk [00:00:40]

The upfront tax bill from conversions increases expenses and withdrawal rates in early retirement, raising sequence of returns risk precisely when one bad market year can derail the entire plan. This front-loading of costs for benefits that may never materialize is particularly damaging in the first 5 years.

### Longevity Reality vs. Software Assumptions [00:00:40]

Most couples need at least three decades for a Roth conversion to recover the upfront taxes. Software assumes 95+ year lifespans, but research shows only a small percentage of 65-year-olds live that long, making conversions statistically unlikely to break even for the majority.

### The Correct Decision Framework: Spending First [00:01:42]

Reverse the typical recommendation sequence: first determine sustainable spending, then reverse engineer taxes and withdrawals. Convert only if it demonstrably increases lifetime spending capacity or reduces plan risk. Most Roth conversion recommendations fail this test, making them net negative for retirees despite appearing beneficial on software outputs.
