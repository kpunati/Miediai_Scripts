# If You're THIS Retiree, You Will Die Too Rich

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-11-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=g10xZy-eFLc
**Duration:** 17:54

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP at Peak Financial Planning, presents a pointed critique of industry-standard retirement spending guidance, arguing that fear-based approaches like the 4% rule keep retirees underspending because advisors profit from larger assets under management. He cites multiple research findings showing the average retiree withdraws only 2.1% annually and that the 4% rule leaves most retirees with multiples of their starting wealth. He then introduces risk-based guardrail planning (Income Lab software) as an alternative that gives retirees a dynamic, personalized spending framework with explicit trigger points and adjustment mechanisms—allowing higher spending while maintaining safety. A detailed client example shows over $650,000 in foregone life experiences while the couple still would have left $800,000+ in estate value.

## Key Takeaways

- Average retirees withdraw only 2.1% (married) or 1.9% (single) of assets annually—roughly half of the 4% safe withdrawal rate—because of fear-based financial guidance.
- The 4% rule results in retirees dying with double or more starting wealth in over two-thirds of historical scenarios, meaning most conservative guidance is excessively conservative.
- Financial advisors have a fee-based incentive to keep clients in fear and minimize spending, since lower withdrawals preserve assets under management and the associated fees.
- Risk-based guardrail planning tools like Income Labs provide a dynamic system with explicit spending adjustment triggers, guardrail widths, and probability of spending adjustment—replacing static rules.
- Clients can control three key parameters: probability of a spending adjustment, the size of that adjustment, and the guardrail width that triggers it.
- In 93% of historical Monte Carlo scenarios, a client couple using guardrails would have received spending increases rather than cuts, demonstrating a bias toward living better than expected.

## Detailed Notes

### The Underspending Problem: Research Evidence [00:00:55]

Research by Blanchett and Finke shows married retirees withdraw only 2.1% and single retirees only 1.9% annually—about half the widely cited 4% safe withdrawal rate. Kitces research shows that using the 4% rule, retirees end 30-year retirements with more than double starting wealth in over two-thirds of scenarios, and five times starting wealth in 20% of scenarios. A Financial Planning Association study of high-wealth retirees found a consumption gap as high as 47% even after setting aside 40% for healthcare, longevity, and gifting.

### The Industry Incentive Problem [00:00:55]

Amzalag argues advisors and financial services firms profit from keeping clients fearful and not spending, since portfolio balances under management determine AUM-based fees. Only 1% of advisors use risk-based guardrail tools like Income Labs, per Kitces' advisor software survey. JP Morgan, despite managing billions in assets, has itself acknowledged that static withdrawal rules cause dramatic underspending and called for more dynamic approaches.

### How Guardrail Planning Works: The Three Sliders [00:09:21]

Income Labs uses three adjustable parameters: (1) probability of spending adjustment—the likelihood of needing a spending change, set at 25% for the case study couple; (2) spending adjustment size—how much to cut if the lower guardrail is triggered, set at 5%; and (3) guardrail width—how much the portfolio must decline before triggering an adjustment, set at 22%. This dynamic system tells retirees exactly what to do when markets fall rather than relying on hope or permanent conservative cuts.

### Client Case Study: Steve and Amanda [00:05:31]

Steve and Amanda, both age 65, had $1.2M saved and were told to withdraw $48,000/year (4% rule). They were actually withdrawing only $35,000 due to fear. Income Labs showed they could safely withdraw $118,000/year for the first two years before Social Security, then maintain $122,000/year total income after Social Security (Steve at $3,400/month, Amanda at $2,100/month at FRA). In 93% of Monte Carlo scenarios they would receive spending increases over their retirement. The fear-based approach would have cost over $650,000 in foregone spending while still leaving $800,000+ in median end-of-life assets.
