# I'm 61 With $1.03M, How Much Can I Spend in Retirement?

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-04-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cm-Ivf8ZXrA

## Summary

This video follows a case study of Steve and Amanda Doe, a couple aged 61 with approximately $1.03M in savings and $6,300/month in guaranteed income, who want to spend $100,000/year in retirement. Eric Amzalag, CFP, compares two planning methods: a traditional Monte Carlo plan (Right Capital) producing a 9% probability of success and a dynamic guardrails plan (Income Labs) producing 56% for the same inputs, explaining why the difference occurs. The video then walks through four incremental scenarios — spending cuts, wiggle-room gifting, part-time work, and a combined approach — showing how small behavioral changes can unlock retirement confidence. The conclusion argues that modern guardrails plans better serve retirees because they specify when and how much to adjust spending, unlike static Monte Carlo outputs.

## Key Takeaways

- Monte Carlo probability scores measure the likelihood of needing any spending adjustment, not the probability of running out of money, and are often misinterpreted.
- Income Labs guardrails planning incorporates dynamic spending, age-based patterns (retirement spending smile), and tells retirees the maximum they can spend, yielding more actionable and higher success scores.
- Small behavioral tweaks — reduced spending, conditional gifting, part-time income — can meaningfully shift a retirement plan's viability.
- The lower guardrail depth (the portfolio decline that triggers a required spending cut) is driven primarily by total starting portfolio value and does not change across spending scenarios.
- Guardrails plans tell retirees precisely when a spending reduction will be triggered and by how much, allowing them to track portfolio value in real time and avoid surprises.
- Part-time work creating new income sources has the largest impact on spending speed limit in dynamic retirement plans because it directly reduces portfolio withdrawal pressure.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Study Setup: Steve and Amanda Doe [00:00:47]

Steve and Amanda are 61 with $730,000 in pre-tax accounts, $320,000 in after-tax accounts, a paid-off home worth $417,000, two small annuities, and projected Social Security totaling $6,300/month. They want to spend $100,000/year including $6,000 travel and $4,000 gifting budgets.

### Monte Carlo vs. Guardrails: Why the Scores Differ [00:02:32]

Right Capital returned a 9% probability of success for the couple's $8,300/month spending desire, using inflation-adjusted straight-line spending. Income Labs returned 56% for the same inputs because it calculates the maximum safe spending, applies an age-based retirement spending smile, and defaults to a dynamic spending strategy that mirrors real human behavior.

### Four Incremental Scenarios to Improve Retirement Readiness [00:09:32]

Scenario 1 trims $3,000/year baseline spending. Scenario 2 shifts gifting to a wiggle-room approach (gifts come from year-end unspent surplus). Scenario 3 adds $20,000/year of part-time income for 6 years. Scenario 4 combines all three, which Steve and Amanda found acceptable — the part-time work scenario produced the largest improvement in spending speed limit because it added new income sources.

### Key Metrics: Spending Speed Limit, Guardrail Depth, and Wiggle Room [00:10:35]

Four metrics are tracked per scenario: the spending speed limit (max annual spend), percent of modeled scenarios allowing a spending increase, the lower guardrail depth (how much portfolio decline is absorbed before a required cut), and cash flow wiggle room. Only the lower guardrail depth stays constant across all scenarios because it is determined by total starting portfolio value, not spending level.

### Conclusion: What Modern Guardrails Plans Provide [00:14:07]

Steve and Amanda chose the combined scenario, accepting modest trade-offs to retire at 62 with confidence. Guardrails plans distinguish themselves by specifying exactly when and by how much spending should change if portfolio values decline, enabling proactive tracking rather than reactive re-planning.
