# Retirement Expert Explains: Why Bonds Are Riskier Than Stocks

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-08-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FOzWbKougoA
**Duration:** 16:34

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents backtested portfolio data to challenge four widely held retirement investing conventions. Using Portfolio Visualizer, he shows that directly held stocks with a quarterly rebalancing mechanism can match the risk profile of a mutual-funds-only portfolio while outperforming by approximately 3% annually. He argues that bonds are not universally 'safe'—TLT fell nearly 41% in 2022 while consumer staples stocks were positive—and proposes interest rate sensitivity as a more useful risk classification than the traditional stock/bond binary. He also shows that global diversification has provided little actual hedging benefit versus US-only portfolios over the past 22 years.

## Key Takeaways

- Directly held stocks with a trimming/rebalancing mechanism can match or beat the risk profile of a funds-only portfolio while generating ~3% more annual return.
- Tolerance band rebalancing (e.g., 2% absolute bands) reduces maximum drawdown and volatility in directly held portfolios, offsetting their inherent concentration risk.
- TLT (long-duration government bonds) fell nearly 41% in 2022 while value stocks like Kroger rose 44%, exposing the flaw in placing all bonds on the 'safe' side of asset allocation.
- Interest rate sensitivity—not the stock vs. bond label—is a more accurate risk classification; long-duration bonds and growth stocks share interest rate sensitivity.
- Global diversification failed to hedge US market downturns over the past 22 years; US, developed ex-US, and emerging market funds all declined simultaneously during every major correction.
- VTSAX outperformed both VGTSX and VEXMX on return and risk measures over a 22-year period, suggesting US-only exposure was superior to global diversification in this backtest.

## Detailed Notes

### Myth 1: ETFs/Mutual Funds Are Always Superior to Directly Held Stocks [00:00:00]

Using Portfolio Visualizer backtests from January 2013 to August 2024, Amzalag compares a Vanguard mutual funds-only 60/40 portfolio against a 60/40 portfolio with 24 directly held stocks plus index funds. With quarterly rebalancing applied, the directly held portfolio achieved ~10.8% annualized return versus ~9.2% for the funds-only portfolio, with nearly identical standard deviation (~9.8% vs ~11.5%) and maximum drawdown (~20.1% vs ~23%).

### Myth 2: Active Strategies Are Inferior to Passive Buy-and-Hold [00:05:12]

Applying 2% absolute tolerance band rebalancing to the directly held portfolio dramatically reduces volatility (standard deviation from 20% to 10.1%, drawdown from 42.3% to 20.7%) at the cost of return reduction (20.2% to 11.2%). The combined active+directly held approach ends with ~$3.42 million versus ~$2.42 million on a $1 million starting balance, representing 244% cumulative return versus 143%.

### Myth 3: Bonds Are Safe, Stocks Are Risky [00:10:24]

VBTLX holds 20% in mortgage-backed securities, 25% in corporate bonds, and 26% in A/BBB-rated instruments. TLT fell 41% in 2022 while Kroger rose 44% and Pepsi rose 6.77%. Amzalag argues interest rate sensitivity is a better risk axis: long-duration bonds and growth stocks are both interest rate sensitive, while value/dividend stocks behave more like defensive assets.

### Myth 4: Global Diversification Provides Meaningful Hedging [00:13:31]

A 22-year chart of VGTSX, VEXMX, and VTSAX shows all three declined simultaneously during every major market correction, providing no inverse hedging benefit. VTSAX delivered the highest return with the lowest risk metrics of the three, suggesting that for US investors, global diversification added risk without a compensating return benefit in this historical period.
