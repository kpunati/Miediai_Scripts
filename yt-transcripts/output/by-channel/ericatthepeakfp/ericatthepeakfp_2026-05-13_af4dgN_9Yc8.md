# Vanguard Says You Need THIS Much To Retire — Here’s Why They’re Wrong

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=af4dgN_9Yc8
**Duration:** 13:37

## Summary

The video challenges Vanguard's widely-cited income replacement ratio framework for retirement planning, which the host argues steers people toward unrealistically large portfolio targets. Using a 62-year-old couple with $2.5 million saved as a case study, it demonstrates that a spending-first, guardrails-based model produces a retirement date 9.5 years earlier than the replacement ratio approach. Key variables that income replacement ignores include actual spending versus income, temporary expenses, Social Security timing, tax account location, and a retiree's willingness to make modest adjustments.

## Key Takeaways

- Vanguard's 75-85% income replacement ratio can imply portfolio requirements of $6 million for high-income earners, far exceeding what most households actually need.
- Income replacement ratios ignore actual spending, temporary expenses like college costs, and exact Social Security amounts.
- A spending-first approach using real expense data results in retirement targets that are dramatically lower than replacement ratio calculations.
- Tax account location matters enormously; the same $2.5 million split differently between pre-tax and post-tax can determine whether a couple can retire now or years from now.
- A willingness to make even a modest 5% spending reduction if guardrails are triggered can materially reduce the required portfolio size.
- Retirement is funded by multiple 'slices' — portfolio withdrawals, Social Security, pensions, rental income — not one giant number.
- Broad research should not make household-specific retirement decisions; personal spending, tax situation, and flexibility need to be individually modeled.

## Detailed Notes

### Problems with Vanguard's Income Replacement Framework [00:00:00]

Vanguard's research recommends replacing 75-85% of pre-retirement income, which for a $300,000 earner implies needing $237,000/year in retirement income and a nearly $6 million portfolio under the 4% rule. The host argues this methodology ignores the difference between income and actual spending, causing people to chase an inflated target and delay retirement unnecessarily.

### Case Study: Spending-First vs. Replacement Ratio [00:02:54]

A 62-year-old couple with $2.5M saved, $179,000 actual spending ($40,000 of which will end in 4 years when their child finishes college), and projected $52,000 Social Security at FRA. The replacement ratio method points them to $6M — 11 years away. Running through Income Lab's spending-first model with guardrails, the couple is only 17 months away from retirement, with a 28% lower guardrail and 68% probability of never running out of money.

### The Retirement Withdrawal Pie Chart [00:07:41]

Rather than asking what percentage of income to replace, the host recommends mapping out the actual sources that will fund retirement: portfolio withdrawals (adjusted for taxes and account type), Social Security, pensions, and other income. Temporary expenses must be excluded from permanent spending needs. Portfolio account mix — pre-tax, Roth, taxable — significantly impacts both taxes owed and the sustainability of withdrawals.

### Flexibility and Guardrails [00:10:45]

Real retirement spending is not a straight line; early years are typically higher due to travel and activity, middle years dip, and late years rise with healthcare costs. Planning should model this curve, not assume flat spending. A key variable is flexibility: a willingness to reduce spending by 5% if the lower guardrail is triggered gives the plan significant additional resilience and can allow earlier retirement.
