# You Need THIS MUCH To Retire & Spend $12k/$10k/$8k A Month

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents a six-step systematic process for determining what a person can sustainably spend in retirement and which actions most efficiently close spending gaps. Using a detailed case study of a 58-year-old couple with $1.2 million and a $10,000 monthly spending goal, he demonstrates through live Income Labs guardrail software testing that asset location — where money is saved across pre-tax versus taxable accounts — has roughly 10 times the spending impact of Social Security timing optimization. The framework categorizes retirement levers into behavioral, asset-location, and investment tiers, and argues that obsessing over investment allocation (tier three) while ignoring tier-one and tier-two levers is a common and costly mistake. The final lesson is that combining multiple levers in a sequenced execution plan, with built-in guardrail contingencies, can close large spending gaps without requiring years of extra work.

## Key Takeaways

- The required portfolio income (annual spending goal minus guaranteed income) is the single most important number in a retirement plan because it reveals true portfolio dependency before Social Security offsets it.
- Guardrail software stress-tests spending capacity against sequence-of-returns risk, inflation, and taxes — and often reveals that portfolios labeled adequate are actually severely underfunded.
- Asset location (pre-tax vs. taxable vs. Roth) is a tier-two lever that can improve spending capacity by $950/month in some cases, far outpacing the $90/month gain from optimal Social Security timing.
- Changing portfolio allocation from 60/40 to 75/25 improved spending capacity by only $300/month and left overspending risk unchanged at 49%, illustrating the limited marginal benefit of investment risk-taking for underfunded plans.
- Combining multiple levers — asset location, savings rate, and working six additional months — allowed the case study couple to achieve their full $10,000/month goal with 31% overspending risk.
- A built-in guardrail contingency (a 5% temporary spending cut if portfolio drops 27%) allows retirees to spend confidently without relying on markets cooperating.

## Detailed Notes

### Step 1-2: Required Portfolio Income and Funding Status [00:00:36]

The required portfolio income (RPI) formula subtracts guaranteed income from annual spending goals. A $1.2 million portfolio supporting $120,000 in annual spending requires 10% annually before Social Security starts, not 5% as surface math suggests. Running numbers through Income Labs revealed the couple was severely underfunded at 49% overspending risk, with a spending capacity of only $8,640 per month against their $10,000 goal.

### Step 3: The Three Tiers of Retirement Levers [00:04:14]

Behavioral levers (retirement date, savings rate, Social Security timing) are tier one and offer full control. Asset location — splitting savings across pre-tax, Roth, and taxable brokerage — is tier two and highly impactful. Investment allocation is tier three and is mostly outside a retiree's control, with modest impact on spending capacity. Most people focus on tier three while ignoring tiers one and two.

### Step 4: Testing Each Lever's Impact [00:07:03]

Social Security optimization added $90/month and cut overspending risk by 3 percentage points. Doubling savings from $1,100 to $2,200/month for 5 years added $270/month and cut risk by 4 points. Shifting to a 50/50 pre-tax/taxable brokerage split added $950/month and cut risk by 7 points. Combining all three levers plus working 6 additional months achieved the full $10,000/month goal with 31% overspending risk.

### Steps 5-6: Execution Sequence and Guardrail Contingencies [00:15:08]

The execution plan prioritizes highest-impact levers first: shift savings toward taxable accounts, increase savings rate immediately, target a retirement date of 63.5, then finalize Social Security timing closer to retirement. The guardrail contingency specifies a 5% ($450/month) temporary spending cut if the portfolio drops 27% from its starting value, protecting the plan from 2008-style market crashes without permanently sacrificing retirement lifestyle.
