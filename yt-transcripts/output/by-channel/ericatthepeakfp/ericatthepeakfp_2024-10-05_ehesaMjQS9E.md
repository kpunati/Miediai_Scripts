# The Retirement Tax You Shouldn't Pay For But Still Do

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2024-10-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ehesaMjQS9E
**Duration:** 18:30

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents a detailed case study of a 59-year-old recently divorced retiree with $2.5 million concentrated in pre-tax accounts, who came in paralyzed between protecting ACA subsidies and addressing a looming RMD tax problem. Using a guardrails withdrawal framework, Amzalag shows she had $418,000 more in spending tolerance than her current plan utilized, which resolved both her ACA vs. Roth conversion dilemma and helped her feel genuinely retired. The client ultimately deferred Roth conversions for one year to test higher spending, took five bucket-list trips, and reported feeling wealthy for the first time.

## Key Takeaways

- Fear of taxes and subsidy loss can lead retirees to under-spend significantly, defeating the purpose of wealth accumulation.
- Guardrails-based withdrawal planning provides dynamic, data-driven spending boundaries that probability-of-success models cannot offer.
- Understanding portfolio spending tolerance first makes the ACA subsidy vs. Roth conversion trade-off far clearer and less paralyzing.
- Roth conversions increase withdrawal rates; clients must decide if current experiences or future tax savings matter more given their time horizon.
- The $418,000 gap between desired spending and maximum tolerable spending represents the practical budget for Roth conversions.
- Sequencing conversions dynamically over 10 years gives the client year-by-year flexibility to choose between conversions and cash gifts.
- Paying taxes in the future can be viewed as purchasing present-day flexibility and optionality, a valid philosophical trade-off.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Background and the Core Dilemma [00:01:01]

A 59-year-old recently divorced woman had $2.5 million, mostly in pre-tax accounts, with minimal Roth savings. She was living on $70,000 in withdrawals ($55,000 actual spending) to stay below four times the federal poverty limit for ACA subsidies. She felt like she hadn't actually retired and was paralyzed between her two fears: large future RMDs and the cost of private health insurance.

### Three Withdrawal Scenarios [00:04:06]

Amzalag presented three scenarios over 10 years: current plan (roughly $150,000 total withdrawals prioritizing ACA subsidies), want-to-withdraw (about $1.2 million supporting all discretionary desires), and able-to-withdraw (about $1.6 million, the portfolio's maximum tolerable withdrawal). The large gap between current and tolerable withdrawal made the client's constraint immediately visible.

### Guardrails Framework Explained [00:06:10]

The guardrails recommendation showed she could spend $1,762 per month initially. The lower guardrail triggered at a portfolio value of $1.768 million (a 30% decrease), requiring a 5% spending cut of roughly $8,800 per year. The upper guardrail triggered at $2.69 million (7% increase), allowing a $122,000 per year spending increase. As long as the portfolio stayed between these rails, she simply inflation-adjusted her year-one withdrawal.

### ACA Subsidies vs. Roth Conversions Trade-off [00:08:15]

Over five years from age 60 to 64, maintaining ACA subsidies yielded roughly $1,000 in annual financial benefit while constraining withdrawals to about $370,000 total. Guardrails showed she could withdraw approximately $770,000 over that same period, leaving $360,000 of unused capacity. With $418,000 in wiggle room above desired spending, absorbing the cost of private health insurance was clearly manageable.

### Roth Conversion Decision Framework [00:10:20]

Adding Roth conversions pushed withdrawal rates to 5.5-7.2%, versus 4-4.8% without conversions. The decision depends on whether paying tax now crowds out current experiences more than paying tax later would crowd out future spending. The client had $418,000 of Roth conversion capacity above her desired spending, closely matching the financial planning tool's optimal conversion recommendation. Amzalag recommended sequencing conversions over 10 years dynamically.

### Outcome and Philosophical Framework [00:15:30]

The client deferred conversions in year one, increased her spending, and took five bucket-list trips, reporting she finally felt wealthy. Amzalag frames the tax trade-off as a philosophical seesaw: paying taxes today costs current spending, while paying taxes later risks higher future rates. He positions paying future taxes as purchasing present-day flexibility, and emphasizes helping clients maximize present-day joy first, with predetermined contingency rules for adjustments.
