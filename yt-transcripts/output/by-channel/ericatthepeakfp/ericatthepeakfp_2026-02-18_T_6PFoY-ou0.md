# Once Retirees See This, They Stop Working

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, CFP, builds a full retirement plan live for a hypothetical couple—Mike (59) and Laura (61)—with $2.05 million in investable assets, nearly all in pre-tax accounts. The video covers how to assess whether a couple can retire immediately, how to structure Social Security claiming, and how to sequence withdrawals across four distinct tax phases to minimize lifetime taxes and maximize healthcare subsidies. The planning tool Income Labs drives the analysis, and the video explicitly shows the three-criteria retirement approval framework and a six-category contingency model.

## Key Takeaways

- Accurate expense entry—distinguishing baseline from variable and cliff/trickle expenses—is the single most impactful factor in plan quality.
- Retirement plan approval requires three green checks: spending capacity exceeds desired spending, lower guardrail above 24%, and probability of underspending above 65%.
- Mike and Laura's plan scores a 96% underspending probability, making them overfunded and good candidates for Roth conversion even at retirement.
- Retirement has four distinct tax phases (COBRA, ACA/pre-Medicare, Medicare-to-RMD, and post-RMD), each requiring its own withdrawal strategy.
- During COBRA, aggressive Roth conversions are optimal because there are no ACA or IRMAA income cliffs to avoid.
- Preserving the taxable brokerage account instead of depleting it in year one is critical to maintaining ACA subsidy eligibility for multiple years.
- Social Security claiming at 68 for both spouses offers only a $3,000 lifetime difference versus a 67/69 split, making simplicity the tiebreaker.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Study Setup and Data Entry Importance [00:00:00]

The video introduces Mike (59) and Laura (61), a Texas couple with $2.05M investable assets—$800K and $900K in separate pre-tax IRAs, $250K in a joint taxable brokerage, and a $100K inherited IRA subject to the 10-year rule. Eric emphasizes that 90% of retirement plans fail during data entry: accurate life expectancies (90 for men, 95 for women), properly classified expenses (baseline vs. variable), and payroll deductions that disappear at retirement are the most commonly missed items.

### Three-Pillar Framework and Retirement Approval Criteria [00:04:43]

Peak Financial Planning uses three pillars: foundational questions (can I retire and how much can I spend?), optimization questions (how do I minimize taxes?), and contingency questions (what could go wrong?). Three criteria are required to approve a retire-ASAP recommendation: spending capacity must exceed desired spending ($156K vs. $126K for Mike and Laura), the lower guardrail must exceed 24% (theirs is 29%, implying a $600K portfolio drop before spending cuts), and the probability of underspending must exceed 65% (Mike and Laura score 96%).

### Social Security Optimization [00:25:22]

Two objectives are tested: maximizing lifetime benefits and optimizing for the tax/withdrawal situation. Income Labs recommends claiming at 68/68 for lifetime benefit maximization and 67/69 for tax optimization. The lifetime benefit difference between the two strategies is only $3,000, making the simpler 68/68 approach preferable. Both strategies provide roughly the same 7-year window without Social Security, which is an ideal period for tax planning.

### Four-Phase Withdrawal Road Map [00:29:02]

Phase 1 (COBRA, ~12 months): No ACA or IRMAA cliffs; ideal for aggressive Roth conversions of ~$70K funded by spending surplus. Phase 2 (ACA marketplace, ages 60-65): Must keep MAGI below $84,600 to retain subsidies worth $15K-$20K/year; use a mix of taxable brokerage and small pre-tax withdrawals. Phase 3 (Medicare, ages 65-73): Focus on micro-Roth conversions staying below IRMAA brackets. Phase 4 (post-RMD, age 73+): Government-mandated distributions; effective planning in earlier phases shrinks the pre-tax balance subject to RMDs.

### Contingency Analysis [00:40:15]

Six lower-guardrail triggers are reviewed: sudden market crash, overspending, low-growth plus normal spending (most common), runaway inflation, unexpected major expenses, and incorrect plan assumptions. Mike and Laura's 29% lower guardrail and high underspending probability insulate them from most risks; their primary uncontrolled variable is market behavior. Spousal survivor income coverage exceeds 90% for both, meaning either spouse retaining full solo expenses is funded even after the loss of one Social Security benefit.
