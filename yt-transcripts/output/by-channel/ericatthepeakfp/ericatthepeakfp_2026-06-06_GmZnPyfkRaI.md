# I'm 55 With $1M+ Saved, How Can I Retire By 60?

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2026-06-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=GmZnPyfkRaI
**Duration:** 16:52

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, a CFP and RICP at Peak Financial Planning, presents a structured three-part retirement readiness process for people who are 55 and targeting retirement at 60. The first part establishes a 'foundational question': determining whether safe spending capacity at 60 already covers desired retirement spending, and using the gap (or surplus) to direct the next five years of financial decisions. The second part focuses on how to optimize account type, tax efficiency, and withdrawal sequence — particularly during the Medicare gap years from 60 to 65. The third part involves stress testing the plan against five scenarios (portfolio risk, early market correction, required rate of return, long-term care, and unexpected large expenses) to build the confidence needed to actually follow through on retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- The foundational question — what can the plan safely support versus what retirement actually needs to cost — determines whether the next five years should focus on saving more or optimizing what already exists.
- Four levers address a spending gap: saving more, reducing fixed expenses, lowering the retirement spending target, or working longer — with working longer often the most reliable option.
- The type of account (pre-tax, post-tax brokerage, or Roth) matters as much as the total balance, because account type determines taxable income and therefore ACA subsidy eligibility during the 60–65 bridge.
- Mapping out exactly which accounts fund each year from 60 to 64 prevents the mistake of depleting a post-tax account too quickly and losing tax control.
- Roth conversions during the Medicare gap can reduce future required minimum distributions and protect a surviving spouse from the widow/widower single-filer bracket jump.
- Stress testing against five scenarios — portfolio risk, early correction, required return, long-term care, and a large unexpected expense — transforms a projection into a plan that can be trusted in real retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Establishing Spending Capacity vs. Desired Spending [00:00:00]

The foundational question is what a plan can safely support in spending if retirement begins at 60 with no behavioral changes. Amzalag recommends using dynamic withdrawal planning tools (Boldin or Income Labs) to generate a realistic safe spending number, then comparing it to a carefully constructed desired retirement budget that adds travel and healthcare costs while subtracting job-related expenses, mortgage payments, and payroll taxes. The gap — or absence of a gap — determines the entire strategy for the next five years.

### Four Levers to Close a Spending Gap [00:03:41]

If spending capacity falls short of desired spending, the video outlines four levers: saving more (ideally into the account type that best addresses whether the gap is an asset problem, an account problem, or a tax problem), reducing fixed expenses before retirement, adjusting the retirement spending target (with caution that 'spending less' must reflect a lifestyle the future self can actually sustain), and working longer. Working longer is highlighted as offering five simultaneous compounding benefits and is often the most reliable lever.

### Account Optimization and Withdrawal Sequencing (Ages 60–65) [00:07:40]

For those whose plan already works at 60, the five-year runway should focus on building tax-efficient 'bridge' assets for the Medicare gap years. Pre-tax withdrawals generate taxable income that can eliminate ACA premium subsidies, making after-tax or Roth dollars more valuable during ages 60–64. Amzalag recommends explicitly mapping which accounts fund ages 61, 62, 63, and 64, and calculating whether specific accounts will last through the full bridge period before Medicare eligibility begins.

### Five Stress Tests Before Committing to Age 60 [00:11:34]

The five tests are: (1) portfolio risk — does the allocation require more risk tolerance than the retiree can sustain through withdrawals? (2) early market correction — how large would a spending adjustment need to be if markets fall in the first five years? (3) required rate of return — does the lifestyle require an aggressive return just to keep the plan alive? (4) long-term care — how does a care event affect taxes, withdrawals, and the surviving spouse? (5) large unexpected expense — does the plan show a clear source of funds for a $25,000–$200,000 shock? Passing these tests converts a projection into a trustworthy plan.
