# I am 62 with $1.6M. What Role Will My Social Security Income Play?!

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-02-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=DJjs5BX4NZM
**Duration:** 16:44

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents a real client case study of a 62- and 59-year-old couple with $1.6M in savings—roughly 70% pretax—who were paralyzed by Social Security claiming complexity and could not pull the trigger on the husband's retirement. Using a modern guardrails approach with an 80% confidence threshold and 5% spending adjustment tolerance, three scenarios were modeled: both delay to 70 (most aggressive), one delays and one claims early at 62 (moderate), and only one retires now while the other works to 62 (least aggressive). All three scenarios proved viable; the least aggressive scenario achieved the highest Monte Carlo success rate (94%), and the exercise gave the couple a framework rather than a fixed commitment, allowing John to retire immediately.

## Key Takeaways

- Couples with ample savings are often overfunded but lack confidence because Social Security complexity creates analysis paralysis rather than actual financial risk.
- Testing three scenario extremes—most aggressive, moderate, least aggressive—creates a guardrail range so retirees can see exactly how much margin for error they have.
- Delaying both partners' Social Security to 70 requires the highest early withdrawal rates but results in the lowest portfolio withdrawal rates after age 74 (as low as 1.6% per year).
- The least aggressive scenario (one partner works until 62) achieved a 94% historical Monte Carlo pass rate, outperforming the more aggressive delay-everything scenario.
- Social Security can function as a triaging mechanism—a reserve of guaranteed income to activate if markets underperform or cost of living rises unexpectedly.
- Retirees do not need to commit to a specific claiming strategy before retiring; a flexible framework with multiple decision points is sufficient to proceed with confidence.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Situation and the Confidence Problem [00:01:02]

John (62) and Jane (59) held $1.6M, roughly 70% in pretax accounts. John had health concerns and wanted to retire immediately; Jane planned to retire at 64 but was open to retiring with John. Their full-retirement-age Social Security benefits were $3,100 and $2,500 respectively. Jane also had a pension of approximately $40,000 per year starting at 65. Despite knowing Social Security optimal strategy from tools like Open Social Security, they could not cross the confidence threshold.

### Most Aggressive Scenario: Both Retire Now, Both Delay to 70 [00:06:12]

This scenario required the highest and longest sustained portfolio withdrawals before any Social Security income. Guardrails modeling showed year-one withdrawals approaching $200,000 (roughly 11% of portfolio), declining over time to 1.6% of portfolio once both collected Social Security in 2037. Historical Monte Carlo testing showed 88% of scenarios allowed more spending than projected.

### Moderate and Least Aggressive Scenarios [00:09:21]

The moderate scenario had John delaying to 70 while Jane claims at 62; Monte Carlo success improved to 89%. The least aggressive scenario had Jane work until 62, then claim at 62, while John deferred to 70—showing 94% Monte Carlo success and actually lower withdrawal rates in the years between John's retirement and Jane's Social Security claim date. Each scenario produced the same annual spending money; only the source mix between portfolio and Social Security differed.

### Framework and Decision Outcome [00:13:29]

The adviser explained Social Security as a triaging mechanism rather than a one-time optimization problem. The couple identified two rounds of ammunition: claiming Jane's Social Security at 62 if additional income was needed, and claiming John's benefit at any point between age 62 and 70. Seeing scenarios as guardrail ranges rather than fixed commitments eliminated the need to make a permanent claiming decision before John retired.
