# The 4 Step System To Living Your Dream Retirement

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-06-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Ks7AoDBfh44
**Duration:** 42:54

## Summary

Eric Amzalag, a certified financial planner and retirement income specialist, presents a structured four-step system for retirement planning during a webinar. The framework moves from assessing current financial standing and required portfolio income, to prescribing a prioritized action plan based on one of three retirement readiness phases, to monitoring key metrics over defined timeframes, and finally to building a proactive accountability system. The video makes the case that personalized professional guidance outperforms generic rules of thumb, citing advisor-value research from Vanguard and the American College of Financial Services.

## Key Takeaways

- Required Portfolio Income (RPI) is the central calculation: annual spending minus after-tax guaranteed income, used to back-calculate total portfolio size needed at retirement.
- Retirement planning phases are triage (below 59% probability of success), stabilized (60-79%), and optimized (80-100%), each with distinct action priorities.
- A retirement monitoring grid tracks expenses, savings or distribution rate, rate of return, and portfolio value at defined frequencies to allow proactive course-correction.
- A retirement system with pre-documented contingency plans prevents emotional decision-making during market downturns or spending overruns.
- Generic rules of thumb like the 4% rule or universal Roth conversion advice may be inappropriate for individual situations and should be validated against personal financial planning tools.
- Vanguard's Advisor Alpha research suggests professional guidance may add up to 3% equivalent additional portfolio return annually.

## Detailed Notes

### Step 1 – Retirement Assessment [00:06:16]

The assessment has two components: a financial assessment that calculates Required Portfolio Income (RPI = annual spending minus guaranteed after-tax income) and the portfolio size needed at retirement; and a non-financial assessment that gauges savings behavior, expectations about spending in retirement, and investing experience. Accuracy in spending estimates is emphasized, as people commonly miss annual expenses like insurance premiums, property taxes, and gift budgets.

### Step 2 – Retirement Prescription [00:14:40]

Based on a probability-of-success score from a financial planning tool, each person is placed in one of three phases: triage (below 59%), stabilized (60-79%), or optimized (80-100%). Each phase has a tailored action list—triage focuses on working longer or saving more; stabilized adds Social Security and pension optimization; optimized introduces Roth conversions and charitable giving. Items are tested individually in a planning tool to rank by impact.

### Step 3 – Retirement Monitoring [00:19:54]

A monitoring grid tracks six universal metrics—expenses, actual savings or distribution rate, required savings or distribution rate, actual rate of return, required rate of return, and portfolio value—each at defined frequencies (monthly, quarterly, or annually). Separate grids exist for pre-retirees and retirees, differing mainly in savings rate versus distribution rate terminology. A gap column and adjustment timeframe column signal when corrective action is needed.

### Step 4 – Retirement System and Contingency Planning [00:27:12]

Drawing on James Clear's Atomic Habits quote ('people fall to the level of their systems'), this step emphasizes pre-documenting contingency plans for scenarios like overspending or a negative 22% market year. Portfolio guardrails define upper and lower spending boundaries triggered by portfolio value changes, allowing structured spending adjustments without reactive decision-making. The system also incorporates financial and life satisfaction assessments to maintain alignment between financial and personal goals.
