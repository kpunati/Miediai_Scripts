# DON’T PANIC (But Check This Rule Before You Retire)

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-05-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=kk-xLKUS6fM
**Duration:** 17:03

## Summary

Eric Amzalag presents a rules-based approach to managing emotional responses to market volatility, centered on the '72-hour recovery rule.' After sharing a cautionary client story in which selling at the April 2025 market bottom caused the client to miss a rapid recovery, he analyzes historical data on both the 20 worst market days since 1929 and the 20 most recent 2%-or-greater declines, demonstrating that markets reliably recover within 1–3 days in the vast majority of scenarios. The video concludes with quotes from Lance Roberts and Brett Estrada arguing that intelligent risk management—incremental position trimming, not market timing—is the key to long-term portfolio success.

## Key Takeaways

- Emotional decisions to exit the market on the day of a sharp decline almost always result in missing the rapid recovery that follows in 1–3 days.
- Historical data on the 20 worst single-day drops shows that in 20 of 20 cases, the market produced positive returns both 2 and 3 days after the decline.
- The 72-hour recovery rule recommends waiting at least 48–72 hours after a 2%-or-greater decline before making any portfolio adjustment, using that window as the better risk-reduction opportunity.
- Large single-day declines tend to cluster within seven trading days of each other, supporting incremental risk reduction rather than full liquidation.
- The key distinction is between reducing exposure (trimming winning positions to target weights) and eliminating exposure (going to cash), with only the former recommended.
- Aligning investor expectations with realistic outcomes—avoiding both full exit panic and passive buy-and-hold complacency—is framed as the foundation of disciplined retirement investing.

## Detailed Notes

### Cautionary Client Case and Problem Statement [00:00:00]

Amzalag describes receiving a 'sell everything' email from a client on April 7, 2025, after the S&P 500 fell 10.44% in three days. The client's own portfolio was only down 5% at the trough, but they refused to wait. Three trading days later the index was up over 10% from its lows including one of the largest single-day gains in market history on April 9th. The client missed the full recovery, illustrating the cost of emotional decision-making.

### Historical Market Decline Data and the 72-Hour Rule [00:04:54]

Analysis of the 20 worst single-day drops (1929–2020) shows positive 2- and 3-day returns in all 20 scenarios. The 20 most recent 2%-or-worse declines show a similar pattern. The 72-hour recovery rule formalizes this insight: wait 48–72 hours post-decline, then consider reducing (not eliminating) market exposure. Data also shows that waiting 1, 6, or 12 months after a big decline produces only roughly 50/50 odds of recovery, reinforcing the short-window advantage.

### Clustering of Declines and Risk Management Framework [00:09:01]

Large single-day declines tend to cluster within seven trading days, supporting the argument for timely, incremental position trimming. Amzalag and Roberts (RAIA) distinguish between market timing (all-in or all-out) and portfolio risk management (trimming winners to target weights, holding raised cash for the next opportunity). The simulation comparing missing the 10 best days, buy-and-hold, and missing the 10 worst days demonstrates that avoiding the worst days provides the highest return scenario.
