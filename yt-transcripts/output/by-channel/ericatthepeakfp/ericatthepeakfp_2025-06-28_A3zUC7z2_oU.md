# 5 “Must Have” Purchases EVERY Retiree Regrets

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-06-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=A3zUC7z2_oU
**Duration:** 13:51

## Summary

Eric Amzalag identifies five purchases that retirees frequently regret despite making them with good intentions. The video uses client case studies to illustrate how long-term care insurance premiums have become financially destabilizing, second homes drain liquidity without proportional enjoyment, first-time landlording is far from passive income, hasty relocations often cost more than they save, and paying off a mortgage can leave retirees asset-rich but cash-poor. The common thread is that each purchase reduces financial flexibility—which is the most valuable asset in retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- Long-term care insurance premiums have doubled or more for many policyholders, and with fewer than 15 remaining insurers, the industry's viability is increasingly uncertain.
- Second homes typically cost far more than expected in maintenance, property taxes, and carrying costs, while being used far less than anticipated—limiting flexibility for other travel.
- First-time real estate investing in retirement is not passive income; it is a part-time job with unpredictable hours and often thin returns after management fees.
- Relocating without thoroughly testing a new location first—including off-season visits and rental trials—frequently results in higher total costs than the anticipated savings.
- Paying off a mortgage by liquidating portfolio assets increases withdrawal rates on the remaining portfolio, potentially causing anxiety and lifestyle restrictions.
- Every regretted purchase limits future flexibility; in retirement, the ability to adapt and redirect resources is more valuable than eliminating any single expense or obligation.

## Detailed Notes

### Long-Term Care Insurance Regrets [00:01:02]

The long-term care insurance market has contracted from hundreds of insurers to fewer than 15. Remaining policyholders face premium increases averaging 110–120%, with some over 400%. Eric cites a client offered three choices: 50% benefit reduction at current premiums, 100% premium increase for same benefits, or a lump sum payout far below paid-in premiums. The product designed to provide security has itself become a source of financial stress.

### Second Home Pitfalls [00:03:53]

A client couple who purchased a lakehouse found after two summers that coordinating family schedules and managing repairs from across the state was more work than enjoyment. They sold within two years at a nominal profit but were actually down nearly $100,000 after all carrying costs and transaction fees. Second homes reduce liquidity and limit spontaneous travel, creating an obligation to use an expensive asset.

### First-Time Investment Property [00:06:51]

Investment properties require experience, judgment, and risk management that first-time investors lack entering retirement. A client couple buying a duplex near retirement faced a non-paying tenant and constant repair demands within 18 months. Professional property management fees further erode returns. For first-timers in retirement, this is a part-time job, not passive income.

### Rushed Relocation Mistakes [00:07:54]

A client moved to a zero-income-tax state to save on taxes but found sky-high property taxes, a different healthcare system, and no social connections. The total cost of fixing the move exceeded all anticipated tax savings. The advice: rent first, test the location in multiple seasons, and distinguish between enjoying a vacation destination and being able to live there full-time.

### Paying Off the Mortgage [00:09:55]

A client who paid off a $700,000 mortgage the day he retired immediately found his remaining portfolio withdrawal rate unsustainable, leading to paralysis around spending decisions. Tying up large liquid assets in illiquid home equity reduces financial flexibility. When mortgage rates are low and investment accounts may earn more, keeping the mortgage can preserve optionality that outweighs the psychological comfort of being debt-free.
