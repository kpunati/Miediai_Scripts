# Its Boring, But This Is The Secret To Being A "Rich Retiree”

**Channel:** Eric at The PeakFP
**Published:** 2025-04-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=JiYB0YFRJvQ
**Duration:** 14:25

## Summary

Eric Amzalag argues that the common belief that retirement accounts — especially Roth accounts — are always the best savings vehicle is incorrect and leads to costly mistakes. He demonstrates mathematically that pre-tax and Roth accounts have equivalent after-tax values when tax brackets remain the same, then shows using a 'tax path' framework that the optimal account type depends on the saver's current tax bracket and trajectory. He illustrates with client stories how ignoring account-type selection leads to either underestimating retirement readiness (all pre-tax savings) or overpaying taxes (all Roth savings during peak earning years), and adds that oversaving into retirement accounts can trap would-be early retirees behind penalty walls.

## Key Takeaways

- Pre-tax and Roth accounts produce equal after-tax wealth when the tax bracket at contribution equals the bracket at withdrawal — the 'better' account depends on tax bracket trajectory.
- Saving into Roth accounts is generally optimal during low-income, low-bracket early career years; shifting to pre-tax accounts in high-bracket peak earning years reduces lifetime tax burden.
- Oversaving in pre-tax accounts creates value confusion: retirees may overestimate their financial readiness by treating pre-tax balances as fully spendable.
- Oversaving in Roth accounts during peak earning years in high-tax states can waste 46-47% of every saved dollar in taxes, leaving the saver with substantially fewer retirement dollars.
- Pre-tax retirement account balances can trigger Medicare IRMAA surcharges in years immediately before Medicare eligibility, adding an unexpected cost.
- Wealthy retirees differentiate themselves by paying active annual attention to account-type selection, investment choices, and avoiding idle money in conservative default funds.

## Detailed Notes

### The Value Confusion Problem with Pre-Tax Accounts [00:00:36]

Retirees with all savings in pre-tax accounts (401k, 403b, IRA) often overestimate their retirement readiness because they mentally treat the gross balance as spendable. A $500/month saver over 30 years at 6% accumulates roughly $502K, but after 22% federal taxes the spendable amount is only $391,760. Amzalag shares a client story of someone with $1.5M in pre-tax savings who had not accounted for taxes as a line item and discovered their withdrawal rate would be 2% higher than planned.

### Roth vs. Pre-Tax: The Tax Path Framework [00:04:23]

Pre-tax and Roth contributions have mathematically identical after-tax outcomes when the saver's bracket is the same at contribution and withdrawal. The strategic difference is bracket timing: in early career years at lower brackets (12%), Roth contributions make sense because the tax cost is low. As income and brackets rise (22%, 24%, 35%+), pre-tax contributions preserve more dollars. Amzalag shows a 20-year tax path where the tax effect of saving $6,000/year grows from $720 in year 1 (12% bracket) to nearly five times that at year 20 in a higher bracket if savings also grow proportionally.

### The Costly Mistake of Roth Savings in Peak Earning Years [00:09:38]

A client in the 37% federal bracket plus 11-12% California state income tax was directing all retirement savings into Roth accounts based on the belief that Roth money is always 'better money.' At a combined 46-47% marginal tax rate, this meant only 53 cents of every saved dollar actually entered the Roth account. Projections showed the client would never exceed the 22% federal bracket in retirement, making the Roth contributions extremely suboptimal during peak earning years.

### Non-Retirement Account Flexibility and Early Retirement [00:11:32]

Oversaving into retirement accounts of any type can trap early retirees behind penalty walls (10% early withdrawal penalty before age 59.5). Amzalag also notes that high pre-tax balances can trigger Medicare IRMAA surcharges because all withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income in early retirement years. He closes by arguing that 'rich retirees' succeed by dedicating ongoing annual attention to account-type selection, investment oversight, and avoiding idle cash in default conservative target date funds — rather than following any set-it-and-forget-it strategy.
