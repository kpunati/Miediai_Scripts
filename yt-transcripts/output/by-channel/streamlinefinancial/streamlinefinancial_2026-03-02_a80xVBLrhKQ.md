# Why People Get Richer After Retirement

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2026-03-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=a80xVBLrhKQ
**Duration:** 10:02

## Summary

Dave Zoller argues it's common for retiree net worth to grow rather than shrink, walking through six drivers: (1) taxes often drop materially (much less than working-years take-home math); (2) expenses generally decline despite the go-go-years bump (no commute, paid mortgage, kids gone, fewer conveniences); (3) healthcare is most expensive pre-65 — Medicare drops premiums dramatically (one couple went from $2K to ~$400/month); (4) Social Security covers most basic.

## Key Takeaways

- Net worth often continues to grow in retirement if you plan well.
- Taxes commonly drop materially due to lower income, Social Security partial taxability, and the standard deduction.
- Despite go-go-year spending bumps, expenses generally drop (commuting, work clothes, lunches, mortgage payoff, kids gone, fewer paid conveniences).
- Healthcare is most expensive pre-65 — Medicare dramatically lowers premiums (one couple: $2K -> ~$400/month).
- Social Security averages ~$1,800/month single, ~$3,000/month for married couples — guaranteed for life.
- Downsizing the home unlocks liquid capital, often tax-free under the primary-residence capital gains exclusion.
- ~1 in 4 retirees take low-stress flexible part-time jobs, further reducing withdrawals.

## Detailed Notes

### Why net worth can grow in retirement [00:00:00]

Common feeling: nest egg is supposed to shrink. Reality from 16+ years at Streamline: net worth often continues to grow if you do a few things right. First lever — taxes.

### Taxes often drop materially [00:01:03]

Working: 30-50% taken in federal, state, SS, Medicare. Retirement example: $100K working becomes ~$60K of withdrawals; only ~$28K of that is actually taxable after SS partial-taxability rules and the standard deduction.

### Go-go-year spending bump, then decline [00:02:53]

Expectation of higher spending in early retirement is real for many. But over time expenses decline — no commuting/parking/work wardrobe/work lunches. Mortgage typically paid off. Kids out of the house — no more food, activities, or college bills.

### Reduced conveniences when time-rich [00:04:29]

Working full-time means time-poor and convenience-heavy (takeout, paid yard work, cleaners). Retirement frees time to choose between continuing those conveniences or doing them yourself — a real expense lever.

### Healthcare: most expensive pre-65 [00:05:04]

Pre-65 private insurance is expensive — a couple paid $2K+/month at 62 and dropped to ~$400/month after Medicare at 65, freeing ~$20K/year. Medicare isn't free but dramatically cheaper than private insurance.

### Social Security covers basics [00:06:07]

Average check ~$1,800/month, married couple ~$3,000/month — guaranteed for life. Covers most needs (not wants), so retirement accounts withdraw less, stay invested, and continue compounding.

### Downsizing the home [00:07:09]

Lived in the house 30 years, paid off, worth far more than purchase. Sell at $500K, buy at $250K — $250K of liquid capital plus tax-free under the primary-residence capital-gains exclusion. Lower property taxes, utilities, maintenance going forward.

### 1 in 4 retirees do flexible part-time work [00:07:59]

Example: 70-year-old usher at an MLB spring-training stadium — works during spring training and events, enjoys it, generates extra income, reduces portfolio withdrawals.

### Closing: retirement isn't a financial cliff [00:09:02]

It's normal for net worth to go down too — that's what saving was for. King Solomon (Ecclesiastes): if blessed with wealth and the ability to enjoy it, that's a gift.
