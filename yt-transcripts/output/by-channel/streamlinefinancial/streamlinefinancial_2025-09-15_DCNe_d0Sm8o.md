# 750 Retirees Give Harsh Advice To Anyone Working Past 60

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-09-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=DCNe_d0Sm8o
**Duration:** 9:25

## Summary

Dave Zoller cites a New York Life study: 48% of retirees aged 62-70 wish they had retired sooner — by an average of four years. Outlines five psychological traps that keep people working too long: one-more-year mentality, safety illusion of a paycheck, identity crisis, success trap (high achievers), and health reality gap.

## Key Takeaways

- New York Life study: 48% of retirees 62-70 wish they had retired ~4 years sooner.
- Trap 1 — One More Year mentality: Robert worked 3 extra years to go from 94% to 96% success rate.
- Trap 2 — Safety illusion: the paycheck feels safer than withdrawals, but the cost is time with family.
- Trap 3 — Identity crisis: career-defined high achievers fear losing themselves; reframe retirement as expansion, not loss.
- Trap 4 — Success trap: entrepreneurs keep chasing 'one more big contract' while their magical triangle (money + health + time) slips out of balance.
- Trap 5 — Health reality gap: 60s are peak active years; health doesn't decline slowly and predictably.
- Action: define your 'enough number,' run Monte Carlo simulations, factor opportunity cost of working longer.

## Detailed Notes

### The shocking statistic [00:00:00]

New York Life study: 48% of retirees 62-70 wish they had retired sooner — by an average of four years. Biggest regrets come from waiting too long, not retiring too early.

### Trap 1 — One More Year mentality [00:00:41]

Robert, 62, had a 94% success-rate plan. Kept saying 'one more year' for security — three years later, success rate was 96%. He traded 3 years of life for 2 percentage points.

### Trap 2 — Safety illusion of a paycheck [00:02:09]

Client (63) kept working because the salary felt safer than withdrawals. Meanwhile, grandkids' first steps and words happened in another state. Choice isn't work vs not — it's guaranteed income vs guaranteed time.

### Trap 3 — Identity crisis [00:03:01]

High achievers struggle: 'if I'm not the executive, who am I?' Reframe retirement as expansion, not loss — space to rediscover passions, relationships, mentoring.

### Trap 4 — Success trap [00:03:47]

Entrepreneurs keep pushing for one more contract even as returns shrink. Client (65) kept expanding his company until he realized he was already 'winning at life' — health, marriage, finances all solid, but time was running out.

### Trap 5 — Health reality gap [00:05:24]

Health doesn't decline slowly and predictably. Family friend retired in his 60s, diagnosed with cancer 6 months later, died 6 months after that. 60s are peak active years for hiking, travel, grandkids.

### Step 1 — Define your enough number [00:06:22]

Not maximum — enough. Monte Carlo simulation = retirement stress test running 1,000+ scenarios. 85% success doesn't mean 15% failure — it means 15% chance of needing to adjust.

### Steps 2-4 — Health, cost, honest journaling [00:07:24]

(2) Health timeline assessment using family history. (3) Calculate true cost of working longer (taxes, missed Roth conversion windows, lost SS optimization, opportunity cost of family time). (4) Journal honestly — is it passion, or fear/anxiety?
