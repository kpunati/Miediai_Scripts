# When To Start Collecting Social Security Benefits

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-07-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=rEfyv1XHIOw
**Duration:** 6:28

## Summary

Dave Zoller walks through a visual decision-tree for when to start Social Security (62, full retirement age, or 70), using a hypothetical 63-year-old retired example. Shows the key flaw — the diagram ignores spousal/survivor-benefit dynamics. Then introduces a JPMorgan longevity-probability chart and a return-vs-longevity grid: every year deferred past full retirement age boosts benefits ~8%, so compare against your expected portfolio return.

## Key Takeaways

- There's no universal 'right age' for Social Security — it depends on health, expected longevity, other income, and portfolio return.
- The simple decision-tree treatment ignores spousal/survivor benefits — important for married couples.
- If the higher earner waits to 70, the surviving spouse inherits the higher benefit; claim early and the survivor is locked into the lower amount.
- Each year of delay past full retirement age adds ~8% to the benefit through age 70.
- JPMorgan's longevity tables show high odds of one spouse living past 80+ — argues for some delay strategy for couples.
- Compare ~8% Social Security 'return' from delay against your portfolio's expected return when deciding.
- Run a real side-by-side breakeven analysis with a planner — don't rely on diagrams alone.

## Detailed Notes

### Setup: the claiming question [00:00:00]

Dave Zoller frames one of the most common questions over 15 years: 62, full retirement age (67), or 70? Walks through a decision-tree using a hypothetical 63-year-old, married, retired, with brokerage + IRA + Roth income sources.

### Walking the decision tree [00:01:04]

Path: not working → other income sources available → prefer larger benefit later → don't need to claim to protect portfolio → expect to live past 70 → expect to live past 81 → wait until 70.

### The flaw: spousal/survivor dynamics [00:02:08]

Diagram ignores that the lower-earning spouse will receive half the higher earner's benefit (or the full survivor benefit on death). Higher earner waiting to 70 = higher floor for the surviving spouse for the rest of their life.

### JPMorgan longevity probabilities [00:03:12]

JPMorgan Asset Management chart shows odds of living to 67, 70, 77, 81+ — joint-life probabilities are notably high. Couples should generally bias toward delay for the higher earner.

### Return vs longevity grid [00:04:14]

Crossover view: expected portfolio return on one axis, longevity on the other. With 6% expected return + 77 life expectancy, claim at 62; 6% + 85, claim at 67; longer life expectancy → wait until 70. Each delay year ≈ 8% benefit boost.

### Action: real side-by-side analysis [00:05:17]

Use the JPMorgan report as a thought-starter, but run a real planner-led side-by-side analysis with all your income sources before deciding.
