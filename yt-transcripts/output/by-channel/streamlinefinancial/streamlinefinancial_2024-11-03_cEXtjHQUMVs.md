# Ask Your Spouse Before Retirement

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-11-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cEXtjHQUMVs
**Duration:** 9:54

## Summary

Dave Zoller walks a couple through a guided exercise of conversation prompts to discuss with their spouse before retirement — designed to take ~10 minutes of pause-and-write between questions. Covers the three Cs (creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution), what they want to do that they can't now, what they'd miss about work, confidence ratings, location preferences, role with adult children/grandkids, deepest connections to build, and contribution.

## Key Takeaways

- Real retirement planning starts with non-financial questions, not numbers.
- Use this video as a guided exercise: pause after each question, write individually, then discuss with your spouse.
- Introverted spouses need time to think — extroverts should give space, not lead the answer.
- The questions trace the three Cs: creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution.
- Confidence-level rating on a -2 to +2 scale clarifies what would have to change to feel fully confident.
- Discuss role with children and grandchildren ahead of time — finances, time, location decisions all flow from it.
- Identify what breaks your heart in the world to surface your contribution priorities.
- If your advisor doesn't help with non-financial planning, look around — it's the foundation that prevents the 'is this all there is?' trap.

## Detailed Notes

### Why couples need this conversation: '33 years of marriage and I didn't know that about you' [00:00:00]

Even longtime spouses haven't navigated the post-work life stage. Sharing 15+ years of client experience to help couples avoid the surprises.

### Format: pause and write between each question [00:01:01]

Video is designed as a guided exercise with both spouses present, notepad in hand. Pause after each prompt; discuss; resume.

### Pacing tip for extroverts and introverts [00:02:03]

Introverted spouses need quiet time to think. Don't rush them or answer for them.

### Q1: What do you want to do in retirement that you can't do now? [00:02:03]

First prompt — the still-working spouse leads off.

### Q2-3: Curiosity, new interests, fascinations [00:03:05]

Stay-curious framework. Two words to remember: fascinating and motivating.

### Q4-6: 10/10 confidence date, missed routines, travel/luxury expectations [00:04:10]

If you felt fully confident in finances, when would you stop working? What would you miss? What does travel/big-ticket spend look like?

### Q7-8: What does a really bad retirement look like? What worries you? [00:04:10]

Inversion technique: easier to describe what to avoid than what to want. Fears often become the things you work hardest to prevent — and so reduce.

### Confidence rating on a -2 to +2 scale [00:05:12]

Rate your confidence in the current plan from -2 to +2. Then: what would have to happen to feel like a +2?

### Q11-12: Where to live; role with kids/grandkids [00:06:16]

Could you see yourself living anywhere else? Full-time/part-time? What role do you want with adult children and grandkids — financial support, time, college funds?

### Q13-14: Values to pass to kids; connections to deepen [00:07:19]

Adult children lean more on parents for wisdom once they have their own kids. Who do you want to deepen connection with? Faith may belong here.

### Q15-16: Contribution — what breaks your heart? Who/what causes do you want to help? [00:08:22]

Surface contribution priorities by asking what hurts to watch in the world. Could be local, immediate-circle, or global.

### Three Cs frame the questions; non-financial is the foundation [00:09:24]

Questions trace creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution. If your advisor doesn't touch this, look elsewhere — it's the foundation.
