# 75-Year-Olds Share Their Biggest Regret at 55

## Summary

Dave Zoller argues that the most common regret of successful 75-year-olds isn't 'I wish I'd saved more' — it's 'I wish I hadn't waited so long to live' or 'I wish I'd retired sooner.' Frames retirement as freedom from money-driven time control, not as stopping work. Three insights: the sustainability trap (stress capacity declines with age — burnout is the real cost); experiences compound like investments in a different currency; a real retirement plan is a.

## Key Takeaways

- Most common regret of successful 75-year-olds: 'I wish I hadn't waited so long to live.'
- Retirement isn't stopping work — it's freedom from being controlled by money.
- Jesse Cole's 10-visits framing: a 75-year-old parent you see annually = 10 visits, not 10 years.
- Sustainability trap: your capacity to handle stress declines with age — what worked at 30 burns you out at 60.
- Experiences compound just like investments — in a different currency, often more important long-term.
- Monte Carlo 80% success = 20% chance of needing an adjustment, NOT failure.
- The plan is the process, not the document — life changes, stay adaptable.
- Dave's clients have retired before every major crisis (2008, March 2020) — zero had plans blow up.

## Detailed Notes

### The 75-year-old regret [00:00:00]

After 16 years working with retirees, the common regret isn't 'I wish I'd saved more.' It's 'I wish I hadn't waited so long to live.' Retirement isn't sitting on a beach — it's freedom from money-driven time.

### Jesse Cole's 10 visits [00:01:39]

Cole's parents are 75; he sees them once a year. That's not 10 years left — it's 10 visits. Every dollar saved buys future time; every dollar spent prioritizes present time. The question is intentionality.

### The optimize-everything trap [00:02:17]

58-year-old, 25-year career, 'optimize-everything' decade. Tired, hasn't taken vacation, missing weekend activities with spouse. Following the right financial steps but heading for burnout.

### Stress capacity declines with age [00:02:52]

Your stress capacity at 30 was high; at 60, late nights and long commutes hit different. Sustainability trap: if your current plan drains energy, it isn't sustainable, so it isn't effective. Plans are math AND energy problems.

### Experiences compound [00:04:35]

Weekend camping trip with grandkids beats squeezing 1% from the budget. Experiences compound in a different currency. Never met a client who regretted spending on meaningful experiences — many regretted missing them.

### The actual planning process [00:06:36]

Step 1: baseline plan in real retirement-specific software. Step 2: see your Monte Carlo success rate. Step 3: 80% doesn't mean 20% failure — it means 20% chance of needing adjustment over 30 years. The plan is the planning process, not the document.

### What-if scenarios for confidence [00:08:39]

Plug various what-ifs: stock returns worse than expected, higher inflation, downsizing, retiring earlier. A good planner assigns odds. Eases retirement-decision fear with real numbers, not anxiety.

### Clients have retired through every crisis [00:09:29]

Streamline clients retired right before 2008 GFC, during March 2020 pandemic, before every major scary headline. Zero had their plans blow up — because plans were built knowing unexpected things happen, with plan B's.

### Closing — make the days count [00:10:09]

Perfect plan executed flawlessly is worthless if you missed the life it was supposed to fund. You only die once — you live every day. Make them count.
