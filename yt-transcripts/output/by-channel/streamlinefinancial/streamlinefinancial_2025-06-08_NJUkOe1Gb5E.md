# Your 401(k) Isn't Enough for Retirement

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-06-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NJUkOe1Gb5E
**Duration:** 10:23

## Summary

Dave Zoller outlines three hidden 401(k) flaws (tax trap, inflation, RMDs) and a multi-bucket retirement strategy using tax-deferred, Roth, brokerage, and HSA accounts. Cites a Vanguard study claiming a tax-efficient withdrawal sequence can add up to 1% of annual return. Steps: max HSA, get 401(k) match, fund Roth IRA, then taxable brokerage, with Roth conversions during the post-retirement / pre-Social-Security sweet spot.

## Key Takeaways

- Most 401(k) money isn't yours — about 30% goes to taxes when withdrawn (the 401(k) tax trap).
- Inflation cuts purchasing power roughly in half every 20 years — the invisible second risk.
- Other 401(k) downsides: limited investment options, contribution limits, RMDs at 73, early-withdrawal penalties, no step-up in basis, no tax-rate-increase protection.
- Diversify across three tax buckets: tax-deferred (401k), tax-free (Roth), tax-efficient (brokerage) — Vanguard says this can save up to 1% annually.
- HSA is the fourth bucket — triple-tax-advantaged for medical, and a quasi-traditional-IRA for non-medical after age 65.
- Roth conversion sweet spot: post-retirement, pre-Social-Security, paying tax at lowest bracket in decades.
- Action plan: max HSA, get 401(k) match, fund Roth IRA, then taxable brokerage — start with one and add over time.

## Detailed Notes

### The 401(k) tax trap and inflation risk [00:00:00]

Most 401(k) money isn't fully yours — about 30% is owed to the IRS at withdrawal. Inflation is the second hidden threat: cuts purchasing power roughly in half every 20 years. Example: client Maggie realized 30% of her 401(k) wasn't usable at retirement.

### The cost of emotion-driven decisions (2008 example) [00:02:00]

Story of a divorcing couple in 2008-2009 who split assets 50/50. Wife stayed invested and recovered to her high-water mark in 3-4 years. Husband moved to ultra-conservative investments after the crash and never recovered — inflation continued to erode his purchasing power.

### Other 401(k) downsides [00:02:59]

Limited investment options (high-fee funds), 2025 contribution cap $23,500 ($30,500 over 50), RMDs at 73 that can push you into higher brackets, early withdrawal penalties before 59½, no step-up in basis for heirs, all-or-nothing tax treatment, no protection from tax-rate increases.

### The three-bucket framework [00:03:50]

Smart retirees use three account types: tax-deferred 401(k), tax-free Roth IRA, and tax-efficient brokerage. Vanguard study found a smart tax-efficient withdrawal strategy can save up to 1% annually — over $100K on a $1M portfolio over a decade.

### HSA — the triple tax advantage [00:04:52]

Health Savings Account is pre-tax in, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical at any age. After 65, non-medical withdrawals taxed like a traditional IRA. Medical expenses keep rising in retirement, so it's a built-in fund for known future expenses.

### Roth conversions and the sweet spot [00:06:44]

Convert pre-tax IRA/401(k) money to Roth, pay tax now, grow tax-free forever. Best timing: after retirement but before Social Security starts, when income (and bracket) is at a multi-decade low. Especially useful if you expect higher tax rates or want to reduce future RMDs.

### Social Security strategy [00:07:44]

2025 average monthly benefit ~$1,850. Delaying to age 70 increases payments by up to 76% vs starting at 62. Often makes sense to delay SS while drawing from other accounts early in retirement to enable both Roth conversions and a higher SS benefit later.

### Step-by-step action plan [00:08:45]

Step 1: max HSA if eligible ($4,300 single / $8,550 family in 2025). Step 2: get the full 401(k) company match. Step 3: open and contribute to a Roth IRA. Step 4: build a taxable brokerage for liquidity and flexibility. Progress over perfection — start with one bucket, add others over time.
