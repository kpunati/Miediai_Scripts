# They Retired 10 Years Early Due To 3 Things

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-06-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=e7J5hZiMkCA
**Duration:** 9:58

## Summary

Dave Zoller walks through a real-client case: Mary and John, 58 with $2M saved, told by a CFP they had to work 12 more years before retiring. Reframes the planning conversation around life values FIRST (faith, family, fitness, fun, friends) rather than numbers-first. After exploring what-if scenarios (move to South Carolina to be near grandkids, drop $25K/yr country club, reduce travel spend), the plan shifted from 12 more years to ~2 — same risk tolerance, totally different outcome.

## Key Takeaways

- Numbers-first financial planning misses what actually matters — start with the client's story and values.
- Same dollar Nest Egg can support very different retirement timelines depending on lifestyle choices.
- Dropping high-cost lifestyle items (country club, frequent travel) that aren't producing joy can accelerate retirement readiness.
- Values exploration framework: faith, family, fitness, fun, friends (the "five Fs").
- Mary & John's plan shifted from 12 more work-years to ~2 with the same investment allocation — change was in life design, not portfolio.
- Non-financial retirement readiness (the "three Cs": creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution) matters as much as financial readiness.

## Detailed Notes

### The case: Mary & John at 58, $2M, told to work 12 more years [00:00:00]

Dave Zoller introduces a real-client case study: a couple with $2M saved told by a CFP they had to work 12 more years before retirement. Plan failed at retirement before age 70 in the original model.

### The mistake: numbers-first planning [00:02:07]

Most advisors collect all the financial details first, then build a plan from numbers. Great planners flip the order: explore the client's story and ideal future first, THEN plug numbers in to support that vision.

### The Streamline conversation: getting curious [00:03:08]

Sample dialogue: asking 'what are you most looking forward to doing that you can't do now?' surfaced kids in South Carolina, charity work, faith, travel, golf they weren't using. Genuine curiosity is what separates great planners from average ones.

### Five Fs values framework [00:05:13]

Mary & John's top five values in order: Faith, Family, Fitness/health, Fun, Friends. Forms the basis for the what-if exploration.

### What-if scenarios applied [00:06:15]

Explore alternatives: spend less on travel (~$10K), drop the country club ($25K/yr in Chicago area), sell Illinois home and move closer to grandkids in South Carolina. Each scenario tested against the plan.

### Drastic plan improvement, same risk [00:07:19]

After what-if scenarios: working 2 more years (vs 12) with aggressive savings made the plan work. Investment allocation unchanged.

### Great planners are part counselor [00:08:22]

A great retirement planner has the credentials AND the genuine curiosity of a counselor/coach. Most of the industry is still numbers-leading.

### Three Cs for retirement fulfillment [00:09:24]

Beyond money: continue being creative/curious, prioritize connection (family, God), and find ways to contribute to others. Common across the happiest retirees.
