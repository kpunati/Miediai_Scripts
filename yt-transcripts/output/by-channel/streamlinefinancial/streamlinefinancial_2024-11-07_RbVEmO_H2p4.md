## Summary

Tim interviews Kristen Welch, CEO and founder of Mercy House Global, a Christian nonprofit that runs three maternity homes in Nairobi, Kenya and a fair-trade artisan supply chain providing dignified work to women escaping survival prostitution and human trafficking. They cover Kristen's path from frustrated writer to mom-blogger to nonprofit founder, the practice of gratitude as a discipline, why she lives by a 'rule of life' that includes daily silence, solitude,.

## Key Takeaways

- Generosity (time, money, opportunity) and gratitude are practices, not personality traits — both are disciplines.
- Kristen's career path: frustrated writer → mom blogger → Compassion International trip → founder of Mercy House Global.
- Mercy House Global runs maternity homes in Nairobi and a fair-trade Texas warehouse, employing trafficking overcomers.
- Gratitude reframes scarcity — comparing the privilege of being born in the US to Kenya's largest slum was Kristen's catalyst.
- Eugene Peterson: 'The workplace is the number one place for spiritual formation.'
- Rule of life (trellis, not punishment): start and end the day the same way, including silence and Sabbath.
- Overwork is a form of addiction culturally applauded; Henri Nouwen called burnout 'spiritual death.'

## Detailed Notes

### Intro: Kristen Welch and Mercy House Global [00:00:00]

Mercy House Global empowers women and families in extreme poverty toward sustainable income. Two years ago Kristen was named CEO and asked for a sabbatical as her first request.

### Kristen as Storyteller and unorthodox career path [00:02:08]

Career has been 'doing the one right thing next' over and over. Followed open doors. Eighth-grade teacher told her she was a good writer — set the trajectory.

### From curriculum-writing to mom blogger in 2010 [00:04:15]

Bought 'Blogging for Dummies' in 2000. Started blog as frustrated writer with no publishing connections. Found community of moms who needed encouragement.

### Invitation to Kenya with Compassion International [00:06:22]

Invited to Kenya as mom blogger before 'influencer' was a word. Connected with a young woman from the world's largest slum — that proximity was the catalyst.

### Crossroads experience: 34, tired of returning to comfort [00:08:27]

Other crossroads: NICU baby, marriage problems, financial struggle. At 34 she chose to let this one change her life.

### Founding Mercy House Global in 2010 [00:13:41]

After returning from Kenya, saw a documentary on the world's largest slum identifying survival prostitution. Motive wasn't to start a nonprofit — was to raise money for someone else. Got pulled into hands-on founding.

### Maternity homes, artisan products, fair trade [00:16:48]

Three maternity homes in Nairobi for girls escaping survival prostitution. Stay 2-3 years to have child. Indigenous artisanship (basket weaving, looms) provides dignified work.

### Houston warehouse: refugee employment [00:17:52]

Started replicating the model with vulnerable refugees outside Houston ~9 years ago. Now hires trafficking overcomers. 100% woman-owned and led.

### Gratitude as the key discipline [00:18:56]

Gratitude unlocks contentment, is a daily practice. Kristen's daughter independently started a daily gratitude journal in a hard senior year.

### Kenya as the contentment teacher [00:21:04]

Kristen had more physical possessions, less joy. Slum residents had less, more gratitude. Reframed her contentment as not contingent on circumstances.

### Gratitude as practice — three small things daily [00:23:12]

Some days the list is shallow: 'this coffee, this dog, the sun came up.' Gratitude slows you down to see what's around you.

### Discipline > motivation (high school football example) [00:25:22]

Football player she was mentoring: 'discipline means you don't need motivation.' She agrees — applies to faith, exercise, gratitude.

### How Enneagram 1s rest: rule of life [00:26:24]

Kristen has had to learn rest the hard way through burnout. Run 15 years of nonprofit as a sprint instead of a marathon.

### Henri Nouwen: burnout = spiritual death [00:27:27]

Nouwen's framing struck deep. Doing things for God is not the same as being with God.

### Rule of life as trellis, not rules [00:28:30]

Rule comes from 'trellis' — a structure that supports the plant, not punishes it. Created in community with accountability.

### Daily structure and Sabbath [00:30:38]

Same start and end to the day, phone alarms for gratitude/breathing/Clarity breaks, weekly Sabbath. Acknowledges 'I am finite; if I don't rest I'll die sooner.'

### Family practicing rule of life: son in med school [00:31:41]

Her son cited his rule of life in a med-school interview as how he'd manage stress. Created 'space for my soul.'

### Working from a place of surrender accomplishes more [00:32:44]

Discovered she accomplishes more by not overworking — production from surrender beats overproduction.

### Loads vs. burdens (Matthew yoke metaphor) [00:34:51]

A load is your calling; burdens are what you carry outside your calling. Becoming a micromanager turned her load into a burden.

### Working in tandem: spreadsheet partner [00:36:56]

Kristen isn't a spreadsheet person; her colleague is. Trust + interdependence = effortful but light.
