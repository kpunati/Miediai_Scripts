# 7 Financial Goals Everyone Should Hit By 60

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-01-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=b2GZuf5MqhI
**Duration:** 12:28

## Summary

Dave Zoller lists seven financial goals to hit by age 60, in order: (1) move from emergency fund to a retirement cash management system (checking-account floor of ~3 months); (2) track expenses and split needs vs wants; (3) track net worth; (4) be debt-free; (5) map future cash flow; (6) draft a real retirement plan; (7) button up the estate plan. Recommends Boldin (formerly NewRetirement) as the best DIY consumer planning tool.

## Key Takeaways

- Move from an emergency fund to a retirement cash management system — set a checking-account floor (e.g., 3 months of expenses) as the new zero.
- Track and budget expenses using a tool (Monarch, Tiller, YNAB) and split them into needs vs wants.
- Track net worth at least quarterly.
- Be debt-free; mortgages can be a financial vs emotional decision but credit-card and auto debt have no upside in retirement.
- Map out future cash flow: a draft of when each account starts producing income at what age.
- Draft a real retirement plan (with allocation, return target, Monte Carlo) — Boldin is recommended as the best consumer-facing tool.
- Button up the estate plan: wills, trusts, beneficiaries, successor trustees — review every few years.

## Detailed Notes

### Goal 1: graduate to a retirement cash management system [00:00:00]

Emergency-fund thinking is for the 20s-50s. In/near retirement, use a brokerage account as second-line emergency reserve; set a checking-account floor (e.g. 3 months of expenses) instead of zero.

### Why the checking-account floor matters [00:02:05]

If expenses are $10K/month and income is $10K/month, a zero floor cuts it close. Setting a $30K floor (3 months) provides buffer for taxes/larger expenses.

### Goal 2: budget / track expenses (and split needs vs wants) [00:03:08]

Use Monarch Money, Tiller, or YNAB to auto-track and categorize expenses. Splitting needs (housing, healthcare, food, auto, insurance) from wants (travel, dining, big-ticket) gives confidence — a subscriber realized he could retire sooner once he saw his needs alone were covered.

### Goal 3: track net worth [00:06:16]

Use the same tools to track net worth (what you have minus what you owe). Quarterly minimum, monthly is fine if automated.

### Goal 4: be debt-free (and the mortgage debate) [00:06:16]

Credit cards and auto loans have no place in retirement; pay them down. Mortgage in retirement is a financial-vs-emotional decision — plans often work fine with one, but peace of mind sometimes outweighs the math.

### Goal 5: map out future cash flow [00:08:21]

Beyond a Monte Carlo success rate, draft a cash-flow projection: list assets on the left, apply 4% to investment accounts, add Social Security, total it, and compare to budget. Then map age-by-age when each account starts producing income.

### Where Roth IRAs typically sit in the spend order [00:09:23]

If leaving money to kids, Roth IRAs often go last — tax-free money passes to kids.

### Goal 6: draft a real retirement plan [00:09:23]

A real plan factors in assets, expenses, investment allocation, and target rate of return. The minimum rate of return for highest probability of success often surprises clients on the downside. DIY option: Boldin (formerly NewRetirement).

### Goal 7: button up the estate plan [00:10:26]

Review wills, trusts, beneficiaries, successor trustees at least every few years. Make sure heirs know what to do and where documents are. If you don't have documents, find an attorney.
