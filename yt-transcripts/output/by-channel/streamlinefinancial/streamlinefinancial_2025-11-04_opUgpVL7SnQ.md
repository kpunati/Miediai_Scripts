# Generosity Is the Cure for Fear With Liz Fay

**Channel:** Streamline Financial

**Duration:** 71:58

## Summary

Interview with Liz Fay — Tim's first-ever financial planning client (since 2009) and a former 19-year high school teacher — on her decision to leave teaching for an open-ended Sabbath season. Topics: her early-career generosity (hosting three friends rent-free over the years), fighting scarcity with generosity, the moment in August 2024 she realized she didn't want to continue teaching, role exit and identity, walking a non-defined path, the practice of Sabbath as.

## Key Takeaways

- Liz read getting-out-of-debt books at the library before ever having debt — preventative financial mindset.
- Money is a tool through which values get expressed — generosity is alignment, not sacrifice.
- Liz hosted three friends rent-free over years to incubate them out of debt while continuing to grow themselves.
- Fight scarcity with generosity — even when it doesn't get easier, the neural pathway deepens.
- Liz's August 2024 classroom realization: 'I don't want to do this anymore' — listened and made it her last year.
- The most vulnerable conversations are about money — open budget, open finances, no shame.
- You don't have to teach 35 years to be a successful teacher — 3 or 19 is also valid.
- Sabbath practice (one day/week off) prepares you for extended rest seasons — practice the muscle.
- Stealing from future retirement savings for a present rest season can be wise when projections show overfunding.
- The gift of a happy, healthy parent is an incredible investment in children's future.

## Detailed Notes

### Cold open and intro [00:00:00]

Liz teases the framework: 'we agree to a lot of things as non-negotiable — but WE decided they're non-negotiable. Test them like Jenga blocks.' Tim introduces Liz as his first-ever client (since 2009) and a 19-year teacher walking into an open-ended Sabbath season.

### How they met — June 2009 [00:01:54]

Liz was Tim's first client after he left teaching to start in financial services. They taught together for 3.25 years before Tim transitioned. June 2009 Caribou Coffee meeting to review her financial plan.

### Pre-emptive debt research [00:03:12]

Liz never had credit card debt — but went to the library and pulled every book on getting out of debt. Found a Mary-something author (early '90s big-hair book) with great principles on financial margin and 'financial fasting' tied to clarifying personal values.

### Money as a tool for values [00:05:16]

Money expresses values. Quality time over gifts. Generosity over consumption. Don't be caught off guard by need — being raised to honor parents and never want to ask them for bailout money.

### Hosting friends rent-free [00:11:08]

Liz hosted three different women in her house over the years — rent-free or low-rent. She'd cover utilities. Refused to 'get richer off their need.' Walked them through paying down credit card debt without ever asking to see their numbers.

### Why she hosted — incubator framing [00:14:19]

Liz creates spaces for people to incubate — 'I think that's what makes me a good teacher.' Learning is vulnerable. Money conversations are the most vulnerable. She provided a safe space for both.

### The privilege piece [00:16:29]

Liz acknowledges privilege — grandfather's GI Bill, parents and grandmother through Depression with savings. She didn't earn those advantages. Wealth gained when people are willing to be generous and not exploit.

### Fighting scarcity with generosity [00:17:07]

Scarcity is generational in Liz's family — 'if I give it to you, I won't have it.' Writing the check is the active proof that she trusts it'll be OK. Doesn't get easier with practice, but the neural pathway deepens.

### The classroom moment [00:26:23]

August 2024, Wednesday before 19th year started, Liz sat in her empty classroom and was overcome with: 'I do not want to do any of this anymore.' That clarity was jarring — she listened.

### Yellow flags that took 4 years to recognize [00:30:23]

From March 13, 2020 (pandemic shutdown), through frozen grades, online teaching, hybrid block schedule, return-to-in-person — the pivots took everything. Took 4 years to distinguish 'this is hard for me' from 'this is just hard.'

### Tipping point between OK and not OK [00:31:46]

Liz told her boss six days in: 'I don't know if I can make it to Thanksgiving. I want to make it to May, but I don't know if I can.' She didn't want to find out.

### Three reactions from colleagues [00:42:00]

Reactions she got: 'I didn't know you were having a hard time' (guilt), 'What you're doing is so courageous' (admiration), and 'I wish I could do what you're doing but never could' (vicarious living). To the third: test the Jenga blocks of your supposed constraints.

### Money isn't the only gift [00:43:53]

'Money is not the only thing you can offer your kids. The gift of a happy and healthy parent is an incredible investment in your children's future.' Stoicism isn't the only model — vulnerability teaches kids how to navigate hard places.

### Sabbath practice as preparation [00:55:25]

Liz has practiced Sabbath on Saturdays for years — no work, no shoulds, no worry about work not done. That regular muscle is what makes this open-ended Sabbath season possible.

### Tim's view on stealing from future retirement [00:56:40]

Many clients are projected to have more than they'll ever spend. Tim's growing belief: use some of those future-overfunded dollars to fund a present season of rest. Step into next season with energy, clarity, joy, no resentment.
