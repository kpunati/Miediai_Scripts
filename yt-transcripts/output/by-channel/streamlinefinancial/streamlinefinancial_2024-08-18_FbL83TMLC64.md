# The Month You Retire Really Matters

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-08-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=FbL83TMLC64
**Duration:** 9:59

## Summary

Dave Zoller breaks down how the month you retire can affect your finances and well-being. Covers three categories: tax benefits (retiring in the first half of the year opens Roth-conversion, tax-gain-harvesting, and 401(k)-max-out options), non-financial benefits (spring/summer retirement avoids Northern-state winter depression), and financial considerations (pension full-eligibility dates, Medicare/COBRA timing, year-end bonuses, accrued PTO).

## Key Takeaways

- Retiring in the first half of the year lowers that year's income and creates room for Roth conversions, tax gain harvesting, and 401(k) max-out.
- Increase 401(k) contributions in January if planning a spring retirement to reduce taxable income.
- Spring/summer retirement avoids the winter mental-transition struggle common in northern states.
- Don't ignore the three Cs in early retirement: creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution.
- If retiring in winter, plan a near-term warm-climate trip to maintain momentum and avoid the slump.
- Schedule major medical procedures before the retirement date and time the date around Medicare eligibility (often using COBRA's 18-month window).
- Pension full-eligibility date, year-end bonus timing, and accrued PTO can each shift the optimal retirement date.

## Detailed Notes

### Three categories: tax, non-financial, financial reasons [00:00:00]

Frames the video: month of retirement affects (1) tax outcomes, (2) non-financial well-being, and (3) financial timing. Most retirees only realize the optimizations a few months in.

### Tax benefit of first-half retirement [00:01:02]

Example: $150K married income; retiring in April halves the year's income to ~$50K. That opens room for Roth conversions and tax-gain harvesting (0% LTCG bracket) — clients have realized capital gains tax-free this way.

### January 401(k) contribution adjustment [00:02:07]

If retiring in April, increase 401(k) contributions in January to max it out before leaving — reduces taxable income further and increases headroom for Roth conversions or tax-gain harvesting.

### Three Cs and the spring vs. winter mental contrast [00:03:10]

Highlights the three Cs (creativity/curiosity, connection, contribution) as non-financial retirement essentials. Sets up the example of two Illinois retirees: one retires April, one November.

### Why northern winter retirement is harder [00:04:13]

November retirement in northern states means dark days, indoor time, and a tougher transition. Spring retirement provides outdoor time and natural mood support during the adjustment window.

### Plan a warm-climate trip and count summers [00:05:15]

For winter retirees, schedule a warm-weather trip shortly after the date. Also asks: at 65, how many active summers do you have left? Carpe aestatem — seize the summer.

### Work milestone and 30-year career markers [00:06:18]

Some retirees want to hit a clean 30-year career milestone (e.g., from February to February). Transitions to the financial-timing factors.

### Healthcare timing and COBRA-to-Medicare bridge [00:07:22]

Schedule procedures before the retirement date when employer coverage is still in place. COBRA lasts 18 months — many back-calculate the retirement date so COBRA bridges to Medicare eligibility at 65.

### Pension, bonus, and PTO triggers [00:08:26]

Pension full-eligibility date may dictate retirement timing — request the benefit summary from HR. Year-end bonus payout months and accrued PTO are other common financial anchors.

### Recap and audience prompt [00:09:29]

Recap of tax, non-financial, and financial reasons month matters. Invites comments on planned retirement month — references a viewer named JP who has been counting down for years.
