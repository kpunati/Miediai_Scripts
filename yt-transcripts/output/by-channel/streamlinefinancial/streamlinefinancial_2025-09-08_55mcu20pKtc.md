# Get More From Your 401K - Mega Backdoor Roth Explained

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-09-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=55mcu20pKtc
**Duration:** 5:07

## Summary

Dave Zoller explains the Mega Backdoor Roth strategy: most people max their 401(k) at $23,500 ($31,000 for 50+), but the real total contribution limit is ~$70,000. The gap can be filled with after-tax contributions converted immediately to Roth. Example: Sarah ($31K personal + $10K match + $29K after-tax converted to Roth) over 20 years at 7% adds $1.35M+ in tax-free retirement savings.

## Key Takeaways

- 401(k) total contribution limit is ~$70,000 — not the $23,500/$31,000 most people think.
- Mega Backdoor Roth: contribute the gap as after-tax, then immediately convert to Roth.
- Tax-free growth forever once in Roth — potentially $1M+ extra over 20 years.
- Sarah example: $31K personal + $10K match + $29K after-tax = $70K total; the $29K alone grew to $1.35M+ tax-free at 7% over 20 years.
- HR script: 'Does our 401(k) plan allow after-tax contributions with in-service withdrawals or conversions to Roth?'
- Most HR teams aren't trained on this — plan details are buried in 40-50 page documents.
- Requires maxing standard 401(k) first AND having cash flow for the after-tax contributions.

## Detailed Notes

### The $70K real 401(k) limit [00:00:00]

Most assume $23,500 ($31K if 50+) is the cap — but the real limit is closer to $70,000 with employer match and after-tax contributions.

### Why we don't hear about it [00:00:34]

Two reasons: (1) details buried in 40-50 page plan documents; (2) HR isn't trained on advanced retirement strategies — they answer questions about health insurance and PTO, not Roth conversions.

### The strategy steps [00:01:36]

Step 1: max regular 401(k) ($23,500 / $31K). Step 2: employer match (e.g., $10K). Step 3: additional after-tax contributions to fill to $70K cap. Step 4: immediately convert after-tax dollars to Roth — all future growth is tax-free.

### Sarah example — $1.35M of tax-free growth [00:02:38]

Age 50. Maxes 401(k) at $31K. $10K employer match. Adds $29K after-tax, immediately converts to Roth. Over 20 years at 7%, the $29K alone grows to $1.35M+ tax-free. Compared to a brokerage account paying yearly tax on gains, hundreds of thousands of dollars in difference.

### HR question to ask [00:02:38]

Exact phrasing: 'Does our 401(k) plan allow after-tax contributions with in-service withdrawals or conversions to Roth?' Some plans auto-convert; some require a manual step; some convert to Roth 401(k); others to Roth IRA.

### Who should consider this [00:04:27]

High earners who already max standard 401(k) and have surplus cash flow. Not for everyone — requires maxed contribution first plus extra disposable income.
