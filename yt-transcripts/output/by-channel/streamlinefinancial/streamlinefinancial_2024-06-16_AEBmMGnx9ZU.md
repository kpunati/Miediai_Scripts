# 4 Reasons To Do ROTH Conversions If You Have A Pension

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-06-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AEBmMGnx9ZU
**Duration:** 11:54

## Summary

Dave Zoller argues retirees with pensions have a stronger case for Roth conversions than those without. Three reasons: (1) pensions plus Social Security plus RMDs at 73 can push retirees into higher brackets they don't need the cash for; (2) higher taxable income drives Medicare IRMAA surcharges; (3) the surviving spouse's tax filing status (single vs married) compounds the bracket impact. Recommends using the pre-RMD/pre-Social-Security window as the Roth conversion 'sweet spot.'

## Key Takeaways

- Retirees with pensions face a stronger Roth-conversion case because of compounding taxable-income sources.
- Three income sources at age 73+: pension + Social Security + RMDs — often more than the retiree needs.
- Historical income tax rates are near historic lows; many experts expect them to rise given US federal debt.
- Roth conversion sweet spot: pre-RMD (before 73) and pre-Social-Security (before 70).
- Roth IRAs have no RMD — converting now permanently reduces future RMD-driven taxable income.
- Qualified Charitable Distributions can satisfy RMDs without creating taxable income.
- IRMAA Medicare surcharges (2024) jump significantly above ~$206K joint income — Roth conversions can keep you below.
- Widow tax trap: surviving spouse drops from married-filing-jointly brackets to single-filer brackets, often increasing taxes — Roth conversions ahead of time provide flexibility.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Roth conversions are popular now [00:00:00]

Two big reasons: (1) most retirement savings is pre-tax — every withdrawal is taxed at ordinary income rates; (2) historical tax rates are near historic lows, and given US debt, many expect rates to rise.

### Looking at historical tax rates [00:01:02]

Tax rates have varied wildly across decades. Today's rates are very low by historical standards. Projection math often supports paying tax now via conversion.

### Pension-specific consideration [00:02:04]

Pensions add a third taxable income stream alongside Social Security and RMDs. Many of our clients with pensions don't NEED the RMD income at 73 but are forced to take it (and pay tax).

### Roth conversion sweet spot timing [00:03:09]

The window with most flexibility: pre-RMD (under 73) and pre-Social-Security (under 70). Pensions and other income are largely fixed; this is where you have control.

### Roth IRAs have no RMD [00:04:13]

Money converted to Roth doesn't require distributions at age 73 — permanently reduces RMD-driven taxable income.

### Don't need RMD? QCD option [00:05:18]

If you don't need the RMD income, a Qualified Charitable Distribution sends money directly from IRA to a 501(c)(3). Satisfies the RMD without creating taxable income for you or the charity.

### IRMAA Medicare surcharges [00:06:21]

Higher taxable income drives Medicare Part B/D IRMAA surcharges, based on income from two years prior. 2024 brackets: standard $174/mo for joint <$206K, jumps significantly above, nearly doubles at top.

### Widow tax trap [00:08:27]

Married-filing-jointly brackets ~$206K become single-filer ~$103K when one spouse passes away. The surviving spouse can be pushed into a much higher bracket and IRMAA tier. Roth conversions ahead of time provide more flexibility.

### Recap and action step [00:09:32]

Recap: historical low rates, RMD/SS/pension stacking, IRMAA, widow trap. Action: use a Roth-conversion calculator and consult a CPA/CFP for your specific scenario.
