# Money, Meaning & Making a Difference with Jessica Jackley

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-04-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=wSEMoEMpyIM
**Duration:** 50:50

## Summary

Tim Meisenheimer interviews Jessica Jackley (co-founder of Kiva, USC social entrepreneurship professor) on generosity. Covers the Altadena fires displacing her family, broken giving systems (uneven GoFundMe outcomes, private firefighting), the ~$300B/yr volunteer economy as wildly under-designed, her Kiva origin (accidental entrepreneur, microloans), and her own money-scarcity beliefs (her mother's tutoring hours commoditizing her time).

## Key Takeaways

- After the LA wildfires Jackley saw the giving infrastructure: lots of generosity, but broken matching/distribution systems.
- Kiva originated from an unpaid East Africa internship — Jessica was an 'accidental entrepreneur,' not a goal-driven one.
- The volunteer economy is ~$300B/yr of donated time — wildly under-designed. Most platforms aren't built for the volunteer experience.
- Single moms volunteer more than any other demographic segment.
- Structure-as-destiny: nonprofit vs for-profit isn't binary; 4 of her board memberships have converted from one to the other.
- Best advice to high-schoolers entering finance/accounting: name and prioritize your values BEFORE you start; you become what muscles you exercise.
- Money is great but not the most important thing — work joyfully, beautifully, redemptively.
- Kiva community feature: Streamline created a $25/loan team for clients circa 2009.

## Detailed Notes

### Eaton Canyon fires: the dinner that turned [00:02:08]

Family was at Taco Tuesday when a tree flicker became a mountainside rip-up. Evacuated 4 hours later with masks. Didn't lose house but landlord is selling. 6 of 24 preschool families lost homes; 2 of 4 teachers.

### Inequities revealed in giving systems [00:06:08]

Altadena (where Black families bought homes during civil rights era) vs Palisades — same disaster, very different outcomes via GoFundMe response. Friends in Palisades hit $100K targets; friends in Altadena couldn't hit $15-20K.

### Generosity is high; systems are broken [00:08:50]

People want to give but local schools/centers fill up; folks default to GoFundMe. Wealthy pockets hire private firefighters. Need better systems for rapid-response philanthropy beyond personal networks.

### The volunteer economy: ~$300B and under-designed [00:12:12]

95% of people say they want to volunteer but can't find opportunities/can't bring kids/can't schedule. ~$300B/yr in donated time and expertise. Single moms volunteer most. Almost no platforms designed FOR the volunteer experience.

### Altruisticly: DIY service project boxes for families [00:12:12]

Jackley's first attempt — 12 monthly boxes (unhoused, food insecurity, pollinators, etc.) with both education and action. Working on the next iteration with a nonprofit partner.

### Structure as destiny: nonprofit ↔ for-profit [00:15:57]

Four boards she's served on have converted nonprofit → for-profit. Speculates many ventures should start as nonprofits (subsidized capital, room to experiment) and transition. Taboo and fear around the transition is overblown.

### Asking for advice vs asking for money [00:18:17]

Classic wisdom: ask for advice you get money; ask for money you get advice. The boomer generation transferring trillions has appetite for purposeful involvement — give them ownership/expertise opportunities, not just check-writing roles.

### Kids book about giving: the best thing to give is yourself [00:19:21]

Jessica wrote 'A Kid's Book About Giving' (akidsco.com). Pushes back on the make-a-pile-then-give-later default — your day-to-day actions already express values, not just retirement check-writing.

### Money mindset: tutoring hours and the price tag on her mother's time [00:34:09]

A coaching exercise revealed her core belief: wealth means compromise; entrepreneurs = gang leaders of business. Origin: as a child, learning her mother (a schoolteacher) tutored after-school for money — felt nauseated that her mother's time had a price tag. Has dissociated her time from a dollar value as a defense.

### Pursuing joy, beauty, redemption — paid mortgages followed [00:34:49]

Despite avoiding money as a driver, has always paid mortgage/rent. Pursued joy/beauty/redemption first; the financial outcomes followed. Now reckoning with her own bias.

### Closing advice to high-schoolers in finance/accounting [00:46:36]

Name and prioritize your values BEFORE entering a profession. Work builds muscles — law trains you to find risk, entrepreneurship trains you to find opportunity. You become what you exercise. Write your future self a note from your present self.

### Streamline Kiva community circa 2009 [00:27:14]

Tim recounts setting up a Streamline Kiva lending team in 2009 after seeing Jessica interviewed at a leadership summit. Clients lent $25/each to bakers in Afghanistan, goat herders in Uganda, farmers in Peru.
