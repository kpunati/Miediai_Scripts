# Common Withdrawal Mistakes Retirees Make

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-07-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=M1fq6ONPHzc
**Duration:** 10:04

## Summary

Dave Zoller covers three common retirement-withdrawal mistakes: (1) following the default 'taxable first, then tax-deferred, then Roth' order without modeling — can lead to forced early Social Security and inflated RMDs; (2) not mapping fluctuating spending across go-go vs slow-go years; (3) misaligning investments with the income plan, so a sequence-of-returns shock forces selling at a loss. Cites the Vanguard Advisor Alpha study claiming withdrawal-order choice is worth up to 1.2%/yr.

## Key Takeaways

- Choosing the right withdrawal-account order can be worth up to 1.2%/yr (Vanguard Advisor Alpha).
- The conventional 'taxable-first, then tax-deferred, then Roth' rule isn't optimal for everyone.
- Defaulting to taxable-first can deplete that bucket faster, push you into early Social Security, and create outsized RMDs at 73+.
- Model multiple what-if scenarios: blended withdrawals, partial Roth conversions, varied Social Security claim ages.
- Plan for variable spending — go-go years, one-time events (weddings, big trips), then slower years.
- Align investments with the income plan to survive sequence-of-returns shocks: short-term, mid-term, long-term buckets.
- Cover the four economic seasons (rising/slowing inflation × rising/slowing growth) so something is always available to tap.

## Detailed Notes

### The 1.2% withdrawal alpha [00:00:00]

Dave Zoller cites Vanguard Advisor Alpha: withdrawal sequencing decisions can add up to 1.2%/yr. Most people don't optimize and default to rules of thumb.

### Mistake 1: Wrong withdrawal order [00:01:04]

Conventional rule: taxable first (to avoid yearly dividend/interest tax), then tax-deferred, then Roth. Problem: depletes taxable faster, can force early Social Security claiming and inflate the IRA at RMD age, raising taxes and Medicare premiums.

### What-if scenarios for withdrawal order [00:03:10]

Model alternatives: full taxable + early SS; delayed taxable + delayed SS; blended taxable + IRA + delayed SS; early Roth conversions to shrink future RMDs. Use planning software or a retirement specialist to narrow to the top 2-3 options.

### Mistake 2: Static spending assumption [00:05:14]

Most plans assume flat spending × 3% inflation. Reality: go-go years have higher discretionary spend (travel, hobbies, family events), then plateau, then slow-go years. Bake these variations in so you don't feel forced to pinch pennies.

### Mapping variable expenses [00:06:19]

Include one-time big expenses (weddings, big trips) and bursts of higher travel spend so you can see the plan still holds — confidence to actually do those things.

### Mistake 3: Investments misaligned with income plan [00:07:21]

If a longer-than-expected bear market or new economic season hits, the wrong allocation forces selling at the bottom — sequence-of-returns risk. Two fixes: (1) cover the four economic seasons so something is always producing; (2) bucket strategy sized to the withdrawal amount.

### Bucket sizing example [00:08:25]

Hypothetical $10K/yr withdrawal need: ~$20K in short-term (1-2 years), ~$100K in medium-term (modest growth/interest), the rest in long-term growth + inflation protection. Numbers illustrative.

### Three-step recap [00:09:27]

(1) Find your optimal withdrawal-order via what-if modeling — worth up to 1.2%/yr; (2) plan for variable spending across retirement phases; (3) align investments with the income plan so any season is survivable.
