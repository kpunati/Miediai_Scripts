# The Worst Retirement Advice People Actually Follow

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2026-01-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=YU2_sbH6Txs
**Duration:** 9:28

## Summary

Dave Zoller compares two early-2020 retirees: Jane and Kevin. Both had solid plans before COVID hit. Kevin followed seemingly-prudent 'go to cash and wait for clarity' advice, sold during the March 2020 crash, and never got back in. Jane used a three-bucket strategy (seven years of conservative buffer), then executed tax-loss harvesting, Roth conversions during the dip, and rebalancing into stocks at lower prices.

## Key Takeaways

- Two early-2020 retirees with identical plans had wildly different outcomes based on March 2020 decisions.
- The 'go to cash and wait for clarity' advice is the pessimism trap — pessimism sounds smart but optimism makes money.
- Jane used a three-bucket strategy: 7 years of conservative withdrawals meant she didn't have to sell growth investments during the drawdown.
- Three moves during the crash: tax-loss harvesting, evaluate Roth conversions while values are down, rebalance from conservative to stocks.
- Kevin went to cash and never got back in — the next 'better' moment never arrived.
- Market timing requires being right twice (sell and buy) — almost nobody can.
- Kevin lost more than money — he lost time and peace of mind worrying about markets.

## Detailed Notes

### Two retirees, same plan, different outcomes [00:00:00]

Jane and Kevin both retired early 2020 with solid plans. Same starting setup. One piece of seemingly-smart advice destroyed Kevin's plan while Jane thrived.

### Backstory: solid plans, but no more contributions [00:00:44]

Both retirees lived through 2001 dotcom, 2008 GFC, and corrections in between. The new dynamic: no contributions, only withdrawals — bear markets hit differently.

### March 2020 crash and the choice [00:02:07]

Market dropped 30%+ in weeks. Peter Lynch quote: 'far more money has been lost by investors preparing for corrections than has been lost in the actual corrections.' Jane and Kevin face a decision.

### Jane's three-bucket setup [00:03:11]

Seven years of withdrawals in conservative buckets — those were positive YTD even when growth was down 20-30%. Allowed her to focus on the gain (buffer), not the gap (drawdown). She didn't need to touch growth investments.

### Kevin and the pessimism trap [00:04:04]

Kevin followed doom-and-gloom influencers and sold to cash. Pessimism feels intellectually superior — sophisticated language, historical charts, predicting disaster feels like outsmarting the naive. Warren Buffett: 'pessimism sounds smart, but optimism makes money.'

### Jane's three moves during the dip [00:05:45]

(1) Tax-loss harvesting: sell at a loss, buy similar to keep allocation — thousands in tax savings. (2) Roth conversions while values are depressed — same tax cost, more shares. (3) Rebalance from conservative to stocks at lower prices.

### Kevin couldn't get back in [00:06:49]

Markets recovered in months. Kevin kept saying 'not yet — double dip coming' — the next reason to stay out kept multiplying. Market timing requires being right twice. He lost months and years of retirement that he can't get back.

### Lost more than money — peace of mind [00:07:51]

Kevin's emotional decision consumed his thinking. Retirement needs investments handled but not worried-about daily. Jane recovered and came out ahead with more money and more peace of mind — following a system instead of emotions.
