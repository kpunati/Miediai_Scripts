# My 3 Step Retirement Formula

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2025-03-27

## Summary

Dave Zoller's 3-step retirement formula: (1) write down the monthly dollar amount you want, multiply by 12 — guess if needed; (2) subtract guaranteed income (usually Social Security); (3) divide the gap by 4% to get the investment target. Worked example: $6K/month × 12 = $72K need; -$24K SS = $48K gap; ÷ 4% = $1.2M target. Caveats: the 4% rule has flaws, tax-efficient income planning and inflation hedging matter.

## Key Takeaways

- Step 1: Write down the monthly dollar amount you want in retirement (multiply by 12). Start with a guess if needed.
- Optional: split the number into needs and wants — Dave prefers starting with just needs.
- Step 2: Subtract guaranteed income (usually Social Security).
- Step 3: Divide the gap by 4% (0.04) to get the investment target.
- Worked example: $6K/month → $72K/yr → $48K gap after $24K SS → $1.2M target.
- Caveats: the 4% rule has flaws; tax-efficient income planning, inflation hedging, and the four economic seasons matter.
- Use Monarch Money or similar to get a precise expense number over 3-6 months.

## Detailed Notes

### Step 1: monthly income number × 12 [00:00:00]

Write down what monthly amount you want in retirement. If you don't know, guess in $5K/7K/$10K buckets. Multiply by 12. Optionally split needs (housing, food, insurance) from wants (travel, hobbies, gifts) — Dave prefers starting with needs.

### Step 2: subtract guaranteed income (usually SS) [00:01:03]

Subtract Social Security and any pension or guaranteed sources. Example: $72K need - $24K SS = $48K gap that investments must fund.

### Step 3: divide the gap by 4% [00:02:05]

$48K ÷ 0.04 = $1.2M investment target. Approximate amount needed for a 30-year retirement at that gap. Pause for the success-rate chart.

### Caveats: the 4% rule has flaws [00:03:08]

The 4% rule is a starting point but has flaws (covered in other videos). Real optimization includes tax-efficient income planning, inflation hedging, four-economic-seasons preparation.
