# Ideal Order of Successful Retirement Plans

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-09-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=kQXpOFh25o8
**Duration:** 10:15

## Summary

Dave Zoller lays out the order Streamline uses for successful retirement plans: (1) start with the income plan (vision of retirement, then numbers), not investments or risk tolerance; (2) let the income plan drive the investment plan; (3) control what's controllable — taxes (tax-efficient funds and tax-loss harvesting), costs (lower-fee fiduciary advice), and risk (preparing for all four economic seasons rather than guessing).

## Key Takeaways

- Start with the income plan (life-first, then numbers) — not with investments or a risk-tolerance quiz.
- Risk tolerance fluctuates wildly (2008 vs. 2020); the income plan is the stable anchor.
- Three things to control in investing: tax, cost, and risk.
- Tax-efficient funds/ETFs avoid passing capital gains to you when you didn't sell — beware tax inefficiency in mutual funds.
- Tax-loss harvesting can offset gains for material tax savings (~$8K in the example case).
- Choose lower-cost investments — fee differences compound.
- Risk = preparing for all four economic seasons (high/low growth, high/low inflation), not guessing what comes next.

## Detailed Notes

### Model what successful retirees do — there's an order [00:00:00]

Reframes 'risk tolerance first' approach. Successful retirees focused on income plan and tax planning have lower taxes, lower risk, more confidence.

### Step 1: income plan before investments [00:01:04]

Don't start with account balances and risk tolerance. Start with the vision for retirement and what income that requires. Numbers fit the life, not vice versa.

### Risk tolerance is unstable; the income plan isn't [00:02:06]

Risk tolerance in 2008 or March 2020 is very different than today. The income plan is the stable anchor. 'Least risk while maintaining highest plan success rate.'

### Story: John's $60K surprise capital gain tax bill [00:03:09]

John picked up his taxes expecting normalcy; saw $60K of capital gains from an advisor-driven reallocation. At top bracket on short-term gains and dividends — ~35% tax.

### Tax-efficient fund selection [00:04:11]

Some funds and ETFs pass capital gains and distributions to holders even without sales. Use tax-efficient vehicles so you control when gains are recognized.

### Tax-loss harvesting case math [00:06:18]

$20K capital gain at ~35% = $7K tax. Available unrealized losses absorbed the gain and rolled $2K to the next year — ~$8K total tax savings.

### Cost: fee differences compound [00:07:21]

0.6% vs. 0.05% on the same risk/return/efficiency is an easy choice — but many advisors don't pick low-cost because their commissions favor high-cost. Fiduciary CFPs prioritize lower-cost vehicles.

### Risk: four economic seasons, not guesses [00:08:23]

Higher/lower-than-expected growth and inflation produce four seasons. Don't guess which is next — own asset classes that perform across each so the income plan stays intact.
