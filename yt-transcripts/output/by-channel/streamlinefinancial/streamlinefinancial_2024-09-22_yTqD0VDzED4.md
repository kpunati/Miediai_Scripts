# Spousal Social Security Explained (w/ Examples)

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-09-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=yTqD0VDzED4
**Duration:** 13:52

## Summary

Dave Zoller explains spousal Social Security: the spousal benefit is up to 50% of the higher earner's full retirement age benefit but is reduced if claimed before the spouse's own FRA. Key rules: the spouse claiming a spousal benefit can't claim until the higher earner has; the spousal benefit is based on the claimant's own age, not the spouse's; divorced spouses (married 10+ years, not remarried) can claim on an ex's record without the ex knowing or being affected.

## Key Takeaways

- Spousal Social Security can be up to 50% of the higher earner's full retirement age benefit — but only if the claimant waits until their own FRA.
- Claiming at 62 reduces the worker's benefit to ~70% AND reduces the spousal benefit to ~35% of the higher earner's FRA amount.
- The spousal-benefit reduction is based on the claimant's own age, not the higher earner's age.
- The spouse taking the spousal benefit can't start until the higher earner has started theirs (married case).
- Health concerns, lack of other income, or break-even calculations can make claiming at 62 the right call.
- File and suspend was killed in 2016 — that loophole is gone.
- Divorced spouses married 10+ years (and not remarried) can claim on an ex's record without affecting or notifying the ex.

## Detailed Notes

### Why the claiming date matters [00:00:00]

The date you start Social Security affects your benefit, your spouse's spousal benefit, and lifetime household income — one case study saved over $100K. Inspired by viewer Bill.

### Linda and John at 62: claim now or wait? [00:01:03]

Both 62. They wanted income now to delay tapping investments. Walking through the trade-offs.

### Reductions: 70% at 62, 35% spousal benefit [00:02:06]

If John claims at 62, he gets 70% of FRA = $2,100/mo (vs $3,000 at FRA 67). Linda's spousal at 62 = 35% of John's FRA = $1,050/mo.

### Spousal benefit basis: claimant's own age [00:03:10]

Spousal is computed off the higher earner's FRA amount but reduced by the claimant's own age at claiming.

### Lifetime cost of early claiming [00:04:12]

Combined reduction = $1,350/mo less. Over 20-30 years, the lifetime cost is roughly $100K+. Alternative: live on investments first, let benefits grow.

### Two key rules for spousal benefits (married) [00:05:14]

Rule 1: spousal claimant can't start until the higher earner does. Rule 2: spousal reduction is based on the claimant's own age.

### Example: Linda 67 (FRA), John claims at 62 [00:06:18]

If Linda is at her own FRA and John claims early at 62, Linda's spousal is still 50% of his FRA ($1,500). John's early claim doesn't reduce her spousal.

### Example: John 67 at FRA, Linda 62 [00:07:21]

Reverse case. John gets full $3,000; Linda at 62 still gets only 35% of his FRA. Each year Linda waits until her FRA, the spousal grows toward 50%.

### Delay to 70: worker's benefit grows 8%/year past FRA, spousal does not [00:08:26]

Waiting to 70 gives John $3,720/mo (124% of FRA). But Linda's spousal is still capped at 50% of John's FRA — delay only helps the worker, not the spousal.

### When claiming early at 62 makes sense [00:09:29]

Health concerns shortening expected lifespan, no other income sources, or break-even math (often mid-80s) supporting early claiming.

### File-and-suspend gone (2016) [00:10:36]

The pre-2016 strategy of claiming one's own benefit early then switching to spousal at FRA is no longer available.

### Divorced-spouse benefits [00:11:40]

If married 10+ years and not remarried, can claim on an ex's record once they turn 62 — even if the ex hasn't started benefits. The ex is not notified and not affected.

### Case: Susan claims on ex-husband's record [00:12:42]

A real client (Susan) didn't realize she qualified for benefits on her ex-spouse's record — added meaningful retirement income with zero impact on the ex.
