# Retirement Feels Better When You Do This

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2026-01-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=YSvJgs4LvYg
**Duration:** 4:05

## Summary

Dave Zoller introduces the 'small joy principle' — once retirement quiets the big milestones, small everyday moments (long lines, traffic, dropped Wi-Fi) start to dominate emotional life. If small annoyances can ruin a day, small joys can make one. Recommends a nightly 3-small-joys list to train attention, build gratitude, and recover the feeling that life is full.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement changes the scoreboard: from how much you do to how your days actually feel.
- If small annoyances can ruin a day, small joys can make a day — the small joy principle.
- Lowering the bar for what counts as joy increases the amount of joy you experience.
- Practice: each night, write down three small joys or wins.
- Two effects: trains your brain to notice the good, and builds gratitude.
- Gratitude is one of the most research-backed reliable paths to lasting happiness.

## Detailed Notes

### Why retirement feels different [00:00:00]

For decades life is measured by promotions, milestones, big trips. Retirement quietly changes the scoreboard to how days actually feel. Small things — long lines, Wi-Fi dropping, someone cutting you off — disproportionately throw off balance.

### The small joy principle [00:00:47]

If small things can ruin a day, they can also make one. Not about lowering standards — about changing what you pay attention to. Retirement quiets the noise so ordinary moments become more visible (walk after dinner, morning coffee, laughter from another room).

### Is this all there is? [00:01:50]

Many retirees subconsciously feel 'is this really all there is?' Reframing it: the question isn't how to create bigger moments but how to notice the small ones already there. Lowering the joy bar paradoxically produces more joy.

### Practice: three small joys per day [00:02:39]

Each night, write down three small wins or joys — nothing impressive, nothing social-media-worthy. Calling an old friend, a soup that turned out right, watching birds. Trains noticing the good; builds gratitude — research says gratitude is one of the most reliable paths to lasting happiness.
