# I Retired With Millions BUT Made 6 BIG Mistakes

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-05-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=XMgFR40Jmck
**Duration:** 13:42

## Summary

Dave Zoller walks through six post-sale mistakes business owner client 'Dan' made transitioning into retirement: (1) underestimating loss of control, (2) lifestyle-expense changes when business deductions vanish, (3) jumping into 'attractive' deals too fast, (4) missing pre-sale tax strategies like charitable pre-funding, (5) skipping asset-protection planning, (6) handing wealth to unprepared heirs. Opens with Arthur Brooks' fluid-vs-crystallized intelligence frame.

## Key Takeaways

- Selling a business is more than a financial event — identity and control shifts often blindside successful owners.
- Arthur Brooks' fluid-vs-crystallized intelligence frame: aging shifts strength from raw processing to accumulated wisdom.
- Mistake 1: Lost control after sale — manage by controlling outcome ranges (tax, withdrawal plan) instead.
- Mistake 2: Lifestyle costs change when business deductions go away — many business owners under-budget for personal expenses.
- Mistake 3: Attractive investment deals flood in post-sale — wait, build the plan first, then size private deals to a target allocation.
- Mistake 4: Pre-sale charitable prefunding (e.g., gifting business interest to a DAF) can cut taxes dramatically.
- Mistake 5: Post-liquidity asset/creditor protection is often more important than tax or investment optimization.
- Mistake 6: Estate planning ≠ wealth transfer — values, family history, and intent matter alongside legal documents.

## Detailed Notes

### Setup: Dan's story and six mistakes [00:00:00]

Dan built a successful company, sold it, then realized he made several mistakes during the transition — two lifestyle, one investment, one tax, and two legal/estate. Useful for any retiree, not just business owners.

### Fluid vs crystallized intelligence (Arthur Brooks) [00:01:03]

From Strength to Strength: fluid intelligence (raw problem-solving) peaks early and declines; crystallized intelligence (wisdom, depth of experience) keeps growing. Reframes business-owner retirement as a strength shift rather than a loss.

### Seller's remorse after the sale [00:04:12]

Selling a business can feel like a child leaving home — you can't help it anymore. Awareness of the intelligence-shift can reframe that loss.

### Mistake 1: Loss of control [00:05:14]

Business owner had granular control over outcomes; after the sale, much of the liquidity moves into passive investments with little control. Solution: shift the goal from control of decisions to control of outcome ranges (taxes, withdrawal sequencing, investment risk).

### Mistake 2: Lifestyle change underestimated [00:06:18]

As an owner, Dan deducted a lot of lifestyle (cars, dining, travel). Post-sale that disappears — but expenses he hadn't tracked personally now hit cash. Need a draft income/withdrawal plan even if 'running out' isn't the worry.

### Mistake 3: Investment FOMO post-sale [00:07:20]

After the sale, cash on hand attracts pitches. Advice: wait, build the plan first, then size private/alternative deals to a deliberate allocation. Don't let the trait that made you successful (risk appetite) lead you into speculative concentration.

### Mistake 4: Missed pre-sale tax reduction [00:08:26]

Another client gifted a percentage of his business to a DAF in the two high-income pre-sale years — cut taxes meaningfully and prefunded the next 30 years of giving. Also includes a real-estate-share-to-DAF anecdote where a partner contributed 5% of a building to his DAF to fund a $100K grant.

### Mistake 5: Asset/creditor protection ignored [00:11:36]

Post-liquidity, asset-protection planning matters more than ever — often a bigger risk than tax/investment mistakes. Review through that lens.

### Mistake 6: Wealth transfer vs estate planning [00:12:39]

Estate planning = legal docs, taxes, asset distribution. Wealth transfer = values, family history, charitable intent passed alongside dollars. Both need to work together — most estate plans skip the wealth-transfer conversation.
