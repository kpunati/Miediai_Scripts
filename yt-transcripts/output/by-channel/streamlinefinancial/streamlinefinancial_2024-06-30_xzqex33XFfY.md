# He Never Expected THIS Problem In Retirement

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-06-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=xzqex33XFfY
**Duration:** 8:34

## Summary

Dave Zoller shares a real client (early-50s retiree, one year in) who faced unexpected social pressure: family and acquaintances kept saying he was too young, asking what he'd do all day. Reframes the answer with three practices — rest, test, learn — plus play. Highlights investing not just money but also TIME in three areas often missed by financial advice: curiosities (hobbies/passions), relationships, and contribution to others.

## Key Takeaways

- Many early retirees face unexpected social pressure: people keep asking what you'll do all day.
- A few months into retirement, the 'is this really all I'm going to do' subconscious doubt is common.
- Framework for answering 'what will you do?': rest, test, learn (plus play).
- Test = explore curiosities and hobbies you couldn't make time for (welding, gardening, writing, etc.).
- Investing isn't just financial — invest time in curiosities, relationships, and contribution.
- The most fulfilled retirees use money and time in service of others, not just for themselves.
- When humans ask 'what do you do?' they're really asking 'how do you help people?' — work has often been the answer.

## Detailed Notes

### The unexpected problem: social pressure [00:00:00]

A real client (early-50s, one year retired) hit unexpected negative reactions from family, friends, and new acquaintances: 'you're too young to retire,' 'what will you do all day?' Dragged on him and led to second-guessing.

### The 'is this all there is' subconscious doubt [00:01:03]

Common a few months in: even with hobbies and travel, retirees can subconsciously wonder 'is this really all I'm going to do?' Another retired business owner described the first few months as 'boring as heck' before finding fulfillment on the other side.

### What people are really asking [00:02:08]

When someone asks 'what do you do?' they're really asking 'how do you help people?' Work was a default answer for contribution and value to society — retirement breaks that link unless you actively rebuild it.

### Rest, test, learn [00:03:12]

Three practices for the early retirement question. Rest (most people get this part). Test (try out curiosities and hobbies you couldn't before). Learn (deepen into what fascinates you).

### The welding-attorney story [00:04:14]

An attorney client became fascinated by welding videos. Took a class, then a few months later invested in his own welding equipment in his garage. Spending money on curiosity is a valid 'investment' — alongside the financial side.

### Three non-financial investments [00:05:18]

Beyond optimizing the money side, invest time and money in: (1) curiosities (new hobbies/passions), (2) relationships (especially in-person time, even with travel costs), (3) contribution to others (the happiest retirees use money/time in service of others).

### Listening as contribution [00:06:21]

One client invested 60-90 minutes mostly listening to a friend — paid big dividends to both. Contribution doesn't have to be money or grand gestures; listening counts.

### Rest, test, learn, play [00:07:23]

Closing answer set: rest, test, learn, play. Don't feel bad if family pushes back about retiring young. Retirement is a chance to maximize curiosity, connection, contribution — not stop them.
