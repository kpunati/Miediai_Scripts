# How Trump Can Impact Your Financial Future

**Channel:** Streamline Financial
**Published:** 2024-11-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=dgCG8VM7roY
**Duration:** 12:58

## Summary

Dave Zoller analyzes how the 2024 Trump election outcome (Republican president plus Republican House and Senate) could affect markets, taxes, inflation, and Social Security. Data shows party in power has no statistically significant effect on S&P 500 returns (1933-2022). With Republican control, the 2017 TCJA tax cuts are now more likely to extend past 2025, giving more flexibility for Roth conversions, capital-gains harvesting, and charitable bunching with.

## Key Takeaways

- Historical data: party in power has no statistically significant impact on S&P 500 returns — politics doesn't drive long-term markets.
- Sector outcomes often defy assumption (clean energy outperformed under Trump; traditional energy under Biden).
- With Republican trifecta, TCJA tax cuts are likely to extend past 2025 — buying more time for Roth conversions.
- Lower tax rates extending past 2025 means more flexibility for capital-gains harvesting strategies.
- Charitable bunching with a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) helps high-standard-deduction filers capture itemized-deduction benefit.
- Markets reacted in two directions: stocks up on growth optimism; bond yields up on inflation/deficit concerns.
- Prepare for higher-than-expected inflation seasons via four-quadrant allocation, not just inflation-protected bonds.
- Trump committed to protecting Social Security benefits; eliminating tax on benefits would speed trust-fund depletion.

## Detailed Notes

### Framing: market, tax, economy, inflation, Social Security [00:00:00]

Dave will cover each domain and give practical takeaways. Streamline has been helping people through every political cycle since the Clinton era.

### Sector myth: clean vs. traditional energy under each party [00:01:01]

Clean energy outperformed traditional 43%/year under Trump; reverse under Biden (traditional outperformed by 53%/year). Sector outcomes defy assumed political tilts.

### Party in power has no significant market impact [00:02:03]

1957-2022: average S&P 500 returns 9.3% (D) vs. 10.2% (R) — not statistically meaningful. Same conclusion holds even with one-party-controls-everything scenarios from 1933-2022.

### Tax planning preview: TCJA likely to extend [00:03:07]

With Republican trifecta, TCJA provisions (lower individual rates, higher standard deduction, child tax credit, lower corporate rate, estate exemption) more likely to extend past 2025.

### Practical tax moves: Roth conversions and capital gains [00:04:10]

Possibility of TCJA extension gives more years to spread Roth conversions across at favorable rates. Same flexibility applies to long-term capital gains harvesting.

### Charitable bunching with a DAF [00:05:11]

With high standard deduction, fewer households itemize. Bunch giving into one year (double up to $20K, itemize to $40K) using a DAF — then skip the next year. Charities still get regular payments via the DAF.

### Inflation and bond-market signals [00:08:24]

Stocks surged on tax-cut/growth optimism. Bond yields jumped — market signaling deficit-spending-and-tariff-driven inflation risk even with Fed cutting rates.

### Four-quadrant economic-season planning [00:09:26]

Diversify across higher/lower growth and higher/lower inflation seasons. Not betting on inflation — preparing for it. Inflation-protected bonds are one option.

### Cash allocation: percentage, not feeling-driven [00:10:30]

Resist the urge to hold extra cash during uncertainty — bucket strategy delivers peace of mind without giving up returns to inflation.

### Social Security: no benefit cuts promised [00:11:32]

Trump committed to no benefit cuts and no retirement-age increase. Proposed elimination of income tax on Social Security would accelerate trust-fund depletion.

### Closing: focus on your strategy, not the politics [00:12:34]

Don't change your Social Security strategy based on the election. Focus on the specific claiming approach (married, divorced).
