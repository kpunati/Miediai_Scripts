# The ROTH Conversion TRAP That Destroyed $1M in Tax-Free Wealth

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=xG07OC5LJtQ
**Duration:** 16:51

## Summary

Kevin Lum, CFP at Foundry Financial, challenges the conventional wisdom that retirees should wait for a post-retirement 'golden window' to begin Roth conversions. The key insight is that the earlier conversions start — even while still working — the more time assets have to compound tax-free, and for households with extra cash flow, the tax-cost can be paid from outside the IRA, magnifying the benefit. Using RightCapital software and a case study of a dual-income couple (Phil and Claire) with $1.4 million in deferred accounts, Lum demonstrates that starting conversions at age 60 to the top of the 24% bracket produces a $2.4 million lifetime advantage over waiting until retirement. The video covers tax bracket mechanics, investment selection, the role of outside cash, and two mechanics for converting while still working: in-plan Roth conversions and IRA rollovers.

## Key Takeaways

- Timing is the most impactful variable in Roth conversion success: the earlier the conversion, the more years for tax-free growth to compound.
- The 'golden window' (retirement to RMD age) is theoretically optimal for low-bracket conversions, but high earners with excess cash flow can profitably convert at 24% while still working.
- Paying conversion taxes from outside the IRA preserves the full converted amount inside the Roth; using IRA funds to pay taxes reduces the effective conversion by the tax liability.
- Investment selection inside the Roth matters — high-expected-return assets (equities) benefit most from the tax-free growth shelter; money market funds do not justify the conversion cost.
- There is no universal bracket rule; whether to convert at 12%, 22%, 24%, or even higher depends on the individual's full tax picture, not a blanket guideline.
- Two mechanics exist for converting while still working: in-plan 401(k) Roth conversions (where the plan permits) or rolling old 401(k) balances to a traditional IRA and then converting.

## Detailed Notes

### Roth Conversion Basics and Why Timing Dominates [00:01:02]

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) into a Roth IRA, triggering ordinary income tax on the converted amount but eliminating all future taxes on growth and distributions. Beyond tax deferral, Roth money avoids RMDs, reduces Medicare IRMAA exposure, sidesteps additional Social Security taxation, and can protect against the widows penalty. Lum argues that the single most important factor — more than bracket selection or investment choice — is the timing of when conversions begin.

### Tax Bracket Mechanics and the Fill-the-Bracket Strategy [00:02:05]

Tax brackets function like a waterfall: only the portion of income in each tier is taxed at that rate. For a married couple, this means there can be substantial headroom between current income and the next bracket threshold. Smart Roth conversions fill that headroom without spilling into the next bracket. Lum cautions that there is no single correct bracket to target — individual circumstances, including future income sources, RMD projections, and state taxes, all affect the optimal level.

### Factors That Determine Conversion Power [00:05:15]

Three factors amplify conversion effectiveness: (1) investment selection — high-return equities compound far more tax-free than cash equivalents; (2) the bracket chosen — must reflect the full household tax picture, not a rule of thumb; and (3) whether taxes are paid from external cash — funding conversion taxes from a taxable account leaves the full converted amount growing tax-free inside the Roth, while using IRA funds to pay taxes reduces the effective amount converted.

### Case Study: Phil and Claire — Converting While Still Working [00:08:26]

Phil and Claire are both 60, still working, earning $120,000 each ($240,000 combined), plan to retire at 65, and have $1.4 million total in 401(k) accounts with no outside cash. Because they spend only $8,000 per month, they have significant surplus cash flow. RightCapital modeling shows they can convert ~$180,000 per year to stay inside the 24% bracket at a federal tax cost of ~$43,000, and that starting this at age 60 rather than waiting for retirement generates a $2.4 million lifetime advantage, compared to less than $400,000 for waiting until their golden window.

### Mechanics of Converting While Working [00:14:42]

Two options exist: an in-plan Roth conversion within a 401(k) that offers both traditional and Roth options, or rolling a prior employer's 401(k) balance to a traditional IRA and then converting from the IRA. The latter involves caveats — the ability to roll over while still contributing to the active plan varies by employer — and Lum notes that a full treatment of rollover rules is beyond this video's scope.
