# Don’t Do a Roth Conversion — Until You Watch This

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-06-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=wrwBl3TGzCc
**Duration:** 17:28

## Summary

Kevin Lum, CFP at Foundry Financial, argues that Roth conversion projections shown by calculators are directionally useful but should not be treated as precise forecasts because they are built on a 'tower of guesses': unknown future tax rates, investment returns, inflation, and spousal death sequencing. He identifies three situations where conversions are inadvisable — small IRA balances headed for full spend-down, retirees who will spend to the top of their bracket anyway, and highly charitable individuals using QCDs. He then provides five year-by-year factors to evaluate: target tax bracket, IRMAA thresholds, ACA subsidy cliff, senior deduction, and cash available to pay conversion taxes. The video also flags sequence-of-return risk as an overlooked downside of aggressive conversions. Legitimate reasons to convert include the widow's penalty, leaving tax-efficient inheritances, and mitigating RMD growth on large balances.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth conversion models are built on multiple unverifiable assumptions; use them for directional insight, not precise dollar guarantees.
- Three groups who likely should not convert: those with small IRAs they will fully spend down, those who will spend to the top of their bracket without converting, and those giving most assets to charity via QCDs.
- IRMAA surcharges operate as a cliff with a 2-year look-back; even $1 over the threshold triggers the full surcharge two years later.
- Optimizing for ACA subsidies by keeping income near the poverty line can cause significant under-spending in the best retirement years, and the tradeoff is not always worth it.
- Aggressive conversions in a market downturn can force selling at a loss to pay the tax bill, compounding sequence-of-return risk.
- The widow's penalty (bracket compression for surviving spouses) and the 10-year inherited IRA drain rule for heirs are strong reasons to convert for those with large balances and estate goals.

## Detailed Notes

### The Tower of Guesses Problem [00:00:00]

Calculator projections showing, for example, $400,000 or $700,000 in lifetime tax savings depend on assumptions about future tax rates, investment returns, inflation, and the order of spousal deaths. The presenter notes the prior urgency driver — 2017 tax cuts expiring at end of 2025 — has been eliminated by those cuts being made permanent. Use calculators directionally, not as prophecy.

### Three Cases Where You Should NOT Convert [00:02:41]

First: small IRA balances (roughly $300-400k) that will be fully spent in retirement — converting offers little benefit. Second: retirees who plan to spend aggressively from tax-deferred accounts anyway — spending to the top of the bracket achieves the same tax result as converting. Third: highly charitable individuals who can offset RMDs via qualified charitable distributions up to $100,000 per year after age 70½.

### The RMD Growth Problem [00:03:31]

A common mistake is running RMD calculations on today's balance. A $2M IRA at age 60 growing at a rapid clip can reach $4M by age 75, making future RMDs far larger than anticipated. Conversion decisions must account for projected future account values, not just current ones.

### Year-By-Year Conversion Factors [00:06:16]

Five factors to evaluate each year: (1) target tax bracket — there is no universally correct bracket, it is plan-specific; (2) IRMAA threshold — a 2-year look-back cliff that can surprise retirees with unexpected Medicare surcharges; (3) ACA subsidy — the 400% poverty line cliff can cause loss of the entire subsidy for $1 over, and over-optimizing for the subsidy can cause under-spending in key retirement years; (4) senior deduction — aggressive conversions can erase the $6,000-per-senior deduction; (5) cash available to pay taxes — many clients are surprised by the actual tax bill even when warned in advance.

### Sequence-of-Return Risk from Conversions [00:11:07]

Roth conversions are voluntary withdrawals; in a down market, paying conversion taxes forces additional selling at depressed prices on top of regular retirement withdrawals, shrinking the portfolio base at the worst possible time. This risk is manageable with strategic planning but is often overlooked by those simply following calculator outputs.

### Legitimate Reasons to Convert [00:13:07]

Three strong reasons to convert: the widow's penalty (pre-converting while both spouses are alive reduces the survivor's tax burden from compressed single-filer brackets); leaving tax-efficient inheritances (heirs must drain inherited IRAs within 10 years, often at peak earning rates); and mitigating RMD growth on large balances that may otherwise compound into outsized mandatory distributions. The advisor converts for roughly 75% of clients but emphasizes it must be optimized and recalibrated each year, not set and forgotten.
