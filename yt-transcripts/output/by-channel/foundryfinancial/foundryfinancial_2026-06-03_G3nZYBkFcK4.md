# He Made the 'Wrong' Financial Decision. It Turned Out to Be the Best One of His Life

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-06-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=G3nZYBkFcK4
**Duration:** 12:35

## Summary

Kevin Lum uses a viral Reddit post from a grandfather three years into retirement to illustrate three principles he sees consistently in practice. The grandfather claimed Social Security early not because the math supported it but for peace of mind, and found his spending came in lower than expected. Lum argues that not every financial decision can be optimized on a spreadsheet, that real-world retirement spending is often lower than feared, and that having a meaningful purpose to retire toward is as important as financial readiness.

## Key Takeaways

- Fear and the desire for peace of mind legitimately drive major financial decisions like Social Security timing — and that is not inherently wrong.
- Many retirees end retirement with more money than anticipated; Federal Reserve data shows retirees aged 65-74 are growing wealth, not spending it down.
- The biggest retirement risk for many people is not running out of money, but failing to live fully due to prolonged over-caution.
- Retiring 'to something' — a purpose, identity, or person — is as important as having enough savings to retire from work.
- We have a narrow window of 'health span' compared to lifespan, making early retirement inaction especially costly.
- Money is a tool for building the life you want, not a scoreboard — treating net worth as a scoreboard leads to unnecessarily delayed retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### The Reddit Story [00:00:00]

A grandfather shared on Reddit how, three years into retirement, he was finally fully present with his grandson — something he could not do while still working. He had claimed Social Security early for peace of mind and found retirement spending was less than expected. The story illustrates the gap between financial optimization and life satisfaction.

### Fear Drives More Decisions Than Math [00:02:39]

Lum argues that not every financial decision can or should be made purely by spreadsheet. Paying off a mortgage early, keeping excess cash, or claiming Social Security early can all be rational choices if they provide genuine peace of mind, even if they are not mathematically optimal. The value of an advisor, like any peace-of-mind purchase, may justify its cost even in a theoretical zero-sum scenario.

### Retirement Costs Less Than Expected [00:05:19]

Research and client experience consistently show people spend less in retirement than feared. Federal Reserve data reveals retirees aged 65-74 are net savers, not net spenders. Many over-savers keep deferring retirement in pursuit of a larger number that may never be needed. The more impactful risk for this cohort is under-spending and under-living, not running out of money.

### Retiring To Something [00:08:11]

Lum stresses that a successful retirement requires identifying what one is retiring toward — relationships, hobbies, identity, or purpose — not just what one is retiring from (work). Retirement can be a reset and rediscovery of values outside of professional identity. The grandfather's man cave and time with his grandson served as his 'retirement to' destination. Those who have not identified this often struggle with the transition.
