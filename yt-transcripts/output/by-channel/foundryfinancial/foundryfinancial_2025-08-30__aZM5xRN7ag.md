# I'm 62 with 80% (401k, IRA, etc.). How Do I Minimize Taxes?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=_aZM5xRN7ag
**Duration:** 14:05

## Summary

Kevin Lum illustrates the 'silent partner' nature of pre-tax retirement accounts: every dollar saved in a 401k or IRA eventually faces taxation, often at higher rates than the deduction was taken at. Using RightCapital software and the Phil and Claire case study ($2M tax-deferred, $300K taxable, $200K cash, retiring at 62), he models how RMDs beginning at 73-75 force hundreds of thousands of dollars in unwanted taxable income, trigger IRMAA surcharges, and can push a surviving spouse into the 35% bracket. Roth conversions to the top of the 12% bracket are shown to save $1.3M in taxes with 1.6M more in tax-adjusted ending wealth.

## Key Takeaways

- Pre-tax accounts have a 'silent partner'—state and federal governments—that claims 25-35%+ of balances, so nominal account size overstates real wealth.
- Early retirement can create several years of near-zero taxes, which is wasted low-bracket space unless proactively used for bracket filling or Roth conversions.
- RMDs can force annual income far above spending needs, creating tax bills on money that was never intended to be spent.
- IRMAA surcharges are a cliff-based Medicare penalty that can add hundreds of dollars per person per month with no offsetting benefit.
- Converting to the top of the 12% bracket dominates converting to the 22% bracket in terms of tax-adjusted ending assets, though heirs' tax rates should also inform the decision.
- Delaying Social Security creates a larger window for tax-efficient pre-tax withdrawals or Roth conversions before benefits add to income.

## Detailed Notes

### The Silent Partner Concept [00:00:00]

A potential client claimed $2M in an IRA; Kevin reframed it as ~$1.3-1.4M after taxes. Contributing $1K/month to a 401k for 30 years at 10% annual growth turns $360K of contributions into ~$2M, but the deduction was taken at a lower rate while withdrawals are taxed at a potentially higher rate. The government essentially collects taxes on compounded growth.

### Phil and Claire Case Study: The RMD Tax Buildup [00:03:55]

Phil and Claire retire at 62 with ~$2.5M total savings. Their first several retirement years show near-zero taxes while drawing from taxable accounts. Social Security of ~$91-102K/year kicks in after they delay claiming. Starting at age 73-75, RMDs begin at $28K and grow to $320K/year by age 80, pushing total income above $400K and resulting in $70-100K/year in taxes. If a spouse dies, the survivor files single and reaches the 35% bracket.

### Roth Conversion Strategies and Diminishing Returns [00:08:41]

Rather than leaving all pre-tax money to grow into large RMDs, two approaches are compared: filling the 12% bracket vs. the 22% bracket. Filling to 12% saves $1.3M and adds $1.6M in tax-adjusted ending assets. Filling to 22% saves more in taxes but reduces tax-adjusted ending wealth due to the higher conversion cost. When heirs in the 24% bracket are factored in, considering terminal tax rates lifts the optimal conversion target, and overall tax savings reach $1.9M. The One Big Beautiful Bill introduced new phase-outs that must now be factored into conversion math.
