# ALARMING Market Signal! Should You PANIC?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=H0d37xND7ZY
**Duration:** 14:29

## Summary

Kevin Lum presents data on U.S. equity market valuations via the CAPE ratio and price-to-book metrics, showing readings comparable to the 2021 peak and second only to the dot-com bubble in historical terms. He argues that concentration in the Magnificent Seven — now over 31% of S&P 500 weight — amplifies risk by coupling high valuations with sector concentration. Drawing on the 2000-2011 period as a historical analog, Lum advocates for a diversified factor-weighted portfolio tilted toward international, small, and value stocks as a hedge against extended U.S. underperformance, while stopping short of predicting a crash.

## Key Takeaways

- The CAPE ratio currently sits above its level 98% of the time since 1990, and the inverse CAPE yield of ~2.6% has historically predicted near-zero or negative real returns for the S&P 500 over the following decade.
- Seven Magnificent Seven technology companies now make up over 31% of the S&P 500, a concentration not seen in nearly 30 years, amplifying portfolio risk in cap-weighted index funds.
- Cap-weighted funds like VTI automatically increase exposure to overvalued companies as their market cap grows, creating a self-reinforcing concentration problem.
- During the 2000-2011 period when S&P 500 returns were essentially zero, diversified alternatives (U.S. small value, international small value, emerging markets) posted 7-9% annualized returns.
- A balanced factor-weighted equity portfolio outperformed the S&P 500 from 2000-2024 (9.18% vs. 7.83%) while providing more stability for retirees drawing down assets.
- Lum is not predicting a market crash but recommends tilting client portfolios away from high-valuation segments toward areas with higher expected future returns and lower valuations.

## Detailed Notes

### The CAPE Ratio and What It Signals [00:02:05]

Created by Yale economist Robert Shiller, the CAPE ratio measures price relative to 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings. A high CAPE historically signals lower future returns. The current U.S. CAPE ratio exceeds its level 98% of the time since 1990, matching the 2021 high and second only to the dot-com peak. The inverse CAPE yield of ~2.6% has historically correlated with 0.5% nominal to -4% real annualized returns over the following decade.

### Concentration Risk in the Magnificent Seven [00:05:15]

Seven mega-cap technology companies account for over 31% of S&P 500 valuation, and the top 10 positions represent nearly 40% of the index — nine in the same sector. This level of concentration was last seen 28 years ago when the top seven represented only 22% of the index. In cap-weighted funds like VTI, these stocks are 27% of the portfolio and grow larger automatically as market caps rise, compounding exposure.

### Historical Analog: 2000-2011 [00:07:25]

A JP Morgan 27-year study (1988-2014) found that buying the S&P 500 at PE multiples similar to today always produced 10-year annualized returns between +2% and -2%. During the dot-com bust era (2000-2011), S&P 500 cumulative returns were essentially zero while U.S. small value returned ~9.27% annually, international small value ~9.07%, and emerging markets ~7.91%. BlackRock data from 1971 onwards shows international stocks outperformed U.S. every time U.S. returns were below 4%.

### The Case for Diversification and a Devil's Advocate View [00:09:33]

Lum argues for balancing across U.S./international, large/small, and growth/value to reduce sequence-of-returns risk. He then steelmans the bull case: the Magnificent Seven are among the most profitable companies in history, are well-connected to the current administration, and may justify elevated valuations through AI-driven efficiency. Ultimately, Lum continues to hold these stocks via ETFs but tilts client portfolios toward lower-valuation, higher-expected-return segments.
