# Roth Conversions Just Got UGLY (Tricky) Under the Beautiful Bill

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-07-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=XhTV5vxk5PQ
**Duration:** 19:49

## Summary

Noah Hankin (CPA/CFP) and Kevin Lum of Foundry Financial deliver a technical breakdown of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act's tax provisions and their complex interaction with Roth conversion planning. The central message is that conversion planning can no longer be done with simple bracket math — numerous phase-out ranges (enhanced senior deduction, SALT cap, no-tax-on-tips, no-tax-on-overtime, AMT) can add 2-10% to effective conversion costs at specific income bands, making sophisticated tax software essential. Key planning opportunities include wider 12% brackets, large capital gains harvesting windows for seniors, and bunching charitable donations in 2025 before new floors take effect.

## Key Takeaways

- The One Big Beautiful Bill Act creates numerous phase-out ranges that can significantly increase effective Roth conversion costs above nominal bracket rates.
- The enhanced senior deduction ($6,000/person for ages 65+) phases out over an income range that can add 2.6-2.9% to the effective cost of conversions at those levels.
- The 12% tax bracket is expected to widen to over $100,000 in 2026 for married filers, creating substantial low-rate conversion and capital gains harvesting space.
- Married couples both over 65 may be able to harvest up to $143,400 in long-term capital gains at 0% tax, which also raises questions about whether taxable accounts are underutilized relative to traditional IRAs.
- The SALT cap increase to $40,000 applies equally to single and married filers (unlike most provisions), creating an implicit marriage penalty and a 9.6% effective rate spike in its phase-out band.
- Tax planning software is now essentially mandatory for accurate Roth conversion planning under the new bill due to the number of interacting phase-outs.

## Detailed Notes

### Overview of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act [00:01:38]

Passed July 4th, the Act is a large collection of provisions rather than a single change. Key areas affecting retirement planning include: tax bracket changes (extension and widening of TCJA brackets), enhanced senior deduction, SALT cap increase, no tax on tips, no tax on overtime pay, itemized charitable deduction floor, itemized deduction limitations (37% bracket), and AMT changes. IRS interpretation of some provisions is not yet final.

### Tax Bracket Changes and Capital Gains Opportunities [00:03:34]

The 12% bracket is extended and widened, potentially exceeding $100,000 for married filers in 2026 under a 2.5% inflation assumption. Notably, the gap between the 12% ordinary income bracket top and the 15% capital gains threshold has widened to over $1,000, creating a scenario where ordinary income is still in the 12% bracket but additional ordinary income from a Roth conversion could push capital gains into the 15% bracket.

### Enhanced Senior Deduction: Opportunity and Phase-Out Risk [00:05:40]

The $6,000 additional deduction per person (ages 65+) through 2025-2028 was widely misunderstood as eliminating Social Security taxation — it is an additional deduction. Its phase-out creates a hidden effective rate increase of approximately 2.6-2.9% at income levels around $151,000 for married filers, making conversions in that range cost 24.6% rather than 22%. However, the combined standard plus senior deductions allow couples both over 65 to potentially harvest approximately $143,400 in long-term capital gains at 0% rate.

### SALT Cap and Other Deduction Changes [00:09:25]

The SALT deduction cap increases to $40,000 through 2029 for all filers (single or married alike), reverting to $10,000 when the phase-out applies. The equal cap for single and married filers is an implicit marriage penalty. The phase-out range around 32% bracket creates a 9.6% effective rate spike for Roth conversions in that band. An itemized charitable deduction floor of 0.5% of AGI takes effect in 2026, making 2025 bunching into donor-advised funds attractive. AMT phase-out thresholds drop to pre-2018 levels and the phase-out rate doubles to 50%, potentially adding ~9% effective rate for those subject to AMT.

### Planning Recommendations and Resources [00:16:49]

Noah's key recommendations include: always model how conversions interact with specific phase-outs; evaluate standard vs. itemized deductions annually; bunch charitable donations in 2025 before the new floor; consider bunching property taxes to maximize SALT deductions; reassess whether taxable accounts warrant more emphasis given large capital gains harvesting windows. Tax planning software like Holista Plan is considered essential for accurate analysis. Recommended external resources include Roger Pine (Holista Plan), Ben Henry-Morland (Kitces.com), Jeffrey Levine, and Michael Kitces.
