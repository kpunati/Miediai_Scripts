# Once Retirees See This Data, They Stop Worrying About Crashes

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3XeCOnc8Hmc
**Duration:** 13:54

## Summary

Kevin Lum presents concrete historical data demonstrating that market crashes and large intra-year drawdowns are not exceptional events but near-certainties over a typical retirement horizon. He uses the Peter Lynch paradox and Dalbar research to show that emotional responses to volatility are the primary reason investors underperform the markets they invest in. The video argues that building a portfolio that is psychologically holdable—not just mathematically optimal—is the key to successful long-term retirement investing.

## Key Takeaways

- Market corrections of 10% or more have occurred in roughly half of all years since 1980, making them a predictable feature investors should expect rather than fear.
- A retiree who retired 25 years ago has likely experienced nine separate drawdowns of 20% or more—roughly $200,000 per million in portfolio value each time.
- The Peter Lynch paradox illustrates that even outstanding fund performance cannot help investors who let greed draw them in at peaks and fear push them out at troughs.
- Dalbar data shows the average investor earns roughly half the market's long-run return due to emotional timing decisions.
- New all-time highs are routine occurrences, with markets setting a new high approximately every 14 trading days since the 1950s.
- The solution is building a portfolio that is diversified and appropriately structured so it can be held through inevitable drawdowns without triggering panic selling.

## Detailed Notes

### Historical Market Return Data [00:01:03]

Since 1928, US markets have returned approximately 10.5% annualized; since 2009, close to 15% annually. Markets are up about 75% of all years, but the focus on annual returns obscures severe intra-year swings that test investor discipline.

### Intra-Year Drawdowns: The Real Data [00:02:07]

The average intra-year drawdown since 1928 is 16.4%. Since 1980 this moderates to 14.1%. Over the past 25 years, the market was down 10% or more in 15 of 25 years and down nearly 20% in nine of those years. For a $2M portfolio, a 20% drawdown means $400,000 in paper losses—real enough to trigger panic.

### The Peter Lynch Paradox and Behavioral Finance [00:06:37]

Peter Lynch's Magellan Fund averaged 29% annually from 1977 to 1990, yet the average fund investor lost money because greed attracted flows after big up years (e.g., 70% in 1980) and fear caused exits during subsequent underperformance. Dalbar research confirms this pattern broadly: S&P returned ~10% from 1990–2019 while the average investor earned only ~5%, with a 60/40 portfolio earning 8.7% vs. that same 5% for the average investor.

### All-Time Highs Are Normal, Not Warning Signs [00:11:06]

Citing Ben Carlson's research, Lum notes that over the past 10 years markets have reached nearly 350 new all-time highs. In 2025 alone there were 39 all-time highs—while the market was also down 19% intra-year. New highs appear on about 7% of all trading days since the 1950s, or roughly every 14 days. Investors who wait for pullbacks after new highs consistently miss gains.

### Building a Holdable Portfolio [00:12:10]

The practical takeaway is to build a portfolio that is diversified enough to be held through volatility without creating an income death spiral—where panic selling at lows locks in losses and forces liquidation at the worst time. The key is matching asset allocation to actual spending needs so that market downturns do not force selling.
