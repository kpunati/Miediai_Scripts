# The Month You Retire Really Matters

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-12-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=4IUBd6g9gNo
**Duration:** 15:59

## Summary

Kevin Lum examines whether the month of retirement matters across three dimensions: personal health and well-being, tax efficiency, and financial logistics. He cites research linking retirement to a 40% increase in clinical depression risk and observes that retiring into favorable weather can ease the psychological transition. On the tax side, retiring early in the calendar year creates a low-income year ideal for Roth conversions and tax-gain harvesting, illustrated with a detailed example using retirement planning software. The video also covers IRMAA's two-year look-back, COBRA timing for the Medicare coverage gap, bonus vesting, equity compensation, and maximizing employer benefits before departing.

## Key Takeaways

- Retiring in spring (February–April) provides favorable weather for psychological adjustment and captures most of the year in lower income, maximizing tax planning flexibility.
- A low-income retirement year creates room for Roth conversions at the 12% bracket and for realizing long-term capital gains at a 0% federal rate.
- IRMAA has a two-year look-back, meaning a high earner retiring at 63 may still owe Medicare premium surcharges at 65 based on prior wages — though a qualifying life event like retirement can allow an appeal.
- COBRA timing should align so the 18-month coverage period ends just as Medicare eligibility begins, avoiding a coverage gap.
- Retirees should inventory employer benefits before leaving: HSA contributions, 401(k) match, PTO payouts, unvested RSUs, and bonus payment schedules can all shift the optimal retirement date by days or months.
- There is no universally best retirement month — the right answer depends on location, tax situation, pension or bonus structure, and personal preferences around weather and transition.

## Detailed Notes

### Health, Well-Being, and Seasonal Timing [00:01:03]

A study from the Institute of Economic Affairs found retirement increases clinical depression risk by 40%. Retiring into winter can compound the psychological adjustment challenge, especially in cold-weather states. Kevin recommends targeting a February–May retirement to capture improving weather during the emotionally critical first six months.

### Medical Considerations: Procedures, COBRA, and IRMAA [00:03:46]

Retirees should complete any dental, vision, or medical procedures while still on employer insurance, as Medicare coverage differs. COBRA for 18 months can bridge to Medicare at 65 if timed correctly. High earners should note IRMAA's two-year look-back: income at age 63 determines Medicare Part B and D surcharges at 65, though retirement counts as a qualifying event to appeal the surcharge.

### Tax Efficiency: Roth Conversions and Tax-Gain Harvesting [00:06:15]

Retiring early in the year creates a low-income year suitable for tax planning. In a software-illustrated example, a single filer earning $50,000 in wages (from January–March work) has room to convert $14,000 to Roth while staying at the 12% bracket ceiling, paying approximately $5,520 in total federal tax. Alternatively, selling appreciated stock (with a 100% gain on Nvidia in the example) produces $14,000 in long-term capital gains taxed at 0% at that income level. Frontloading 401(k) contributions before retirement further reduces taxable income for the year.

### Financial Logistics: Benefits, Bonuses, and Equity [00:12:50]

Key financial factors affecting the ideal retirement month include: pension vesting dates, annual bonus payment timing, equity compensation vesting schedules (RSUs, stock options), PTO payout policies, and HSA contribution maximization. Kevin cautions that stock option vesting schedules are often used to delay retirement one more quarter; retirees should assess whether the incremental vesting value justifies the additional work time.
