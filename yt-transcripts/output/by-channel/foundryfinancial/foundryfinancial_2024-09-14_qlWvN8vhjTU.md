# The Most UNDERRATED Account to Minimize TAXES in Retirement

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qlWvN8vhjTU
**Duration:** 15:05

## Summary

Kevin Lum argues that the taxable brokerage account is one of the most underrated retirement planning tools because long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are taxed at preferential rates—often 0%—when combined with the standard deduction, making the account nearly as powerful as a Roth IRA for many retirees. Using a hypothetical couple, Phil and Claire, who are 65, need $120,000 per year, hold $500,000 in an IRA and $500,000 in a taxable brokerage with a $250,000 cost basis, and receive $60,000 in Social Security, he shows three tax scenarios that dramatically illustrate the tax gap between IRA and brokerage withdrawals. The video also covers tax gain harvesting, the IRMAA surcharge threshold, and the stark difference in tax treatment between qualified and ordinary dividends.

## Key Takeaways

- The taxable brokerage account can rival a Roth IRA in tax efficiency because long-term capital gains are taxed at 0% for many retirees.
- Married couples over 65 filing jointly can deduct roughly $32,400 in standard deductions, creating significant space for tax-free capital gains.
- Taking income from a taxable brokerage instead of an IRA can save retirees thousands in federal taxes annually on the same total income.
- Tax gain harvesting—selling appreciated shares to reset cost basis—makes sense when there is unused space in the 0% capital gains bracket.
- Retirees who delay Social Security have a larger low-income window to draw from taxable accounts and execute Roth conversions.
- Qualified dividends (e.g., from individual stocks like AT&T) are taxed at capital gains rates, while ordinary dividends from many ETFs are taxed as ordinary income—a critical distinction for tax-efficient income planning.
- Keeping modified adjusted gross income below IRMAA thresholds avoids Medicare Part B and D surcharges that can substantially increase healthcare costs.

## Detailed Notes

### Two Tax Structures: Ordinary Income vs. Capital Gains [00:01:03]

Federal taxes divide income into ordinary income (wages, IRA distributions, pensions, interest, ordinary dividends) taxed at marginal rates from 10% to 37%, and long-term capital gains (assets held over one year) taxed at preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%. The 0% long-term capital gains rate is available to those with taxable income up to approximately $94,000 for married filers in 2024. The standard deduction of $29,800 (plus $1,550 per qualifying spouse over 65) reduces taxable income before these brackets apply.

### Case Study: Phil and Claire's Three Scenarios [00:04:12]

Phil and Claire, age 65, need $120,000 per year. They have $500,000 in an IRA, $500,000 in a taxable brokerage with $250,000 cost basis, and $60,000 combined Social Security. Scenario 1 draws $60,000 from the brokerage account (half gains, half cost basis): total federal tax is $0. Scenario 2 draws $60,000 from the IRA plus Social Security: total federal tax is approximately $8,200. Scenario 3 has no Social Security and draws $125,000 in long-term gains from the brokerage: still pays $0 in federal tax because $92,700 in taxable income falls below the $94,000 zero-rate capital gains threshold.

### Tax Gain Harvesting and IRMAA Considerations [00:09:28]

When retirees have unused space in the 0% capital gains bracket, they can sell appreciated shares, realize gains, and reset the cost basis—reducing future tax liability without paying any tax now. This is called tax gain harvesting. Modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) also determines Medicare Part B and Part D premiums through the IRMAA surcharge; keeping MAGI below threshold thresholds avoids these additional costs.

### Qualified vs. Ordinary Dividends [00:12:36]

Dividends from individual stocks that meet holding-period requirements (e.g., AT&T) are qualified dividends taxed at long-term capital gains rates. Dividends from many ETFs—particularly options-based income ETFs on indices like the NASDAQ—are classified as ordinary dividends and taxed at marginal rates. The tax difference is dramatic: $100,000 in qualified dividends can result in $0 federal tax, while the same amount in ordinary dividends results in approximately $8,800. Interest from CDs and high-yield savings accounts is also taxed as ordinary income.
