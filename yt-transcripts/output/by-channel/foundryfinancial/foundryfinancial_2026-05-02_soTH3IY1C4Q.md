# Fidelity Just Proved Your Retirement Anxiety Has Nothing to Do With Money

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=soTH3IY1C4Q
**Duration:** 15:02

## Summary

This video presents findings from Fidelity's 2026 State of Retirement Planning Study and corroborating research from Charles Schwab and the FINRA Foundation to argue that retirement confidence is driven primarily by planning, not portfolio size. The presenter Kevin Lum outlines four pillars shared by confident retirees: a written plan, knowing key financial numbers, planning for identity and purpose beyond money, and working with a financial professional. Cognitive psychology research on 'cognitive offloading' is cited to explain why written plans reduce anxiety. The video challenges viewers to recognize that most retirement uncertainty is an information problem, not a math problem.

## Key Takeaways

- Having a written financial plan roughly doubles the likelihood of feeling confident about retirement, regardless of account balance.
- Cognitive offloading theory explains why writing down a plan reduces anxiety: it frees working memory and enables clearer decision-making.
- Less than half of pre-retirees have calculated basic retirement costs such as healthcare expenses, which contributes to unnecessary anxiety.
- Planning for purpose, identity, and social connection in retirement is as important as financial preparation.
- Working with a financial professional reduces financial worry not because advisors have secret knowledge, but because they provide an informed second perspective during market volatility.
- Retirement uncertainty is largely an information problem—once numbers are stress-tested and documented, most people discover they are in better shape than feared.

## Detailed Notes

### The Fidelity Study and the Plan-Confidence Link [00:01:02]

Fidelity's 2026 study compared retirees with and without a financial plan, finding an 83% vs. 38% confidence gap. A separate Charles Schwab Modern Wealth Survey reinforced this, showing 96% of people with a written plan feel confident about their goals. Both studies point to the plan itself—not wealth level—as the confidence driver.

### Why Written Plans Work: Cognitive Offloading [00:05:21]

Academic research in cognitive psychology shows that trying to hold complex variables in working memory overloads it, worsening decisions and raising anxiety. Writing the plan down externalizes that complexity, freeing mental bandwidth. This explains why a documented plan showing income, expenses, withdrawal order, and tax strategy by year is far more reassuring than a general sense of financial readiness.

### Knowing Your Numbers [00:07:24]

The Employee Benefit Research Institute found only 52% of workers have estimated monthly retirement income needs, only 44% have thought about withdrawal amounts, and only 41% have calculated healthcare costs. Fidelity estimates a single 65-year-old retiring in 2025 needs to plan for approximately $172,000 in out-of-pocket healthcare costs, making this a critical but often overlooked number.

### Identity, Purpose, and Professional Support [00:09:14]

The American Psychological Association's research finds that retirement satisfaction correlates with planning for purpose, community, and relationships, not just finances. Clients who saved adequately but neglected this dimension often struggled emotionally after retiring. Working with a financial advisor is correlated with saving twice as much and, per Fidelity, with reduced financial worry during retirement.

### Retirement Uncertainty as an Information Problem [00:12:23]

The presenter argues that the 62% of near-retirees who feel uncertain about whether their savings will last are experiencing an information gap, not a financial shortfall. When real numbers are stress-tested against conservative scenarios, most people find they will be fine. A plan converts invisible anxieties about unknowns into specific, measurable, and manageable factors.
