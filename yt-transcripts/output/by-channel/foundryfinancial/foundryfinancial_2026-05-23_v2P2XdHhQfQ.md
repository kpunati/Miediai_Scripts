# You Don't Know When Enough Is Enough | Retirement Reality Check

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=v2P2XdHhQfQ
**Duration:** 13:23

## Summary

Kevin Lum challenges the widespread tendency to delay retirement out of fear rather than necessity, making the case that delaying retirement costs people their most valuable remaining years of health and vitality. He draws on research from EBRI, Michael Kitces, and others to show that the majority of retirees dramatically underspend and end up with far more money than they started with. He offers a practical five-step 'know your number' framework and advocates for dynamic or guardrail withdrawal strategies rather than static 4% assumptions to allow for higher early-retirement spending.

## Key Takeaways

- Delaying retirement for one more year pulls time from the front of retirement — the healthiest years — not from the end, making the cost far higher than it appears financially.
- Most people delaying retirement have never actually run their retirement numbers; anxiety about running out of money is driven by absence of data, not real analysis.
- Only 11% of retirees identify as spenders; the majority remain in accumulation mode, systematically underspending relative to what their portfolio can support.
- Research shows over two-thirds of retirees on a 4% withdrawal rate end with more than double their starting portfolio, meaning most will never spend down their savings.
- A dynamic or guardrail withdrawal strategy increases the sustainable withdrawal rate above the static 4% rule by allowing spending flexibility with market conditions.
- The five-step 'enough is enough' framework is: know your fixed and discretionary monthly costs, calculate guaranteed income, find the gap, assess portfolio value, and apply the right withdrawal rate.

## Detailed Notes

### The Real Cost of One More Year [00:00:00]

Lum argues that most people stuck in the 'one more year' loop have never run the actual numbers — fear fills the gap in their reasoning. He distinguishes two types of retiree spenders: those whose spending rises in early retirement because work was suppressing it, and the larger group whose spending drops structurally as they eliminate 401k contributions, payroll taxes, commuting costs, work clothing, and often mortgage payments.

### Health Span vs Lifespan and Demographic Context [00:03:50]

The average American lives to 77 with healthy years ending around 66, but this average undercounts the audience of financially secure, college-educated viewers. For this group, healthy years realistically extend into the 70s or early 80s. Lum emphasizes that delaying retirement from 65 to 66 subtracts from the go-go years — hiking, international travel, time with grandchildren — not from sedentary late-life years.

### Most Retirees Underspend: The Research [00:07:20]

Kitces research cited by Lum shows that over two-thirds of retirees on a 4% withdrawal rate end a 30-year retirement with more than double their starting balance, with a median ending balance of nearly 2.8x. EBRI data shows 89% of retirees identify as savers, not spenders. The implication is that running out of money is not the dominant retirement risk for this demographic — underspending during healthy years is.

### Five-Step Framework: Knowing When Enough Is Enough [00:08:00]

The five steps are: (1) split monthly spending into fixed and discretionary amounts; (2) calculate guaranteed income from pensions, Social Security, or annuities; (3) determine the monthly portfolio gap; (4) identify the portfolio balance; (5) apply the appropriate withdrawal rate — noting that a guardrail or dynamic strategy can support a higher rate than the static 4% rule, potentially making retirement feasible sooner than a simple 4% calculation suggests.
