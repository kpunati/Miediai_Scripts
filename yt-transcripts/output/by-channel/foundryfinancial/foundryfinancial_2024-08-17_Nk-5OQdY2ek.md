# 48% of Retirees Will Require This Care: Is the INSURANCE worth it?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-17
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Nk-5OQdY2ek
**Duration:** 16:46

## Summary

Kevin Lum, CFP at Foundry Financial, argues that long-term care costs represent one of the largest unknown variables in retirement planning, with nearly half of 65-year-olds requiring paid care. He systematically reviews the three main insurance options—traditional LTC, hybrid life-LTC, and hybrid annuity-LTC—along with non-insurance alternatives including HSAs, home equity, and Medicaid. He proposes a three-group framework: wealthy retirees should self-insure, those with minimal assets should rely on Medicaid, and middle-market retirees concerned about legacy may benefit most from insurance coverage.

## Key Takeaways

- Nearly half of all retirees will need paid long-term care; women average 3.7 years of care versus 2.2 years for men, making gender a significant planning variable.
- Traditional LTC insurance premiums can rise substantially over time; insurance companies historically underestimated costs and have updated pricing accordingly.
- Hybrid life-LTC policies offer fixed premiums and a death benefit if care is not needed, but premiums are significantly higher than standalone policies.
- Hybrid annuity-LTC products require a large upfront lump sum and sacrifice investment growth potential, but offer simpler underwriting.
- People with large asset bases are generally better off self-insuring; those on fixed incomes with no budget flexibility are likely better served by Medicaid; insurance is most valuable for the middle group concerned about legacy.
- HSAs offer a triple-tax-advantaged way to fund long-term care costs or premiums for those who have accumulated significant balances.

## Detailed Notes

### The Case for Worrying About Long-Term Care [00:00:00]

Morningstar data cited: 48% of 65-year-olds will need some paid LTC; 24% will need 2+ years. Regional cost variation is extreme—$159,000/year in New York versus $64,000/year in rural Louisiana. A two-year care stay accounting for inflation should be estimated at roughly $350,000 per person, more for women who statistically need longer care.

### Traditional Long-Term Care Insurance [00:02:09]

Traditional policies pay a daily benefit (e.g., $200/day for two or four years) when the insured cannot perform a specified number of ADLs. Pros include comprehensive inflation-adjusted coverage and possible tax deductibility of premiums. Key cons are premium increases over time, use-it-or-lose-it structure (no benefit if care is never needed), and medical underwriting that may deny coverage if applied for too late.

### Hybrid Policies and Alternatives [00:04:15]

Hybrid life-LTC policies combine permanent life insurance with an LTC rider; premiums are fixed and a death benefit is paid if care is not needed, but premiums are significantly higher and benefits may be less rich than traditional LTC. Hybrid annuity-LTC products offer simpler underwriting and guaranteed income that increases for care needs, but require large upfront cash and limit investment growth. Shared-care options allow married couples to pool a single benefit limit, reducing cost but adding complexity and concentration risk if one spouse exhausts the pool.

### Non-Insurance Alternatives and Decision Framework [00:09:28]

Alternatives include: HSAs (triple-tax-advantaged; can pay LTC premiums tax-free), home equity via HELOC or reverse mortgage (an 'escape hatch'), and Medicaid (problematic for healthy surviving spouses due to asset/income limits). The three-group framework: (1) large nest egg—self-insure and invest premiums instead; (2) paycheck-to-paycheck retirees—Medicaid or home equity; (3) middle-market with legacy concerns and budget for premiums—insurance may be worthwhile.
