# What Order Should You Invest Your Money? A CFP® Explains

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-03-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Kmrh1X_Kfc0
**Duration:** 18:01

## Summary

Kevin Lum walks through the optimal sequence for deploying investment dollars, emphasizing that choosing the wrong account type today can result in tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars in unnecessary future taxes. The video covers foundational steps including emergency reserves and employer match capture, then progresses through tax-advantaged vehicles — 401(k), HSA, Roth IRA, backdoor Roth, and mega backdoor Roth — before addressing taxable brokerage accounts and 529 education savings plans.

## Key Takeaways

- Always capture the full employer 401(k) match before directing money elsewhere — it is an immediate guaranteed return.
- Match Roth versus traditional 401(k) contributions to current tax bracket: use Roth at 24% or below, traditional at 32% or above.
- HSAs offer triple tax advantage and should be invested rather than spent, making them a powerful long-term retirement vehicle.
- High-income earners above Roth IRA income limits can access Roth benefits through backdoor or mega backdoor Roth strategies.
- Taxable brokerage accounts offer maximum flexibility and should be prioritized for early retirees or those needing funds for Roth conversions.
- 529 plans allow tax-free growth for qualified education expenses, and unused funds can now be rolled into a Roth IRA under recent tax law changes.
- Emergency reserves of 3-12 months of expenses should be held in liquid instruments like high-yield savings or short-term government treasuries, not CDs or annuities.

## Detailed Notes

### Emergency Savings (Step 1) [00:00:00]

Lum recommends 3 months of cash for dual-income stable households, 6 months for single earners, and 12 months for the self-employed or volatile industries. At least one month should be in a liquid bank account; the rest can be held in high-yield savings or ultra-short government treasuries for return without lockup risk.

### 401(k) Match and Debt Payoff (Steps 2-3) [00:03:08]

After starting emergency savings, the second priority is capturing the full employer 401(k) match. The third step is eliminating high-interest revolving debt using either the avalanche or snowball method. Entering retirement with no credit card or personal loan balances is strongly recommended.

### Maxing 401(k) and HSA by Tax Bracket (Steps 4-5) [00:06:19]

At 24% bracket or below, Lum recommends Roth 401(k) contributions; at 32% or above, traditional 401(k) is preferred to defer taxes. HSAs are highlighted as triple-tax-advantaged and best utilized by investing contributions and paying medical costs out of pocket to let the balance compound.

### Roth IRA, Backdoor Roth, Mega Backdoor Roth (Step 6) [00:10:31]

Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out around $161,000 (single) and $240,000 (married) in 2025. Backdoor Roth allows high earners to contribute via nondeductible traditional IRA conversion but requires care around the pro-rata rule. Mega backdoor Roth can enable up to $70,000 per year if the employer plan supports after-tax contributions and in-plan conversions.

### Taxable Brokerage and 529 Plans (Steps 7 and Bonus) [00:13:39]

Taxable brokerage accounts are the most flexible savings vehicle — no contribution limits or withdrawal restrictions — and are particularly useful for early retirees or those funding Roth conversions. Dividends, interest, and capital gains are taxable annually. 529 plans grow tax-free for qualified education expenses and excess balances can now be converted to a Roth IRA under recent law changes.
