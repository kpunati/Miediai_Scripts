# Should I delay my Social Security? Use This 5-Step Test

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ZxsRWyDBmAY
**Duration:** 19:36

## Summary

Kevin Lum, a CFP at Foundry Financial, walks through a structured five-factor framework called LIFTS (Longevity, Income gap, Family, Taxes, Security) to help retirees decide when to claim Social Security benefits. Each factor is explained in depth — including the survivor benefit implications for married couples, the Roth conversion window created by delaying benefits, and the role of Monte Carlo simulations in assessing plan security. An experimental 0-5 point scoring system maps to three claiming strategies: delay to 70, claim at full retirement age, or claim early. Three example scenarios illustrate how the framework applies to people with different health, wealth, and family situations.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security benefits increase for every year of delay past full retirement age (approximately 67), and decrease for every year of early claiming starting at age 62.
- Longevity is the primary driver: if there is a good chance of living past age 82, delaying Social Security is often mathematically optimal due to inflation protection.
- The income gap between retirement and Social Security claiming requires either a cash bucket or portfolio withdrawals — but early portfolio pressure with delayed Social Security often improves overall plan success probability.
- For the higher-earning spouse in a couple, delaying to age 70 creates a larger survivor benefit that the surviving spouse receives for life, regardless of the higher earner's own life expectancy.
- Delaying Social Security benefits creates a valuable Roth conversion window in early retirement when taxable income is low, potentially enabling tax-free conversion of large traditional IRA balances.
- IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums can be triggered by Social Security income combined with other income, making tax planning software essential for modeling the optimal claiming age.

## Detailed Notes

### Social Security Basics [00:01:03]

Benefits are based on the Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) at full retirement age (approximately 67). Claiming at 62 reduces benefits for each early year, while delaying past full retirement age increases them. Benefits include built-in inflation protection tied to the real rate of inflation.

### Longevity and Income Gap (L and I in LIFTS) [00:03:08]

The break-even for delaying versus claiming early falls between ages 78 and 82; research shows investment portfolios have difficulty outperforming the inflation-adjusted bump from delay once someone lives past 82. The income gap requires covering living expenses from portfolio or pension for potentially 8 years if retiring at 62 and delaying to 70; a bucket strategy with dedicated cash can help manage this psychologically.

### Family Dynamics (F in LIFTS) [00:06:33]

The lower-earning spouse's spousal benefit is capped at 50% of the higher earner's full retirement benefit (not the delayed benefit), so there is no advantage to the lower earner delaying to 70. The higher-earning spouse should strongly consider delaying, because the surviving spouse receives the higher of their own or the deceased spouse's benefit for life, making delayed claiming a survivorship protection strategy.

### Taxes and Security (T and S in LIFTS) [00:08:57]

Social Security income can cause up to 85% of benefits to be taxable and can crowd out space for Roth conversions in the 12% bracket. Delaying creates an early-retirement window to convert traditional IRA balances efficiently, potentially avoiding all taxation of Social Security permanently. Delaying also transfers inflation risk from the portfolio to the federal government, which improves overall plan security.

### Decision Matrix and Worked Scenarios [00:13:29]

The LIFTS scoring system awards one point per factor answered yes: living past 80, having resources to bridge the income gap, having a dependent spouse, taxes favoring delay, and needing more guaranteed income. Scores of 4-5 indicate delay to 70; 2-3 indicate full retirement age claiming; 0-1 indicate early claiming. Three scenarios — a healthy single person with tax-deferred assets, a married couple needing Roth conversions, and a single woman with poor health and a small portfolio — illustrate the framework in practice.
