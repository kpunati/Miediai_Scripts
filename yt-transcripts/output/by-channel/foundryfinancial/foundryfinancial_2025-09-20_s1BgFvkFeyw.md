# 5M Retirees Don’t Lie: Real Data on Retirement Spending

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-09-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=s1BgFvkFeyw
**Duration:** 12:36

## Summary

This video presents findings from two JP Morgan retirement spending studies covering over 5 million households, distilling three key patterns relevant to retirement planning. The presenter, Kevin Lum CFP, explains that spending peaks in one's late 40s to early 50s and then gradually declines through retirement, forming a 'spending smile' curve. The actual inflation rate for retirees averages 1.8% annually (not the commonly assumed 3-4%), with healthcare as a significant exception inflating at 6% per year. Sequence-of-return risk is identified as a larger threat than general inflation for most retirees.

## Key Takeaways

- Spending peaks in late 40s to early 50s, then gradually declines through retirement — overall retirement inflation averages about 1.8% per year.
- 53% of households partially retire and experience a spending surge, particularly those earning under $150K annually; fully retired households spend more cautiously.
- 60% of retirees face spending volatility in early retirement, with annual swings of 20% or more, increasing sequence-of-return risk.
- Healthcare is the major inflation exception: plan for 6% annual healthcare cost increases; couples should budget roughly $13,720/year today, growing to ~$24,500 in 10 years.
- 75% of women and 65% of men will need some form of long-term care, which is separate from general healthcare costs and also rises faster than general inflation.
- Retirement planning software should inflate healthcare and long-term care at separate, higher rates than other expenses to avoid distorting the overall plan.

## Detailed Notes

### JP Morgan Study Overview [00:00:00]

JP Morgan conducted two studies on retirement spending: a 2019 study with over 5 million households and an updated study with an additional 280,000 households. The data was anonymized from banking and credit card clients. Three key findings are explored: the spending surge, spending volatility, and the spending smile (spending curve).

### The Spending Curve and Basic Patterns [00:01:44]

Spending peaks between ages 45-49, driven by mortgages, college costs, and lifestyle expansion. After retirement, spending initially may increase due to more leisure time, but overall spending declines as mortgages are paid off and children become independent. The common rule of thumb of spending 70-80% of pre-retirement income is broadly true but oversimplified.

### The Spending Surge in Partial Retirement [00:04:29]

53% of households partially retire rather than stopping all at once. Partially retired households show a notable spending surge, including increased credit card debt, concentrated in healthcare, food, and apparel spending. This surge only occurs in households earning under $150,000 per year; higher-income households and fully retired households do not exhibit this pattern.

### Spending Volatility in Early Retirement [00:06:34]

60% of households experience volatile spending in their first few years of retirement, with annual variation of up to 20% higher or lower. This volatility creates short-term liquidity needs and can worsen long-term funding risk by heightening sequence-of-return risk. Planners should build liquidity buffers to avoid selling investments during downturns.

### The Spending Smile and Healthcare Inflation [00:08:57]

Spending declines across nine out of ten categories as retirees age, producing a 'smile' shape when graphed — higher early, lower in middle years, then spiking again at end of life due to healthcare. True average inflation for retirees is approximately 1.8% per year, not 3-4%. Healthcare is the exception at 6% annual inflation; individuals should budget $6,860/year and couples $13,720/year for Medicare, exclusive of long-term care costs.
