# 6 types of income not taxed in retirement.

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ybmWh88MWog
**Duration:** 13:58

## Summary

Kevin Lum surveys six tax-free or tax-advantaged income sources retirees can use to minimize their tax burden. The video covers the mechanics of HSA triple tax advantage, the 0% long-term capital gains bracket, municipal bond income, home equity exclusion on home sales, Roth IRA qualified distributions, and strategies to keep Social Security benefits tax-free. Each source has specific eligibility requirements and potential pitfalls, and the video stresses the importance of coordinating all these strategies to keep total taxable income as low as possible.

## Key Takeaways

- HSAs offer triple tax advantages—deductible contributions, tax-free growth, and tax-free medical withdrawals—and unused balances can reimburse past out-of-pocket medical expenses.
- Retirees who can manage taxable income below the 0% capital gains threshold can receive substantial dividend and capital gains income completely free of federal tax.
- Municipal bond interest is generally exempt from federal and sometimes state taxes, though it counts toward Social Security provisional income.
- Selling a primary residence after living in it two of the last five years allows exclusion of up to $500,000 in gains for married couples; home improvements increase the cost basis and expand this exclusion.
- Roth IRA withdrawals are fully tax-free after age 59.5 and a five-year holding period, making them a powerful tool for managing taxable income in retirement.
- Social Security benefits are tax-free for roughly half of all recipients; effective tax planning around provisional income can preserve this tax-free status.

## Detailed Notes

### Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) [00:01:02]

HSAs require a qualifying high-deductible health plan. Contributions are tax-deductible, money grows tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. A key strategy is to invest rather than spend HSA funds and then reimburse yourself later for past medical expenses. Contributing $500 per month at 8% over 20 years yields approximately $274,000.

### Qualified Dividends and Long-Term Capital Gains [00:04:10]

For 2025, couples filing jointly can earn up to $96,700 in qualified dividends or long-term capital gains at a 0% federal rate. Adding the $30,000 standard deduction raises the effective threshold to about $126,000 of potentially zero-tax income. This strategy requires a taxable brokerage account and careful income management.

### Municipal Bonds [00:06:16]

Interest on state and local government bonds is generally exempt from federal income tax and may be exempt from state taxes if issued by the investor's home state. However, municipal bond income is added to provisional income for Social Security tax calculations, and some private activity bonds may trigger AMT. Yields are typically lower than corporate bonds, but the tax-adjusted yield may be higher for investors in elevated brackets.

### Home Equity and Downsizing [00:08:22]

Married couples can exclude up to $500,000 in capital gains from a primary residence sale tax-free (single filers: $250,000), provided they lived there two of the last five years. Home improvements increase the property's cost basis, further reducing taxable gains. This can be combined with the 0% capital gains bracket for additional tax savings.

### Roth IRA Distributions and Social Security [00:10:28]

Qualified Roth IRA distributions after age 59.5 with a five-year holding period are completely tax-free, making Roth assets a powerful tool for controlling taxable income. Social Security is tax-free for about 50% of recipients; provisional income (AGI plus non-taxable interest plus half of Social Security) determines how much is taxable. Roth conversions and other strategies can help keep provisional income below the tax thresholds.
