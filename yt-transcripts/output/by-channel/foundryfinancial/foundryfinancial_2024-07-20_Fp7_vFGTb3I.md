# Top 3 Retirement Income Methods EXPLAINED!

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-07-20
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Fp7_vFGTb3I
**Duration:** 18:00

## Summary

Kevin Lum introduces the four L's retirement framework — longevity, lifestyle, liquidity, legacy — as the organizing structure for building a retirement income plan. He then demonstrates three distinct income strategies: the probability-based approach using a diversified portfolio and 4% withdrawal, the safety-first approach using annuities or TIPS ladders to floor essential expenses, and the bucket strategy using tiered cash reserves to protect against sequence-of-returns risk. Each approach is illustrated with case studies run in Right Capital financial planning software.

## Key Takeaways

- The four L's framework provides a purpose-driven structure for retirement income planning before selecting any strategy.
- The probability-based approach is the most common but can cause retirees to underspend out of fear, leaving large unintended estates.
- The safety-first approach using annuities can dramatically increase ending net worth by freeing the remaining portfolio to take higher equity risk.
- Annuities typically lack inflation adjustments, meaning their purchasing power erodes significantly over a 30-year retirement.
- The bucket strategy is Lum's personal preference as it provides sequence-of-returns protection and inflation protection while maximizing equity growth.
- Choosing the right approach depends on risk tolerance, psychological comfort with volatility, legacy goals, and whether guaranteed income matters more than flexibility.
- Annuities are frequently sold rather than bought and come with commission-driven conflicts of interest; buyers should use fiduciary fee-only advisers.

## Detailed Notes

### Four L's Framework and Reliable Income [00:00:00]

Lum introduces the four L's — longevity (essential expenses), lifestyle (discretionary spending), liquidity (emergency access), and legacy (wealth transfer) — borrowed from retirement researcher Wade Pfau. The first step after establishing this framework is identifying reliable income sources such as Social Security, pensions, or annuities to partially cover the gaps.

### Probability-Based Approach [00:03:08]

A typical couple wanting $10,000 per month with $5,000 from Social Security needs $5,000 from their portfolio. With $1.2 million invested in a 60/40 allocation, a 4% withdrawal provides roughly $4,000 per month. The main risks are volatility, longevity, and sequence-of-returns; behavioral underspending is a documented side effect of this approach.

### Safety-First Approach with Annuity Case Study [00:06:18]

Phil and Claire (60/40 portfolio, $1.2 million invested) have an 82% success probability. By contrast, Jay and Gloria invest $500,000 in an annuity paying $37,500 per year starting at age 62. With essential income secured, the remaining portfolio can go 100% equities, potentially generating $8-9 million versus the $2-3 million projected for Phil and Claire.

### Bucket Strategy and Conclusion [00:13:40]

Lum's preferred approach uses 2-3 years of expenses in a liquid safe bucket, 2-3 years in intermediate bonds, and the remainder in equities. This provides sequence-of-returns protection without locking up money in an annuity. He stresses that no single approach is universally superior and emphasizes balancing return on investment with 'return on sleep.'
