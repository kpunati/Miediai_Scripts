# Avoid this BIG ROTH Mistake - Roth IRA 5 Year Rule Explained

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-03-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=1N45l1gG9oA
**Duration:** 14:44

## Summary

Kevin Lum explains the two distinct Roth IRA five-year rules that govern when Roth funds can be withdrawn without tax or penalty. The first five-year rule relates to whether earnings can be taken out tax- and penalty-free and requires the account to have been open at least five years combined with being age 59.5. The second five-year rule is an anti-abuse mechanism that applies the 10% early distribution penalty to converted funds if withdrawn before both the five-year conversion period and age 59.5 are met. The video walks through several real-world scenarios and addresses common questions about Roth 401k rollovers, inherited IRAs, and how changing custodians affects the clock.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth IRA ordering rules dictate that contributions come out first, then conversions, then earnings.
- The first five-year rule applies to earnings withdrawals and requires both a five-year-old Roth and being age 59.5.
- The second five-year rule is per-conversion and applies a 10% penalty to converted principal if withdrawn before five years and before age 59.5.
- Once you are over 59.5, the second five-year rule no longer applies regardless of how long the conversion has been in the account.
- Rolling a Roth 401k into a newly opened Roth IRA restarts the five-year clock for the IRA contribution rule.
- The five-year clock can be backdated by making a prior-year Roth contribution before the April 15 tax deadline.

## Detailed Notes

### Roth IRA Ordering Rules Overview [00:00:44]

Funds come out of a Roth IRA in a specific order: contributions first (always tax- and penalty-free), then conversions, then earnings. Within conversions, pre-tax amounts precede after-tax amounts. Understanding this sequence is foundational to applying the five-year rules correctly.

### First Five-Year Rule: Earnings Withdrawals [00:04:27]

Earnings from Roth accounts are tax- and penalty-free only if two conditions are both met: the account holder is at least 59.5 and any Roth IRA has been open and funded for at least five years. The five-year clock starts on January 1 of the year the first Roth IRA was funded and does not reset when new accounts are opened or when moving to a different custodian.

### Second Five-Year Rule: Conversion Penalty [00:07:15]

This rule prevents circumventing the 10% early withdrawal penalty by converting traditional IRA funds to Roth and immediately withdrawing. Each conversion carries its own five-year period; withdrawing converted principal before five years and before age 59.5 triggers the 10% penalty. The rule becomes irrelevant once the account holder reaches 59.5.

### Scenarios and Special Cases [00:11:38]

Key Q&A topics include: transferring a Roth between custodians does not reset the clock; inherited IRAs are not eligible for Roth conversions; Roth 401k plans each have their own independent five-year clock; rolling a Roth 401k into an existing Roth IRA backdates to the IRA's original open date, but rolling into a new Roth IRA resets the clock for the contribution five-year rule.
