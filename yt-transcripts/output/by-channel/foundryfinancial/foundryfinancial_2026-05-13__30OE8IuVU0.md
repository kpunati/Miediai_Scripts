# 18% of Americans Are Millionaires… But Most Can’t Afford to Retire

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=_30OE8IuVU0
**Duration:** 13:37

## Summary

This video challenges the headline statistic that 18% of Americans are millionaires, explaining that net worth calculations include illiquid assets like home equity that cannot fund retirement spending. The host introduces the 'illiquid stress machine' concept to describe households that are paper millionaires but income-poor in retirement. Three households with comparable net worth are compared to show how liquidity and planning dramatically impact actual retirement spending capacity. The core argument is that net worth is not a retirement plan — what matters is a strategy for converting assets into reliable income.

## Key Takeaways

- The 18% millionaire statistic includes home equity and retirement accounts, making it misleading as a measure of retirement readiness.
- Being 'house rich and income poor' is a common trap where significant net worth is locked in illiquid real estate.
- A household with $1 million in net worth heavily tied to a paid-off home may only be able to generate $42,000-$46,000 per year in retirement income.
- A similar net worth with $1 million in investable assets can generate nearly twice as much annual retirement income.
- Using a dynamic withdrawal strategy, Roth conversions, and optimized Social Security claiming can substantially increase sustainable spending.
- Net worth is a scorecard, not a retirement plan; the real goal is converting assets into sufficient monthly income to cover lifestyle expenses.
- The gap between guaranteed income (Social Security, pensions) and desired spending is the most important number to focus on.

## Detailed Notes

### The 18% Millionaire Statistic and Its Limitations [00:01:28]

Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances data shows 18% of U.S. households have a net worth over $1 million, but this figure includes home value, 401k, IRA, cars, and all other assets. For many retirees, a large portion of this net worth is the appreciated value of a paid-off home, which cannot be accessed for retirement spending without selling and relocating.

### Three Layers of Being a Millionaire [00:03:07]

Household 1 has $650k home + $300k 401k + $50k cash = $1M net worth, generating only $42,000-$46,000/year with Social Security. Household 2 has $400k home + $1M investable assets = $1.4M net worth, generating $75,000-$80,000/year. Household 3 has $1.2-$1.3M in investable assets with no home ownership, using dynamic withdrawal and optimized Social Security to spend over $95,000/year. The difference in spendable income across households with similar net worth is almost entirely driven by asset liquidity and planning sophistication.

### The Real Goal: Income, Not Net Worth [00:09:50]

Net worth is not a retirement plan and should not be the primary goal. The author argues that focusing on net worth as a scorecard distracts from the true objective: creating sufficient income to cover retirement lifestyle costs. A retirement plan must address withdrawal strategy, income sources, and how to fill the gap between guaranteed income and spending needs.

### Building a Retirement Paycheck [00:11:50]

The host summarizes that retirement planning is fundamentally a gap equation: guaranteed income minus desired spending. Retirees should use software like RightCapital to analyze their specific situation rather than relying on general headlines about retirement savings benchmarks.
