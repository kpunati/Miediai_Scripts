# Why Smart Retirees Still Run Out of Money (It's Not What You Think)

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=DOAWeZ8-qRQ
**Duration:** 23:19

## Summary

Kevin Lum argues that retirement failures are rarely caused by insufficient savings but rather by five underappreciated risk factors. He demonstrates sequence of returns risk using a custom calculator showing how the year of retirement and portfolio composition interact to produce wildly different outcomes with identical average returns. He further covers spending shocks, tax drag from improper withdrawal sequencing, inflation's compounding erosion of purchasing power, and the counterintuitive psychological risk of retirees spending too little and missing the 'go-go years' of early retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- Sequence of returns risk — not average return — is what determines whether a retiree's portfolio survives; early large losses can trigger an income death spiral.
- The bucket strategy (1-2 years cash, short-duration bonds, then longer-duration assets) protects against forced equity selling during market downturns.
- 83% of retirees face at least one unexpected spending shock per year averaging 10% of income, requiring a spending buffer.
- Tax drag from withdrawal order can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars; Roth conversions during the pre-RMD window can significantly lower lifetime taxes.
- Healthcare inflation averages approximately 4.5% annually versus general CPI, requiring a separate higher inflation rate assumption in retirement planning.
- Nearly 46% of retirees experience anxiety about spending savings, causing many to underspend and forgo meaningful experiences.
- Of the five risk factors, only spending shocks are largely outside the retiree's control — the others can be meaningfully mitigated through planning.

## Detailed Notes

### Sequence of Returns Risk [00:01:22]

Two retirees with identical starting wealth of $1 million, identical $50,000/year withdrawals, and identical 7% average returns can have dramatically different outcomes based solely on when losses occur. Morningstar research indicates a 15%+ first-year loss makes running out of money six times more likely over 30 years. The bucket strategy — holding 6-8 years of expenses across cash, short bonds, and intermediate bonds — allows waiting out equity bear markets without forced selling.

### Calculator Walkthrough: Retiring in 2000 [00:03:02]

A live calculator shows four portfolios (100% S&P 500, global 60/40, 40/60, and balanced equity tilting to small-cap value) all starting with $1 million and withdrawing $50,000/year inflation-adjusted. The S&P 500 portfolio was depleted by ~2016; global 60/40 by 2024; the balanced equity and 40/60 portfolios survived. At a 4% withdrawal rate, the S&P 500 portfolio retained ~$98,000 while the balanced equity portfolio grew to ~$5 million, showing how portfolio composition interacts with sequence risk.

### Spending Shocks and Tax Drag [00:07:57]

Boston College research shows 83% of retirees experience unexpected expenses annually averaging 10% of income, including home repairs ($15-20K for roof replacement), car replacement, and health crises (58% of households). Tax drag compounds through IRMAA Medicare surcharges, bracket creep from IRA withdrawals, and increased Social Security taxation; strategic Roth conversions during the pre-RMD window can save hundreds of thousands in lifetime taxes.

### Inflation and Spending Psychology [00:12:29]

At 3% inflation, purchasing power halves in 25 years; healthcare inflation runs at ~4.5% annually per BLS, requiring a separate inflation rate in planning models. The underspending trap affects nearly half of retirees: EBRI data shows retirees spend only about half of their safe withdrawal capacity, driven by decades of savings conditioning. A good financial plan provides explicit 'permission to spend' and shows the client what they can safely withdraw, preventing the squandering of the early healthy years of retirement.
