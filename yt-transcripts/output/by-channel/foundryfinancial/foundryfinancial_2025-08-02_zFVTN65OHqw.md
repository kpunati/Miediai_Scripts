# Why Retirees With $1M Actually End Up With $5M

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=zFVTN65OHqw
**Duration:** 16:38

## Summary

Kevin Lum introduces the 'consumption gap'—a documented research finding that many retirees spend far less than their portfolios could support, often ending retirement richer than they started. He cites Michael Kitces' historical analysis showing that 4% annual withdrawals more often lead to wealth multiplication than portfolio depletion. The video argues that psychological barriers, lack of planning, and perverse incentives in the wealth management industry drive chronic underspending, and proposes a dynamic income strategy paired with a bucket approach as the solution.

## Key Takeaways

- Many retirees will end their retirement with more money than they started with, not from great investing but because they were afraid to spend.
- Michael Kitces' research shows that historically, a 4% withdrawal rate led to double the starting wealth in over two-thirds of scenarios going back to 1870.
- You are statistically more likely to end retirement with five times your starting wealth than with less than you started with on a 4% withdrawal strategy.
- The wealth management industry has a financial incentive to encourage underspending because larger account balances generate higher advisory fees.
- A dynamic income strategy—adjusting spending based on market performance with a bucket approach—provides evidence-based confidence to spend appropriately.
- Retirees should define the purpose of their money and write down spending goals to overcome the psychological barrier of switching from a savings to a spending mindset.

## Detailed Notes

### The Consumption Gap Defined [00:01:00]

Research from the Journal of Financial Planning found that many retirees spend much less than the 4% rule would allow, with assets holding steady or increasing even during poor market periods like 2000-2008. The 'wealthy' threshold in the study was approximately $700,000 or more in assets.

### Michael Kitces Research on Retirement Spending [00:04:27]

Kitces modeled a couple with $1 million in a 60/40 portfolio over a 30-year retirement at 8% returns and 3% inflation; they could spend $61,000/year (6.1%) and end at zero, touching principal only after year 17. Under more conservative assumptions (7% return, 4% inflation), the sustainable spend was $49,000/year (4.9%). Historical Monte Carlo analysis back to 1870 showed retirees using 4% were more likely to quintuple their wealth than to deplete it.

### Causes of Underspending [00:08:57]

The Journal of Financial Planning identified fear, failure to plan, and lack of strategy confidence as the primary causes of conservative consumption. Most retirees have never systematically thought about withdrawal strategy; of those who have, only 40% have a formal plan. The wealth management industry's fee structures further incentivize advisors to keep account balances large.

### The Dynamic Income Strategy Solution [00:11:01]

Lum recommends a dynamic income strategy: an evidence-based plan stress-tested against historic market conditions that builds in spending flexibility during downturns. Combined with a bucket strategy for liquidity tiering and regular plan reviews, this approach allows retirees to spend more than a rigid rule-based plan while maintaining appropriate risk controls.
