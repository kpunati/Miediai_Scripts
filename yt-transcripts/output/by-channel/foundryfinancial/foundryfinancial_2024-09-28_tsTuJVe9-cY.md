# 80% of Retirees Make This Mistake - It's Easy to Avoid

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=tsTuJVe9-cY
**Duration:** 14:25

## Summary

Kevin Lum presents evidence from a 2021 JP Morgan Chase study that the majority of retirees delay drawing from tax-deferred accounts until RMDs force them to, allowing balances to compound and then requiring large distributions that push income into higher tax brackets. He walks through how ordinary income tax brackets work in 2024, demonstrates the tax torpedo effect where additional withdrawals cause more Social Security income to become taxable, and models the long-term impact using Right Capital: a couple starting at age 62 pays roughly $171K in lifetime taxes versus $430K if they wait until age 72 to begin withdrawals from the same $500K starting balance.

## Key Takeaways

- Delaying qualified account withdrawals until RMDs are mandatory allows balances to compound, which increases the forced distribution amounts and pushes income into higher tax brackets.
- The conventional wisdom that retirement tax rates are lower than working tax rates is not always true — late-stage RMDs can generate the highest tax bill a retiree has ever had.
- Starting withdrawals or Roth conversions early uses lower tax brackets more efficiently and permanently reduces the future RMD burden.
- The Social Security tax torpedo means additional IRA withdrawals or conversions can cause a disproportionate increase in total taxes by bringing more Social Security into taxable income.
- Proactive early withdrawals into a taxable brokerage account can be more tax-efficient than letting compounding create an unmanageable RMD problem later.
- The difference in lifetime federal taxes between starting withdrawals at 62 versus 72 can exceed $250,000 even from a modest starting balance.

## Detailed Notes

### The JP Morgan Study and the RMD Delay Problem [00:00:00]

A 2021 JP Morgan Chase study found that 80% of retirees under RMD age take no withdrawals from tax-deferred accounts, and 84% of those already in RMDs take only the minimum required. The study was based on EBRI data from 23 million accounts and JP Morgan's own 62 million household dataset. Lum argues that waiting for RMDs allows balances to compound, resulting in larger mandatory distributions that are taxed as ordinary income — potentially at higher rates than during working years.

### How Ordinary Income Tax Brackets Work in Retirement [00:03:09]

Lum walks through 2024 standard deductions ($16,500 for single filers 65+, $32,300 for married couples) and the progressive federal brackets (10%, 12%, 22%, and beyond). The key insight is that retirees with low income can fill lower brackets with IRA withdrawals at minimal tax cost, whereas delaying forces future distributions into higher brackets. He uses four scenarios to show how different income combinations result in federal tax bills ranging from $95 to $380.

### The Social Security Tax Torpedo [00:09:29]

When provisional income exceeds $44,000 for married filers, up to 85% of Social Security benefits become taxable. Adding $20,000 of additional IRA income to an already-tight bracket can more than double the tax bill because it simultaneously pushes Social Security inclusion from 22% to 56% of benefits taxable, cascading income from the 10% into the 12% bracket. Lum describes this as the 'tax torpedo' and advises careful planning before additional conversions or withdrawals in years with Social Security income.

### Long-Term Modeling: Age 62 vs. Age 72 Withdrawals [00:11:36]

Using Right Capital with a couple pulling $2,000/month from a $500,000 60/40 qualified account, starting at age 62 yields lifetime RMDs of $2.3M and total federal taxes of $171,000. Delaying to age 72 grows the account larger, producing $3.5M in RMDs and total federal taxes of $430,000. The conclusion is straightforward: early proactive withdrawals or Roth conversions keep balances manageable and taxes in check.
