# Eight Things To STOP DOING After 60

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=4LSX-zitbD0
**Duration:** 19:39

## Summary

Kevin Lum addresses the behavioral and psychological shifts required for a fulfilling retirement, drawing on ideas from writers Sahil Bloom and Morgan Housel. He argues that many pre-retirees have optimized for accumulation so long that they struggle to transition to spending, prioritizing experiences, and accepting that their portfolio balance should decline. The eight recommendations move from relational priorities (spending time with family, treating money as a tool, reducing financial anxiety) through practical financial shifts (moving from accumulation to purposeful deaccumulation, buying flexibility over possessions) to health and authenticity, closing with a strong argument against market timing in favor of a rules-based process such as the bucket strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- By the time a child turns 18, a parent has already spent approximately 95% of the total time they will spend with them — a reframing that should shift how retirees allocate time and money.
- Treating money as a scoreboard creates anxiety and perpetually moving goalposts; reframing it as a tool to fund specific goals enables clearer spending decisions.
- People who have already funded their retirement with a high probability of success should shift from maximum accumulation to purposeful deaccumulation and intentional spending.
- Buying time and independence — through flexibility and experiences — typically delivers more sustained satisfaction than material purchases, which create ongoing maintenance obligations.
- Health is the most valuable asset in retirement; spending on exercise, nutrition, and preventive care has compounding returns that financial assets cannot replicate.
- Market timing consistently causes retirees to miss recoveries; a process-driven approach with cash, bonds, and equities in structured buckets provides income stability without requiring predictions.

## Detailed Notes

### Experiences Over Possessions: Time with Loved Ones [00:00:40]

Drawing on Sahil Bloom's concept of counting remaining interactions with parents and family, Lum argues that retirees should treat experiences as investments rather than expenses. He contrasts spending $3,000 on a meaningful trip with the passive spending on rarely-used insurance premiums or cars.

### Money as a Tool, Not a Scoreboard [00:02:13]

Lum cites Morgan Housel's framework distinguishing money as a tool versus a scoreboard. Scoreboard thinking creates endless goalposts — wanting $2 million after reaching $1 million — and transforms portfolio drawdown into a feeling of losing. The antidote is defining specific jobs for the money: security, freedom, legacy, or experiences. A client example illustrates someone with $3.2 million who was paralyzed by portfolio decline even though $1.8 million would have fully funded their retirement.

### Stopping Blind Saving and Embracing Deaccumulation [00:08:01]

Lum distinguishes between people who still need to save more and those who have already won the retirement game — essential expenses covered, emergency reserves full, plan stress-tested — but continue saving out of habit and fear. For the latter group, the shift required is from maximum accumulation to purposeful deaccumulation: spending intentionally on people, experiences, and causes aligned with personal values.

### Health Investing and Market Timing [00:12:02]

Health is characterized as the most valuable asset, with millions in savings becoming irrelevant after a health event, while modest savers can live richly with energy and mobility. On market timing, Lum notes that investors who asked about getting out of the market in 2012 or March 2020 missed major rallies. The recommended alternative is a bucket strategy — cash for near-term needs, bonds for medium-term stability, equities for long-term growth — that works in both good and bad market seasons without requiring timing predictions.
