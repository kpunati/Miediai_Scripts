# The 6 Levels of Retirement Wealth — Where Do You Fall?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=XoPuQl2CxRE
**Duration:** 19:51

## Summary

Kevin Lum frames retirement planning advice through six wealth tiers derived from Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances data, arguing that most YouTube advice is scattershot because the correct playbook varies dramatically by net worth. Each level has distinct vulnerabilities: the financially vulnerable focus on maximizing Social Security and accessing benefits; the stable-but-limited must get pension survivor elections and Social Security tax interactions right; the comfortable middle needs income coordination and long-term care planning; the upper middle class must model Roth conversions and manage IRMAA; the affluent need advanced tax strategies and concentration management; and the elite must plan for estate taxes and wealth transfer. The video concludes that regardless of wealth level, every retiree needs an income plan, a tax strategy, a long-term care plan, and an updated estate plan.

## Key Takeaways

- The six levels of retirement wealth each require a fundamentally different planning playbook, making generic retirement advice unreliable.
- At the lowest wealth levels, maximizing Social Security, accessing Medicare savings programs, and understanding Medicaid rules are more important than investment optimization.
- At middle wealth levels ($500K-$1.5M), building a coordinated income plan and addressing long-term care insurance is critical, as Medicaid may not be available but self-insurance is risky.
- At upper-middle wealth ($1.5M-$5M), Roth conversions during the retirement window and active IRMAA management can prevent significant long-term tax drag.
- At affluent and elite levels ($5M+), advanced strategies like options collars, 351 exchanges, GRATs, SLATs, and dynasty trusts become relevant for concentration management and estate tax minimization.
- Estate planning is essential at every wealth level, but complexity and urgency increase significantly at higher net worth tiers.

## Detailed Notes

### Level 1: Financially Vulnerable (Under $100K) [00:01:02]

Retirement plan is almost entirely Social Security. Priority actions include maximizing Social Security through delayed claiming or higher earnings, getting Medicare right (including savings programs and prescription assistance), checking for SNAP and utility assistance programs, and understanding the Medicaid 60-month look-back period to avoid clawbacks. A will and healthcare directive are the minimum estate planning requirements.

### Level 2: Stable but Limited ($100K-$500K) [00:04:09]

Income covers basics but with little margin. Key priorities include getting pension survivor election right (joint-and-survivor likely appropriate for married couples) and understanding how IRA withdrawals interact with Social Security taxation. Home equity is often the primary asset, and strategic use of that equity warrants consideration. Medicaid estate recovery rules matter here, and long-term care may ultimately be Medicaid-dependent.

### Level 3: Comfortable Middle ($500K-$1.5M) [00:05:52]

This level requires a formal income plan to understand spending capacity and overcome the psychological difficulty of shifting from accumulation to spending. Roth conversions may make sense but require modeling. Long-term care insurance or hybrid life/LTC policies are worth serious consideration because this tier often has too much money for Medicaid but not enough to comfortably self-insure. Coordination of accounts and income sources becomes important.

### Level 4: Upper Middle Class ($1.5M-$5M) [00:10:33]

Roth conversions should be modeled, especially during the golden retirement window before Social Security and RMDs. IRMAA surcharges at each cliff level require careful income management. Asset location (tax-efficient assets in IRA, growth in Roth, equity in taxable) becomes important. Estate planning with revocable trusts becomes more valuable as taxable and non-IRA assets grow. The central planning question shifts from 'how much can I spend' to 'where should I withdraw from and why.'

### Levels 5-6: Affluent and Elite ($5M+) [00:13:21]

At $5M-$15M, survival is not the concern — optimization and legacy planning dominate. Net investment income tax, capital gains timing, donor-advised funds, and concentration management via options collars, 351 exchanges, and NUA strategies become relevant. Above $15-20M, estate tax planning with GRATs, SLATs, dynasty trusts, and ILITs becomes critical, as compounding can push current assets far above current exemption levels. Family governance and philanthropy also emerge as major planning considerations.
