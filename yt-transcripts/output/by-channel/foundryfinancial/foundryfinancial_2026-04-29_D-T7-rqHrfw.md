# Do You REALLY Need Long-Term Care Insurance?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=D-T7-rqHrfw
**Duration:** 14:35

## Summary

Kevin Lum examines long-term care planning through a data-driven lens, presenting actual 2025 cost figures alongside probability statistics that show most people will not face catastrophic care needs. He compares the three main funding approaches—self-funding, traditional standalone LTCi, and linked-benefit life/annuity products with LTC riders—explaining the tradeoffs of each. His overarching thesis is that fear-based LTCi purchases are poor planning; instead, decisions should flow from a complete financial plan that accounts for probability, home equity as a backup, and individual health and family history.

## Key Takeaways

- About half of people aged 65+ will never need paid long-term care; only 6% will need five or more years of care, making catastrophic outcomes a minority scenario.
- The 'tricky middle' of households with $500,000 to $3 million in net worth is most exposed because they cannot rely on Medicaid and may not be able to absorb large care costs without disrupting the plan.
- Self-funding is the default for households with sufficient assets and flexibility; home equity provides a meaningful backup that most planning software does not model.
- Traditional standalone LTCi premiums are not guaranteed to remain level—insurers can raise them on entire policy classes subject to state approval—which has burned many policyholders.
- Linked-benefit life insurance policies with LTC riders eliminate the 'use it or lose it' objection but are typically more expensive and can be oversold.
- Long-term care planning should protect the downside without allowing worst-case scenarios to suppress spending and quality of life throughout retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Actual Long-Term Care Costs [00:01:03]

Based on the 2025 CareScout Cost of Care Study, national median assisted living costs approximately $X per year (the transcript caption was unclear on the assisted living figure), semi-private nursing home rooms run about $114,000–$115,000 annually, private rooms about $129,000, and in-home non-medical care around $80,000 assuming 44 hours per week for 52 weeks. Costs vary significantly by state, with California and New York being substantially higher.

### Probability Data Most People Miss [00:03:09]

HHS research indicates roughly 50% of people will need paid long-term care at some point, but only 14% will need two or more years and only 6% will need five or more years. About 15% will face lifetime costs above $250,000 from age 65. Women tend to spend longer in care than men. For the general population, catastrophic outcomes are real but not the most likely scenario.

### Three Funding Options and Their Tradeoffs [00:05:15]

Self-funding works best for healthy households with sufficient assets; RightCapital typically models two years of care per spouse and while it looks large in projection, home equity is an additional lever not captured in the software. Traditional standalone LTCi has flexible pricing risk and psychological 'use it or lose it' friction, but suits people with strong family history, single status, or a preference for certainty. Linked-benefit products (life insurance or annuity with LTC rider) resolve the use-it-or-lose-it objection but cost more; they can also be funded by converting an unneeded whole life policy.

### Lum's Recommendation Framework [00:12:20]

Self-funding is Lum's baseline for most clients, supplemented by a case-by-case assessment of whether insurance makes sense given family history, support networks, and planning horizon. He explicitly warns against reactive purchasing driven by fear-inducing statistics or salespeople, arguing that fear-based planning often leads to buying products that are not actually needed given the individual's situation.
