# Social Security: Stop Waiting Until 70? The Math Nobody Shows You

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-10-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=7NkIQsw8P9M
**Duration:** 17:55

## Summary

Kevin Lum, CFP, presents a detailed review of Derek Tharp's academic article challenging the conventional wisdom to delay Social Security until age 70. Tharp argues that the expected value framework ignores opportunity cost, and identifies eight risks of delay including mortality risk, sequence of return risk, policy risk, regret risk, health span risk, loss of optionality, and underspending risk. Lum uses the Income Lab planning software to illustrate how opportunity cost can erode the benefit of delay. He then offers substantial counterpoints: flexibility to change plans later, the significant tax planning window that delay creates for Roth conversions, the critical spousal survivor benefit protection from delaying the higher earner's benefit, built-in COLA inflation protection, and the reverse glide path strategy that starts conservative and adds equity risk as guaranteed income increases.

## Key Takeaways

- Tharp argues that conventional advice to delay Social Security until 70 relies on flawed expected value math that ignores opportunity cost and human utility preferences.
- At a 5% real return assumption, the lifetime benefit difference between claiming at 62 versus 70 becomes much smaller once opportunity cost is accounted for.
- Eight risks of delaying include mortality risk, sequence of return risk, policy risk, opportunity cost, regret risk, health span risk, loss of optionality, and underspending risk.
- Lum's strongest counterpoint is spousal protection: the higher-earning spouse delaying locks in maximum lifetime survivor income for the lower-earning spouse.
- Delaying Social Security creates a tax planning window for Roth conversions and tax-efficient withdrawals that can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.
- The reverse glide path strategy—starting conservative and increasing equity allocation as guaranteed income rises—is enabled by delaying Social Security and is not addressed in Tharp's analysis.

## Detailed Notes

### Tharp's Critique: Flawed Assumptions in Conventional Advice [00:02:05]

Tharp argues retirement planners rely on an expected value framework that treats every dollar equally, ignoring the higher utility of dollars received at younger, healthier ages. They also typically use low or 0% discount rates, failing to account for the opportunity cost of portfolio withdrawals made while delaying Social Security. Income Lab modeling using a 5% real return shows very little lifetime benefit difference between claiming at 62 versus 70.

### Eight Risks of Delaying Social Security [00:06:50]

Tharp identifies: (1) mortality risk—significant probability of dying before the break-even point at year 17; (2) sequence of return risk—drawing down a portfolio during a market downturn compounds losses; (3) policy risk—Social Security trust fund projected to deplete in 2033; (4) opportunity cost of approximately 4-5% real portfolio return; (5) regret risk if diagnosed with terminal illness after delaying; (6) health span risk—money is worth more at 62 when healthy; (7) loss of optionality—portfolio is flexible, Social Security is not; (8) underspending risk—retirees psychologically spend Social Security more freely than portfolio withdrawals.

### Lum's Counterpoints: Why Delay Often Still Makes Sense [00:12:34]

Lum emphasizes that delay preserves flexibility—you can always claim earlier later, but cannot undo an early claim. The tax planning window created by delaying is potentially worth hundreds of thousands in tax savings through Roth conversions and managing RMDs. The spousal survivor benefit is the most critical factor: the higher earner delaying maximizes lifetime income for a surviving spouse. Social Security's COLA provides inflation protection that bonds and fixed income cannot match, and the reverse glide path—starting conservative and adding equities as guaranteed income rises—is a viable strategy the article ignores.
