# Do You Need a Brokerage Account If You Already Have Retirement Accounts?

**Channel:** Kevin Lum, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-04-18
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=uxiTHSqFvSQ
**Duration:** 16:59

## Summary

Kevin Lum, CFP, provides a nuanced framework for deciding whether to open or maintain a taxable brokerage account when one already has retirement accounts. He argues that those near retirement without surplus cash flow gain little from opening one, but that high savers in the accumulation phase benefit substantially from its tax flexibility. The video walks through how brokerage accounts act as a pressure valve for RMDs, facilitate efficient Roth conversions, and provide legacy advantages via step-up in basis. Specific 2026 capital gains tax brackets are cited to illustrate how significant tax-free withdrawals can be for retirees.

## Key Takeaways

- Opening a taxable brokerage account is unnecessary for retirees who lack surplus cash flow and whose current retirement accounts are sufficient.
- During the accumulation phase, taxable brokerage accounts create unique planning flexibility that tax-deferred and Roth accounts alone cannot provide.
- Using brokerage account funds to pay taxes on Roth conversions preserves the full converted amount rather than diluting it.
- Step-up in basis makes taxable brokerage accounts the second most advantageous account type for inheritance, behind Roth accounts.
- Capital gains rates stack on top of ordinary income, so having other income sources can push gains into the 15% bracket even for otherwise lower-income retirees.
- Tax gain harvesting allows investors to reset their cost basis annually using the 0% capital gains bracket.
- The account's absence of contribution limits, withdrawal penalties, and age restrictions provides psychological freedom alongside financial flexibility.

## Detailed Notes

### When a Brokerage Account Is Not Worth Opening [00:00:43]

If a retiree has no surplus cash flow and is not actively saving beyond expenses, opening a taxable brokerage account adds complexity without benefit. Moving money from tax-deferred accounts to a taxable account is essentially rearranging dollars; Roth conversions are almost always a better use of those funds. Simplicity in retirement has real value, especially as one ages.

### When Taxable Brokerage Accounts Are Highly Valuable [00:04:29]

Investors still accumulating with surplus cash flow and maxed-out retirement accounts benefit most. A brokerage account creates a source of funds that can finance Roth conversions without reducing the converted principal, and it provides flexibility around ACA subsidies in early retirement. It also acts as a 'pressure valve' against oversized RMDs that could push retirees into higher tax brackets and trigger elevated Medicare premiums.

### Legacy Planning and Step-Up in Basis [00:07:15]

Taxable brokerage accounts rank second only to Roth accounts in inheritance value because heirs receive a step-up in basis to the date-of-death market value, potentially eliminating capital gains entirely. In contrast, inherited traditional IRAs are taxed as ordinary income and require distribution within a 10-year window, making them the least advantageous account type to pass on.

### 2026 Capital Gains Brackets and Tax Gain Harvesting [00:10:38]

Specific 2026 figures show zero-percent long-term capital gains thresholds of roughly $65,000 for single filers under 65 and over $131,000 for married couples under 65, both inclusive of the standard deduction. Capital gains rates stack on top of ordinary income, so other income sources can erode the 0% bracket. Tax gain harvesting involves intentionally realizing gains within the 0% bracket and immediately repurchasing a different investment to reset the cost basis.

### Psychological and Practical Freedom [00:13:30]

Unlike retirement accounts, taxable brokerage accounts have no contribution limits, no age-based withdrawal penalties, no 5-year rules, and allow margin borrowing. The presenter argues that this freedom reduces anxiety about spending in retirement, supporting the principle that money is a tool rather than a scoreboard.
