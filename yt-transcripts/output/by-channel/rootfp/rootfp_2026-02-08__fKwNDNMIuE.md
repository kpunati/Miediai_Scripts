# Why $5 Million is the Tax "Danger Zone"

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=_fKwNDNMIuE
**Duration:** 17:42

## Summary

This video by James Conole of Root Financial addresses the tax complexities that arise once a portfolio exceeds $5 million, arguing that the challenge shifts from accumulation to protection. Conole frames the core goal as minimizing lifetime tax liability rather than just current-year taxes. He walks through four specific strategies: strategic multi-account withdrawals, intentional Roth conversions calibrated to the tax-planning window between retirement and RMD onset, asset location to optimize after-tax returns, and capital gains and charitable planning including donor-advised funds and qualified charitable distributions.

## Key Takeaways

- At $5M+, portfolios typically span multiple account types — brokerage, pre-tax, HSA, Roth — and how money is withdrawn matters more than how much is saved.
- Roth conversions must be compared against future projected tax brackets, not assumed tax directions; converting blindly into the 35% bracket can cost nearly $1M.
- Asset location — placing tax-inefficient holdings like REITs in tax-deferred accounts — can meaningfully increase after-tax returns on large portfolios.
- Gifting highly appreciated securities to a charity or donor-advised fund avoids capital gains taxes while providing the full fair-market deduction.
- Qualified charitable distributions from a traditional IRA for those 70.5+ reduce RMDs, avoid income tax, and benefit the charity — a triple win.
- Combining Roth conversions with charitable deductions in the same year can allow larger conversions at low effective tax rates.

## Detailed Notes

### Why $5M Changes the Tax Equation [00:00:00]

Above $5M, portfolio income alone can push investors into high tax brackets without any earned income. Pre-tax account balances grow into large future RMDs, risking IRMAA surcharges and elevated ordinary income taxes. Conole reframes the goal from minimizing this year's taxes to minimizing lifetime tax liability.

### Strategy 1: Strategic Multi-Account Withdrawals [00:02:18]

In retirement, investors can choose which account types to draw from and in what amounts, enabling intentional tax-bracket filling. Unlike working years where wages are fully subject to ordinary income tax, retirees can manufacture their income mix across brokerage, pre-tax, HSA, and Roth accounts to stay within targeted tax brackets.

### Strategy 2: Intentional Roth Conversions [00:03:40]

Roth conversions require comparing today's tax rate to projected future rates based on personal income mix, not generic federal bracket forecasts. A case study with a couple earning $1M and holding $7.5M shows that over-converting into the 35% bracket cost nearly $1M in taxes, while targeting the 22% bracket yielded roughly $250K in tax savings. The optimal conversion window typically falls between retirement and when RMDs begin.

### Strategy 3: Asset Location Optimization [00:08:54]

Asset location determines which accounts hold specific asset classes to maximize after-tax returns, distinct from asset allocation. High-dividend investments such as REITs should be held in tax-deferred accounts; their dividends are typically taxed as ordinary income, not at preferential rates, making brokerage account placement costly in high-income-tax states.

### Strategy 4: Capital Gains and Charitable Planning [00:10:49]

For 2026, the 0% long-term capital gains threshold for married filers is $98,900 of taxable income. Investors with low taxable income can harvest gains and reset cost basis annually. For charitable giving, donating appreciated securities directly avoids capital gains, delivers the full market-value deduction, and can be combined with donor-advised funds for multi-year deduction acceleration. Qualified charitable distributions from IRAs for those 70.5+ reduce RMDs and generate no taxable income.
