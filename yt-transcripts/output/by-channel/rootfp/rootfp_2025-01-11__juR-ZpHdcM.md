# Here’s Why Working One More Year is the Biggest Retirement Decision You’ll Ever Make

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=_juR-ZpHdcM
**Duration:** 14:26

## Summary

James Conole uses a detailed case study of Kurt and Eva (both age 67, $1.1-1.2M in investments, $870K home) to demonstrate why the decision to work one more year is far more complex than a purely financial calculation. He models how one extra year raises their probability of success by 10 percentage points through additional 401k contributions, portfolio growth, and an 8% Social Security benefit increase, but then pivots to argue that the personal costs — deteriorating health, lost travel years, and the deaths of colleagues right before planned retirement — must be weighed equally. A creative restructuring of their spending plan (front-loading travel for 10 years then reducing) significantly raises probability of success without extending work.

## Key Takeaways

- One additional year of work generates a compounded financial benefit: more 401k savings, continued portfolio growth, and an 8% Social Security increase from delayed retirement credits.
- The most overlooked factor in the 'one more year' decision is the personal cost — health, time together, and the non-guaranteed nature of a long retirement.
- Working 13 more years to maximize financial probability of success is an absurdly extreme scenario that illustrates the point that purely financial optimization misses what actually matters.
- Accurately modeling spending patterns — front-loading travel in active years then reducing — can significantly improve probability of success without requiring additional work years.
- Including home equity as a backstop option can cover scenarios where the portfolio runs short, providing a meaningful buffer in success projections.
- The real challenge is balancing financial security with living intentionally, avoiding the trap of 'just one more year' that extends indefinitely.

## Detailed Notes

### Kurt and Eva's Baseline Plan: Retire at 67 [00:00:00]

Kurt and Eva are both 67 with $1.1-1.2M in investments and a $870K paid-off home. They want to spend $8,000/month ($6,000 core expenses plus $2,000 travel) with an additional $4,500/year in out-of-pocket healthcare costs. Their combined Social Security at full retirement age is approximately $5,750/month. Initial modeling shows their portfolio lasting to age 90 at an assumed 6.5% growth rate, with an 83% probability of success.

### Impact of Working One More Year [00:05:13]

Delaying retirement to age 68 adds one more year of 401k contributions at 10% each, one more year of portfolio growth before drawdown, and one year of delayed retirement credits adding 8% to their Social Security benefit. Combined, these factors increase probability of success from 83% to 93% — a 10 percentage point gain. However, Conole argues this framing ignores the personal side: the couple's health is deteriorating due to work stress, and colleagues have died before reaching retirement.

### The Personal Cost Argument [00:06:18]

Conole presents the counter-argument to pure financial optimization: the couple's health is not guaranteed, travel goals are best fulfilled while they have energy, and no projection guarantees a 25-30 year retirement. He uses the extreme example of working to age 80 and delaying Social Security to 70 to illustrate that maximizing financial probability of success to 100% comes at the cost of everything that gives retirement meaning.

### Restructuring the Plan to Improve Success Without Working Longer [00:10:27]

By accurately front-loading the $2,000/month travel budget for only the first 10 years (ages 67-77) and reducing spending thereafter, the probability of success jumps significantly without any additional work. Including home equity as a fallback resource further improves the outlook. Conole concludes that the spectrum between financial and personal priorities requires a deliberate balance — over-indexing on either side leads to regret.
