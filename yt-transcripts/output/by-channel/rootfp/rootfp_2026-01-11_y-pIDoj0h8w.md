# I’ve Never Seen So Many Retirees Make This Same Mistake

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-01-11
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=y-pIDoj0h8w
**Duration:** 17:40

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial presents two contrasting but equally damaging portfolio mistakes commonly made by retirees: the DIY investor who stays concentrated in NASDAQ-style growth stocks from the accumulation phase, and the advisor-guided retiree placed into a generic 60/40 portfolio without underlying cash-flow analysis. Through two hypothetical case studies — John with $2M in NASDAQ holdings and Lisa with $2M in a 60/40 — Conole shows how sequence-of-returns risk devastates the concentrated portfolio and how an unnecessarily conservative allocation leaves millions on the table. The solution is to build a personalized portfolio starting from cash-flow needs, not from a default asset allocation.

## Key Takeaways

- What worked to build wealth during accumulation — concentration in high-growth stocks — can destroy retirement if a market decline occurs early in the drawdown phase.
- Sequence-of-returns risk means a 38% decline requires a 61-70% recovery just to break even, making early large drawdowns potentially unrecoverable.
- A generic 60/40 portfolio may leave significant wealth on the table for retirees with strong guaranteed income and modest portfolio needs.
- Bonds serve a specific purpose — protecting against sequence risk by providing a drawdown buffer — not as an arbitrary diversification rule.
- The amount held in bonds (or 'root reserves') should reflect how many years of living expenses need to be covered during potential bear markets of up to 5 years.
- Portfolio design should begin with a financial plan: cash-flow needs, guaranteed income sources, risk tolerance, and retirement timeline — allocation follows, it does not lead.

## Detailed Notes

### Two Paths to the Same Portfolio Mistake [00:00:00]

Conole identifies two retirement portfolio failure modes: DIY investors who over-concentrate in growth stocks (what worked during accumulation) and advisor clients placed into generic cookie-cutter 60/40 portfolios. Both fail because they are not grounded in the retiree's specific financial situation, especially cash-flow needs during drawdown.

### Case Study 1 — John and Sequence of Returns Risk [00:02:36]

John, age 62 with $2M in NASDAQ positions, retired believing 5% annual withdrawals were easily sustainable given double-digit compounding. In 2022, the NASDAQ declined ~33%, and combined with a $100K withdrawal, his portfolio fell to $1.24M. To recover, he needed a ~70% gain in a single year. This illustrates that early large losses during retirement withdrawals can be mathematically unrecoverable, regardless of long-term average returns.

### Case Study 2 — Lisa and the Cookie-Cutter 60/40 [00:08:33]

Lisa, also with $2M, was placed in a 60/40 portfolio by her advisor. In reality, she had strong pension and social security income and only needed $40,000/year from her portfolio. The appropriate allocation, derived from her cash-flow needs, was 90/10 — with only 5 years of expenses ($200K) held in high-quality short-term bonds. Modeled over 30 years, the 90/10 portfolio would produce over 85% more wealth than the 60/40.

### How to Build a Personalized Portfolio [00:11:49]

Bonds should be sized to cover 5 years of portfolio withdrawals — enough to buffer the longest typical bear markets — not allocated by an arbitrary percentage. The right stock-to-bond ratio is derived by working backwards from spending needs, not forward from a generic rule. The framework: first build a financial plan based on cash flows, then design the portfolio to support that plan.
