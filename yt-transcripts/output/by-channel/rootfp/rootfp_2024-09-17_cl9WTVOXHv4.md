# Retire Happy: Build a Personalized Retirement Plan that You Can't Wait to Live Out

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-17
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=cl9WTVOXHv4
**Duration:** 43:00

## Summary

This episode of Ready for Retirement features an interview with Monique Blasquez, a 56-year-old early retiree from the tech finance world, reflecting on her first two years of retirement. Monique discusses how a deteriorating workplace culture and health impacts triggered her decision to plan for early retirement, and how she ultimately gained confidence to retire after working with Root Financial Partners. While she has thrived in terms of health, hobbies, and personal relationships, she continues to wrestle with deep-seated saving instincts and family expectations around travel.

## Key Takeaways

- A hostile workplace event and declining health — not just financial readiness — can be the catalyst that pushes someone toward serious retirement planning.
- Having a clear plan for what to do in retirement (hobbies, structure, purpose) prevents the common 'feeling of loss' phase many retirees experience.
- Natural savers often remain psychologically disconnected from their portfolio even when financially secure, making the shift from accumulation to spending a real challenge.
- Family dynamics in retirement can be a source of friction when spouses and relatives have different expectations about travel and shared activities.
- Delegating portfolio management to a financial advisor can paradoxically make a saver feel they have less money, since they only feel in control of their checking account.
- Health outcomes — sleep, diet, exercise, and mental well-being — tend to improve dramatically once the stress of demanding work is removed.
- Educating yourself on stock compensation (RSUs, stock options, tax implications) earlier in your career can meaningfully affect when you are able to retire.

## Detailed Notes

### Monique's Background and Retirement Trigger [00:00:00]

Monique spent over 30 years in finance, with 21 years at a large tech company in cost analysis and forecasting roles. A department meeting where she was reprimanded for asking too many questions in front of junior analysts catalyzed her realization that the company's culture had shifted toward hierarchy, prompting her to begin seriously planning retirement five years before actually retiring.

### Five Years of Preparation [00:03:07]

During the five-year run-up to retirement, Monique consumed financial podcasts, joined a lottery-ticket group with co-workers dreaming of early retirement, and eventually sought out a fee-based financial advisor. Work demands had eroded her health through sleep deprivation, poor diet, and lack of exercise. She paid off her mortgage and invested steadily but lacked confidence that her savings were sufficient until getting professional confirmation.

### The Transition to Retirement [00:10:36]

Monique retired at 56 in 2022, roughly a year later than originally intended. She bypassed the typical 'feeling of loss' retirement phase because she had already identified meaningful hobbies and structured her day into a productive morning block and a creative or leisure afternoon block. Her brother's prediction that she would miss work proved wrong almost immediately.

### Challenges: Spending and Travel [00:15:54]

Two persistent challenges in retirement are her inability to shift from saving to spending, and family friction over travel. Monique has rationalized selling her car to avoid upcoming service and insurance costs, illustrating how deep the saving instinct runs. Separately, her family expected retirement to mean more travel, but Monique has no interest in traveling and has been unable to fully communicate or resolve this mismatch of expectations.

### A Spending Exercise and Saver Psychology [00:25:29]

James described a behavioral finance exercise: if Monique does not spend a sustainable portfolio withdrawal (framed as $100,000), it will be donated to a political candidate she dislikes. Even in this hypothetical scenario, Monique's first instinct was to find a way to route the money back into savings via her brother. This illustrates how identity as a saver persists even when there is clear financial capacity to spend.

### Health and Personal Transformation [00:32:49]

Since retiring, Monique sleeps better, eats better, and exercises regularly — a marked reversal from working conditions where she resented having to stop for basic human needs. She has become more positive, socially engaged, and present with family and friends, including regular multi-hour phone calls she previously had no time for. She no longer dreads waking up and appreciates small daily moments.

### Advice for Future Retirees [00:39:15]

Monique's key advice is to educate yourself on your own finances earlier, particularly on equity compensation like RSUs and stock options and their tax implications. She also recommends taking care of large health expenses before leaving employer coverage, planning what you will do in retirement (not just what you are retiring from), and approaching unforeseen challenges with confidence that the problem-solving skills built during a career will carry forward.
