# Top 5 Most Avoidable Retirement Mistakes

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=wAZJBNLm0BE
**Duration:** 16:39

## Summary

James Conole catalogs five retirement mistakes that are largely avoidable with better information: spending too little (the far more common problem than overspending), retiring at the wrong time relative to one's financial readiness, fixating on market volatility while ignoring inflation's purchasing-power erosion over a 30-year retirement, providing financially unsustainable support to adult children often disguised as 'investment' decisions, and entering retirement without a coherent strategy covering taxes, withdrawals, Social Security, and investment allocation. The video frames retirement success not just as financial survival but as living a purposeful and enjoyable retirement while remaining financially sound.

## Key Takeaways

- Underspending is far more common than overspending among well-prepared retirees; fear of running out causes people to forgo experiences that would have meaningfully improved quality of life.
- Retiring too late is not a financial mistake but a life mistake—sacrificing the healthiest retirement years for marginal portfolio growth that will go unspent.
- Inflation over a 30-year retirement can require spending $250,000 to replicate what $100,000 buys today; this long-run purchasing power risk is a bigger threat than short-term market volatility for well-diversified investors.
- Financially supporting adult children should be evaluated honestly as a family transfer, not rationalized as an investment—particularly when the household cannot truly afford it.
- Sequence of return risk can be mitigated through portfolio management strategies, but the bigger unmitigated risk is holding too much in conservative assets early in retirement while inflation silently erodes purchasing power.
- A documented retirement strategy covering expense projections, tax-efficient withdrawal sequencing, Social Security timing, and investment allocation is essential before retiring—not something to figure out ad hoc.

## Detailed Notes

### Mistake 1: Spending the Wrong Amount [00:00:00]

While overspending carries obvious risks, the more pervasive problem is identity-driven underspending: savers who lived beneath their means for decades continue that behavior in retirement even when their portfolio could support more. The recommended solution is understanding the sustainable upper spending limit—not to spend that amount necessarily, but to have the context to make informed choices about what experiences and expenditures to prioritize.

### Mistake 2: Retiring at the Wrong Time [00:04:11]

Retiring too early can strain finances through a smaller portfolio, lower Social Security benefits, and more years of full portfolio dependency. Retiring too late does not usually hurt financially but costs the healthiest and most active years of retirement, often driven by fear, inertia, or chasing one more bonus or stock vest. The right time is determined by the financial plan, not by fear or external incentives to keep working.

### Mistake 3: Focusing on Only One Risk [00:06:15]

Market volatility and sequence of return risk are widely discussed but historically temporary and manageable through diversification and portfolio structure. The underappreciated risk is inflation: $100,000 of retirement expenses today could require roughly $250,000 in 30 years to maintain the same lifestyle. Holding too much in conservative stable assets to avoid volatility leaves a portfolio unable to maintain purchasing power over a multi-decade retirement.

### Mistake 4: Excessive Support for Adult Children [00:09:24]

Financial transfers to adult children are frequently disguised as investments—such as purchasing a property for children to live in without collecting rent. The adviser emphasizes calling these transfers what they are (a family subsidy) rather than justifying them as investments, because the investment math collapses when there is no cash income. When the household genuinely has excess assets and this is a deliberate part of the financial plan, such gifting is encouraged.

### Mistake 5: No Retirement Strategy [00:12:29]

Entering retirement with a 'figure it out as I go' mindset leads to tax inefficiency, missed pension election opportunities, suboptimal Social Security timing, and inappropriate investment allocation. A proper strategy answers: What will expenses be? What non-portfolio income covers those expenses? How large must the portfolio be? Which accounts should be drawn first for tax efficiency? These questions must be answered before retirement, not in real time.
