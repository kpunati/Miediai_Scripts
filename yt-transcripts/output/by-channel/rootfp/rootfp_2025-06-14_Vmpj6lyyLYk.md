# Stop Worrying About Running Out of Money: Here's What You Need to Do

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-06-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Vmpj6lyyLYk
**Duration:** 14:42

## Summary

James Conole reframes the retirement money fear by showing that the 4% rule was designed as a worst-case floor, not a ceiling, and that historically the more common outcome is significantly growing wealth over a 30-year retirement. He argues that underspending is an equally serious risk because it squanders the purpose of the accumulated portfolio. The video presents two practical levers—broader portfolio diversification beyond the original 4% rule's assumptions and dynamic guardrails-based withdrawal rules—as ways to increase sustainable spending while maintaining appropriate downside protection.

## Key Takeaways

- The 4% rule is a worst-case safe floor derived from the worst historical 30-year market periods, not a typical or optimal withdrawal rate.
- In two out of three historical scenarios, a 4% withdrawal rate led to more than doubling the starting portfolio balance by end of retirement.
- Being too conservative with spending is a real risk: retirees who underspend may miss irreplaceable experiences and die with far more money than intended.
- Diversifying beyond the original 60/40 US-stock-plus-bond model—adding small cap, international, emerging markets, and real estate—provides more flexibility about which assets to draw from when specific markets are down.
- Dynamic guardrails frameworks allow a higher initial withdrawal rate while incorporating rules for spending cuts in downturns and raises in strong markets.
- A complete financial plan starts with desired lifestyle, estimates its cost, identifies all income sources including Social Security, and then quantifies the portfolio's precise role.

## Detailed Notes

### The 4% Rule: Origins and Historical Context [00:00:00]

Bill Bengen designed the 4% rule to find the maximum withdrawal that would not exhaust a portfolio in any historical 30-year period, including the Great Depression, two world wars, and periods of severe inflation. Michael Kitces's research on a 60/40 portfolio back to 1870 confirmed that the 4% rate almost never failed and that portfolio balances more often than not grew substantially over the retirement period.

### The Risk of Being Too Conservative [00:03:38]

Many retirees over-index on the fear of running out of money and end up dying with far larger portfolios than they intended, having foregone experiences and spending that would have materially improved their quality of life. A retiree who retired in 1975 on the 4% rule could have safely spent at a 7.5% rate, meaning they left roughly $35,000 per year of spending on the table every year for 30 years.

### Diversification and Dynamic Withdrawal Strategies [00:07:53]

The original 4% rule assumed a simple 50/50 US stock and intermediate bond portfolio. Broader diversification—including small cap, international developed, emerging markets, and real estate—provides more asset classes to draw from selectively based on which are performing well. Combining this with dynamic guardrails (adjusting withdrawals based on portfolio performance, not just inflation) allows a higher starting withdrawal rate while managing long-term portfolio sustainability.

### Building a Complete Retirement Plan [00:11:57]

Conole recommends constructing a full financial plan that sequences from desired lifestyle, to its cost, to available income sources like Social Security and pensions, to the remaining portfolio withdrawal need. He also flags the importance of maintaining margin for one-off large expenses such as health events or major home costs, which can disrupt even well-constructed income plans if not separately budgeted.
