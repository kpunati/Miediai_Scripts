# Work at SpaceX? Watch This Before your IPO

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-02-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=6sKvM9V2kdM
**Duration:** 18:54

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial Partners addresses SpaceX employees who may face a liquidity event around a potential 2026 IPO, arguing that the most common mistake is asking 'what should I do with my stock' before answering 'what do I want my life to look like.' Using a detailed case study of a 51-year-old SpaceX employee with $14M in RSUs, the video demonstrates how selling enough equity to fund early retirement — not all of it — creates financial independence while preserving upside participation. The video then covers three tactical strategies for managing concentrated SpaceX positions: direct indexing with tax loss harvesting, charitable giving via donor-advised funds, and option strategies for hedging downside risk.

## Key Takeaways

- The strategic question is not how much SpaceX stock to sell but what life outcome the equity should enable.
- Employees do not need to sell everything; they only need to sell enough to lock in early retirement and financial independence.
- SpaceX's lack of a 401k match means equity compensation dominates most employees' net worth, creating extreme concentration risk.
- Direct indexing and long-short separately managed accounts can generate tax losses to offset large capital gains from stock sales.
- Gifting appreciated stock to a donor-advised fund allows a large up-front deduction while spreading charitable distributions over decades.
- Put options can narrow the range of outcomes and allow a multi-year tax diversification strategy rather than one large taxable event.

## Detailed Notes

### Starting with Life Goals, Not Stock Decisions [00:00:00]

Conole argues that SpaceX employees typically ask the wrong first question — what to do with their stock — instead of defining what outcome they want. Without a clear life vision, any equity decision is a guess. The framework is to work backwards from desired lifestyle to determine liquidity, tax, and investment priorities.

### Case Study: $14M in SpaceX RSUs [00:02:24]

The hypothetical Andrew and Wendy are 51-year-old SpaceX employees in Los Angeles with $14M in RSUs and a small 401k balance. Andrew's initial plan was to wait until 60 to retire, believing SpaceX would appreciate further. The analysis shows this approach leaves an enormous, unnecessary portfolio at death while deferring freedom for nine years.

### Taking Enough Chips Off the Table [00:05:32]

Retiring at 51 rather than 60 reduces the terminal portfolio but delivers the freedom Andrew actually wants. The planner's exercise determines the minimum amount to diversify — enough so that even if the retained SpaceX position went to zero, the plan still succeeds. In the example, approximately $8M–$11M needs to come off the table, with $3M–$5M that can remain in SpaceX for upside.

### Tax and Investment Strategies for Diversification [00:11:40]

Three tactical approaches are presented: (1) direct indexing / separately managed accounts to harvest tax losses at scale, including long-short SMAs that use leverage to amplify harvestable losses against capital gains; (2) gifting appreciated SpaceX stock to a donor-advised fund to obtain a large current-year deduction while funding decades of charitable giving; (3) purchasing put options to hedge downside and spread taxable stock sales over multiple years rather than incurring one large taxable event.
