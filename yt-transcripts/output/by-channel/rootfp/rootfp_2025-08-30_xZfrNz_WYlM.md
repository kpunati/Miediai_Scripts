# How to Avoid the Widow’s Tax (Before It’s Too Late)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=xZfrNz_WYlM
**Duration:** 17:31

## Summary

James Conole, CFP at Root Financial Partners, addresses the widow's tax — the dramatic increase in marginal tax rates a surviving spouse faces when filing as a single taxpayer with the same income level as when married. Using 2025 tax bracket data, he illustrates how the marginal rate can jump from 12% to 24% purely through filing status changes and standard deduction reductions. He then presents three proactive strategies: (1) Roth conversions modeled to also account for the single-survivor scenario, not just the joint-filer case; (2) maximizing Social Security benefits to create a stronger, tax-advantaged income floor; and (3) selecting the correct IRS life expectancy table for RMDs, including the joint and last survivor table when spouses are more than 10 years apart in age. The video uses a case study of clients with $2.7 million in investments to show the quantitative impact of each strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- The widow's tax is not a formal IRS tax but refers to bracket compression when a surviving spouse files as single, potentially doubling the marginal tax rate with unchanged income.
- Roth conversion planning must include a single-survivor scenario, not just optimize for the joint-filer years, especially if an early death is plausible.
- Converting only to the 12% bracket is often insufficient; converting to the 22% bracket may be necessary to meaningfully protect the surviving spouse from future high bracket RMDs.
- Maximizing Social Security (potentially delaying to age 70) creates a tax-efficient income floor that partially offsets the additional taxes a surviving spouse will owe.
- Surviving spouses under 59½ can keep an inherited IRA as a beneficiary IRA to avoid the 10% early withdrawal penalty while still accessing funds under the single life table.
- If one spouse is more than 10 years younger, the joint and last survivor RMD table reduces mandatory distributions during both spouses' lifetimes, preserving more IRA assets.

## Detailed Notes

### The Widow's Tax Illustrated [00:00:00]

Using 2025 tax brackets, the video shows a couple with $120,000 income in the 12% marginal bracket (taxable income $90,000 after $30,000 standard deduction). If one spouse dies, the standard deduction drops to $15,000 and single-filer brackets compress, pushing the same $120,000 income into the 24% marginal bracket — a 100% increase in the marginal rate.

### Strategy 1 — Roth Conversions Modeled for Survivor Scenario [00:02:42]

The case study clients (Luke and Mary, ~$2.7M in investments) are projected to be in the 22-24% bracket together, but Mary alone could reach the 32-35% bracket due to required distributions after Luke's death. Converting only to the 12% bracket provides limited relief; converting to the 22% bracket materially reduces Mary's projected future tax exposure and improves tax-adjusted ending wealth. The advisor stresses that Roth conversion modeling must account for the single-survivor scenario from the outset.

### Strategy 2 — Maximize Social Security Benefits [00:08:56]

Under Social Security survivorship rules, the surviving spouse receives the higher of their own benefit or the deceased spouse's benefit. Maximizing at least one benefit (potentially delaying to age 70) strengthens the income floor for the survivor. Social Security's favorable tax treatment — maximum 85% inclusion in federal taxable income, with most states providing additional exemptions — makes it a relatively tax-efficient income source compared to IRA distributions.

### Strategy 3 — Understanding IRS Life Expectancy Tables for RMDs [00:10:46]

Three tables exist: the uniform lifetime table (standard), the single life table (typically for non-spouse inherited IRAs, but also used by surviving spouses under 59½ needing penalty-free access), and the joint and last survivor table (used when the account owner's spouse is more than 10 years younger, reducing mandatory distributions while both are living). Each table serves different planning scenarios, and selecting the wrong one can result in higher-than-necessary distributions and taxes.

### Takeaways and Caveats [00:16:57]

None of the three strategies is universally optimal; each has pros and cons dependent on the couple's ages, account balances, health, spending, and investment approach. The presenter encourages working with a financial advisor and using modeling tools (including Root Financial's Retirement Planning Academy) to assess the specific impact for each household.
