# Retiring After 65? The Rules Change (Hint: You Can Spend More)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-12-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=yXxyNonnZVI
**Duration:** 17:00

## Summary

This episode addresses the distinct financial planning challenges facing people who retire after age 65, arguing that standard retirement frameworks built around 30-40 year horizons are often misapplied to shorter retirements. James Conole covers six key areas: recalibrating withdrawal rates to match a shorter timeline, front-loading spending in compressed go-go years, managing sequence-of-return risk with higher Social Security income as a buffer, executing Roth conversions within a narrower pre-RMD window, navigating IRMAA surcharges strategically, and planning for the surviving spouse's income shock. The overarching message is that retiring later is not a disadvantage but requires a deliberately different and tighter plan.

## Key Takeaways

- Traditional withdrawal rates like 4% assume a 30-40 year retirement; later retirees may sustainably spend 6-10% or more depending on their time horizon.
- Go-go years (roughly ages 60-75) are compressed for later retirees, making intentional front-loading of travel and experiences financially and practically important.
- Higher Social Security income from delayed claiming reduces sequence-of-return risk by lowering the portfolio's burden during market downturns.
- Roth conversion windows are dramatically shorter for later retirees—sometimes only 3 years—requiring larger annual conversions to manage future RMD brackets.
- IRMAA surcharges are cliff-structured, not progressive; staying just below a threshold by choosing income sources carefully can avoid a jump to the next premium tier.
- When one spouse dies, the surviving spouse faces compressed tax brackets, reduced Social Security, and unchanged RMDs—making survivor planning essential, not optional.
- Investing in health is treated as one of the highest-return financial decisions for late retirees given the direct impact on their ability to enjoy compressed go-go years.

## Detailed Notes

### Rethinking Withdrawal Rates for Shorter Retirements [00:00:00]

The 4% rule and guardrails approach assume a 30-40 year retirement. Someone retiring at 70 with a 20-year horizon may sustain 6-7%; at 75 with only 10 years, even 10%+ may be appropriate. Applying conservative frameworks to a short timeline risks significant underspending in the most active years.

### Optimizing the Go-Go Years [00:03:07]

Go-go years (roughly 60-75) are already reduced for later retirees—someone retiring at 70 may have only 5 go-go years versus 13 for someone retiring at 62. The presenter urges intentional front-loading of experiences, travel, and giving because health deterioration can abruptly end these opportunities.

### Market Downturns and Sequence-of-Return Risk [00:04:43]

Later retirees have less recovery time after market downturns but often have higher Social Security income that reduces dependence on portfolio withdrawals. Root Financial recommends holding 5 years of portfolio-sourced cash flow in conservative 'root reserves' to avoid selling equities during downturns.

### Compressed RMD Window and Roth Conversion Strategy [00:07:26]

A retiree at 70 with an RMD age of 73 has only 3 years of tax planning runway versus 13 years for someone who retires at 62. The framework remains the same—compare current bracket to projected future bracket and convert at the lower rate—but implementation must be faster and more aggressive.

### QCDs, IRMAA, and Survivor Planning [00:09:47]

Qualified charitable distributions from IRAs (available at 70.5, counting toward RMDs) are a primary tax tool for charitable retirees. IRMAA surcharges default to a 2-year look-back but can be contested with documentation of lower current income. When a spouse dies, bracket compression and unchanged RMDs can sharply increase the survivor's tax burden, making proactive planning critical.
