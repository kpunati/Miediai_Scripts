# The Roth Conversion Mistake that Could DOUBLE (or more!) Your Medicare Costs

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=U87US5828LE
**Duration:** 14:29

## Summary

James Conole uses a detailed case study of a couple called Bob and Sally to show that conventional Roth conversion advice — 'fill up the 22% tax bracket' — can silently trigger IRMAA Medicare surcharges of roughly $5,800 per year, which when compounded over a 25-year retirement represents tens of thousands of dollars in hidden cost. A revised strategy that respects IRMAA thresholds while still converting meaningfully raised projected tax-adjusted portfolio value from about $485,000 to nearly $760,000. The video concludes with five layered tax implications that must be evaluated simultaneously when planning conversions.

## Key Takeaways

- IRMAA (Income Related Monthly Adjustment Amount) applies a 2-year look-back to modified adjusted gross income; even $1 over a threshold triggers a full-tier surcharge on Medicare Part B and D.
- The 22%-bracket Roth conversion that looked optimal in isolation triggered $5,828 in extra annual Medicare costs, equivalent to a 3.4% additional tax rate on the converted amount.
- Optimizing Roth conversions to stay below IRMAA thresholds rather than filling the 22% income tax bracket raised Bob and Sally's projected tax-adjusted assets from ~$485,000 to ~$760,000.
- Five tax factors must be assessed together for Roth conversions: ordinary income taxes, IRMAA surcharges, Social Security tax torpedo, long-term capital gains bracket impact, and heirs' tax brackets.
- The Social Security tax torpedo means Roth conversions increase provisional income, which can pull more Social Security benefits into taxable income, compounding the cost of each converted dollar.
- Even $1 over an IRMAA threshold triggers the full tier surcharge, making precise income management critical in Medicare-eligible years.

## Detailed Notes

### Bob and Sally's Starting Situation [00:00:00]

Bob and Sally hold a large portion of their savings in pre-tax accounts. Projections show their taxable income rises sharply at age 75 when RMDs kick in, pushing them into the 24% bracket and beyond. The initial analysis suggests filling the 22% bracket with Roth conversions to reduce future forced distributions.

### The 22%-Bracket Conversion Looks Good — At First [00:01:02]

Filling the 22% bracket adds approximately $485,000 in tax-adjusted ending assets by prepaying taxes at a lower rate today versus a higher rate in the future. This result appears to justify stopping the analysis — but a critical hidden cost lurks in their Medicare projections.

### IRMAA: The Hidden Medicare Tax [00:03:17]

The conversions pushed Bob and Sally's MAGI above the lowest IRMAA threshold (2023 base $106,000 for singles). Their Part B premium for Bob jumped from $693 to $981 per month, and the total annual Medicare surcharge impact was $5,828 more than without conversions. IRMAA uses a 2-year look-back, so conversion income today affects premiums in two years.

### Quantifying the Long-Term Cost [00:05:22]

To net $5,800 after taxes at a 25% bracket requires pulling roughly $7,700 pre-tax from an IRA. That $7,700 not invested for 25 years — growing at an estimated 75% cumulative return — represents an opportunity cost of over $47,000 over retirement. The compounding effect of the surcharge makes this far more expensive than it appears in any single year.

### The Better Strategy: IRMAA-Aware Conversions [00:08:33]

By recalibrating conversions to stop at IRMAA thresholds rather than ordinary-income bracket limits, Bob and Sally preserve Medicare premiums and grow projected tax-adjusted assets from ~$485,000 to ~$760,000. Conole notes that some of this improvement was independent of IRMAA (the 22% conversion was also slightly suboptimal for other reasons in their specific case).

### Five Tax Implications of Roth Conversions [00:10:38]

Conole lists the five factors: (1) ordinary income tax brackets, (2) IRMAA Medicare surcharges, (3) Social Security tax torpedo where conversions increase provisional income and make more SS benefits taxable, (4) long-term capital gains bracket impacts where conversion income can push LTCG from 0% to 15%, and (5) heirs' tax brackets when conversions are part of a legacy strategy.
