# Retiring at 54: Why Darren Says It Feels Surreal | Retirement Reality

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=eGeh8T7F-v0

## Summary

In this episode of Retirement Reality, host James Conole interviews Darren, a 54-year-old who retired eight months prior after a 30-year career. Darren describes the journey from hitting his retirement number and paying off his mortgage to waiting four years for the right professional moment — a company reorg — before finally resigning. The conversation explores the emotional and psychological dimensions of early retirement: adjusting to unstructured time, navigating healthcare costs without an employer, maintaining social connections when peers still work, and reframing 'retirement' as a flexible, reversible phase rather than a permanent identity.

## Key Takeaways

- Darren describes retirement as 'surreal' because a decades-long theoretical financial number suddenly became a tangible, lived reality the day he stopped working.
- Paying off the mortgage — not just hitting an investment target — was Darren's decisive psychological trigger; the freed monthly cash flow gave him confidence to absorb healthcare costs.
- The company reorg provided a natural, low-disruption exit window: Darren had handed off existing work and had not yet embedded deeply into the new team, making the departure feel aligned with his values around not letting teammates down.
- Eight months in, Darren has settled into a routine of morning exercise, personal programming projects, afternoon walks, and occasional travel — though large international travel plans have been deferred to a later 'phase.'
- Darren deliberately avoided using the word 'retirement,' telling colleagues he was 'taking a year off,' which reduced finality and kept open the option of part-time or contract work later.
- Self-created false urgency — scheduling too many tasks in a day out of habit — emerged as an unexpected psychological challenge, requiring conscious effort to slow down and embrace unstructured time.

## Detailed Notes

### Financial Milestones and the Decision to Retire [00:01:54]

Darren worked for 30 years and saved consistently, treating his investment portfolio as something he could only put into, not take from. He had a target number that evolved over time with input from a financial adviser, but the more decisive trigger was paying off his mortgage — his last financial goal. Once mortgage-free, he felt he could leave at any time and worked for approximately four additional years before the right moment arose.

### The Company Reorg as a Catalyst [00:04:53]

A company reorganization created a clean transition window: Darren had just handed off his existing workload but had not yet taken on deep responsibilities in the new division. This alignment with his 30-year work anniversary and the end of the calendar year made the timing feel natural. He emphasizes he had been searching for a low-disruption moment rather than rushing to leave, and the reorg delivered that.

### Adjusting to Early Retirement: Day-to-Day Life [00:07:35]

Darren settled into retirement more easily than he expected, contrary to warnings from friends and family about boredom. His days typically include morning exercise, programming projects, lunch, and afternoon walks. He is highly routine-oriented and has a mental list of long-deferred projects — fitness, video games, books, home improvements — which provide purpose without pressure. Large travel ambitions, such as extended European trips, have been deferred to what he calls a later 'phase.'

### Social Isolation and the Weekend vs. Weekday Divide [00:14:15]

With only about two peers who are also retired, Darren acknowledges weekdays feel socially different — most friends and former colleagues are still working. He visits his former office about once a month to maintain connections. He is comfortable with solitude, noting that solo activities during weekdays and group activities on weekends have become his natural rhythm.

### Reframing Retirement and Advice for Others [00:26:00]

Darren advises reframing 'retirement' as 'stopping work' or 'taking a year off' to reduce the psychological weight of finality. He set a hard personal deadline of age 55 to prevent inertia from extending his career indefinitely. His core message: if the finances are solid and the person is truly ready, they owe it to themselves to make the jump — adjustments can always be made later, including returning to part-time or contract work if needed.
