# What Should You Do With Your 401k When You Retire?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-07-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3_t6bFByNs4
**Duration:** 16:26

## Summary

James Conole provides a comprehensive framework for deciding whether to leave a 401(k) in place or roll it to an IRA at retirement. The decision hinges on six practical factors — cost, control, investment options, consolidation, ease of use, and cross-account coordination — and three advanced tax strategies. The age-55 rule makes keeping a 401(k) strategically important for early retirees. In-plan Roth conversions for after-tax contributions and Net Unrealized Appreciation treatment for company stock are highlighted as high-value techniques that are frequently overlooked.

## Key Takeaways

- Employees retiring at age 55 or older from the sponsoring company can access 401(k) funds penalty-free, 4.5 years earlier than the IRA age-59.5 threshold.
- Keeping pre-tax funds in a 401(k) avoids the IRS aggregation rule that can create unexpected tax costs when doing backdoor Roth contributions through an IRA.
- IRAs typically offer greater control, more investment options, easier trading and conversions, and a better user experience than most 401(k) plans.
- After-tax 401(k) contributions should be converted to a Roth 401(k) via in-plan conversion immediately to prevent the growth from becoming taxable.
- Employer stock with substantial appreciation may qualify for Net Unrealized Appreciation treatment, where accumulated gains are taxed at lower capital gains rates rather than ordinary income rates upon distribution.
- Six factors to compare — cost, control, investment options, consolidation, ease of use, coordination — provide a practical checklist for the rollover decision.

## Detailed Notes

### When Not to Roll Over: Age-55 Rule and Backdoor Roth [00:00:00]

Employees retiring at or after age 55 from the company sponsoring their 401(k) can take penalty-free distributions immediately, avoiding the normal 10% early withdrawal penalty that applies to IRAs until age 59.5. A planning strategy is to roll previous employer 401(k)s into the current company's 401(k) before retiring to make more assets accessible under this rule. The second reason to keep funds in a 401(k) is backdoor Roth contribution eligibility: pre-tax IRA balances trigger the IRS aggregation rule, but 401(k) balances are excluded, preserving clean backdoor Roth access.

### Six Factors for the Rollover Decision [00:02:39]

Cost: 401(k) plans can carry hidden fees up to 2–2.5% (declining in larger plans); self-directed IRAs often offer lower-cost access to index funds. Control: IRAs generally offer more flexibility for distributions, trades, and conversions. Investment options: 401(k)s have limited fund menus that may be protective for some but restrictive for others. Consolidation: rolling multiple old 401(k)s into one IRA simplifies oversight. Ease of use: IRA platforms typically offer a cleaner digital experience. Coordination: holding all accounts at one institution makes asset location and allocation management easier.

### After-Tax Contributions and NUA Strategy [00:09:14]

A 401(k) balance is not homogeneous: pre-tax contributions and employer match roll to a traditional IRA; Roth contributions roll to a Roth IRA; after-tax contribution principal rolls to a Roth IRA but growth on after-tax funds is pre-tax unless an in-plan conversion was done. The NUA strategy allows company stock gains to be taxed at long-term capital gains rates rather than ordinary income rates, which can be worth tens of thousands of dollars for employees with highly appreciated company stock. The immediate tax recognition of NUA versus deferral in a rollover requires analysis with a financial advisor.
