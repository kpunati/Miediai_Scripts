# Taxes on a $3M Retirement Portfolio (What You'll Actually Owe Each Year)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NdOHYjraF38
**Duration:** 17:00

## Summary

James Conole walks through the real-life tax math on a $3 million retirement portfolio, showing that the key driver of tax liability is account type rather than portfolio size. Using two couple examples—one with all assets in a traditional IRA and one diversified across IRA, Roth, and brokerage—he demonstrates how required minimum distributions, IRMAA surcharges, and bracket compression after a spouse's death can dramatically increase taxes over time. The video argues for proactive Roth conversions and account diversification as tools to control when and how much tax is paid. Key strategies covered include asset location, Roth conversions, qualified charitable distributions, and social security timing.

## Key Takeaways

- Account type—not portfolio balance—is the primary determinant of retirement tax liability.
- Required minimum distributions at age 73 or 75 are calculated from portfolio balance and age, not spending needs, and can push retirees into higher brackets involuntarily.
- Diversifying across pre-tax, Roth, and brokerage accounts gives retirees flexibility to manage taxable income year by year.
- Roth conversions in lower-income years allow retirees to pay taxes at current lower rates rather than letting RMDs force distributions at higher future rates.
- IRMAA surcharges add a cliff-structured cost to Medicare premiums above certain income thresholds.
- After one spouse dies, tax brackets compress by roughly half while RMD income often stays the same, sharply increasing the surviving spouse's effective tax rate.
- Qualified charitable distributions let IRA owners direct up to $105,000 per year straight to charity tax-free, satisfying RMD requirements without adding to taxable income.

## Detailed Notes

### Why Account Type Matters More Than Portfolio Size [00:00:00]

The presenter argues that most people fear the wrong tax problem. Taxable income from voluntary withdrawals is manageable; the real risk is the IRS-mandated required minimum distributions that grow automatically with portfolio value and age regardless of spending needs.

### Two Couple Comparison Setup [00:01:45]

Couple One has $3M entirely in traditional IRAs. Couple Two has $1.8M traditional IRA, $600K brokerage, and $600K Roth. Both plan to spend $120K/year and receive $40K in Social Security. All projections use 2026 tax brackets and standard plus senior deductions.

### Couple One: Early Retirement Tax Picture [00:04:23]

At age 65, Couple One's AGI is $114,000 (IRA withdrawals plus 85% of Social Security). After a $47,500 combined deduction (standard + senior + enhanced senior), taxable income drops to $66,500 and federal tax is approximately $7,500, a 6.2% effective rate. However, this favorable picture deteriorates significantly once RMDs begin.

### RMD Impact at Ages 75 and 80 [00:07:08]

If the IRA grows to $3.5M by age 75, the mandatory RMD is roughly $142,000—far above the couple's actual spending needs. By age 80 with a $4M IRA, the RMD approaches $200,000. Stacked on top of Social Security, this forces the couple into the 22% bracket and potentially triggers IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums.

### Couple Two and the Diversification Advantage [00:09:45]

With a mixed-account portfolio, the couple can pull $40K from the traditional IRA, $20K tax-free from the Roth, and $20K from the brokerage (likely at the 0% qualified dividend rate given their income level). The resulting federal tax bill is approximately $3,300—less than half of Couple One's bill despite identical spending.

### Strategic Roth Conversions and Other Tools [00:13:49]

The presenter recommends using low-income early retirement years to convert traditional IRA balances to Roth, doing charitable giving via qualified charitable distributions instead of cash gifts, and carefully timing Social Security to limit how much is included in taxable income each year.
