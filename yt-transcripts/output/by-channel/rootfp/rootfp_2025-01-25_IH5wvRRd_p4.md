# Roth Conversion Checklist: 5 Key Factors to Maximize Your Conversion Strategy

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=IH5wvRRd_p4
**Duration:** 16:07

## Summary

James Conole presents a systematic five-factor framework for Roth conversion planning, using Eric and Michelle's $4 million retirement case as a running example. He demonstrates that most people only consider ordinary income tax brackets (Factor 1) and miss IRMAA surcharges (Factor 2), long-term capital gains brackets (Factor 3), the terminal tax rate their heirs will face (Factor 4), and the need to dynamically review and sometimes stop conversions (Factor 5). Each additional factor materially changes the optimal conversion amount and duration, with the full five-factor approach adding over $600,000 to the couple's ending balance versus no conversions.

## Key Takeaways

- Filling up tax brackets is only the first of five factors; ignoring the other four can cost tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars over retirement.
- IRMAA surcharges function as an implicit tax on higher conversion income; avoiding them sometimes means not fully filling a bracket.
- Long-term capital gains brackets at 0% federal create an alternative optimization target for households with large brokerage accounts relative to pre-tax accounts.
- The terminal tax rate of heirs is a critical input: a high-income heir inheriting a traditional IRA may face 50%+ marginal rates, substantially raising the value of converting today.
- Roth conversions should be reviewed every year rather than implemented as a fixed rule; discontinuing conversions at the right point can add six figures to outcomes.
- A dynamic, year-by-year conversion strategy outperforms any static rule based on a single factor.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Setup: Eric and Michelle [00:00:00]

Eric and Michelle are both 65, just retired, with a net worth just over $4 million including about $2.5 million in investments. They plan to spend $6,500 per month. Eric defers Social Security to 70 ($3,900/month) and Michelle claims at 67 ($2,600/month). The case illustrates how different conversion strategies affect their tax-adjusted ending balance.

### Factor 1: Ordinary Income Tax Brackets [00:03:08]

Without conversions, the couple sits below the 10% bracket in early retirement. Converting to fill the 22% bracket adds $455,000 to their tax-adjusted ending balance. However, converting to the 24% bracket actually reduces the benefit, showing there is a sweet spot rather than an automatic 'more is better' rule.

### Factor 2: IRMAA Medicare Surcharges [00:05:13]

Pushing conversions into the full 22% bracket inadvertently triggers IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums. Recalibrating to avoid those thresholds raises the benefit from $455,000 to $563,000—more than $100,000 better—even though the couple converts less. This illustrates that IRMAA can make a partial bracket fill superior to a full fill.

### Factor 3: Long-Term Capital Gains Brackets [00:08:23]

Converting just enough to stay within the 0% federal long-term capital gains bracket is useful for households with large brokerage accounts. For Eric and Michelle this factor is less impactful than IRMAA optimization, but for households with more brokerage and less pre-tax money it can be the dominant consideration.

### Factors 4 and 5: Terminal Tax Rate and Conversion Duration [00:09:26]

Factor 4 asks what tax bracket heirs will face on inherited pre-tax assets; a child who is a high-income professional in a high-tax state could pay close to 50%, dramatically raising the value of converting now. Factor 5 highlights that stopping conversions after a set number of years—here illustrated as 10—can add over $150,000 more than converting indefinitely, because at some point further conversions provide diminishing or negative returns.
