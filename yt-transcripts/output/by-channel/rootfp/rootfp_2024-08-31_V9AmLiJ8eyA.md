  The Saving 10 Times Your Income by 67 Rule

  The idea of saving 10-15% of your income is another common piece of advice. While saving something is better than nothing, this rule doesn’t consider when you start saving.   This rule suggests you can safely withdraw 4% of your retirement savings each year without running out of money. Although it was groundbreaking when introduced, it has limitations. # 5 Dangerous Rules of Thumb to Rely on in Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-31
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=V9AmLiJ8eyA
**Duration:** 16:05

## Summary

James Conole systematically deconstructs five popular retirement rules of thumb, showing through numeric examples why each one can lead to either significant over-saving or dangerous under-saving depending on individual circumstances. He argues that the 4% rule established critical foundations but should be the floor of a dynamic spending system, not a fixed ceiling, and that the age-based stock allocation rule misidentifies the primary retirement risk as short-term volatility when it is actually long-term inflation. The overall message is that rules of thumb are useful starting heuristics but should be replaced by personalized projections as one approaches retirement.

## Key Takeaways

- The 10x income rule conflates income with spending and ignores pension and Social Security income, causing the same target to be wildly inappropriate for different individuals.
- The 10-15% savings rate rule ignores the time value of money; someone starting at 55 needs to save a dramatically higher percentage to achieve the same outcome as someone starting at 25.
- The 4% rule was designed for worst-case market scenarios; in average or better markets, retirees following it rigidly often die with far more money than they started with, sacrificing experiences unnecessarily.
- Sequence of returns risk means that the average 10% market return is irrelevant if a large portfolio loss occurs in early retirement before the portfolio has time to recover.
- The age-from-100 stock allocation rule protects against short-term volatility but exposes retirees to the larger risk: a 30-year inflation-driven erosion of purchasing power.
- Personalized, dynamic retirement planning that accounts for actual spending, income sources, and market conditions consistently outperforms any single rule of thumb.

## Detailed Notes

### Rule 1: Save 10x Income by Age 67 [00:00:00]

Two individuals both earning $100,000 can have radically different portfolio needs: one spending $80,000 per year with only Social Security may need $1.25 million (using the 4% rule), while another spending $60,000 with both Social Security and a pension may only need $250,000. The 10x income rule gives both a $1 million target, leaving one underfunded and delaying the other's retirement unnecessarily.

### Rule 2: Save 10-15% of Income [00:03:09]

A 25-year-old saving 10% of $100,000 at 8% growth accumulates $2.6 million by 65; a 55-year-old following the same rule accumulates only $145,000—an 18x gap. The rule is directionally helpful for beginners but becomes actively harmful later in a career when it may result in saving either too much or too little relative to an individual's specific trajectory.

### Rule 3: The 4% Withdrawal Rule [00:06:16]

The 4% rule was based on the worst historical market entry point for a 30-year retirement and solved for the minimum safe withdrawal rate. In normal or strong markets, it results in massive unspent accumulation. Conole argues for a dynamic, guardrails-based approach where withdrawal rates adjust up or down based on market conditions, allowing retirees to spend more in good years without risking ruin in bad years.

### Rule 4: Assume 10% Average Market Returns [00:09:26]

Annual S&P 500 returns have ranged from +54% to -43%, and the sequence in which returns arrive matters far more than the average. A major early-retirement drawdown permanently impairs a portfolio even if long-term averages recover. Retirement projections must model return variability, not just average returns.

### Rule 5: Subtract Age from 100 for Stock Allocation [00:11:34]

This rule reduces equity exposure as a retiree ages, which lowers short-term volatility but maximizes exposure to purchasing-power erosion over a 30-year horizon. US long-term stock real returns average roughly 7% versus 2% for 20-year government bonds, meaning an overly bond-heavy portfolio may preserve dollar values while steadily losing the ability to pay for real goods and services.
