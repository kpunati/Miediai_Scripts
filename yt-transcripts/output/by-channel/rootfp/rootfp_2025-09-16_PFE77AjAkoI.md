# What’s the Smartest Investment Mix for Retirees Right Now?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-09-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=PFE77AjAkoI
**Duration:** 13:42

## Summary

James Conole explains why the traditional 60/40 portfolio is not universally appropriate for retirees and proposes a personalized three-step framework. The first step is protecting against sequence of return risk by setting aside five years of stable investments called Root Reserves. The second step is ensuring sufficient allocation to growth assets to combat inflation over a long retirement. The third step is managing emotional and behavioral risk by sizing fixed income to match the investor's actual comfort level, not just their financial need.

## Key Takeaways

- Sequence of return risk is the primary near-term danger: early portfolio losses combined with withdrawals can permanently impair retirement outcomes.
- Root Reserves — roughly five years of living expenses held in cash, CDs, or short-term bonds — serve as a buffer allowing the equity portfolio time to recover.
- Inflation eroding purchasing power is a greater long-term risk than short-term volatility, making some equity allocation essential even for conservative retirees.
- Emotional and behavioral risk is a legitimate factor; extra fixed income beyond pure financial need may be justified to prevent panic selling.
- Portfolio construction should start from individual cash flow needs and risk tolerance, not a generic stock/bond split.

## Detailed Notes

### Three Core Retirement Risks [00:00:43]

Conole identifies three risks every retiree must address: volatility/sequence of return risk, inflation risk, and behavioral/emotional risk. He argues against defaulting to a 60/40 portfolio because it does not account for individual circumstances.

### Protecting Against Sequence of Return Risk via Root Reserves [00:01:28]

Sequence of return risk means that the order of returns matters more than the average return, especially when withdrawals begin. Conole recommends holding five years of living expenses in stable, non-stock assets — internally called Root Reserves — structured as a laddered series of cash, CDs, and short-term bonds so that retirees never need to sell equities during a downturn.

### Inflation Risk and Growth Asset Allocation [00:06:27]

Over-allocating to conservative investments to reduce volatility exposes retirees to purchasing power erosion. Even modest 3% annual inflation can significantly increase cost of living over a 20-30 year retirement, making a meaningful equity allocation necessary to preserve real wealth.

### Behavioral and Emotional Risk [00:09:17]

Beyond pure financial calculations, investors who are uncomfortable holding a high equity allocation risk panic-selling at market lows. Additional fixed income held as portfolio ballast — negatively correlated to equities — can smooth volatility and protect against poor decision-making, even if it is not strictly required by cash flow analysis.

### Practical Portfolio Construction [00:12:37]

Conole summarizes the framework: first define cash flow needs and size Root Reserves accordingly; second ensure adequate growth assets to offset inflation; third calibrate total fixed income to the investor's actual risk tolerance. This personalized approach replaces the outdated one-size-fits-all 60/40 model.
