# How Much Do I Need to Retire? (3 Simple Steps to Determine the Right Amount)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-04-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Es0Y-qOIJtk
**Duration:** 16:43

## Summary

James Conole outlines a simple three-step process to determine the retirement portfolio needed: accurately calculate retirement expenses (removing payroll taxes, retirement savings contributions, a paid-off mortgage, and income taxes), subtract guaranteed non-portfolio income sources to find the annual gap, then apply a sustainable withdrawal rate (4–5.5%) to size the required portfolio. The video illustrates that a $100,000 earner may only need to replace $53,000 annually, and that a $35,000 gap requires roughly $875,000 at 4%. Important complications—uneven Social Security timing, tax treatment disparities between income sources, and the income impact of a spouse's death—are addressed as nuances that can substantially change the answer.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement expenses are almost always lower than current gross income once payroll taxes, retirement contributions, mortgage payments, and income taxes are removed.
- The key portfolio sizing question is not total expenses but the gap between expenses and guaranteed non-portfolio income like Social Security and pensions.
- The 4% rule is a widely used starting point but withdrawal rates between 4% and 5.5% are common; the right rate depends on portfolio composition, investment strategy, and retirement length.
- Tax treatment differs significantly between income sources: pension income is typically fully taxable, Social Security is at most 85% taxable at the federal level, and Roth IRA withdrawals are tax-free.
- Uneven timing—such as retiring at 60 but delaying Social Security to 70—requires higher early withdrawal rates and lower later rates, not a flat 4% throughout.
- The death of one spouse can nearly quadruple the required portfolio withdrawal rate if both spouses had significant Social Security income, requiring proactive planning.

## Detailed Notes

### Step 1: Determining True Retirement Expenses [00:00:00]

The top-down approach starts with gross income and subtracts what will disappear: payroll taxes (7.65% on wages up to the cap), 401k contributions, federal and state income taxes, and mortgage principal and interest payments. A $100,000 earner might arrive at only $53,000 of actual replacement need. The bottom-up approach builds expenses category by category but frequently undercounts because people forget irregular costs; using both methods as a cross-check is recommended.

### Step 2: Non-Portfolio Income Sources [00:06:46]

Social Security, pensions, and annuities reduce how much the portfolio must produce. If expenses are $60,000 and Social Security provides $25,000, the portfolio only needs to generate $35,000. The tax treatment of each source matters: 100% of pension income is typically taxable while only up to 85% of Social Security is taxable federally, and Roth withdrawals are tax-free—making after-tax income the relevant comparison, not gross dollars.

### Step 3: Withdrawal Rate and Portfolio Size [00:08:38]

Dividing the annual gap by the withdrawal rate gives the required portfolio size. At 4%, a $35,000 gap requires $875,000; at 5.5%, the same gap requires only $700,000. The 4% rule assumes inflation-adjusted annual increases and a 30-year retirement horizon; deviating from that assumption requires understanding which market environments and portfolio structures can support a higher or lower rate.

### Key Nuances: Timing, Taxes, and Survivor Risk [00:10:44]

Uneven income timing (e.g., retiring at 60, Social Security at 70) means portfolio withdrawals are front-loaded and decline sharply once guaranteed income kicks in—a dynamic incompatible with a flat withdrawal rate assumption. The survivor scenario is particularly impactful: if both spouses each collect $3,000/month, losing one spouse drops Social Security from $6,000 to $3,000 while expenses remain near $7,000/month, increasing the portfolio shortfall from $1,000 to $4,000 per month.
