# Roth Conversion Strategies for Singles: How Much Should You Convert?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=3ZczPKEbv3Q
**Duration:** 35:14

## Summary

This case study episode features James Conole of Root Financial Partners working through a comprehensive retirement tax plan for Laura, a 60-year-old single retiree with roughly $2.2 million concentrated in pre-tax accounts. The analysis covers cash flow sustainability, Roth conversion timing relative to ACA health insurance subsidy eligibility, and the trade-off between converting to the 22% versus 24% tax bracket. Estate planning considerations, including the impact of heirs' tax brackets and estate tax exposure, are also examined as factors shaping the optimal conversion strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- Single filers face compressed tax brackets compared to married filing jointly, making proactive Roth conversions especially valuable to avoid forced high-bracket distributions later.
- Laura's portfolio supports a 1.8-2.1% withdrawal rate before additional income sources kick in, providing a strong foundation for tax optimization without financial stress.
- Converting up to the 24% bracket in the near term (while current tax law applies) and scaling back to the 22% bracket after a potential sunset offers a strategic two-phase approach.
- ACA marketplace health insurance costs approximately $20,000-$21,000 annually without a subsidy for a 60-year-old, representing a significant trade-off when evaluating conversion amounts before Medicare eligibility.
- Estate planning should factor in the terminal tax rate of beneficiaries; heirs in high tax brackets make Roth conversions even more valuable.
- Spending more today on desired lifestyle goals can simultaneously reduce estate tax exposure and improve quality of life without needing complex estate planning structures.
- Required minimum distributions after age 75 on a large pre-tax portfolio can generate six-figure taxable income, making early conversion during lower-income years essential.

## Detailed Notes

### Laura's Financial Situation and Concerns [00:00:00]

Laura, age 60, retired in May 2023 with the majority of her $2.2 million portfolio in pre-tax accounts (approximately $1.4 million 401(k) and $720,000 IRA). She is on COBRA and concerned about the cost of Roth conversions conflicting with ACA health insurance subsidies and near-term spending needs such as home renovation and travel.

### Cash Flow and Withdrawal Rate Analysis [00:10:35]

Core monthly expenses are $3,500 plus $6,000 annual travel, approximately $21,000 for pre-Medicare health insurance, and periodic large goals such as home repairs and vehicle replacement. Before Social Security and pension income begin, Laura's portfolio withdrawal rate is 1.8-2.1%, characterized by Conole as very sustainable. Withdrawal rates decline as Social Security and pension income phase in starting around age 62 and 67 respectively.

### Tax Bracket Strategy and Roth Conversion Optimization [00:17:55]

Without conversions, required minimum distributions after age 75 could push Laura into high tax brackets on a seven-figure pre-tax balance. Filling the 12% bracket saves an estimated $250,000 in taxes; filling through the 22% bracket creates a seven-figure improvement in tax-adjusted portfolio value. Conole recommends converting to the 24% bracket while current law is in effect, then reverting to 22% after the anticipated 2025 tax law sunset.

### Additional Tax Considerations: IRMAA, ACA, and Estate Planning [00:27:24]

Bond fund income in Laura's brokerage account elevates modified adjusted gross income, increasing health care costs via IRMAA and reducing ACA subsidies. Estate projections show potential seven-figure estate tax exposure given portfolio growth assumptions. Conole suggests increasing current spending on lifestyle goals and charitable giving as a practical method to reduce estate size, noting that heirs in high tax brackets make unconverted IRA balances particularly tax-inefficient to pass on.
