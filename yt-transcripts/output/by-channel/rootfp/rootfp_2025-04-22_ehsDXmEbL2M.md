# The COSTLY Mistake You're Making When Realizing Investment Gains

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-04-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ehsDXmEbL2M
**Duration:** 16:59

## Summary

James Conole addresses a common investor dilemma—whether to realize large taxable gains and pay tax, or hold concentrated positions to avoid it—and argues that investors systematically underweigh the risk of not selling. He presents a decision framework that starts with evaluating portfolio concentration and the risk of inaction, then moves to five strategies that can reduce or eliminate the tax cost: harvesting gains in low-income years using the 0% capital gains bracket, donating appreciated shares to charity, gifting appreciated securities to lower-bracket family members, deferring gains to benefit from step-up in basis at death, and using capital losses to offset gains. The video is specifically focused on brokerage (non-retirement) accounts.

## Key Takeaways

- The risk of holding a highly concentrated position often far exceeds the tax cost of selling; a 70% portfolio decline erases far more value than the capital gains tax would have.
- A well-diversified portfolio holding an investment as a core position does not need to be sold simply because it has gains.
- Married couples with AGI around $126,700 or less may qualify for the 0% federal long-term capital gains rate, enabling tax-free gain realization if timed across multiple years.
- Donating appreciated securities to charity instead of cash avoids capital gains for both donor and charity and provides a full fair-market-value deduction.
- Gifting appreciated securities to lower-bracket family members—such as adult children in the 0% bracket—transfers the gain to a lower tax jurisdiction.
- Step-up in basis at death makes it generally inadvisable for elderly investors to sell heavily appreciated assets they do not need, since heirs inherit at fair market value.
- Capital losses can directly offset capital gains dollar-for-dollar; selling loss positions and waiting 31 days to repurchase avoids the wash-sale rule while resetting basis.

## Detailed Notes

### Framework: When Not to Sell [00:00:00]

If an appreciated investment remains a core, well-diversified holding in the portfolio, there may be no compelling reason to sell and trigger taxes. The framework begins by assessing whether the position is appropriately sized and still plays a needed role in the overall allocation.

### Understanding the Risk of Inaction [00:02:47]

When a position is overweight or concentrated in a single stock, the risk of not selling—potential loss of 40-70% of value in a downturn—can dwarf the maximum tax cost of 20% on the gain. Investors often over-anchor on the certain, quantifiable tax while underweighting the uncertain but potentially much larger downside of concentration.

### Utilizing the 0% Long-Term Capital Gains Bracket [00:06:12]

For 2025, long-term gains are tax-free federally below $96,700 in taxable income (married filing jointly) or $48,350 (single). Investors with gains can strategically realize amounts up to these thresholds each year, potentially spreading large gains over multiple years to stay within the 0% bracket.

### Gifting Appreciated Shares to Charity and Family [00:08:37]

Donating appreciated stock to charity eliminates the capital gain entirely for both donor and charity, while the donor receives a deduction for full fair market value. Gifting to lower-bracket family members is also effective: the recipient inherits the original cost basis but may pay zero or 15% on sale rather than the donor's 20-30% rate. After gifting to charity, the donor can repurchase the same shares to step up the cost basis while maintaining the investment.

### Step-Up in Basis and Tax-Loss Harvesting [00:12:34]

Appreciated assets held until death receive a step-up in basis to fair market value at date of death, effectively eliminating the embedded gain for heirs. This makes selling unnecessary for very elderly investors who do not need the liquidity. Capital losses from other positions can offset gains dollar-for-dollar; the wash-sale rule requires waiting at least 31 days before repurchasing the same security after selling at a loss.
