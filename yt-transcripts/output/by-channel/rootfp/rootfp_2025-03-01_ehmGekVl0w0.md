# Retiring Before You Start Social Security? Make This Crucial Portfolio Adjustment

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-03-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=ehmGekVl0w0
**Duration:** 15:17

## Summary

This video addresses the specific portfolio risk that arises when someone retires before Social Security begins, using Becky's case study—a 62-year-old with a $1 million 401k who will not claim Social Security until 67. The advisor shows that Becky's pre-Social-Security withdrawal rate of 6.5%–8% is dangerously high if she remains fully invested in large-cap growth stocks, because early market downturns at those withdrawal rates can permanently impair the portfolio. The solution is to identify the dollar amount needed for the gap years, insulate it from equity volatility using bonds and dividends, and shift to roughly a 70/30 portfolio—trading some long-term return potential for a meaningfully higher probability of retirement success.

## Key Takeaways

- A high portfolio withdrawal rate in the years immediately before Social Security begins creates outsized sequence-of-returns risk that can permanently impair retirement.
- The five-year Social Security gap period should be treated as a distinct planning horizon requiring dedicated liquid reserves insulated from equity markets.
- Historical bear market duration (average 2.5 years, long end approximately 5 years) provides the logic for choosing how many years of expenses to protect.
- A portfolio generating 2% in dividends, conservatively haircut by 20%, can partially fund the insulated reserve, reducing the amount that must be held in bonds.
- Shifting from 100% stocks to 70/30 reduces terminal portfolio value projections but increases the probability of success by protecting against worst-case early-year drawdowns.
- Portfolio allocation should be revisited dynamically as withdrawal rates change—once Social Security begins and the withdrawal rate drops from 8% to around 3%, a more aggressive allocation may again be appropriate.

## Detailed Notes

### Becky's Plan and the Gap Problem [00:00:00]

Becky is 62 with a $1 million 401k invested 100% in large-cap growth stocks. She plans to retire immediately and spend $5,000/month, but will not claim Social Security until age 67. This five-year gap means her entire lifestyle cost must come from the portfolio, creating withdrawal rates of 6.5%–8% that are unsustainable if markets decline early in retirement.

### Why High Early Withdrawal Rates Are Dangerous [00:04:10]

If the market drops 20% in the first year and Becky is still withdrawing 6.5%+ of portfolio value, her effective withdrawal rate on the shrunken portfolio spikes even higher. This is the mechanism by which retirements are ruined: the combination of falling portfolio value and ongoing withdrawals prevents recovery even when markets eventually rebound. A straight-line 8.5% annual return looks fine but obscures this risk.

### Building the Five-Year Insulation Plan [00:05:12]

Adding up five years of inflation-adjusted withdrawals yields approximately $380,000 that Becky needs to protect from equity volatility. Of this, roughly $80,000 can be sourced from dividends (assuming 2% yield trimmed 20% for safety), leaving $300,000 to be held in short-term, high-quality bonds. This means the $1 million portfolio should be structured approximately 70% in diversified equities and 30% in this reserve, rather than 100% in large-cap growth.

### Portfolio Reallocation and Success Probability [00:11:29]

The 70/30 allocation modeled in the retirement software shows a lower projected terminal portfolio value than the 100% stock scenario but achieves a higher probability of success. The trade-off is exchanging some expected long-term growth for protection against worst-case early drawdowns. Once Social Security begins and Becky's withdrawal rate drops to roughly 3.2%, she can reassess whether a more growth-oriented allocation is appropriate again.
