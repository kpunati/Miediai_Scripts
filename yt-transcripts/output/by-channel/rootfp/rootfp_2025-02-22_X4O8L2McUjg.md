# How Much Can I Spend in Retirement with a $2M Portfolio

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-22
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=X4O8L2McUjg
**Duration:** 18:31

## Summary

James Conole presents a step-by-step retirement analysis for John and Sarah, a 60-year-old couple with a $2 million portfolio and $3.5 million net worth including their home. He models their cash flows year by year through multiple retirement scenarios, showing how Social Security timing, pension income, and spending goals interact with portfolio withdrawal rates. The analysis finds their plan is robust enough to support earlier retirement, higher spending, and significant discretionary purchases like a $125,000 dream car.

## Key Takeaways

- Organizing all retirement income sources before modeling helps couples see how withdrawals decline as Social Security and pensions phase in.
- First-year withdrawal rates may be elevated by one-time expenses but decline significantly as stable income sources begin.
- A portfolio growing faster than withdrawals means retirees may accumulate more wealth than they started with, raising the risk of under-spending.
- Retiring three years earlier (at 62 vs. 65) remains viable and may be preferable given that those are the healthiest early retirement years.
- Adding $2,500 to monthly spending makes only a modest dent in long-term projections for well-positioned portfolios.
- Modeling dream scenarios like a $125,000 car purchase shows clients the true cost is smaller than feared, unlocking permission to pursue goals.
- Retirement planning should start with goals, then model current trajectory, then optimize — not the other way around.

## Detailed Notes

### John and Sarah's Financial Profile [00:00:00]

Both age 60, with a $2 million investment portfolio comprising John's 401k and Roth IRA, Sarah's Roth IRA and 403b, and a joint taxable account. Their home is worth just under $1.5 million and is fully paid off. Total net worth is approximately $3.5 million. Both currently save 15% of income including employer match.

### Retirement Income Sources [00:03:07]

John plans Social Security at age 70 ($4,200/month) and Sarah at her full retirement age 67 ($2,400/month). Sarah also has a pension of $800/month starting at 65 with a cost-of-living adjustment. Both are contributing 10% of income with a 5% employer match through their respective employer plans. Healthcare pre-65 via COBRA would cost $1,000/month each, dropping to Part B and D premiums plus $4,500/year out-of-pocket after Medicare.

### Cash Flow Modeling and Withdrawal Rates [00:05:12]

Year-by-year cash flow shows income ebbing and flowing as pension, then Sarah's Social Security, then John's Social Security layer in. The first-year withdrawal rate is elevated at 5.7% partly due to a planned car purchase, declining to 4.4% then into the low 3% range and eventually 1 to 1.5% as stable income increases. RMD-forced distributions cause a temporary spike when they begin.

### Portfolio Sustainability and Optimization [00:10:24]

At an assumed 6.5% annualized growth rate, the portfolio is projected to grow from $3 million at retirement to over $8 million — meaning the couple may be significantly under-spending their capacity. Retiring at 62 instead of 65 remains viable. Increasing monthly spending from $7,500 to $10,000 barely impacts the plan, and $12,000 to $13,000 per month may be the true upper limit.

### Goal Alignment and Dream Scenarios [00:15:38]

Conole reframes the planning goal: rather than optimizing for maximum terminal wealth, the goal is to align spending with what brings fulfillment. Adding John's $125,000 dream car to the plan makes barely a dent. Increasing travel to $40,000 per year can be accommodated. The exercise transforms clients from a savings mindset to understanding what their accumulated wealth can actually do for them.
