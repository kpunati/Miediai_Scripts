# 5 Simple Ways to Increase Your Social Security Spousal Benefits

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=PUR45ncTebg
**Duration:** 15:18

## Summary

This video provides a detailed walkthrough of Social Security spousal benefit rules, clarifying common misconceptions. The host explains that spousal benefits are capped at 50% of the working spouse's primary insurance amount regardless of whether the worker delays to 70, and that the benefit is structured as own benefit plus excess spousal rather than a standalone 50% amount. The video covers early collection reductions, the requirement for the worker to be actively collecting before spousal benefits can begin, and the critical distinction that survivor benefits—unlike spousal benefits—do include delayed retirement credits, making the worker's claiming strategy directly relevant to the surviving spouse's long-term security.

## Key Takeaways

- Spousal benefits are capped at 50% of the worker's primary insurance amount and do not increase with delayed retirement credits beyond full retirement age.
- You can only collect spousal benefits after your spouse has filed for and is actively collecting their own Social Security benefit.
- Spousal benefit is technically the combination of your own benefit plus excess spousal to reach 50% of the partner's PIA, enabling the lower-earning spouse to collect their own benefit while the higher earner delays.
- Collecting a spousal benefit before your own full retirement age permanently reduces it, with progressively larger reductions the earlier you claim down to age 62.
- Survivor benefits do include delayed retirement credits, making the higher-earning spouse's decision to delay directly valuable to the survivor's long-term income.
- Maximizing Social Security income and maximizing its impact on the overall retirement plan are different objectives that require coordinating spousal, survivor, and own benefits holistically.

## Detailed Notes

### Eligibility and Basic Rules [00:00:00]

To collect a spousal benefit, you must have been married for at least one year to a current spouse or for at least ten years to a former spouse. The maximum spousal benefit is 50% of the working spouse's primary insurance amount (PIA), which is their benefit at full retirement age (age 66–67 depending on birth year). Spousal benefits are entirely based on the worker's earnings record and require no personal contributions to Social Security.

### Delayed Credits Do Not Apply to Spousal Benefits [00:02:02]

A common misconception is that if the higher-earning spouse delays to age 70 to earn 8% per year in delayed retirement credits, the spousal benefit also grows. In fact, the spousal benefit is locked at 50% of the worker's PIA at full retirement age, not the enhanced age-70 amount. This distinction has significant planning implications and is separate from survivor benefit rules.

### Excess Spousal Mechanics and Claiming Strategy [00:04:07]

The spousal benefit is the excess of 50% of the partner's PIA over the claimant's own benefit, not a replacement of their own benefit. This means a lower-earning spouse with some earnings record can collect their own benefit while the higher-earning spouse delays, then add the excess spousal when the partner finally files. Waiting until age 70 to start any collection in this case leaves money on the table.

### Survivor Benefits and Strategic Differences [00:10:22]

Unlike spousal benefits, survivor benefits do include delayed retirement credits, meaning a worker's decision to delay to age 70 directly increases what the surviving spouse can collect. Survivor benefits can be claimed as early as age 60 (versus 62 for own benefits), but collecting before full retirement age reduces them to approximately 71.5% of the full amount. Couples can also strategize switching between survivor and own benefits at different times to maximize total lifetime income.
