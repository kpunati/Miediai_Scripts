# Retirement Makeover: I'm 51 with $600k. When Can I Retire?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-02
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=9atHvLXqH50
**Duration:** 32:02

## Summary

James Conole, CFP, walks through a detailed retirement makeover for Hillary (51) and Mark (55), who have approximately $600k in retirement accounts and a goal of retiring in 10 years to travel the world. Conole builds a cash-flow model incorporating pensions, Social Security, healthcare, and housing costs to show that the previous adviser's recommendation to work until 70 is likely overcautious. The analysis reveals that retiring at 61/57 — or even 57/61 — is financially viable, especially if the couple sells their home and uses the equity to fund overseas living expenses.

## Key Takeaways

- A previous adviser's recommendation to work until 70 was likely driven by liability concerns rather than the couple's actual financial position.
- Pensions and Social Security create a staggered income floor that substantially reduces portfolio withdrawal requirements over time.
- Selling the home at retirement and renting abroad can actually improve financial outcomes by converting illiquid equity into investable assets.
- Retirement in 6 years (at 57/61) produces nearly the same long-term wealth trajectory as waiting the full 10 years, because earlier home sale accelerates equity access.
- Cutting current savings rates in half only marginally reduces probability of success but frees cash flow for extended practice trips abroad.
- Testing the lifestyle through multi-week international trips before committing is advised to avoid overly romanticized expectations.

## Detailed Notes

### Couple's Goals and Starting Financial Snapshot [00:00:00]

Hillary (51) and Mark (55) want to retire within a decade, sell their home, and travel full-time, potentially living for extended periods in Europe and Asia. They have roughly $600k in retirement accounts (401k, IAPD plan, Simple IRA, brokerage trust) and a home worth about $600k with 50% equity. Travel cost research suggests $3,000–$4,000 per month all-in for international living.

### Expense Assumptions and Stress Testing [00:05:13]

Core retirement expenses are estimated at $2,000 per month in today's dollars, separate from housing costs. The $2,000 figure is partly an estimate based on expected lower cost of living abroad. Conole flags the need to stress test this against $2,500/month scenarios and builds in a travel-heavy 'go-go years' budget for the early retirement period.

### Income Sources and Withdrawal Rate Analysis [00:12:31]

Both spouses have pensions and Social Security benefits that kick in on a staggered schedule. Early retirement requires higher portfolio withdrawals (near the 4% range), but once both Social Security benefits are active, withdrawals drop below 1% of portfolio balance. The model shows an 84% probability of success — considered strong for a 10-year planning horizon.

### Impact of Selling the Home [00:19:54]

Selling the home at retirement converts roughly $300k of equity (growing to more over 10 years) into liquid assets. In a sell-and-rent-abroad scenario, the equity more than covers the incremental rent cost (~$1,300/month for Airbnb stays), producing a net wealth outcome nearly identical to retaining the home. This scenario also eliminates the ongoing mortgage, tax, and insurance liability.

### Accelerated 6-Year Retirement Scenario [00:23:04]

Retiring in 6 years instead of 10 yields a nearly equivalent long-term wealth trajectory, because earlier home sale partially offsets fewer years of contributions. The main homework items are: (1) take extended practice trips to validate the lifestyle, and (2) track actual expenses abroad to tighten spending projections before fully committing.
