# How Does Rental Real Estate Impact Your Retirement Strategy?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=DpUgjpnlZfo
**Duration:** 37:46

## Summary

This retirement makeover episode features James Conole, CFP, working through a comprehensive retirement plan for Mark, a 62-year-old professional with three rental properties who intends to work until age 70. James first explains the four components of real estate investment returns — rental income, property appreciation, leverage, and tax benefits such as depreciation — using Mark's first property as a worked example. He then models how Mark's rental income and Social Security together form a strong income floor at retirement, projecting total expenses near $264,000 per year against combined income of roughly the same amount, resulting in a portfolio withdrawal rate under 1%. The analysis shows Mark could potentially retire much earlier than planned while still comfortably supporting an $8,000 per month lifestyle with significant travel spending.

## Key Takeaways

- Real estate investment returns have four components: rental income yield, property appreciation, leverage amplification, and tax benefits from depreciation.
- Comparing net rental yield to the S&P 500's long-term ~10% return is a useful benchmark for deciding whether to hold or sell rental properties.
- Mark's rental income and Social Security together are projected to nearly cover all retirement expenses, leaving a portfolio withdrawal rate under 1%.
- Because rental income acts as a large income floor, Mark can justify a more aggressive portfolio allocation in his investment accounts.
- Property hassle and tax considerations (capital gains on a seven-figure gain) are material factors when deciding whether to hold or sell rental real estate in retirement.
- Running two retirement scenarios — retiring at 70 versus retiring today — illustrates the trade-off between portfolio growth and lifestyle flexibility.
- Spending significantly more in the first 10 years of retirement ($15,000-$20,000 per month) while tapering later can be accommodated within Mark's plan with high probability of success.

## Detailed Notes

### Mark's Situation and Goals [00:00:00]

Mark is 62, plans to work until 70, owns three rental properties, and holds approximately $1.8 million in investment accounts (IRAs, Roth IRAs, brokerage). His main questions are how much he can safely spend in retirement and whether to invest cash in expanding a three-unit apartment building by two additional units.

### Four Components of Rental Real Estate Returns [00:04:11]

James identifies rental income (net yield), property appreciation, leverage, and tax benefits (depreciation) as the four drivers of real estate investment returns. He illustrates leverage with a simple example: a 10% down payment on a $1 million property means a 10% price increase doubles the investor's return. Depreciation acts as a phantom expense that shelters rental income from taxes.

### Benchmarking Real Estate Against Other Assets [00:11:30]

James argues that rental real estate should clear the S&P 500's ~10% long-run return as a hurdle, given the added burdens of managing tenants, maintenance, and property management costs. Considerations for holding versus selling also include capital gains taxes on large unrealized gains and the hassle factor in retirement, particularly if owners want to travel extensively.

### Mark's Retirement Cash Flow Model [00:15:42]

With retirement goals of $8,000 per month plus $15,000 per year for travel in the first decade, total projected expenses are approximately $264,000 per year. Social Security combined with rental income from all three properties is projected to nearly match that figure, leaving a portfolio withdrawal rate under 1% from a projected $4.2-$4.3 million portfolio at age 70.

### Portfolio Allocation and Lifestyle Scenarios [00:25:07]

Because rental income fills most of the retirement income need, Mark can hold a more aggressive (all-equity) portfolio allocation, potentially growing end-of-plan wealth to $22 million versus $14 million in a moderate 60/40 allocation. James also models retiring immediately, finding the plan remains viable but requires drawing more from a smaller portfolio. Spending scenarios up to $20,000 per month in retirement are explored, with the plan remaining high-probability of success at elevated spending levels.
