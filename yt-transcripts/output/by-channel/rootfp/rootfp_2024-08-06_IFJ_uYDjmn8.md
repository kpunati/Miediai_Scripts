# Am I Too Old for Roth Conversions? 3 Things to Consider

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-06
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=IFJ_uYDjmn8
**Duration:** 22:55

## Summary

This video responds to a listener named Gary, a 73-year-old with $8 million in savings, who wonders if it is too late to begin large Roth conversions to reduce future required minimum distributions. The advisor structures his answer around three issues: the philosophical risk that excessive wealth accumulation creates psychological constraints rather than freedom, a practical note that Gary's Roth IRA is suboptimally allocated to a bond fund, and the tax strategy question of when and for what purpose conversions make sense. The discussion covers qualified charitable distributions, the role of children's future tax brackets in conversion decisions, the medical expense deduction as a partial offset to large IRA withdrawals, and the principle that conversions are most advantageous in lower-bracket years before RMDs begin.

## Key Takeaways

- More wealth does not automatically produce more freedom; when financial decisions are driven entirely by tax minimization, wealth becomes controlling rather than enabling.
- The optimal use of a Roth IRA from an asset location standpoint is to hold growth-oriented assets, not bond funds, because Roth withdrawals are tax-free.
- Qualified charitable distributions allow IRA holders to gift directly to charity tax-free — up to $105,000 per person per year — and can be a better strategy than converting assets for heirs who will not need them.
- Roth conversion decisions should account not only for the current tax bracket but also for the projected tax brackets of heirs who will inherit the IRA.
- Large medical expenses in retirement can be partially deductible if they exceed 7.5% of AGI, mitigating some of the tax cost of IRA withdrawals in those years.
- Roth conversions remain viable even after RMDs begin, as long as the conversion can be done at a lower tax rate than projected future rates.

## Detailed Notes

### Gary's Financial Situation and Question [00:00:00]

Gary is 73 with his wife, holding $8 million in total savings: $1.3 million in CDs, $3.2 million in a Vanguard Total Stock Market brokerage account, $400,000 in a Roth IRA (Vanguard High Yield Corporate Bond Fund), and $3.3 million in traditional IRAs. Combined income from a $75,000 pension and Social Security, but annual expenses are only $40,000. First RMD was $120,000, placing them in the 24% bracket with $125,000 of remaining room. Gary asks whether large sequential $500,000 Roth conversions could meaningfully reduce future RMD burden.

### The Wealth-Freedom Paradox [00:02:03]

The advisor's primary concern is that Gary and his wife appear to be living on $40,000/year despite having the capacity to spend $400,000/year, suggesting their wealth is constraining rather than enabling their lifestyle. Using an exercise where the couple must spend $360,000 or forfeit it to their least-preferred political candidate, he challenges the listener to identify genuinely desired uses of wealth — travel, gifts to family, home improvements, charity — rather than optimizing purely for tax efficiency.

### Investment Allocation in Roth IRA [00:11:25]

The advisor flags that Gary holds a high-yield corporate bond fund in his Roth IRA, which is suboptimal from an asset location standpoint. Since Roth withdrawals are tax-free, the most tax-efficient placement is to hold higher-growth, higher-return assets in the Roth and place income-generating or lower-return assets (like bonds) in traditional pre-tax accounts.

### Tax Planning: Conversions, QCDs, and Heirs [00:12:28]

The right conversion strategy depends on intended use of funds. If funds are destined for charity, qualified charitable distributions are often more efficient than converting and then donating. If assets will pass to heirs, the heirs' projected tax brackets at time of inheritance should factor into conversion decisions — especially since inherited IRAs must be distributed within 10 years. For funds intended for personal use, conversions make sense when today's tax rate is lower than projected future rates, which requires modeling the trajectory of RMDs, Social Security, pension, and other income.

### Medical Expense Deduction and Conversion Timing [00:16:38]

Unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI are deductible, which can offset part of the tax cost of large IRA withdrawals in medical emergency years. Roth conversions before RMDs begin are generally most advantageous because lower-bracket capacity is available, but conversions remain valid after RMD age if marginal rates are favorable relative to expected future rates.
