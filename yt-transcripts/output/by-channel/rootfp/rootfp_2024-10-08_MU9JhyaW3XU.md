# Restricted Stock Units: The Basics of RSUs and How to Use Them

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=MU9JhyaW3XU
**Duration:** 19:32

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial Partners answers a listener question about whether to hold or sell vested RSUs, using a hypothetical Google employee named Chris as the example. The core recommendation is to reframe RSU vests as a cash bonus — if you would not use a cash bonus to buy your company's stock, you should sell the vested shares. The video covers RSU mechanics (vesting schedules, tax timing, cliff vs. graded vests), the danger of performance-chasing using Tesla vs. S&P 500 data from 2018-2024, three RSU tax payment methods (sell to cover, same-day sale, cash payment), the gap between the 22% default withholding and actual higher-bracket tax liability, and the wash sale trap for employees who also hold company stock in a brokerage account.

## Key Takeaways

- RSUs have no value until they vest; the grant date does not create a taxable event, but the vesting date does, triggering ordinary income tax on the full vested value.
- Treat vested RSUs as a cash bonus: if you would not use cash to buy your company's stock, sell the vested shares and reinvest according to your actual financial plan.
- There is no special tax benefit to holding RSUs after vesting; the choice to keep company stock is the same as choosing to buy that stock with a cash bonus.
- The default federal withholding on RSU vests is typically 22%, which is too low for employees in the 32%, 35%, or 37% brackets and can result in underpayment penalties.
- RSU vests count as stock purchases for wash sale purposes; employees who also tax-loss harvest company stock in a brokerage account must coordinate to avoid disallowing losses.
- Past outperformance of company stock is not a reliable indicator of future returns; the Tesla vs. S&P 500 performance-chasing example shows how momentum investing leads to poor outcomes.

## Detailed Notes

### How RSUs Work and When Taxes Apply [00:00:00]

RSUs represent a promise of stock that only becomes valuable upon vesting; unvested shares are forfeited if employment ends. Using a hypothetical 4-year graded vesting schedule for a Google employee, the video shows that taxes are owed only on the vested portion at ordinary income rates, not on the full grant. Employees receiving multiple annual grants can accumulate a growing stream of vesting shares over time.

### The Cash Bonus Mental Model [00:04:09]

The key framework: imagine the vesting RSUs are a cash bonus after taxes. If you would not use that cash to buy company stock, do not hold the vested shares. There is no tax advantage to holding RSUs in company stock versus selling; the decision is purely about investment preference and diversification needs. This reframing helps overcome the psychological bias of holding appreciated or familiar positions.

### Performance-Chasing Risk Using Tesla Example [00:07:14]

A year-by-year analysis of Tesla vs. S&P 500 from 2018-2024 shows that a hypothetical investor who always shifted to the prior-year winner would have missed Tesla's 743% gain in 2020 and suffered through its 65% decline in 2022. The exercise illustrates that recent outperformance — including a company doing well — does not predict future returns, making concentrated single-stock exposure risky.

### Tax Payment Methods: Sell to Cover, Same-Day Sale, Cash Payment [00:12:28]

Three methods exist for paying RSU taxes: sell to cover (company sells a portion of vested shares to pay taxes, leaving the remainder), same-day sale (all shares are sold immediately for cash), and cash payment (employee pays taxes from outside funds and keeps all shares). The default withholding rate of 22% is often insufficient for employees in higher brackets, leading to large tax bills or penalties unless proactive planning adjusts withholding.

### Wash Sale Rules and Brokerage Account Coordination [00:16:38]

RSU vests are treated as stock purchases for wash sale rule purposes. An employee who sold company shares in a brokerage account to harvest a tax loss, then had RSUs vest within 30 days, would have the tax loss disallowed. Employees with equity compensation must review wash sale exposure holistically across all accounts, not just the equity compensation account.
