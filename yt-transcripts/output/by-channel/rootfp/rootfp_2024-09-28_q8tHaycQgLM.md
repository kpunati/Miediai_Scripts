# Can We Still Retire if We Don't Receive the $750k Inheritance We're Expecting?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=q8tHaycQgLM
**Duration:** 47:09

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial Partners conducts a live retirement makeover with Matt and Beth, both 57, who want to retire at 59.5. The session models their full cash-flow picture—including two years of remaining high salaries, six years of modest part-time income, a real estate relocation and condo conversion to rental, a $750,000 inheritance, and eventual Social Security—to determine whether the plan works and what happens if the inheritance is reduced or eliminated. Conole emphasizes that the biggest risk is not long-term portfolio sustainability but the high withdrawal rates in the first few years before Social Security and the inheritance arrive. He advises prioritizing liquidity, considering a moderate increase in part-time work, and maintaining flexibility rather than rushing to pay off a low-balance mortgage.

## Key Takeaways

- Withdrawal rates of 11-13% in the first few years of early retirement are not inherently fatal if they are temporary and offset by incoming inheritance or Social Security.
- The inheritance is the single highest-leverage variable in the plan: its presence or absence swings success probability dramatically.
- Reducing early-retirement drawdowns—through even modest consulting income—has a compounding benefit for the entire remaining retirement because it preserves a larger portfolio base.
- Maintaining cash in a high-yield savings account rather than paying down a moderate mortgage preserves the flexibility needed to navigate a multi-year transition period involving property sales, purchases, and relocations.
- Tax bracket management in retirement is less urgent for this couple than sequencing withdrawals intelligently; projected income rarely pushes them into high brackets.
- Roth conversions are worth doing but are a lower priority than cash-flow management and getting the investment allocation right in the early years.
- Relying on an expected inheritance is acceptable if it has been stress-tested against scenarios of zero or partial receipt, giving the planner clear action triggers.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Profile and Goals [00:00:00]

Matt (structural engineer) and Beth, both 57, want to retire in July 2026 at age 59.5 to pursue active outdoor activities. They have a net worth just under $1.5 million across joint investment accounts, IRAs, 401ks, and 403bs, plus a primary home and a condo. Key concerns are healthcare costs, a real estate relocation to northern Michigan, and whether a $750,000 expected inheritance can be relied upon.

### Retirement Income and Cash-Flow Structure [00:17:56]

Conole maps out all inflow sources: two remaining years of full salaries, six years of combined $115,000 part-time income, Social Security at 67, rental income from the condo through 2037, sale of the condo, and the inheritance. Core monthly expenses are $4,900, with $6,000 annually for travel, $18,000 for pre-Medicare health care, a $25,000 wedding, and periodic vehicle purchases layered on top.

### High Early Withdrawal Rates and the Inheritance as a Stop-Gap [00:26:22]

In the first three years of retirement the withdrawal rate reaches 11-13% because Social Security has not started, the inheritance has not arrived, and housing costs increase due to a new mortgage. The inheritance in 2030 acts as a reset, growing the portfolio base and dropping the withdrawal rate to 1-3.5% in later years. Removing the inheritance entirely drops the probability of success significantly.

### Sensitivity Analysis: Inheritance Scenarios and Income Levers [00:28:28]

If the inheritance is zero, success probability falls sharply. At $250,000 the probability is uncomfortably low without other adjustments; working two extra years restores a high probability. Alternatively, if Matt earns $100,000 in consulting rather than $15,000 for six years, the early drawdown pressure is substantially reduced and success probability rises even without the inheritance.

### Tax Planning and Account Withdrawal Strategy [00:39:58]

The couple has a mix of pre-tax and Roth accounts. Conole projects they will rarely breach mid-level tax brackets, making aggressive Roth conversions a lower priority. The key strategy is controlling which account to draw from each year to keep taxable income in the 10-12% brackets, avoid large RMDs at 73-75, and maximize flexibility. Keeping money in a high-yield savings account—already taxed, not subject to market risk—funds the first few retirement years without triggering account-withdrawal decisions under duress.

### Prioritizing Flexibility Over Optimization [00:44:11]

Conole advises against using savings to pay off the condo mortgage because preserving liquidity is more valuable during the multi-year transition of selling a home, buying a new one, and converting the condo to a rental. He concludes the overall plan is a very probable yes, with the main risk being the first five to seven years of elevated withdrawals and the inheritance uncertainty.
