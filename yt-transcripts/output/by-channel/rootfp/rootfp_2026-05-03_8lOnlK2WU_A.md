# You’re Thinking About Social Security Wrong… It’s Not About Not 62 vs. 67 vs. 70

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-05-03
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=8lOnlK2WU_A
**Duration:** 15:06

## Summary

This video by James Conole of Root Financial Partners argues that Social Security timing should be viewed as a risk decision, not merely a break-even calculation. Each claiming age protects against a specific risk—early claiming hedges short life expectancy, while delaying to 70 hedges longevity and strengthens the survivor benefit for a spouse—but every choice simultaneously introduces an opposite risk. A particularly under-appreciated danger is that delaying to 70 while drawing down a portfolio to bridge the gap can expose retirees to devastating sequence-of-return risk if markets decline during those bridge years. On the upside, the years before Social Security kicks in represent a valuable tax planning window for Roth conversions and capital gains harvesting.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security is an insurance product protecting against specific risks, not a portfolio to be maximized in isolation.
- Claiming at 62 yields 70% of the full retirement age benefit permanently; claiming at 70 yields 124%, with 8% annual delayed retirement credits.
- The 2026 earnings limit of $24,480 means early claimants who are still working may have benefits withheld, reducing the appeal of collecting at 62 while employed.
- For married couples, the survivor benefit means the higher earner's claiming decision directly determines the income floor for the surviving spouse.
- Delaying to 70 requires funding 8 years of expenses from the portfolio; a market downturn during that window can undermine the entire plan even if Social Security is ultimately higher.
- The years between retirement and age 70 are an optimal window for Roth conversions and tax gain harvesting while income is low and brackets are favorable.
- No claiming decision should be made without integrating life expectancy, spousal factors, portfolio assets, spending needs, and tax strategy.

## Detailed Notes

### How Social Security Is Calculated [00:00:00]

Social Security uses the 35 highest inflation-adjusted earnings years to calculate the primary insurance amount (PIA), which is the benefit at full retirement age (67 for most current workers). Years with no earnings count as zero, reducing the PIA. The PIA is the anchor from which all other claiming-age adjustments are made.

### Collecting at 62: Risks Protected and Exposed [00:02:52]

Collecting at 62 minimizes the risk of a short life expectancy—dying before recouping deferred benefits. However, it permanently locks in a 30% reduction from the full retirement age benefit. The earnings limit ($24,480 in 2026) means working claimants forfeit $1 for every $2 earned above the threshold. The biggest downside is longevity risk: a lower benefit compounding for 20-30 years, combined with a weakened survivor benefit for a spouse, can leave both parties significantly underserved.

### Collecting at 67: The Neutral Option [00:07:31]

Age 67 represents the full retirement age for most current workers. It avoids the extremes of early or delayed claiming and takes advantage of Social Security's favorable tax treatment—a maximum 85% of benefits are subject to federal tax and many states exempt it entirely. It does not fully protect against longevity risk nor fully cover a spouse, but is a pragmatic middle ground that should still be integrated into a broader tax withdrawal strategy.

### Collecting at 70: Longevity Protection and Sequence Risk [00:08:57]

Waiting to 70 locks in the maximum benefit (124% of PIA) for life and maximizes the survivor benefit for a spouse. The break-even age typically falls in the early 80s. The critical risk is funding the 8-year gap entirely from the portfolio: if markets drop sharply during that window and withdrawals reach 6-8% annually, the portfolio can be decimated even before Social Security begins. The same 62-to-70 window also presents significant tax planning opportunities—Roth conversions and tax gain harvesting are most efficient when other income is low.
