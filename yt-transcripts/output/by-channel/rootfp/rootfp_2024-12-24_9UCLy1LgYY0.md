  Retirement is an exciting milestone, but it often comes with common fears. With proper planning, these concerns can be addressed to ensure a fulfilling and secure new chapter. Here are five major retirement fears and strategies to overcome them:

  - Build and maintain relationships through community activities, clubs, or social groups.

# 5 Biggest Retirement Fears and How to Overcome Them

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=9UCLy1LgYY0
**Duration:** 16:29

## Summary

This episode categorizes retirement fears into five buckets and offers practical strategies for each. The financial fears — running out of money, healthcare costs, and long-term care — are addressed through Social Security timing optimization, HSA accumulation, medical expense deductibility, and a portfolio-based or insurance-based long-term care plan. The non-financial fears — loss of purpose and loneliness — are addressed through intentional community building and replacing work's social and productivity functions.

## Key Takeaways

- Optimizing Social Security strategy — whether delaying to 70 or claiming earlier to reduce portfolio draws — is one of the most impactful steps against outliving savings.
- Doing a retirement spending test-run before retiring can reveal gaps between projected and actual expenses, as one case study showed a client spending $8,000/month versus a projected $5,000.
- A withdrawal guardrails framework, rather than a fixed dollar amount, helps retirees stay within a sustainable spending band as markets fluctuate.
- A Health Savings Account provides triple tax benefits and is an effective tool for accumulating funds for healthcare costs in retirement.
- Medical expense itemization (above 7.5% of AGI) can dramatically reduce the tax cost of large healthcare withdrawals from an IRA.
- Long-term care insurance provides coverage but premiums can increase; alternatively, evaluating whether existing assets (property, pension, portfolio) can self-insure is a valid approach.
- Loneliness affects roughly one-third of retirees; building social infrastructure before retirement and being intentional about community reduces this risk.

## Detailed Notes

### Fear 1: Outliving Savings [00:00:00]

Key strategies include optimizing Social Security claiming age to create an income floor, saving to a specific target portfolio size rather than vaguely, running a retirement spending test-run before actually retiring, and using a guardrails-based withdrawal strategy. A real client example is cited where underestimating monthly spending ($5,000 projected vs. $8,000 actual) required an urgent plan revision.

### Fear 2: Losing Purpose and Fear 3: Healthcare Coverage [00:04:07]

Loss of work's identity, routine, and connection requires intentional substitution — social clubs, volunteering, fitness routines, and community involvement. For healthcare, learning Medicare parts A, B, D and supplemental Medigap policies is essential, preferably with a consultant. HSAs offer tax-free accumulation and spending for qualified medical expenses. Medical expense itemization above 7.5% of AGI can substantially reduce the effective cost of large healthcare withdrawals.

### Fear 4: Loneliness and Fear 5: Long-Term Care [00:10:22]

A MassMutual survey found one-third of retirees report loneliness; building friend groups before retirement and being thoughtful about geographic relocation helps. Long-term care insurance is the most direct hedge against large care costs, though premiums can increase over time. For those with sufficient assets, self-insurance via property, pension, or portfolio is a valid alternative worth modeling before defaulting to an insurance purchase.
