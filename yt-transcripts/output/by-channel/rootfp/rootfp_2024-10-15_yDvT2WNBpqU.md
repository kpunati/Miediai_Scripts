# The Complete Guide to Maximizing Your Social Security Benefit

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=yDvT2WNBpqU
**Duration:** 19:31

## Summary

James Conole walks through the mechanics of Social Security from funding through FICA payroll taxes to the benefit calculation using 35 years of average indexed monthly earnings and means-tested bend points. He then presents four actionable strategies for maximizing lifetime Social Security income: filling out the 35-year earnings record, delaying benefit collection, coordinating spousal benefits, and planning around survivor benefits. The episode emphasizes that Social Security decisions are couple-level decisions requiring coordination of both spouses' ages, health, and benefit amounts.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security is funded by a 7.65% FICA payroll tax on both employee and employer, totaling 15.3% up to the annual wage base.
- Benefits are means-tested through bend points: early dollars of earnings are weighted at 90%, middle dollars at 32%, and higher earnings at only 15%, making Social Security proportionately more valuable to lower earners.
- Working exactly 35 years is the threshold; stopping at 30 years and having 5 zero years meaningfully reduces the benefit regardless of how high prior earnings were.
- Delaying from full retirement age (66-67) to age 70 increases benefits by 8% per year, a guaranteed 24% increase for those with a full retirement age of 67.
- Spousal benefits allow a lower-earning spouse to claim up to 50% of the higher earner's primary insurance amount; ex-spouses married for 10 or more years retain this right.
- Survivor benefits pay 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit including delayed retirement credits, making the higher earner's claiming decision a joint lifetime income decision.
- The choice to delay or claim early should consider the full financial picture including portfolio impact, tax implications, and spouse's longevity — not just the Social Security benefit in isolation.

## Detailed Notes

### How Social Security Is Funded and Calculated [00:00:00]

FICA taxes of 7.65% are withheld from employee paychecks and matched by employers, with 6.2% going to Social Security and 1.45% to Medicare, up to the annual wage base ($168,600 in 2024). Benefits are based on the 35 highest years of inflation-adjusted earnings (average indexed monthly earnings), with years below 35 counted as zeros. The bend point formula means benefits are disproportionately weighted toward lower earners.

### Full Retirement Age and Claiming Windows [00:06:23]

Full retirement age ranges from 66 to 67 depending on birth year, and represents the point at which a worker receives their primary insurance amount. Benefits can be claimed as early as 62 (with a permanent reduction of 5-6.67% per year early) or as late as 70 (with an 8% annual delayed retirement credit). Survivor benefits can be collected as early as age 60.

### Four Strategies to Maximize Benefits [00:07:26]

Strategy 1: Work 35 years, even at lower income in later years, since initial earnings are weighted most heavily in the benefit calculation. Strategy 2: Wait to collect, ideally to age 70, to earn delayed retirement credits — though portfolio impact and taxes must be considered holistically. Strategy 3: Use spousal benefits, which allow the lower-earning spouse to claim up to 50% of the higher earner's primary insurance amount, including for eligible ex-spouses after a 10-year marriage. Strategy 4: Plan for survivor benefits, where the surviving spouse receives 100% of the deceased's benefit including any delayed retirement credits earned.

### Survivor Benefit Planning Complexity [00:16:50]

After one spouse passes away, the survivor can choose to collect the survivor benefit immediately while allowing their own benefit to grow to age 70, or collect their own benefit and later switch to the survivor benefit — creating meaningful planning opportunities. Conole notes that the higher earner delaying to age 70 is not just about their own lifetime income but locks in the survivor benefit for the remaining spouse, which may justify delay even when the higher earner has a shorter expected lifespan.
