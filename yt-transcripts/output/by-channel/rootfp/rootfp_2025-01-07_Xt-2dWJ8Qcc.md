# 3 Simple Steps to Retire in 2025

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Xt-2dWJ8Qcc
**Duration:** 16:55

## Summary

James Conole, CFP, addresses the gap between financial readiness and the psychological ability to pull the retirement trigger, arguing that a simple three-step process can resolve the 'analysis paralysis' that prevents many well-prepared people from retiring. The first step is a thorough financial inventory covering assets, true net income, and debts with known payoff dates. The second step is envisioning a specific retirement lifestyle and working backwards to estimate its cost. The third step connects these two pictures by calculating whether the portfolio can generate the required income, using the 4% rule as a baseline. The video emphasizes that both over-saving workers and under-prepared early retirees make poor decisions without this connective framework.

## Key Takeaways

- Many financially ready individuals fail to retire due to analysis paralysis, not actual financial shortfall.
- True retirement income need is net income minus savings contributions minus debts that will be retired before retirement date.
- Envisioning the specific activities and values of retirement is as important as the financial math, because lifestyle cost cannot be estimated without a lifestyle plan.
- The 4% rule provides a baseline: divide annual portfolio income need by 4% to find the required portfolio size for a 30-year retirement.
- Social Security and other income sources reduce the portfolio withdrawal needed, which in turn lowers the required portfolio target.
- A debt with a defined payoff date, such as a mortgage paid off before retirement, should be excluded from the retirement income target.
- Annual reviews and a clear framework give retirees confidence to spend and enjoy retirement rather than hoarding assets out of fear.

## Detailed Notes

### Step 1: Assess Current Financial Health [00:00:00]

Start by inventorying all accounts that will generate retirement income: 401ks, IRAs, Roth IRAs, brokerage accounts, and HSAs, but exclude earmarked cash. Then determine actual monthly net income after taxes, 401k deductions, and payroll withholding—not the gross salary. Finally, review all debts for their projected payoff dates to identify which expenses will naturally disappear before retirement.

### Step 2: Envision Ideal Retirement [00:06:13]

Most people who struggle with retirement timing have good financial data but no concrete retirement vision. Conole recommends identifying core values such as health, faith, family, adventure, or community, then writing down one or two specific activities per value. A retirement centered on local hiking has very different costs than one involving international travel every quarter, and cost cannot be estimated without this specificity.

### Step 3: Connect the Dots with the 4% Rule [00:09:19]

With a target monthly spend and known income sources such as Social Security, the gap requiring portfolio funding can be calculated. Dividing that annual gap by 4% yields the required portfolio size. The 4% rule was derived from historical research showing it as the maximum sustainable withdrawal rate across both favorable and unfavorable market sequences over 30-year horizons. Conole notes it is a baseline, not an optimal number.

### Practical Example and Conclusion [00:13:27]

A worked example shows a couple spending $6,000 per month net, saving $1,000, and paying $2,000 on a mortgage expiring before retirement. True retirement need is $4,000 per month. With $1,500 from Social Security, the portfolio gap is $2,500 per month or $30,000 per year, requiring $750,000 in investable assets at the 4% rule. The framework gives both a target and a progress benchmark, resolving the paralysis many feel when confronted with abstract retirement complexity.
