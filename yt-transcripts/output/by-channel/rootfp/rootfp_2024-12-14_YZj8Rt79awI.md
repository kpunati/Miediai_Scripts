# I’m 60 with $2M How Much Can I Spend in Retirement?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=YZj8Rt79awI
**Duration:** 23:06

## Summary

This video walks through a detailed retirement readiness analysis for Joe and Jackie, both aged 59, who hold approximately $1.8 million in investments and $585,000 in real estate, targeting $8,000 per month in core expenses plus healthcare and travel. The analysis reveals that their initial withdrawal rates — exceeding 7% before Social Security at 67 — produce only a 33% probability of plan success, far below what a planner would find acceptable. Several actionable strategies are explored including working an additional one or two years, modest spending reductions, leveraging property assets for long-term care costs, and applying the retirement spending smile pattern to reflect how actual retiree spending tends to grow slower than inflation.

## Key Takeaways

- A $2 million portfolio at 60 does not automatically guarantee retirement readiness; withdrawal rates and probability of success are the key metrics.
- Pre-Social Security years are the most portfolio-stressful period; a 6-year income gap before age 67 drives very high early withdrawal rates.
- Even one additional year of work can substantially improve the probability of plan success by reducing early withdrawals and adding portfolio growth.
- The retirement spending smile — where spending grows below the inflation rate — can alone materially improve plan outcomes without requiring lifestyle cuts.
- Illiquid property assets can serve as a contingency backstop for long-term care costs rather than leaving the portfolio as the sole safety net.
- Small, combined adjustments (one less year of travel spending, $1,000/month less in expenses, one more year of work) interact powerfully to transform a marginal plan into a sustainable one.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Profile: Joe and Jackie [00:00:00]

Joe (59) and Jackie (59) have $1.8 million in investment accounts across 401ks, Roth IRAs, HSAs, and brokerage accounts, plus two properties worth $585,000 combined, for a total net worth of about $2.4 million. Jackie has already retired; Joe plans to retire in December 2025. Core monthly expenses are $8,000, with an additional $1,000/month for healthcare and $25,000/year in travel for the first 11-12 years of retirement. Joe earns $225,000/year currently and saves $84,000/year to a taxable account.

### Retirement Cash Flow Analysis [00:04:10]

Once Joe retires, the couple faces six full years of no salary income before Social Security begins at 67. Joe's projected Social Security benefit is $3,869/month and Jackie's is $2,700/month. During the pre-Social Security gap, the portfolio must cover all expenses including $96,000/year in base living expenses (inflation-adjusted at 3%), housing costs, healthcare (inflation-adjusted at 5%), and travel. This creates very high initial portfolio dependency.

### Withdrawal Rates and Probability of Success [00:09:23]

Early retirement withdrawal rates are projected at 7-10.3% annually before Social Security, well above the sustainable range of approximately 4-5.5%. Under Monte Carlo simulation, the baseline plan has only a 33% probability of success, meaning in two out of three scenarios the portfolio would be depleted. Long-term care costs for the final two years of life (inflated at 5% annually) are a significant driver of portfolio stress in later years.

### Scenario Analysis: Levers to Improve Outcomes [00:12:29]

Several adjustments are modeled: (1) Selling both properties in late retirement to offset long-term care costs raises success probability noticeably. (2) Joe working one or two additional years has an outsized effect — each year of added work both grows the portfolio and eliminates one of the high-withdrawal early years. (3) Reducing monthly spending by $1,000 and trimming travel by $10,000/year each independently improve success rates. (4) Applying the retirement spending smile (spending inflating at 2% rather than 3%) alone dramatically closes the gap.

### Combining Small Adjustments [00:20:48]

No single lever makes the plan fully sustainable in isolation, but combining the retirement spending smile with modest expense reductions and one extra year of work transforms the plan dramatically. The key takeaway is that retirement planning is about understanding available trade-offs and combining them intelligently, not assuming a large portfolio number guarantees success.
