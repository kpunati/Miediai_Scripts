# The 5 Biggest Risks to Your Retirement (and How to Insure Against Them)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=zAmB_DZE5LY
**Duration:** 16:38

## Summary

This video identifies five major retirement risks—taxes, longevity, poor market returns, major health events, and inflation—and offers a single targeted insurance strategy for each. James Conole frames Roth conversions as 'tax insurance' that protects regardless of whether tax rates rise or fall. He argues that maximizing Social Security and maintaining equity ownership are the most effective tools for managing longevity and inflation risk, respectively.

## Key Takeaways

- Roth conversions function as tax insurance: they save money if future tax rates rise, and if rates fall, your overall financial plan is still better off.
- Maximizing Social Security by delaying to age 70 creates a guaranteed income floor that reduces the impact of running out of portfolio assets.
- Annuities lower the ceiling on potential returns but raise the floor, providing a predictable income stream against poor early-retirement market returns.
- Health insurance and long-term care insurance are primarily financial tools, not just health tools, protecting the portfolio from catastrophic drawdowns.
- Owning stocks is the most reliable long-run inflation hedge: since 1926, large US stocks have outpaced inflation by a wide margin versus T-bills or gold.
- Gold's annualized return since 1980 is only about 3.2%, meaning it has barely kept pace with inflation over that period.

## Detailed Notes

### Risk 1: Taxes and Roth Conversions as Insurance [00:00:00]

Conole introduces the concept of viewing Roth conversions as tax insurance rather than purely a tax-savings strategy. If taxes rise, conversions save significant money; if taxes fall, the financial plan benefits anyway because the overall environment is better.

### Risk 2: Longevity and Social Security Maximization [00:02:04]

Longevity risk—outliving one's portfolio—is mitigated by maximizing Social Security, primarily by delaying claiming. A fully maxed Social Security benefit creates an income floor that makes portfolio size less critical for covering basic expenses.

### Risk 3: Poor Market Returns and Annuities [00:04:09]

Sequence-of-returns risk in early retirement is the key concern, not long-run market performance. Annuities lower the return ceiling but raise the floor, providing guaranteed income at the cost of likely lower overall returns.

### Risk 4: Major Health Events and Insurance Coverage [00:06:15]

Proper health insurance (pre- and post-Medicare) and long-term care coverage protect the portfolio. Long-term care policies have lifetime maximum benefits, so self-insuring with home equity or other earmarked assets is also discussed as an alternative.

### Risk 5: Inflation and Stock Ownership [00:10:24]

Stocks are reframed as an inflation hedge: since 1926, large US stocks averaged 10.3% annually versus inflation's roughly 17x cost increase, while T-bills barely preserved purchasing power. Gold's post-1980 annualized return of ~3.2% is presented as an inferior inflation hedge compared to equities.
