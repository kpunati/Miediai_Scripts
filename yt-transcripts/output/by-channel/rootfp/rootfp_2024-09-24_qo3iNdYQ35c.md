# How to Overcome Fears of Spending in Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-24
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=qo3iNdYQ35c
**Duration:** 33:34

## Summary

This episode of the Ready for Retirement podcast features Ben, who retired at age 53 in 2017 after decades of disciplined saving, and now faces the opposite challenge: giving himself permission to spend. Despite having a portfolio that has nearly doubled since retirement, Ben has been drawing down less than the 4% rule and even reducing his withdrawal rate each year. He shares the practical strategy he implemented to break this pattern by pre-withdrawing funds into a separate account, which enabled him to book a family Airbnb in Charleston and a last-minute two-week cooking trip to Italy.

## Key Takeaways

- Transitioning from a saving to a spending mindset is a genuine psychological challenge that many early retirees underestimate before retiring.
- Chasing ACA health insurance subsidies can create a psychological trap that discourages spending even when the portfolio has grown substantially.
- Pre-withdrawing a dedicated spending bucket at the start of the year can reduce the mental friction of individual spending decisions.
- Ben reduced his annual withdrawal rate progressively below 4% over time, allowing the portfolio to double while denying himself experiences.
- Awareness of finite healthy years is a motivating factor that helped Ben finally commit to spending more on travel and experiences.
- Having a specific retirement spending game plan — not just a savings target — is critical for enabling a healthy financial and emotional transition into retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Ben's Background and Path to Early Retirement [00:00:00]

Ben worked for over 28 years as an account rep for a manufacturing company in Cleveland. In his mid-30s he set a goal to retire at 50, motivated not by passion for his work but by a desire for autonomy. A lifelong saver, he kept his lifestyle modest and invested aggressively, barely increasing spending as his commissions grew. The 2008 market downturn delayed his target by about three years, but he continued investing monthly through the downturn without panic-selling, ultimately retiring at 53 in 2017.

### The Reality of Retirement vs. Expectations [00:06:22]

The biggest surprise in retirement was social isolation — Ben's entire peer group was still working and accumulating wealth, making spontaneous activities difficult. While he valued the freedom from a set schedule, built a yoga, gym, and biking routine, and took road trips to reconnect with friends, the journey was largely a solo one. He also acknowledges he never developed a detailed plan for how he would actually spend his retirement years, only the savings number needed to get there.

### The Spending Challenge and ACA Subsidy Trap [00:11:37]

Ben identifies spending as his hardest retirement challenge. Over seven years he progressively reduced his annual withdrawal rate below 4%, watching his portfolio nearly double. A major driver was his ACA health insurance plan: realizing income above a threshold would cost him a significant subsidy, he kept distributions artificially low. This created a paradox where a growing portfolio coexisted with self-imposed financial restriction and a loss of experiences.

### Breaking the Pattern: The Spending Bucket Strategy [00:15:51]

At the start of 2024, Ben sold a portion of his taxable portfolio and placed the proceeds in a money market account specifically earmarked for spending over the next three to five years. This acceptance that he would pay full-price ACA premiums was psychologically difficult but liberating. The pre-segregated bucket removed the mental hurdle of pulling from the main portfolio for each purchase, enabling him to pay for a family beach vacation in Charleston and a last-minute two-week culinary trip to Italy without regret.

### Lessons and Forward-Looking Plans [00:26:23]

Ben reflects that having a specific experiential game plan — not just a financial number — would have helped him spend sooner. Looking ahead, he is considering a trip to Thailand, possibly with upgraded airfare, and wants to educate himself on IRMAA look-back periods and begin Roth conversions after reaching Medicare eligibility. He also plans to invite friends on trips and cover some of their costs to create shared experiences and have travel companions.
