# Retirement Makeover: I Have a $2M Portfolio and I'm Burned Out at Work - When Can I Retire?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-23
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=nKNGLjengCw
**Duration:** 35:33

## Summary

James Conole conducts a live retirement makeover for Stacy, a 54-year-old retail manager with a $2M portfolio who plans to retire in 93 days while her husband Michael continues working for roughly two more years. The analysis confirms that a withdrawal rate in the 2.5-3.5% range is sustainable for a 40-plus year retirement, but flags heavy employer-stock concentration and the need to build cash or bond reserves. James encourages Stacy to increase discretionary spending, noting she has under-projected her expenses by not giving herself a lifestyle raise in 14 years.

## Key Takeaways

- Aggressive saving—maxing out contributions from every raise and bonus while keeping personal spending flat—can build a $2M portfolio from a standing start at age 47 within seven years.
- A 2.5-3.5% withdrawal rate from a $2M portfolio is well below typical sustainability thresholds and supports even a 40-plus-year retirement horizon.
- 50% concentration in employer stock (RSUs) is a significant risk; diversification is essential before or at retirement to avoid being forced to sell a concentrated position during a downturn.
- Retiring before Social Security eligibility creates a multi-year income gap fully funded by the portfolio; the sequence-of-returns risk in those early years demands liquid reserves such as cash, CDs, or bonds.
- Projecting Social Security with a 25% haircut relative to the current benefit statement is a prudent planning assumption given likely future legislative changes.
- Retirees who have under-spent for years should explicitly model higher discretionary spending (live music, travel, experiences) rather than defaulting to their pre-retirement austerity budget.
- Setting up children's Roth IRAs and 401k accounts and paying for college entirely are forms of legacy planning that have already been completed, removing legacy pressure from the retirement draw-down plan.

## Detailed Notes

### Stacy's Background and Retirement Motivation [00:00:00]

Stacy is 54 and has worked in retail for 33 years, missing holidays and family time, particularly through COVID. She plans to retire March 1 of the following year (93 working days away at the time of recording). Her husband Michael will continue working for approximately two more years. Their biggest concern is outliving their money without being a burden on family.

### How They Built $2M on a Moderate Dual Income [00:04:16]

Combined household income is approximately $174,000 ($120k Stacy, $54k Michael part-time). Stacy describes not giving herself a personal lifestyle raise since 2010, funneling every bonus and salary increase directly into 401k contributions or children's college expenses. Serious saving began in 2017 at age 47; strong employer stock returns contributed materially to the portfolio growth.

### Withdrawal Rate Analysis and Portfolio Sustainability [00:17:53]

The model shows withdrawal rates of 2.5-3.5% during the pre-Social Security gap years (ages 54-64+), declining as Social Security phases in. At $2M+ and these rates, the portfolio has very high historical probability of success even over 40+ years. Stacy mentions a dividend yield of roughly 1.2-1.8%, meaning yields partially cover expenses in normal years, though a cash/bond buffer is still needed for down markets.

### Portfolio Concentration Risk and Allocation Adjustment [00:22:07]

Approximately 50% of the portfolio sits in employer company stock through RSUs. James stresses that this concentration—while historically successful—creates outsized sequence-of-returns risk early in retirement. He recommends gradually diversifying into hundreds of domestic and international companies while maintaining a broadly diversified equity allocation; going 'super conservative' is not the goal.

### Spending Optimization and Legacy Planning [00:24:15]

James encourages Stacy to model elevated spending—$500/month for live music events, a potential $10,000/year Super Bowl budget—showing that even bumping base spending from $2,500 to $3,000/month and vacation from $12,000 to $20,000/year still leaves the portfolio probability of success very high. Stacy has already front-loaded children's Roth IRAs, paid all college expenses, and set up 401k accounts for her kids, effectively completing her legacy obligations ahead of retirement.
