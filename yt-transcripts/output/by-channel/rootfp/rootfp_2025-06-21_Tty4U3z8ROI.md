# 5 Things Retirees Regret - And What You Still Have Time to Get Right

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-06-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Tty4U3z8ROI
**Duration:** 13:27

## Summary

In this episode James Conole draws on observations from working with hundreds of retirees to identify five recurring non-financial regrets. He frames retirement success as a dual problem: getting the financial numbers right is only half the equation; the other half is knowing what kind of life those numbers are supposed to enable. The video covers purpose, health, relationships, realistic expectations about retirement phases, and the psychological difficulty of transitioning from saver to spender.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement planning without a clear vision for life after work leads to a purposeless honeymoon that ends in restlessness — plan what you are retiring toward, not just from.
- Health is compounding — small daily investments in physical wellbeing now preserve the ability to do the things retirement is supposed to enable.
- Work provides social structure that most people underestimate; proactively building and maintaining relationships before retiring prevents isolation later.
- Retirement does not feel like a perpetual vacation; expecting it to do so sets up disappointment during the natural feeling-lost phase that follows the honeymoon.
- Dr. Riley Moines describes four retirement phases: vacation, feeling lost, trial-and-error reinvention, and purposeful reinvention — moving through them requires intentional effort.
- Treating the retirement portfolio as the goal rather than as a means to a good life causes many retirees to underspend during their healthiest years, accumulating wealth they cannot meaningfully use later.

## Detailed Notes

### Regret 1: Not Planning for Life After Work [00:00:00]

Most people focus on the financial dimension of retirement and neglect designing what they will actually do with their time. Conole distinguishes 'freedom from' (escaping work obligations) versus 'freedom to' (having a clear vision for what to pursue), arguing that only the latter produces lasting satisfaction.

### Regret 2: Neglecting Health [00:02:43]

Delaying health investment until retirement is a losing strategy because health lost is rarely recovered. Conole urges treating daily physical habits as compound interest — small consistent actions produce outsized payoffs for the ability to enjoy travel, grandchildren, and active hobbies in retirement.

### Regret 3: Letting Relationships Drift [00:04:16]

Work inadvertently maintains social ties that dissolve without active maintenance after retirement. Research cited from the Harvard Study of Adult Development shows close relationships predict health, cognitive function, and longevity more reliably than financial security. Conole recommends proactive relationship investment before leaving the workplace.

### Regrets 4 and 5: Vacation Fantasy and Fear of Spending [00:06:21]

The four phases of retirement described by Dr. Riley Moines (vacation, feeling lost, trial-and-error, reinvention) are predictable; people who expect the vacation to last are blindsided by the lost phase. Separately, many retirees who accumulated wealth remain in accumulation mode and spend far less than their assets allow, missing experiences during their healthiest years and ending retirement with more money than when they started.
