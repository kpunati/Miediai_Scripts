# Exactly How Much Should You Save for Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-01
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vpnDpkyI-u4
**Duration:** 18:03

## Summary

James Conole presents two frameworks for determining the right retirement savings target. The first is a free spreadsheet calculator that applies the 4% rule to estimate the portfolio needed and the required monthly savings to reach it. The second is a detailed financial planning software demonstration using a sample retiree named Tina, showing how tax treatment of accounts, uneven spending patterns, the retirement spending smile concept, and life expectancy materially change the required savings and projected plan success.

## Key Takeaways

- The simple 4% rule approach gives a quick savings benchmark but misses important nuances like taxes and spending variability.
- Account type matters significantly: all assets in a traditional IRA can push the effective withdrawal rate much higher than the nominal rate due to taxes.
- Breaking out time-limited expenses such as travel or a mortgage payoff date substantially improves projected plan outcomes.
- The retirement spending smile — based on research showing retirees gradually spend less over time — can raise success probability from 63% to 84% in modeled scenarios.
- Life expectancy assumptions have a large impact on required savings; planning to age 100 versus age 80 dramatically changes portfolio sufficiency probabilities.
- Oversaving has real costs: workers may delay retirement or forgo current lifestyle unnecessarily if they do not model a specific target.
- Root Financial's free spreadsheet tool and the Retirement Planning Academy software are offered as resources for both simple and detailed planning.

## Detailed Notes

### Simple Savings Calculator Approach [00:01:03]

The spreadsheet starts with desired monthly spending, subtracts reliable income sources like Social Security, and inflates the gap to retirement date. Dividing by 4% gives the required portfolio size. A sample scenario needing $4,000 per month from the portfolio over 10 years calculates to roughly $1.62 million.

### Why the Simple Approach Falls Short [00:05:16]

The simple calculator ignores tax treatment of withdrawals, uneven spending across retirement phases, the retirement spending smile, and life expectancy variation. Each factor can meaningfully shift both the required portfolio size and the probability of plan success.

### Detailed Planning with Tina Case Study [00:07:22]

Sample retiree Tina, age 65 with $1.5 million all in a traditional IRA, needs $10,000 per month but receives $3,000 from Social Security. Taxes inflate her effective annual withdrawal to over $117,000 — a 7.3% rate — causing the portfolio to run out by age 82. Switching the same balance to a Roth IRA extends longevity to age 90.

### Impact of Spending Patterns and Life Expectancy [00:12:34]

Treating a $3,000 per month travel expense as a 5-year cost rather than permanent raises success probability from 9% to 63%. Applying the retirement spending smile (2% vs. 3% annual spending growth) further raises it to 84%. Planning for longevity to age 100 reduces probability while planning to age 80 raises it to 99%.
