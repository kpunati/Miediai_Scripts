# 5 Things Happy Retirees do Daily

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=vsU2KWzy3So
**Duration:** 14:57

## Summary

Drawing on experience working with hundreds of retirees, CFP James Conole identifies five daily practices that distinguish happy retirees from those who struggle. The video argues that retirement success is not primarily about financial accumulation but about how time is structured and relationships are cultivated. Each habit is explored through behavioral, physical, and social research lenses, with the overarching message that proactively designing a retirement lifestyle is as important as financial planning.

## Key Takeaways

- Morning journaling and reflection help retirees identify their most impactful activities and stay connected to their core values and priorities.
- Staying physically active in retirement preserves health span, reduces depression risk, improves cognitive function, and creates social connections.
- Travel—even locally—combats boredom and isolation, provides cognitive stimulation, and encourages physical activity.
- The 'Wait But Why' blog framework illustrates that retirees have already used most of their in-person time with adult children, making intentional time with loved ones urgent and precious.
- Pursuing hobbies and interests restores the sense of play and adventure that professional and family obligations often suppress over decades.
- A large portfolio supports lifestyle goals, but the habits themselves—not the financial balance—are what determine daily happiness in retirement.

## Detailed Notes

### Daily Reflection and Journaling [00:00:00]

Inspired by Gary Keller's 'The One Thing,' the presenter advocates asking each morning: what is the one thing I can do today such that everything else becomes easier or irrelevant? Evening journaling to record daily wins reinforces positive momentum and keeps retirees aligned with their personal goals rather than drifting into aimless routine.

### Staying Physically Active [00:04:10]

The distinction between lifespan and health span is central—physical activity expands the years when a retiree can enjoy life independently. Mental health benefits include reduced depression and improved cognitive function; social benefits include the gym or exercise class as a community hub. The analogy to investing (short-term sacrifice for long-term gain) frames staying active as a strategic, forward-looking behavior.

### Traveling and Exploring [00:06:15]

Travel is defined broadly to include local hikes and day trips, not just international trips. Benefits include improved mental well-being, cognitive stimulation from navigating new environments, physical activity, social interaction through group tours, lifelong learning, and stress and anxiety reduction. Travel is also presented as a way to break out of routine and restore a sense of purpose.

### Spending Time With Loved Ones [00:09:23]

Tim Urban's 'Wait But Why' blog post is cited to illustrate that by age 18, children have already spent roughly 93% of their in-person time with their parents. Retirees in their 60s or 70s have likely used most of their remaining in-person days with adult children, making intentional time extremely valuable. The same principle applies to old friendships, emphasizing that connection time is finite and should be treated with urgency.

### Pursuing Hobbies and Interests [00:12:33]

Adult responsibilities often erode the sense of play that characterized childhood. Retirement is the opportunity to rediscover or explore entirely new hobbies. The presenter encourages experimentation without pressure to know in advance what will be fulfilling, emphasizing the importance of committing to exploration rather than deferring it.
