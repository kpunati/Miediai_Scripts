# How Much Can I Spend In Retirement with a $2 Million Portfolio?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-09-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=l_bHhn4u2ls
**Duration:** 13:20

## Summary

James Conole presents a detailed case study of Michael and Lisa, early-sixties retirees with $2 million saved across taxable, pre-tax, and Roth accounts plus substantial home equity. Their plan to spend $14,000 per month—including $4,000 per month in travel—produced an initial 32% success probability because it extrapolated first-year travel spending across their full 30-year plan. Three adjustments—time-limiting travel expenses to 10 years, shifting allocation from moderate (60/40) to growth (70/30), and executing annual Roth conversions to fill the 12% bracket during a pre-Social Security income gap—raised success to 74%. Adding a planned future home downsizing improved the outlook further, demonstrating that retirement planning is an iterative optimization rather than a single assumption-based calculation.

## Key Takeaways

- Projecting first-year discretionary expenses such as travel on a permanent basis is one of the most common and consequential retirement planning errors.
- Time-limiting travel spending to the years when it realistically occurs (first 10 years) dramatically reduces the projected portfolio draw-down without changing actual lifestyle.
- A retiree's portfolio allocation should reflect personal risk capacity and cash flow needs rather than a default rule of thumb like age-based 60/40.
- Pre-Social Security years create a low-income window ideal for Roth conversions; filling the 12% bracket annually shifts assets to tax-free status at low cost.
- Their success probability rose from 32% to 74% through these three adjustments alone—no additional savings, no delayed retirement, no lifestyle cuts.
- Cash flow planning, not investment returns, tax strategy, or estate planning, is the lifeblood of a retirement plan and should be the starting point of any analysis.
- Neglecting tax strategy may leave tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars unrealized—money that could otherwise fund retirement spending.

## Detailed Notes

### Michael and Lisa's Starting Position [00:01:01]

The couple has $2 million split across a joint account, 401(k)s, IRAs, and Roth IRAs, plus $800-900k in home equity. Their goal is to retire at 65 and spend $14,000 per month after tax—$10,000 for base living and $4,000 for travel—plus healthcare out-of-pocket costs. Both plan to collect Social Security at age 70, leaving a five-year income gap at the start of retirement.

### Cash Flow Analysis and Initial Outcome [00:03:13]

In year one of retirement, with no salary and Social Security not yet started, the couple needs $252,000 from their portfolio. Their initial withdrawal rate approaches 10% per year, producing a 32% probability of success—meaning the plan as structured is unlikely to sustain a full retirement without changes.

### Three Strategy Adjustments [00:06:51]

Adjustment one separates $4,000 per month in travel expenses and limits them to the first 10 years, reflecting realistic travel behavior. Adjustment two shifts the portfolio from a moderate 60/40 to a growth 70/30 allocation. Adjustment three implements Roth conversions each year the couple is under the 12% bracket, using the pre-Social Security income gap. Together these three changes raised success probability from 32% to 74%.

### Broader Planning Principles [00:10:44]

Adding a future home downsizing—selling the current home in 15 years and purchasing a $700,000 replacement—further improved outcomes. Conole emphasizes three universally applicable principles: model expenses accurately across time horizons, choose portfolio allocation based on actual needs rather than rules of thumb, and never neglect tax strategy since it can represent tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars in lost retirement spending capacity.
