# 3 Key Factors to Consider Before Paying Your Mortgage Off in Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-07-30
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=Kjvi2Np_Ko0
**Duration:** 18:25

## Summary

This episode of Ready for Retirement addresses a listener question about whether to pay off a $332,000 mortgage at 2.75% or invest the funds as retirement approaches. Host James Conole walks through an illustrative comparison showing that in some low-rate scenarios, investing can mathematically outperform paying down debt, but this advantage depends on realized investment returns. The video covers three key analytical dimensions: the raw interest-rate comparison, the tax-adjusted mortgage cost accounting for itemized deductions, and the often-overlooked sequence-of-returns risk that can undermine investment strategies in early retirement. Finally, Conole acknowledges the significant emotional and psychological value of being mortgage-free, which may justify choosing payoff even when the math slightly favors investing.

## Key Takeaways

- For mortgages originated around 2020 at historically low rates, high yield savings accounts paying 5% may generate more interest annually than the mortgage costs, making payoff less urgent from a pure math standpoint.
- The relevant comparison is not the nominal mortgage rate but the tax-adjusted rate, which factors in whether the borrower itemizes deductions and what marginal tax bracket applies.
- Sequence-of-returns risk is a critical unknown: poor early investment returns combined with an ongoing mortgage payment force higher portfolio withdrawals, compounding losses.
- Even if math favors carrying the mortgage, the emotional peace of mind from being debt-free in retirement is a legitimate and valid factor in the decision.
- Higher current mortgage rates (e.g., 6-7%) make it harder for investments to reliably outperform the debt, shifting the calculus toward payoff.
- A meaningful portion of mortgage interest may not provide a tax benefit if total itemized deductions only modestly exceed the standard deduction.

## Detailed Notes

### Listener Scenario and Illustrative Example [00:00:00]

Listener Connor has a $332,000 mortgage balance at 2.75% from 2020 and is targeting retirement at 57. The host uses this as a case study, noting that answers differ dramatically based on when the mortgage was originated given the wide swing in interest rates since 2020. A $332,000 mortgage at 2.75% over 30 years yields a monthly payment of approximately $1,355.

### High Yield Savings Comparison [00:02:05]

Conole illustrates that placing $332,000 in a 5% high yield savings account and making monthly mortgage payments from it would result in roughly $355,000 remaining after 30 years — more than the original balance. The 5% interest income of $16,600 per year exceeds the annual mortgage payment of approximately $16,260, leaving a small surplus. He cautions this assumes a constant 5% rate, which is unlikely given that savings rates fluctuate with Fed policy.

### Tax-Adjusted Mortgage Rate [00:06:15]

The host explains that the true cost of a mortgage is the nominal rate minus any tax savings from itemized deductions. A 7% mortgage rate for a borrower in the 37% bracket who fully itemizes becomes effectively 4.4%. However, only the portion of itemized deductions that exceeds the standard deduction generates actual savings, so the marginal benefit is often less than the headline number suggests.

### Sequence of Returns Risk [00:12:30]

If early retirement years deliver below-average or negative investment returns, a retiree carrying a mortgage faces a compounding problem: they must sell more depreciated assets to meet mortgage payments. Conversely, strong early returns amplify the advantage of staying invested. Because this outcome is unknowable in advance, it introduces irreducible uncertainty into the invest-versus-payoff decision.

### Emotional and Peace-of-Mind Considerations [00:15:37]

Conole acknowledges that for some retirees, the prospect of carrying any debt in retirement is psychologically intolerable, while others are completely comfortable with it. He encourages viewers to weigh emotional peace of mind alongside mathematical outcomes, suggesting that if eliminating the mortgage would provide significant psychological relief, that benefit may outweigh a modest financial advantage from investing.
