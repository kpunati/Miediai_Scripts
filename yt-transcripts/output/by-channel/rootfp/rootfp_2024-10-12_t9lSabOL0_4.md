# RETIREMENT MAKEOVER: The Roth Conversion Trap: Common Tax Planning Mistakes to Avoid

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-12
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=t9lSabOL0_4
**Duration:** 32:34

## Summary

In this Retirement Makeover episode, James Conole works through a detailed financial plan for Alex and Jeanette, a couple who recently retired with a strong asset base including a pension, Social Security, annuities, and roughly $4.4 million in investment accounts. Conole models Roth conversion scenarios showing up to $1.3 million in tax-adjusted gains but challenges the couple to reconsider whether minimizing taxes should be the primary goal. The central lesson is that tax strategy should serve the life plan, not the other way around — and for a couple this well-positioned, spending more today on family, travel, and legacy goals may produce greater life satisfaction than maximizing tax efficiency.

## Key Takeaways

- Alex and Jeanette's combined income floors from pension, Social Security, and annuities are projected to cover their basic expenses, making large portfolio withdrawals unnecessary in later years.
- Without Roth conversions, RMDs in their 80s could force taxable distributions of $368,000 to nearly $500,000 per year on top of other income.
- Converting to the 22% bracket annually could save up to $1.3 million in tax-adjusted terms, but heavy conversion reduces flexibility for current spending.
- Spending $100,000 per year instead of $36,000 would meaningfully improve retirement quality without jeopardizing long-term financial security given the size of their portfolio.
- The 'Roth conversion trap' is optimizing so heavily for tax savings that one ends up with a larger estate but a less fulfilling retirement.
- The correct order is: define the desired retirement lifestyle first, then build tax and investment strategies around those goals.

## Detailed Notes

### Client Background and Tax Efficiency Goals [00:00:00]

Alex, a retired aerospace engineer from Florida, and Jeanette, who spent 35 years at a medical center, both retired in May of the video's year. Their primary worry is balancing adequate income with tax efficiency, having already experienced a large tax bill from a Roth conversion the prior year.

### Portfolio and Goals Overview [00:05:18]

The couple holds approximately $4.4 million in investment accounts including Roth IRAs, traditional IRAs, and annuities, plus a home valued around $1.8 million. Core monthly expenses are modeled at $3,000, with additional goals for vacations ($10,000/year), a car replacement every 10 years, and a boat motor repair in 2026. Alex's pension provides $78,000 per year with no COLA.

### Cash Flow Modeling and Social Security Timing [00:12:38]

Total annual expenses including housing, healthcare, auto loans, and goals reach approximately $163,000 in early retirement, declining as loans pay off and rising again as healthcare increases. Alex is modeled collecting Social Security at 67 (~$3,800/month) and Jeanette at 62 (~$1,500/month). The annuities are set to be annuitized later to create a third income floor.

### Roth Conversion Analysis and Tax Bracket Filling [00:19:58]

Taxable income is projected to grow significantly when RMDs begin, potentially pushing the couple into the 28% bracket. Converting up to the 12% bracket each year adds $414,000 in tax-adjusted value; converting to the 22% bracket adds $1.3 million. However, more aggressive conversions in early retirement leave less money available for current spending.

### The Spending Trade-Off and Final Recommendation [00:25:17]

Conole argues that the couple's frugal $3,000/month budget is below what their portfolio can support, and challenges them to consider $100,000 per year to help adult children, purchase a beach condo, and enhance experiences. The recommendation is to first determine the desired lifestyle, then implement Roth conversions and other tax strategies in support of that plan — rather than letting tax optimization constrain how they live.
