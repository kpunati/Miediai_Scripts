  Retirement is a phase many look forward to, but it often comes with the fear of outliving one's savings. The 4 percent rule is a guideline to help ensure retirement funds last, but misapplying it can lead to financial insecurity. Here are three major mistakes people make with the 4 percent rule and tips to avoid them for a secure retirement.

  Mistake 1: Misunderstanding Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
  Some retirees misinterpret how RMDs affect the 4 percent rule. RMDs are mandatory withdrawals from retirement accounts starting at age 73. Many fear exceeding 4 percent of their pre-tax account value invalidates the rule. However, the 4 percent rule applies to the entire portfolio, not just pre-tax accounts. If you withdraw more due to RMDs, reinvesting the excess in a taxable account can maintain your portfolio balance. Additionally, as you age, your remaining life expectancy decreases, allowing for a higher withdrawal rate.

  Mistake 2: Misconstruing the Value of Annuities
  Annuities might seem attractive due to higher initial withdrawals compared to the 4 percent rule, but this can be misleading. Many annuities don't adjust for inflation, reducing purchasing power over time. While annuities provide a fixed income, the 4 percent rule allows for adjustments based on inflation. Annuities also cease payments upon death, leaving no funds for heirs, whereas the 4 percent rule often results in a residual portfolio for beneficiaries. Additionally, the 4 percent rule offers more flexibility and control over withdrawals.

  Mistake 3: Ignoring Variability in Income and Expenses
  The 4 percent rule assumes a consistent withdrawal rate, but income and expenses fluctuate. Retirement spending typically follows "go-go," "slow-go," and "no-go" years, with higher expenses early on and reduced spending later. Planning for these phases can prevent overspending early and underfunding later. Retirement income sources also change, such as Social Security benefits starting at age 70. Adjusting withdrawals to account for these changes can help maintain a balanced financial plan. Managing taxes on withdrawals, like using tax-free Roth IRAs in high-expense years, is also crucial.

  The 4 percent rule is a valuable starting point for retirement planning, but it's not one-size-fits-all. Understanding and avoiding common mistakes related to RMDs, annuities, and income and expense variability can secure your financial future. A well-rounded approach that considers your unique circumstances and adjusts over time will provide peace of mind in retirement. Stay informed, consult financial advisors, and regularly review your plan to ensure a comfortable and secure retirement.

# 3 Mistakes Retirees Make Implementing the 4% Rule

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-06-29
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=EII4az9jQS8
**Duration:** 14:26

## Summary

James Conole uses hypothetical case studies ('Roger Retired' and 'Ruby Retired') and financial planning software to debunk three misconceptions about the 4% rule. He demonstrates that RMDs above 4% of a pre-tax account do not violate the 4% rule when measured against the total portfolio, that annuities' higher initial payout rates are misleading because they lack inflation adjustments and residual value, and that real retirement spending is highly uneven across go-go, slow-go, and no-go phases — requiring dynamic rather than fixed withdrawal rates.

## Key Takeaways

- RMDs are computed on pre-tax accounts only; measuring withdrawal rate against the total portfolio often keeps the effective rate well below 4%.
- Excess RMD funds can be reinvested in a taxable brokerage account, neutralizing the forced-withdrawal effect on overall portfolio longevity.
- The 4% rule is calibrated to a 30-year life expectancy; retirees in their 80s can safely withdraw at higher rates due to their shorter remaining horizon.
- Fixed annuities that lack inflation adjustments will deliver declining real purchasing power over a 30-year retirement, making the 4% rule's inflation-adjusted withdrawals superior in most scenarios.
- Spending in retirement follows a pattern of high early expenses (travel, active lifestyle), moderate mid-retirement costs, and lower late-retirement spending — the 4% rule should be applied flexibly to reflect these phases.
- When variable income sources like Social Security and pensions phase in over time, portfolio withdrawal rates fluctuate significantly even if lifestyle expenses stay broadly consistent.

## Detailed Notes

### Mistake 1: Misunderstanding RMDs and the 4% Rule [00:00:00]

Many retirees fear that RMDs exceeding 4% of their pre-tax account will invalidate the 4% rule. Conole explains three reasons this is wrong: (1) the 4% rule applies to the total portfolio, not just the pre-tax portion; (2) excess RMD funds can be reinvested in a taxable account; and (3) as you age, your remaining life expectancy shortens, making higher withdrawal rates appropriate. Example: a 6.25% RMD from a 401k that is only 50% of total assets equals a 3.13% overall portfolio withdrawal.

### Mistake 2: Viewing Annuities as Superior Due to Higher Initial Payouts [00:05:13]

An annuity offering 6% of a $1M premium appears superior to the 4% rule's $40K/year, but most fixed annuities provide no inflation adjustment. Over a 30-year retirement with 3% inflation, the 4% rule's inflation-adjusted withdrawals will exceed the fixed annuity payment within roughly 7-8 years, and the 4% portfolio retains residual value for heirs or extended longevity, while annuity payments cease at death.

### Mistake 3: Assuming Uniform Annual Spending [00:08:19]

Using 'Ruby Retired' (age 63, no Social Security until 70, pension starting at 65, $10K/year extra travel for first 10 years), Conole shows that portfolio withdrawal rates vary widely across retirement phases. Early retirement draws heavily on the portfolio before other income sources kick in; once Social Security and pension begin, portfolio withdrawals drop significantly. The withdrawal rate fluctuates from near-high early to much lower mid-retirement, not a flat 4% — validating a dynamic rather than static application of the rule.
