# We have a $3M Retirement Portfolio. How Much Can We Spend Without Running Out of Money?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-05-10
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=NA-IHhAlqvc
**Duration:** 14:37

## Summary

James Conole presents a full retirement planning walkthrough for Jeffrey and Cindy, a couple with $3 million in assets who want to know how much they can safely spend without running out of money. The analysis shows their actual portfolio withdrawal rate (net of Social Security income) is well below sustainable thresholds, suggesting they could increase spending, retire sooner, or be more generous with travel and gifting. The video emphasizes that understanding cash flows and the true withdrawal rate—not just total dollar spending—is the foundation of any sound retirement plan, regardless of portfolio size.

## Key Takeaways

- The true withdrawal rate from a portfolio must exclude income sources like Social Security; total gross spending over 5% does not indicate an unsustainable plan if much of it is covered by guaranteed income.
- Jeffrey and Cindy's net portfolio withdrawal rate is roughly 2.4–2.3% once both Social Security benefits are active, well within sustainable ranges for a 30-year retirement.
- Retirees with excess margin should consider spending more intentionally—whether on larger trips, family experiences, or charitable giving—rather than defaulting to minimal spending.
- A good financial plan balances long-term prudence with current quality of life; maximizing probability of success at the expense of meaningful experiences is a planning failure, not a success.
- Healthcare costs must be layered into cash flows explicitly, not just monthly living expenses, to avoid understating retirement income needs.
- Financial planning should generate optionality: retire earlier, spend more, give more, or leave a legacy—not just one predetermined outcome.

## Detailed Notes

### Jeffrey and Cindy's Starting Position [00:00:00]

The couple has $3 million across investment accounts, an IRA, and a 401k. They plan to spend $7,000 per month on basics plus $30,000 per year on travel for the first 12 years of retirement. Healthcare costs include $8,000 pre-Medicare premiums for Cindy and $4,600 annual out-of-pocket for each after Medicare.

### Understanding Cash Flows as the Foundation [00:03:18]

Conole starts by mapping annual income flows (Social Security benefits for each spouse) against projected total expenses including taxes, property, travel, and healthcare. The net difference—what the portfolio must supply—is the true measure of portfolio stress, not the total spending amount.

### Portfolio Projections and Probability of Success [00:05:30]

Assuming 6.5% annual growth, the portfolio is projected to grow from $3 million to $8.5 million nominally (under $4 million in today's dollars) at age 90, reflecting a 100% probability of success across varied return sequences. The key insight is that the apparent 5%+ gross spending rate masks a much lower net portfolio withdrawal rate once Social Security offsets are recognized.

### How to Read Withdrawal Rates Correctly [00:07:54]

The actual portfolio withdrawal rate starts at 3.6% in year one (before Jeffrey's full Social Security), drops to 2.3–2.4% after both benefits are active, and stays below 3% for nearly the entire retirement even including RMD years. Sustainable withdrawal ranges for 30+ year retirements are generally 4–5.5%; being well beneath this signals capacity to spend more.

### Identifying and Acting on Spending Capacity [00:09:58]

Increasing monthly spending from $7,000 to $10,000 still leaves Jeffrey and Cindy with a high probability of success and a smaller but still substantial portfolio at age 90. Alternatively, they could take more expensive trips, fund family travel, or direct excess capacity toward charitable or legacy goals. Conole stresses that leaving a large unintended estate through underspending is itself a planning failure.
