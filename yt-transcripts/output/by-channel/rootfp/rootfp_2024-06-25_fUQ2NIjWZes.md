# Retirement Income Strategies: IRA vs. Brokerage Withdrawals (The Tax-Smart Choice for Retirees)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-06-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=fUQ2NIjWZes
**Duration:** 32:10

## Summary

James Conole responds to a listener question from a retired couple (ages 65 and 62) who have a $300,000 gain in a single stock representing 10% of an approximately $4 million portfolio. He systematically outlines when drawing from a traditional IRA first makes sense versus drawing from a taxable brokerage account first, examining factors such as RMD management, current versus future tax brackets, Roth conversion opportunity cost, tax gain harvesting at 0% federal rates, and health insurance subsidy qualification. The episode emphasizes that no single formula applies and that the answer depends on the relative size of IRA balances versus brokerage gains, the tax planning window available, and charitable giving intentions.

## Key Takeaways

- Drawing from the IRA first reduces future RMD balances and can lock in taxes at a lower rate today before Social Security and RMDs push income higher.
- Not doing Roth conversions while spending down the IRA represents a significant opportunity cost, as conversions achieve the same two goals while allowing continued tax-free growth.
- For retirees with low income, 0% federal long-term capital gains rates on qualified dividends and gains under $94,050 (MFJ, 2024) make brokerage withdrawals potentially tax-free.
- Living off the brokerage account can lower MAGI to qualify for ACA health insurance subsidies and create capacity for Roth conversions.
- Asset location decisions made during accumulation can create withdrawal sequence risks—holding all stocks in brokerage forces selling during downturns.
- The duration of the tax planning window (gap between retirement and Social Security/RMD onset) is a primary determinant of which strategy to prioritize.
- Qualified charitable distributions from an IRA can offset RMDs dollar-for-dollar, providing tax benefits to significant charitable givers.

## Detailed Notes

### Listener Question and Framework [00:00:00]

Mark and his wife, ages 65 and 62, hold approximately $4 million in assets with $2.5 million estimated in IRAs and $1.5 million in a brokerage account. A single stock in the brokerage carries $300,000 in gains, representing about 10% of the total portfolio. Conole frames the decision as involving RMD management, current-versus-future tax brackets, Roth conversion opportunity cost, and charitable giving.

### Pros and Cons of Drawing from the IRA First [00:03:08]

Primary benefit is keeping the IRA balance from growing unchecked toward RMD age; a $2.5 million IRA growing at 7% for 8 years would reach $4.3 million, generating first-year RMDs of roughly $150,000-$160,000. A secondary benefit is paying taxes at today's lower rates before Social Security income and the 2025 tax bracket sunset raise effective rates. The primary cost is the opportunity cost of not doing Roth conversions instead, which accomplish the same two goals while allowing tax-free growth.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions and RMD Offset [00:11:29]

QCDs allow direct transfers from an IRA to charity starting at age 70.5, counting dollar-for-dollar against the RMD requirement. A $10,000 QCD against a $160,000 RMD is a modest offset, but $100,000 in QCDs would reduce the personal taxable distribution to just $60,000. Charitable giving strategy is therefore directly relevant to the withdrawal sequencing decision.

### Pros and Cons of Drawing from the Brokerage Account First [00:13:33]

The 0% federal long-term capital gains rate for MFJ filers with taxable income under $94,050 (2024) makes brokerage withdrawals potentially tax-free if other income is low. Living off the brokerage also keeps MAGI low enough to qualify for ACA subsidies (relevant to the wife at 63, pre-Medicare) and creates headroom for Roth conversions. The major risk is not implementing a total tax strategy—people enjoy low rates initially but miss the window for Roth conversions that minimize lifetime tax liability.

### Asset Location and Withdrawal Sequencing Risk [00:23:00]

A common adviser recommendation to hold bonds in IRAs and stocks in brokerage creates a withdrawal sequence mismatch: if the plan is to spend brokerage first, those assets are all equities subject to large drawdowns. Conole recommends aligning asset location with the actual planned withdrawal sequence, accepting some tax inefficiency in exchange for reducing sequence-of-returns risk.

### Things to Consider When Deciding [00:26:09]

Key decision factors: (1) the relative size of IRA balance versus embedded brokerage gains—very large IRAs relative to small gains mean focusing almost entirely on IRA drawdown or conversion; (2) current versus projected future tax bracket; (3) the length of the tax planning window between retirement and RMD/Social Security onset; and (4) the scale and form of charitable giving intentions.
