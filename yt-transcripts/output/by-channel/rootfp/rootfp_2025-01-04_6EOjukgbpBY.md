# What if Social Security Gets Cut 3 Things All Retirees Should Do Now

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=6EOjukgbpBY
**Duration:** 15:37

## Summary

James Conole walks through a concrete planning framework for preparing retirement finances for a potential Social Security benefit reduction of up to 20%. Using a hypothetical couple spending $8,000/month with a $1M portfolio, he calculates three numbers — the discretionary expense share, Social Security's income share, and the current portfolio withdrawal rate — and uses their relationships to determine how much margin exists to absorb a cut. He shows that a couple at a 5.76% withdrawal rate has little margin to increase portfolio draws, but that their $1,600/month discretionary cushion could absorb up to a 50% Social Security cut before core expenses are threatened. The framework concludes with action steps: continue working, saving, and growing the portfolio if the analysis reveals insufficient margin.

## Key Takeaways

- Know three key numbers before retirement: discretionary expense percentage, Social Security's share of total income, and the portfolio withdrawal rate.
- A portfolio withdrawal rate already at 5-6% leaves little room to compensate for a Social Security reduction through increased portfolio draws.
- Discretionary expenses serve as a natural buffer — they can be reduced without threatening core needs, making their size relative to Social Security income a key resilience measure.
- If Social Security provides 40% of income and discretionary spending is 20% of total expenses, Social Security could be cut by 50% before core living standards are impacted.
- Retirees whose Social Security benefit covers all core expenses with no portfolio backup are in the most vulnerable position and may need to delay retirement to build more cushion.
- There is no single silver bullet — every individual should proactively model their specific numbers rather than waiting for benefit changes to occur.

## Detailed Notes

### The Three Numbers Framework [00:00:00]

Three metrics define retirement resilience against a Social Security cut: (1) what percentage of monthly expenses are discretionary versus non-negotiable; (2) what share of total retirement income will come from Social Security; and (3) what portfolio withdrawal rate will be required in retirement. Together these determine the margin available to absorb a benefit reduction.

### Example Walkthrough: $8,000/Month Couple [00:01:01]

The example couple spends $8,000/month, with 80% ($6,400) in essential non-discretionary expenses and 20% ($1,600) discretionary. Their combined Social Security benefit is $3,200/month (40% of income), and they draw $4,800/month from a $1M portfolio — a 5.76% annual withdrawal rate, already on the high end.

### Margin Analysis: Portfolio Withdrawal Rate [00:04:08]

If a retiree is drawing only 2% from their portfolio, absorbing a $10,000 annual Social Security reduction by increasing portfolio withdrawals to 3% is feasible. However, the example couple at 5.76% — already uncomfortably high — has no such margin. This is why withdrawal rate must be assessed before evaluating benefit-cut resilience.

### Margin Analysis: Discretionary Expenses as Buffer [00:07:17]

The couple's $1,600/month discretionary budget is 50% of their $3,200 Social Security benefit. This means Social Security could be cut by up to 50% before essential expenses are threatened. If Social Security were cut by 25%, they would need to give up only half of their discretionary spending. This analysis gives retirees a clear threshold rather than vague anxiety.

### Action Steps: What to Do If Margins Are Insufficient [00:10:23]

If the analysis shows insufficient margin — either through high withdrawal rates or insufficient discretionary cushion — the prescription is clear: continue working, saving, and investing to build portfolio size, and delay claiming Social Security to increase the base benefit (since cuts are typically proportional, a higher base benefit reduces the dollar impact of a percentage cut). There is no substitute for a larger buffer.
