# One Simple Tip to Make Spending Easy in Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-25
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=0pUW9jcMlD4
**Duration:** 17:04

## Summary

James Conole tackles the behavioral challenge of retirement spending, noting that high-achieving savers often cannot transition from accumulation to decumulation even when financially capable. He traces the root cause to ingrained scarcity mindsets formed during earlier, financially constrained life periods. The video provides a practical five-step process—from acknowledging feelings to pre-withdrawing money into dedicated spending accounts at the start of each year—to make aligned retirement spending habitual rather than willpower-dependent.

## Key Takeaways

- The people who save the most for retirement are often the same people who struggle most to spend in retirement, regardless of portfolio size.
- Spending reluctance typically originates from early-life experiences with scarcity, and those mental patterns do not automatically update when financial circumstances improve.
- Step one is to simply acknowledge the savings-oriented feeling; step two is to evaluate whether that feeling still serves your current financial situation.
- Identifying what you actually value—travel, health, experiences, giving—before you need to make spending decisions reduces the emotional drag of each purchase.
- Creating systems and environments (e.g., signing up for personal training, spending time with socially active friends) removes the need for repeated willpower decisions.
- The practical 'pre-withdrawal tip' involves pulling planned annual spending out of the portfolio in January and parking it in a separate account so the money feels already spent when used.

## Detailed Notes

### The Spending Paradox and Client Story [00:00:00]

Conole opens with the irony that disciplined accumulators are the group most likely to under-spend in retirement. A client couple who had sold two businesses and held a large portfolio was shown they could spend significantly more and still not outlive their money, yet the husband still walked blocks to a 7-Eleven to avoid a $5 hotel mini-bar M&M purchase days after that meeting.

### Origins of the Scarcity Mindset [00:03:07]

Conole shares his own childhood in a pastor's family where money was tight, explaining how driving 30–50 minutes to find free parking near a stadium served a real purpose at the time. The core insight is that mental operating systems formed under scarcity persist long after financial circumstances change, and that recognizing this is the first step toward changing behavior.

### Five-Step Framework (Steps 1–4) [00:05:13]

Steps one and two are to acknowledge the feeling and evaluate whether it still serves you. Step three is to identify your values without thinking about money first. Step four is to build systems—signing up for classes, committing to friend groups that do the activities you enjoy—so that spending on valued things becomes automatic rather than a repeated willpower battle.

### Step 5: The Pre-Withdrawal Technique [00:12:33]

Guest Ben, who retired in 2017 and watched his portfolio double without increasing spending, shared the technique of withdrawing his planned annual travel budget in January into a dedicated bank account. Because the money is mentally 'already spent,' the pain of pulling from the portfolio is eliminated, making it far easier to follow through on planned experiences throughout the year.
