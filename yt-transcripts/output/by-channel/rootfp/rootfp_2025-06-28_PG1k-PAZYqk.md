# Watch Me Stress Test a $1 Million Retirement Plan

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-06-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=PG1k-PAZYqk
**Duration:** 19:29

## Summary

James Conole walks through a detailed retirement plan stress test for a hypothetical couple, Jim and Sally, both age 65 with just over $1 million in liquid investments and a home worth roughly $1 million. Their retirement goals, including $6,500 per month in living expenses, $24,000 per year in travel for 10 years, and Medicare healthcare costs, require a 13-14% annual withdrawal rate — nearly three times the sustainable threshold. Conole systematically tests the impact of working longer, cutting expenses, optimizing investments, improving taxes, and downsizing their home, concluding that the home downsize produces the most dramatic improvement in retirement viability with the fewest lifestyle sacrifices.

## Key Takeaways

- A seven-figure portfolio is not automatically sufficient for retirement; the withdrawal rate required to fund a desired lifestyle determines plan sustainability.
- A 4-5% withdrawal rate is the conventional starting benchmark; Jim and Sally's plan starts at 13-14%, making failure nearly certain without changes.
- Tax strategy and investment strategy are optimizers — they improve a plan that already works but cannot rescue a plan with a fundamentally unsustainable withdrawal rate.
- Working three additional years is highly effective but can be a high personal cost for people who are already burned out.
- Cutting travel and reducing monthly spending to $6,200 per month from $6,500 only moves the probability of success to roughly 50%, still insufficient.
- Downsizing to a smaller home produces a triple benefit: eliminating the mortgage payment, reducing ongoing property taxes, and adding investable capital — raising success probability to nearly 80%.
- Retirement planning requires separating fixed and temporary expenses (mortgage, travel) from ongoing living costs so projections accurately reflect the true cost over a 30+ year horizon.

## Detailed Notes

### Jim and Sally's Starting Situation [00:00:00]

Both age 65 with just over $1 million in investment accounts (IRAs and joint brokerage) and a primary home with roughly $1 million in equity after a $145,000 mortgage, giving a total net worth of about $2.1 million. They want to retire immediately, with $6,500 per month in basic living expenses, $24,000 per year in travel for the first 10 years, and Medicare healthcare costs estimated at approximately $3,600 per year per person in out-of-pocket expenses.

### Cash Flow Analysis and Withdrawal Rate Problem [00:04:28]

Sally plans to collect Social Security at 67 ($2,300/month) and Jim at 70 ($3,900/month), leaving a two-to-five year income gap at the start of retirement. Total projected outflows including living expenses adjusted for inflation, housing, healthcare, travel goals, and taxes show the couple needing $140,000+ per year from the portfolio initially, representing a 13-14% withdrawal rate — well above the 4-5% sustainable benchmark. The projections show the million-dollar portfolio depleted before death.

### Testing the Options: Work Longer and Expense Cuts [00:12:16]

Working three more years improves probability of success from 13% to approximately 70% — meaningful but personally costly for an already fatigued couple. Eliminating all travel but keeping $6,500 in monthly expenses yields only a 50% probability of success. Reducing monthly expenses to $6,200 and eliminating travel inches toward acceptable territory but still falls short. Tax strategy (Roth conversions) adds only 3 percentage points; a higher projected return of 9% adds only one to two years of additional runway — neither is a rescue solution.

### Downsizing as the Best Trade-Off [00:16:42]

Purchasing a $700,000 replacement home in Colorado provides three simultaneous benefits: the existing mortgage payment (~$1,500/month principal and interest) disappears, ongoing property taxes decrease by several thousand dollars annually, and the net proceeds from the sale provide additional investable capital. The combination shifts probability of success from 13% to nearly 80% without requiring the couple to work longer, cut travel, or reduce monthly spending — making it the highest-impact, lowest-lifestyle-cost solution available to Jim and Sally.
