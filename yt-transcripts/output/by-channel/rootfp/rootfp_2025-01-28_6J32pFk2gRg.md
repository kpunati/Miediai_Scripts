# 5 Advanced Social Security Strategies to Maximize Your Retirement Income

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-01-28
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=6J32pFk2gRg
**Duration:** 16:47

## Summary

James Conole, CFP of Root Financial, answers a viewer question about the Social Security earnings limit and uses it as a springboard to explain five key Social Security limits that retirees must understand to maximize benefits. The video covers the earnings limit and its monthly mechanics, the wage base and payroll tax structure, maximum benefit amounts at different claiming ages, provisional income thresholds governing how much of Social Security is taxable, and bend points that define the weighted benefit formula favoring lower earners.

## Key Takeaways

- Earnings before starting Social Security benefits do not count toward the earnings limit—only wages earned after benefits begin are tested.
- The monthly earnings limit matters more than the annual figure because Social Security applies limits on a month-by-month basis for new claimants.
- The 2025 Social Security wage base of $176,100 caps both what high earners pay in and the benefit they can receive, explaining why benefits plateau for very high earners.
- Maximum 2025 benefits range from $2,710/month at age 62 to $5,108/month at age 70, assuming 35 years of maximum earnings.
- Provisional income thresholds for Social Security taxation are not inflation-indexed, steadily eroding the tax advantage for retirees over time.
- Bend points reveal Social Security is progressive: the first $1,226/month of earnings yields a 90% replacement rate, creating outsized benefit from even modest part-time work in later years.

## Detailed Notes

### Answering David's Question: The Earnings Limit [00:01:03]

The earnings limit applies only to wages earned after benefits begin—not before. In 2025, the limit is $23,400/year ($1,950/month) for those claiming before full retirement age; Social Security withholds $1 for every $2 earned above it. In the year of reaching FRA, a higher limit of $62,160 applies and the withholding rate becomes $1 for every $3. After FRA, earnings have no impact on benefits. Wages are counted; pensions, investment income, and capital gains are not.

### Social Security Wage Base and Payroll Taxes [00:06:15]

The wage base ($176,100 in 2025) caps the earnings subject to the 6.2% Social Security payroll tax; Medicare's 1.45% continues above this threshold. Earnings above the wage base do not increase future Social Security benefits because contributions stop. The wage base is expected to be modified as policymakers explore ways to shore up Social Security solvency.

### Maximum Benefit Amounts and Claiming Age [00:09:22]

Maximum 2025 monthly benefits are $2,710 at age 62, $4,018 at full retirement age, and $5,108 at age 70—all requiring 35 years of maximum earnings. The family maximum (150-180% of the FRA benefit) limits total household benefits across spousal and child benefits tied to one earnings record.

### Provisional Income and Benefit Taxation [00:11:27]

Provisional income determines federal taxation of Social Security. For single filers: below $25,000 = 0% taxable; $25,000-$34,000 = up to 50% taxable; above $34,000 = up to 85% taxable. For married filers: below $32,000 = 0%; $32,000-$44,000 = up to 50%; above $44,000 = up to 85%. Because these thresholds are not inflation-indexed, a growing share of retirees will find 85% of their benefit included in taxable income over time.

### Bend Points and the Progressive Benefit Formula [00:13:32]

The AIME-to-PIA formula for 2025 applies 90% replacement on monthly earnings up to $1,226, 32% on earnings between $1,226 and $7,391, and 15% on earnings above $7,391. This creates strong incentives for lower-earning years to count in the 35-year record, and makes even part-time work in later years disproportionately valuable for workers who have gaps in their earnings history.
