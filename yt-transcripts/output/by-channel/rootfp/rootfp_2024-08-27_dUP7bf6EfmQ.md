# "The Joy Is Trying New Things and Being Surprised by What Works"

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-27
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=dUP7bf6EfmQ
**Duration:** 33:47

## Summary

James Conole interviews Chris, a 58-year-old who retired after 31 years at a regional utility managing energy efficiency programs. Chris discusses the psychological shift of shedding workplace stress, discovering purpose through mentorship, community service, and family support, and navigating the loss of collegial relationships post-retirement. A central theme is how his decision to retire before his wife served as a low-risk trial run that built her confidence to follow.

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement stress relief often becomes apparent only when specific stressors — such as storm-duty calls or executive report-outs — no longer occur, rather than on day one.
- Purpose in retirement does not require a formal plan; experimenting with committees, mentoring, and church volunteering and iterating on what fits is a viable strategy.
- Losing daily interaction with work colleagues is a genuine and often underestimated emotional challenge of retirement that takes intentional effort to address.
- One spouse retiring first functions as a practical trial that lets the couple observe lifestyle and financial impact before the second spouse makes the same transition.
- Flexibility, an adventurous mindset, and comfort with the unknown are repeatedly cited as the most important psychological traits for a fulfilling retirement.
- Catch-up contributions at age 50 and long-horizon goal-setting from early in one's career were key financial preparation behaviors Chris attributes to retirement readiness.

## Detailed Notes

### Background and Career [00:01:03]

Chris worked 31 years at a large regional utility in energy efficiency and demand response, managing a team and a substantial budget. He retired at 57 and had been thinking about retirement since early in his career, intensifying planning roughly 10 years before actually retiring.

### Pre-Retirement Fears and Expectations [00:04:10]

Key fears centered on portfolio sufficiency over a 30-plus-year retirement and loss of goal structure that a career naturally provides. Chris and his wife held many preparatory conversations, often triggered by work stress such as storm-duty disruptions or major household expenses.

### Decompressing and Discovering Hidden Stress [00:09:30]

Chris was largely enthusiastic from day one, but the full weight of prior workplace stress became clear only when specific triggers — the first major storm he was not called for, the absence of weekly executive report-outs — no longer materialized. He frames this as a gradual, pleasant revelation rather than an immediate realization.

### Finding Purpose and Embracing Mystery [00:12:38]

Chris found purpose in mentoring young people on personal finance, church volunteering, and supporting his adult daughters' social circles. He emphasizes that the mystery of not knowing precisely what will be fulfilling is itself motivating, and that trying activities, accepting some will not fit, and moving on mirrors a healthy career mindset.

### Losing Workplace Relationships [00:20:57]

Missing daily professional interaction is identified as the primary challenge of retirement. Chris notes the shift from being a manager whose messages received immediate responses to a retiree whose texts may go unanswered for hours, requiring an ego check and realistic expectations about how relationships naturally evolve.

### One Spouse Retiring First [00:25:14]

Chris's staggered retirement from his wife served as an extended trial: the household lived on her salary alone, the portfolio was untouched, and both partners could observe the lifestyle change in real time. This gave his wife data and confidence rather than having to take a leap of faith, and she is expected to retire within a year of the interview.

### Final Advice [00:29:23]

Chris encourages retirees to embrace flexibility and outward focus — using accumulated life experience to help others — rather than requiring a fully mapped plan before retiring. He acknowledges he may have as many years in retirement as he had in his career, framing it as an entire second chapter with its own potential for growth.
