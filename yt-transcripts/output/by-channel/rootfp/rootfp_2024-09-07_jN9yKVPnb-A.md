# I have $2.3M in IRAs. How Do I Minimize Taxes in Retirement?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-07
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=jN9yKVPnb-A
**Duration:** 42:54

## Summary

This Retirement Makeover episode follows Andre, a 56-year-old with his 55-year-old wife, as financial planner James Conole builds a comprehensive retirement projection to address their concerns about Roth conversions, IRMAA surcharges, and ACA health insurance cost management during the gap between early retirement and Medicare eligibility at 65. The case study demonstrates that getting expense projections right is a prerequisite for any sound tax strategy, since overstated expenses can make beneficial Roth conversions appear to reduce portfolio longevity. Key takeaways include a phased Roth conversion approach timed to low-income years, the importance of Social Security deferral to age 70, and the mechanics of unwinding employee stock compensation.

## Key Takeaways

- Accurate expense forecasting — accounting for mortgage payoff, children leaving, and realistic lifestyle costs — must come before any Roth conversion analysis, as overstated expenses can make sensible conversions look counterproductive.
- With the majority of assets in pre-tax accounts, Andre and his wife face significant future RMD exposure that strategic Roth conversions in low-income retirement years can help mitigate.
- Filing income taxes below IRMAA thresholds during early retirement reduces Medicare premium surcharges in later years, making the Roth conversion window between retirement and Social Security especially valuable.
- The ACA subsidy cliff makes managing modified adjusted gross income during the employer-to-Medicare coverage gap a meaningful planning lever that interacts with Roth conversion sizing.
- The retirement spending smile — average retiree spending growing slower than inflation — substantially improves long-term portfolio projections compared to inflation-adjusted flat spending assumptions.
- For RSUs and ESPP shares, the key decision point is vesting, not later portfolio withdrawal: treating vested shares as a cash bonus and deciding whether to hold company stock or diversify prevents unintentional concentration risk.
- Smoothing large one-time expenses across adjacent tax years (e.g., pulling half of a vacation budget in December and half in January) can prevent unnecessary spikes into higher tax brackets.

## Detailed Notes

### Andre's Situation and Planning Goals [00:00:00]

Andre (56) and his wife (55) have over $2.6 million in assets, predominantly in pre-tax 401k and IRA accounts, with a target retirement at ages 61 and 60. Their primary concerns are optimizing Roth conversion timing, avoiding IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges, and managing income to qualify for ACA health insurance subsidies during the four-year gap between retirement and Medicare eligibility. Both plan to defer Social Security to age 70 to maximize lifetime income.

### Income and Expense Blueprint [00:03:08]

Current monthly expenses are estimated at $12,000, which includes a mortgage ($1,580 principal and interest), costs for two adult children still at home (approximately $1,000), and significant equestrian hobby expenses for Andre's wife. Planned large goals include car replacements every seven to eight years, biennial vacation spending that Andre hopes to increase, and four gifting events of approximately $40,000 each to help children with down payments or weddings. A potential inheritance from in-laws is also modeled as a lump sum roughly a decade out.

### Tax Bracket and Roth Conversion Strategy [00:13:39]

The projection shows that taxable income will drop sharply at retirement as spending shifts to taxable brokerage accounts first, creating windows in the 10% and 12% brackets where Roth conversions are highly advantageous. After brokerage assets are exhausted, taxable income rises again as IRA withdrawals fund living expenses, then dips again if the projected inheritance provides taxable assets to draw from. The analysis confirms that filling the 12% bracket with conversions saves meaningfully on a tax-adjusted basis, while conversions into the 22% bracket may not be beneficial given the current expense projections.

### RMDs, IRMAA, and Medicare Planning [00:19:56]

Required minimum distributions starting at age 75 for Andre will add roughly $200,000 of taxable income on top of approximately $150,000 in Social Security, creating a $350,000 income floor that may still fall short of projected $410,000 expenses at that stage. IRMAA surcharges kick in above approximately $200,000 MAGI for married filers, meaning the Roth conversion strategy that keeps income lower in early retirement also reduces future Medicare premium costs — a compounding benefit beyond simple income tax savings.

### Expense Refinement and Spending Smile [00:24:07]

Removing the mortgage payment and children's expenses from the $12,000 monthly baseline yields a more realistic $9,500 projection for retirement, and applying the retirement spending smile (spending growing 1 percentage point slower than inflation) further extends the portfolio. These two adjustments together transform the plan from a 42% Monte Carlo success probability to a substantially higher one, underscoring that expense accuracy is the highest-leverage input in retirement modeling.

### Employee Stock Compensation and Capital Gains [00:36:48]

Andre's wife holds RSUs and ESPP shares in her equity compensation plan. James advises treating RSU vesting as a cash bonus event: if the employee would not independently choose to buy company stock with a cash bonus, the shares should be sold at vesting and proceeds diversified. Holding concentrated company stock after vesting provides no tax advantage since ordinary income taxes are owed at vesting regardless of when shares are sold. Capital gains bracket management is less relevant for Andre given the pre-tax account concentration, but would be a primary tool if more assets were in brokerage accounts.

### Action Items and Order of Operations [00:38:55]

James summarizes that the right sequence is: first build a detailed retirement expense budget distinguishing which costs stay, rise, or fall; then model income needs to understand the size of the portfolio withdrawal required; and only after that determine which years and which tax brackets offer the best Roth conversion opportunities. Staggering large planned expenditures across tax years to smooth the income profile is a simple but effective tactic for keeping conversions within optimal brackets.
