# How to Reduce Sequence of Return Risk | 2 Essential Strategies for Early Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-09-14
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=pExHabq-7po
**Duration:** 41:18

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial Partners conducts a live retirement makeover for John, a 52-year-old medical device engineer planning to retire at 55. The session identifies two core drivers of sequence-of-return risk—excessive withdrawal rates and high portfolio concentration—and shows through hypothetical scenarios why a heavily US-equity-concentrated portfolio is especially vulnerable in early retirement when there is no Social Security income to buffer withdrawals. Conole recommends building five years of living expenses in safer assets (primarily bonds in the taxable account) and gradually diversifying away from US large-cap equities, rather than targeting a fixed bond-percentage allocation.

## Key Takeaways

- Sequence-of-return risk is most dangerous when both a high withdrawal rate and high asset concentration occur simultaneously in early retirement.
- John's projected withdrawal rate of 3–3.5% before Social Security and 1.5% after is well within safe territory, significantly reducing his risk profile.
- The practical heuristic for bond allocation is five years of living expenses in safer assets, not a fixed percentage of portfolio, because the dollar amount needed does not scale linearly with portfolio size.
- A diversified portfolio (US large/mid/small cap, international, emerging markets, real estate, bonds) may underperform a pure S&P 500 portfolio during accumulation but dramatically reduces the risk of portfolio depletion during a prolonged early-retirement downturn.
- Roth conversions are a meaningful opportunity for John given his multi-year zero-income gap before Social Security begins, allowing conversion at low marginal rates.
- Healthcare is a key variable: John's company offers permanent COBRA-style coverage at employer cost if he retires at age 55, which is the primary driver of his targeted retirement date.
- Dividends and interest from a portfolio remain relatively resilient even during severe bear markets, partially offsetting the need to sell equities at depressed prices.

## Detailed Notes

### John's Profile, Goals, and Key Concerns [00:00:00]

John is 52, earns a high income in the medical device industry, and has accumulated roughly $3 million in qualified accounts and ~$500,000 in taxable accounts with minimal mortgage remaining. His top concerns are sequence-of-return risk (his portfolio is ~92% in equities), lack of Roth savings due to income limits, and healthcare coverage before Medicare. The primary retirement trigger is an employer benefit allowing permanent health coverage at company cost upon reaching age 55 with any years of service.

### Cash Flow Analysis and Withdrawal Rate Assessment [00:12:36]

John's plan projects $0 income for the roughly 12 years between retirement (mid-2027) and Social Security onset (~2039). Annual portfolio withdrawals are projected at approximately $161,000 in the first full year, covering $6,500/month base expenses, $40,000/year travel, healthcare (~$1,500/month worst case), and periodic goals such as a $50,000 car every eight years. The resulting withdrawal rate of 3–3.5% before Social Security is well within the 4–5.5% guardrails, and drops to ~1.5% after Social Security kicks in.

### Sequence-of-Return Risk: Concentration Scenario Analysis [00:20:02]

Conole illustrates using a hypothetical $1 million portfolio with 100% large-cap US stocks from 2000–2024: average annual return was ~7.4%, yet a 5% annual withdrawal exhausted the portfolio around 2016 because the first three years saw S&P 500 losses of 9%, 12%, and 22%. A diversified portfolio (blending US large/mid/small cap, international, EM, real estate, and bonds) returned slightly less over the same period but preserved capital throughout, leaving the investor still in the game by 2024 despite identical withdrawals.

### Five-Years-of-Expenses Bond Heuristic and Portfolio Positioning [00:29:32]

Rather than targeting a fixed bond percentage, Conole recommends holding five years of living expenses (~$750,000 in John's case) in safer assets in the taxable brokerage account. The rationale: the worst post-Depression S&P 500 recovery took 5.5 years, so this reserve ensures no forced equity sales during any historical bear market. John currently has ~$250,000 in bonds; adding ~$200,000 from home-sale proceeds and three more years of contributions gets him close to the target. Bond placement in the taxable account is preferred because withdrawals will begin there, minimizing taxable events through capital-gain harvesting.

### Roth Conversions and Tax Planning [00:04:12]

John has no Roth savings due to income exceeding contribution limits throughout his career. The multi-year gap between retirement and Social Security onset—when his taxable income is near zero—presents a prime window for Roth conversions at very low effective tax rates. The software projects little to no tax in the early retirement years as John draws down his brokerage account first, validating the value of systematic conversions from his IRA during this period.
