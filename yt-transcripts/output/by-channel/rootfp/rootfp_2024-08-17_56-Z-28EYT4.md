# How Much Can I Expect To Spend In Retirement with a $2.2 Million Portfolio?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-08-17
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=56-Z-28EYT4
**Duration:** 17:58

## Summary

James Conole uses Tommy and Monica — a teacher and attorney in their early 60s with a $2.2 million portfolio — to illustrate how detailed retirement cash flow modeling reveals more flexibility than most pre-retirees realize. The video models their retirement at age 70 as a baseline then demonstrates three alternatives: retiring at 65, increasing spending by $3,000 per month, or stopping retirement savings entirely to enjoy current lifestyle. All three alternatives maintain a high probability of success, challenging the default assumption that maximizing portfolio accumulation is always optimal.

## Key Takeaways

- Many savers default to age 70 as a retirement target without modeling earlier dates; planning software often reveals earlier retirement is feasible.
- Delaying retirement to age 70 to maximize Social Security may result in missing the healthiest travel years in one's 60s.
- A $2.2 million portfolio with continued savings projects to approximately $5 million by age 70, generating withdrawal rates well below 3% — historically very safe.
- Retiring at 65 versus 70 significantly reduces projected end-of-life wealth but still supports a comfortable retirement with high probability of success.
- Stopping contributions entirely for a decade still leaves the plan viable given the compounding of an already-large existing balance.
- Over-saving can be as suboptimal as under-saving: Tommy and Monica might pass away with $12+ million if they keep saving until 70 without increasing spending.
- Retirement planning should optimize both current and future lifestyle, not just maximize the terminal portfolio balance.

## Detailed Notes

### Tommy and Monica's Financial Profile [00:00:00]

Tommy is a private-school English teacher earning $82,000 and contributing 15% to a 403b with no pension. Monica is an attorney earning $215,000, maxing her 401k, and has an IRA from a rolled-over prior 401k. Combined portfolio is $2.2 million. Both plan to delay Social Security to age 70 for maximum benefit.

### Retirement Income and Expense Modeling [00:05:13]

Monthly living expenses are $6,500 plus $20,000 per year for travel, $15,000 per year for home maintenance, and periodic $30,000 vehicle purchases. Health insurance before Medicare is estimated at $800 per month per person plus out-of-pocket costs after Medicare. The model projects inflation-adjusted cash flows from today through retirement.

### Age 70 Baseline: Overfunded Outcome [00:09:23]

Projected portfolio balance of $5 million at age 70 yields a 2.6-2.7% withdrawal rate initially, dropping to approximately 2% after the mortgage is paid off. Withdrawal rates remain well below sustainable historical thresholds throughout retirement. The model projects a 12+ million ending balance — an unintended legacy outcome for a couple that would have preferred earlier retirement.

### Three Alternatives to Optimize the Plan [00:11:27]

Option 1: Retire at age 65 — still shows high probability of success with a significantly better quality-of-life outcome during healthy years. Option 2: Retire at 70 but increase monthly spending by $3,000 to $9,500 — still viable with a high success probability. Option 3: Stop all retirement savings immediately, use freed income for current lifestyle — the existing $2.2 million portfolio still compounds sufficiently, and the plan succeeds with high probability.
