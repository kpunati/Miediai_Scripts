# How to Avoid the RMD Tax Trap: Protect Your Lifetime of Tax Savings

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-10-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=TQeTrfnm0nc
**Duration:** 36:34

## Summary

James Conole presents a detailed retirement makeover for Spencer, a 48-year-old enterprise software professional with roughly $3.5 million in investments, multiple rental condos, and a goal to retire at 50. The central concern is whether decades of pre-tax 401(k) contributions will be negated by large RMDs starting at age 75, which the modeling shows could push Spencer into the 32-33% bracket after stacking with Social Security, rental income, and dividends. James illustrates that retiring at 50 creates a 25-year tax planning window — far wider than someone retiring at 70 — allowing meaningful Roth conversions at 12% or 22% rates to systematically reduce future RMD balances. The session also covers optimal withdrawal sequencing, the risk of over-converting, the role of qualified charitable distributions, and the need for annual strategy reviews as tax rates, market returns, and spending evolve.

## Key Takeaways

- Pre-tax retirement account balances can produce RMDs large enough to push retirees into higher brackets than the rates at which they originally saved, especially when stacking on Social Security and other income.
- Retiring early creates a wide tax planning window; Spencer retiring at 50 has 25 years to do Roth conversions before RMDs begin at 75, whereas retiring at 70 would leave only three years.
- Withdrawal sequencing alone — spending taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred, then Roth — can add over $3.6 million in tax-adjusted ending portfolio value compared to prorata withdrawals.
- Roth conversions should target filling specific tax brackets (12% or 22%) each year rather than converting indiscriminately; converting past the optimal point can over-correct and result in higher lifetime taxes.
- Qualified charitable distributions allow charitably inclined retirees to reduce their RMD liability directly, dollar for dollar up to the donation amount, without triggering taxable income.
- Real-estate sales trigger depreciation recapture and long-term capital gains in the same year as other income, and those sale years must be modeled separately when planning conversion amounts.
- Roth conversion strategy should be revisited annually as market performance, actual spending, tax law changes, and other income sources shift the optimal target balance and cutoff age.

## Detailed Notes

### Spencer's Profile and Core Concern [00:00:00]

Spencer is a 48-year-old enterprise software professional with six children, approximately $3.5 million in investments (traditional 401k, Roth IRA, brokerage, and rental condos), and plans to retire at 50. His key question is whether the tax deductions he received from pre-tax 401(k) contributions over 25 years will ultimately be offset — or exceeded — by future RMD-driven tax bills. He also notes that his rental condos, while useful, have become labor-intensive and he expects to sell them within a few years.

### The RMD Problem: Tax Spike at Age 75 [00:16:53]

The base-case projection (no tax strategy, prorata withdrawals) shows minimal plan distributions until age 75, when the government requires Spencer to begin drawing down his traditional IRA based on life-expectancy tables. In that first RMD year, projected federal taxes jump from roughly $78,000 to $170,000 because the mandatory distribution stacks on top of Social Security income, dividend income, and interest income. The RMD amount itself could grow to over $1 million annually by the mid-80s if accounts continue to compound without withdrawals.

### The Tax Planning Window and Withdrawal Sequencing [00:18:58]

Retiring at 50 rather than 70 gives Spencer a 25-year window of lower taxable income before RMDs begin — versus only three years for someone retiring at 70. During this window, money can be moved from tax-deferred accounts to Roth at today's lower rates. Separately, switching from prorata withdrawals to spending taxable accounts first, then IRAs, then Roth, is shown to produce over $3.6 million more in tax-adjusted ending portfolio value without any Roth conversions at all.

### Roth Conversion Strategies and the Risk of Over-Correcting [00:24:13]

James models three conversion levels: filling the 10% bracket (minimal impact), filling the 12% bracket (meaningfully reduces future RMDs and top-bracket exposure), and filling the 22% bracket (substantially reduces projected cumulative RMDs from over $20 million to under $4 million). He cautions that converting to the 22% bracket every year indefinitely can over-correct — eliminating all RMDs sounds ideal but results in front-loaded tax payments that exceed the savings; stopping conversions around age 65 once pre-tax balances are sufficiently reduced proves more efficient. Property sale years require separate treatment because depreciation recapture and capital gains add to ordinary income, potentially reducing the space available for conversions.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions and Annual Review [00:30:32]

For charitably inclined retirees, qualified charitable distributions (QCDs) from age 70.5 allow RMDs to be satisfied tax-free by directing them to a charity — a $20,000 QCD reduces a $100,000 RMD to only $80,000 of taxable income. James emphasizes that no conversion strategy should be set and forgotten; annual reviews are necessary to adjust for actual market returns, changes in spending, revised tax law, and shifting income sources. The goal is to manage the IRA balance to a level where projected future RMDs remain manageable rather than eliminating them entirely.
