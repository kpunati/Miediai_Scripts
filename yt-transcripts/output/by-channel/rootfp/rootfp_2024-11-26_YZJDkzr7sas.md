# This is the Biggest Way People Waste Money in Retirement

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-11-26
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=YZJDkzr7sas
**Duration:** 20:14

## Summary

This episode uses the case of a financially secure retiree who struggles to spend money on home furnishings to examine why wealthy people often fail to exchange money for value. James Conole argues that refusing to spend when financially able is itself a form of waste, since money derives value only from what it can be exchanged for. He draws on the economic concept of diminishing marginal utility and the increasing value of time with age, illustrated by Charlie Munger's deathbed regret about missing experiences. The episode encourages retirees to audit their decisions through the lens of their future self to ensure alignment between financial behavior and desired life outcomes.

## Key Takeaways

- Money has no intrinsic value — it is only worth what it can be exchanged for in goods, services, or experiences.
- Frugality that builds wealth in early life can become counterproductive in retirement when basic needs are already met.
- Diminishing marginal utility means each additional dollar saved has less impact on well-being as wealth grows.
- Time becomes more valuable as people age, making deferred experiences costlier in terms of lost opportunity.
- Viewing decisions through the lens of a future self can reveal whether current spending choices align with desired life outcomes.
- Dying with a large unspent portfolio can represent a greater 'waste' than spending on enriching experiences or comforts.

## Detailed Notes

### The Client Story: Reluctance to Spend [00:00:00]

A client with a multi-million-dollar portfolio, Social Security covering all expenses, and no mortgage was hesitant to furnish a newly expanded home due to fear of 'wasting' money. James uses this as a launching point to explore the psychology of spending in retirement, noting that the client's financial situation made the hesitation paradoxical.

### Money as a Medium of Exchange [00:02:09]

Money has no intrinsic value — it functions as a store of value and medium of exchange. When people confuse preserving the number with actually using money for what it represents, they fail to extract value. Refusing to spend a growing portfolio means the money ultimately goes unexchanged, making it a true waste.

### Diminishing Marginal Utility of Wealth [00:05:17]

As wealth grows, each additional dollar provides less marginal satisfaction. Early-career frugality mindsets were adaptive when money was scarce, but can persist beyond usefulness — illustrated by James's grandmother who still finishes every meal due to depression-era conditioning, even though food scarcity is no longer a concern.

### Time vs. Money: The Charlie Munger Example [00:11:31]

Charlie Munger, worth approximately $2.6 billion at death, expressed regret about never catching a 200-pound tuna when younger. As people age, time becomes increasingly valuable while money becomes less so. The episode argues that deferred experiences have a real cost that compounds with age.

### Auditing Decisions Through the Future Self [00:14:36]

Drawing on Benjamin Hardy's concept of aligning actions with a future self identity, James encourages retirees to journal and audit spending decisions. The key question is whether current behaviors align with the life the future self would endorse — including spending on meaningful experiences and comforts that will be regretted if missed.
