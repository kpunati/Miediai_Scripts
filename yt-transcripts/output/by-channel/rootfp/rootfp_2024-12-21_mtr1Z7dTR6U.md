# I’m 61 with $1.7M How Much Can I Spend in Retirement?

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-12-21
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=mtr1Z7dTR6U
**Duration:** 19:46

## Summary

James Conole uses Robin's retirement scenario to demonstrate a step-by-step approach to determining if someone can retire early and what they can safely spend. Robin, age 61, has $1.7 million across a TSP, Roth TSP, and taxable accounts, a $40,000 annual pension, and expects Social Security at 67. The analysis builds out projected cash flows including mortgage, healthcare, long-term care, travel, and taxes before assessing whether her portfolio can sustainably support those withdrawals. A 78% Monte Carlo success probability is deemed acceptable partly because Robin's pension and Social Security significantly cushion failure scenarios.

## Key Takeaways

- Cash flow planning requires mapping every income source and expense category year by year, not just using a flat monthly budget.
- Withdrawal rate analysis must account for one-time expenses such as vehicle purchases and multi-year long-term care costs.
- A 78% Monte Carlo success rate can be acceptable if non-portfolio income sources like pensions or Social Security cover essential expenses even in failure cases.
- Failure severity — what happens in the unsuccessful scenarios — is as important as probability of success when evaluating a retirement plan.
- Sensitivity testing around spending levels (e.g., $500 per month more) reveals how much margin of safety exists in a plan.
- The framework demonstrated is generalizable: define goals, map income sources, project expenses, assess withdrawal rate, then stress test with Monte Carlo and spending scenarios.

## Detailed Notes

### Robin's Financial Profile and Retirement Goals [00:00:00]

Robin is 61 with approximately $1.7 million across taxable accounts at Fidelity and Schwab, a $91,000 IRA, a $1 million TSP, and an $80,000 Roth TSP. She owns a home worth $658,000 with a $433,000 mortgage. Her goals include retiring at 62, spending $5,000 per month on core expenses, $10,000 per year on travel through 2040, $500 per month for health insurance, a $30,000 vehicle purchase in 2030, and estimated long-term care costs of $68,000 per year for the last four years of life.

### Income Sources and Cash Flow Projection [00:03:10]

Robin's income transitions from a $70,000 salary to a $40,000 pension starting at retirement, a $3,400 monthly Social Security benefit beginning at age 67, and a one-time $116,000 unused-leave payout. Cash flow projections model income versus expenses annually through age 95, identifying net portfolio draws needed each year.

### Withdrawal Rate and Portfolio Sustainability [00:11:27]

Year-one withdrawal rate is approximately 6%, declining before temporarily spiking for the vehicle purchase, then gradually increasing as long-term care costs approach. Applying a 6.5% assumed growth rate, the portfolio grows on average even while supporting withdrawals. The Monte Carlo simulation across 1,000 scenarios yields a 78% success rate, which Conole considers reasonable given Robin's pension and Social Security backstop.

### Interpreting Failure Severity and Stress Testing [00:15:37]

In the 22% of failure scenarios, Robin would eventually deplete her TSP and IRA but would still receive roughly $3,300 per month from her pension plus Social Security, and she holds full home equity as a reserve. Spending sensitivity testing shows that even $500 per month more in expenses meaningfully reduces the success probability, illustrating the margin of safety in the base plan.
