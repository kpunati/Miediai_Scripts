# The Secret Cost of Claiming Social Security Too Early (or Too Late)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-11-16
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=lj_idfYhoiA
**Duration:** 14:29

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial argues that the Social Security claiming decision is far more consequential than a simple break-even calculation. Claiming at 62 provides immediate income but permanently reduces benefits, triggers an earnings cap if still working, and locks in a lower spousal survivor benefit. Claiming at full retirement age (67) removes the earnings cap and delivers the primary insurance amount. Delaying to 70 maximizes the monthly check and creates a valuable low-income window for Roth conversions. Conole also introduces Social Security as a dynamic lever to protect portfolio assets during market downturns.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security is not just an income maximization problem; claiming age directly affects tax strategy, portfolio withdrawal rates, and spousal protection.
- Collecting early at 62 reduces benefits by 8.3% per year for the first three years prior to FRA and 5% per year beyond that, and imposes an earnings cap of $23,400 in 2025.
- Delayed retirement credits of 8% per year do not represent a true 8% investment return; Social Security actuarially normalizes payouts across average life expectancy.
- Delaying to 70 opens a Roth conversion window for those with large pre-tax balances, potentially reducing RMDs and lifetime tax burden.
- Social Security can be used as a dynamic lever: in a severe market downturn, pulling benefits earlier than planned protects the portfolio from sequence-of-returns risk.
- Survivor benefit implications make the claiming decision for higher earners in a marriage especially important, as the surviving spouse inherits the lower amount if claimed early.

## Detailed Notes

### How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated [00:00:00]

Social Security takes the highest 35 years of inflation-adjusted earnings (average indexed monthly earnings) to determine a primary insurance amount (PIA) payable at full retirement age (FRA), which for most current workers is 67. Years without earnings count as zero in the 35-year average, reducing benefits.

### Early Claiming at 62: Freedom Now Approach [00:04:20]

Claiming at 62 provides immediate income for those who need it but permanently reduces the monthly benefit by 8.3% per year for the three years nearest FRA and 5% per year for earlier years. An earnings cap ($23,400 in 2025) means every $2 earned above it triggers $1 in withheld benefits. Survivor benefits are also locked in at the lower reduced amount if the claimant dies first.

### Full Retirement Age (67): The Middle Ground [00:08:21]

Claiming at FRA delivers the full PIA, eliminates the earnings cap entirely, and still allows substantial outside income without benefit reduction. At the federal level, a maximum of 85% of Social Security benefits may be subject to income tax. Conole notes that $1 of Social Security income is after-tax-more-valuable than $1 of traditional IRA income for most retirees.

### Delaying to Age 70: The Long Game and Roth Window [00:09:57]

Waiting until 70 maximizes the benefit by at least 24% above FRA and creates a valuable Roth conversion window in the years between retirement and age 70 when income is low. This is especially important for those with $2 million or more in pre-tax accounts, where future RMDs could be substantial. The combined strategy of retiring at 65 and delaying Social Security to 70 compresses five years of Roth conversions at a lower tax bracket.

### Dynamic Social Security as a Market Hedge [00:12:12]

Conole introduces a flexible framework: if a retiree is delaying Social Security and the market drops significantly, pulling benefits early can reduce forced portfolio withdrawals during a downturn, protecting against sequence-of-returns risk. This reframes Social Security claiming as an adaptive lever rather than a once-and-final decision framework.
