# This Is What a $10M Retirement Actually Looks Like

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2026-01-04
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=UUJLrAIY5EQ
**Duration:** 14:48

## Summary

James Conole walks through a detailed financial planning case study for Roger and Katherine, a 62-year-old couple with approximately $10 million in assets accumulated primarily through equity compensation. The analysis covers cash flow modeling from retirement at 65 through Social Security at 70, withdrawal rate sustainability, the tax spike from RMDs at 75, and portfolio longevity projections. A key theme is that high-net-worth retirees often struggle not with running out of money but with the psychological challenge of spending it intentionally and avoiding costly planning mistakes. Conole also addresses advanced strategies including Roth conversions, donor advised funds, charitable gifting of appreciated stock, and estate planning.

## Key Takeaways

- At the $10M level, the primary challenge shifts from sustainability to intentional spending—retirees often don't know how much they're allowed to enjoy.
- Starting withdrawal rates around 2% dropping below 1% after Social Security provides ample safety margin, but RMDs at 75 create a tax surge that requires proactive planning.
- Social Security timing at 70 meaningfully reduces portfolio withdrawal pressure during the critical early retirement years.
- The gap between age 65 (retirement) and 70 (Social Security) requires funding all expenses from the portfolio—the largest withdrawal window.
- Roth conversions before RMD age can mitigate the future tax burden; donor advised funds allow tax-efficient charitable giving with a large upfront deduction.
- Gifting highly appreciated employer stock to a donor advised fund avoids capital gains and provides an immediate deduction to offset conversion income.
- The burden of high-net-worth wealth is the anxiety of not wanting to make mistakes, not the fear of running out of money.

## Detailed Notes

### Case Study Overview and Portfolio Structure [00:00:00]

Roger and Katherine are 62, retiring at 65, with nearly $7M in a brokerage stock portfolio (concentrated equity compensation), plus 401ks and IRAs totaling roughly $10M. About two-thirds of their assets are pre-tax. Initial planning assumption is $10,000/month living expenses plus $25,000/year travel and healthcare costs.

### Cash Flow Modeling and Income Gap [00:02:43]

The cash flow model shows three years of salary before retirement, then a gap with zero income from ages 65–70 before both Social Security benefits begin. Total annual outflows at retirement including expenses, mortgage, taxes, and travel reach approximately $288,000, all funded by the portfolio until age 70.

### Withdrawal Rate and Sustainability [00:06:18]

Starting withdrawal rate is approximately 2%—the highest it will be—declining each year as portfolio growth outpaces spending. After Social Security at 70, the rate drops below 1%. A spike occurs at 75 due to required minimum distributions from IRAs, though retirees could reinvest the forced withdrawals rather than spend them.

### Portfolio Longevity and Spending Up [00:08:17]

Baseline projections show portfolio growing to nearly $90M by age 95. Conole uses this to push the couple to think bigger: increasing monthly spending by 50% to $15,000 and doubling travel to $50,000 per year still shows a high probability of success. The lesson is to align spending with actual desired lifestyle rather than defaulting to conservative assumptions.

### Tax Planning, Roth Conversions, and Donor Advised Funds [00:12:03]

With large pre-tax balances, Roth conversions during the low-income window before RMDs can reduce the future tax spike. Gifting appreciated employer stock (e.g., shares bought at $10 now worth $120) to a donor advised fund avoids capital gains entirely, generates a large deduction, and creates a family-foundation-style vehicle for multi-generational giving. Estate planning is equally critical to avoid outsized estate taxes.
