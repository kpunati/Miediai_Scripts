# The 3 Biggest Tax Mistakes I See Retirees Make

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-04-05
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=wrFghHkaKmc
**Duration:** 19:46

## Summary

This video by James Conole of Root Financial identifies three costly tax mistakes retirees commonly make. The first is failing to use the 0% long-term capital gains bracket through deliberate tax gain harvesting to generate income without federal tax. The second is falling into the Social Security tax torpedo trap, where incremental IRA distributions effectively double the marginal tax rate by pulling additional Social Security benefits into taxable income. The third mistake is both under-converting and over-converting to Roth IRAs — the optimal approach depends on the retiree's projected required minimum distributions and future tax brackets.

## Key Takeaways

- Married filers with taxable income under $96,700 and single filers under $48,350 pay 0% federal tax on long-term capital gains in 2025.
- Tax gain harvesting allows retirees to realize capital gains strategically while staying within the 0% bracket, reducing lifetime tax liability.
- Provisional income determines what share of Social Security benefits is taxable; half of the Social Security benefit counts toward provisional income.
- The Social Security tax torpedo can raise the effective marginal rate to roughly 22% for retirees who believe they are in the 12% bracket.
- Roth conversions are not universally beneficial; retirees with smaller IRAs may lose wealth by converting at higher rates than they would face from future required distributions.
- The goal of tax planning in retirement is to minimize lifetime tax liability, not simply to minimize taxes in any single year.

## Detailed Notes

### Tax Gain Harvesting and the 0% Capital Gains Bracket [00:00:00]

Conole explains that US tax law includes a 0% federal rate on long-term capital gains for filers within specific taxable income thresholds. A case study of a single 62-year-old retiree ('Joe') shows how combining a $15,000 IRA distribution (offset by the standard deduction) with $45,000 of long-term capital gains results in $75,000 of income with zero federal tax owed.

### Social Security Tax Torpedo and Provisional Income [00:05:42]

Provisional income — including half of the Social Security benefit — determines what percentage of benefits is taxable. The video illustrates how increasing an IRA distribution from $6,000 to $40,000 for a married couple causes $23,850 of their $50,000 Social Security benefit to become taxable. An additional $100 of IRA income costs $22 in taxes rather than the expected $12, reflecting an effective marginal rate above 22% despite being nominally in the 12% bracket.

### Roth Conversion: Under-Converting and Over-Converting [00:13:24]

Retirees with a $2.5 million IRA who never convert face escalating required distributions that push them into much higher brackets later; systematic conversions up to the 12% bracket can generate near-seven-figure improvements in tax-adjusted wealth. Conversely, retirees with only $250,000 in a traditional IRA have much lower projected required distributions and would lose six figures by converting unnecessarily at higher current rates.

### Wrap-Up and Practical Framework [00:17:47]

Conole summarizes that avoiding all three mistakes requires understanding the 0% capital gains bracket, awareness of provisional income and the Social Security tax torpedo, and a personalized Roth conversion strategy calibrated to the retiree's specific IRA balance and projected RMD path.
