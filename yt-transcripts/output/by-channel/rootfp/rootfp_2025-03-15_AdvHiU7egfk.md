# Here's How to Pay Zero Taxes on a $100k Retirement Income (Easier Than You Think)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-03-15
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=AdvHiU7egfk
**Duration:** 17:40

## Summary

James Conole of Root Financial presents a software-guided case study showing how a retired couple (Joe and Sally, both 67) can receive $100,000 annually while owing zero federal income taxes. The approach stacks Social Security, qualified dividends, a modest IRA distribution, and realized long-term capital gains in a specific sequence that keeps taxable ordinary income within the standard deduction and positions any overflow in the 0% long-term capital gains bracket. The video explains the interaction between provisional income, taxable Social Security, and capital gains brackets, illustrating why the order and type of income sources matter as much as the amounts.

## Key Takeaways

- Different income types — Social Security, ordinary IRA distributions, qualified dividends, and long-term capital gains — are taxed differently and stack in a specific IRS order.
- Provisional income determines how much of Social Security is taxable; adding other income sources gradually pulls more Social Security into the taxable pool.
- The standard deduction offsets ordinary income first (IRA distributions, taxable Social Security) before reaching qualified dividends and capital gains.
- The 0% long-term capital gains bracket allows substantial realized gains before any federal tax is owed, creating an annual harvesting opportunity.
- IRA distributions should be capped to avoid triggering disproportionately more taxable Social Security, which can inadvertently push ordinary income above the standard deduction.
- Minimizing taxes in a single year is less important than understanding lifetime tax liability across all retirement years.

## Detailed Notes

### Joe and Sally's Starting Situation [00:01:03]

Joe and Sally are both 67, retired, and want $100,000/year in income. Joe receives $3,200/month in Social Security and Sally receives $2,000/month, totaling $62,400/year. They have a $500,000 brokerage account with $250,000 cost basis generating $10,000 in qualified dividends, plus $650,000 in traditional IRAs and $150,000 in Roth IRAs.

### Social Security and Provisional Income Mechanics [00:03:09]

With only Social Security as income, none of Joe and Sally's $62,400 benefit is taxable due to provisional income thresholds. Adding $10,000 in qualified dividends causes $4,600 of Social Security to become taxable, demonstrating how provisional income causes a cascading effect — each additional income source increases the taxable portion of Social Security.

### Why a Straight IRA Drawdown Fails the Zero-Tax Goal [00:06:16]

Pulling $18,600 from IRAs to fill the standard deduction appears logical but triggers more Social Security taxation, causing adjusted gross income to exceed the standard deduction. Instead, pulling only $11,600 from the IRA keeps the interplay between provisional income and the deduction in balance, landing at $84,000 total income with no taxable ordinary income.

### Capital Gains Harvesting to Reach $100,000 [00:11:33]

With $84,000 covered, the remaining $16,000 comes from selling $16,000 of brokerage stock — half basis, half gain — generating $8,000 of long-term gains. The total adjusted gross income reaches ~$50,000 but the overflow above the standard deduction is entirely in qualified dividends and long-term gains, which are taxed at 0% since taxable income remains well below the $96,700 threshold. The result: $100,000 income, $0 in federal taxes.
