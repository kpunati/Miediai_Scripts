# I’m Single and 5 Years Out From Retirement. What Should I be Doing? (5 Specific Steps)

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-02-08
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=7Cki8EM9Zcg
**Duration:** 17:21

## Summary

James Conole, CFP at Root Financial, uses Jenny — a 60-year-old single accountant with roughly $702,000 in combined assets and a goal to retire at 65 — as a case study to show the importance of completing five retirement planning steps in the correct sequence. Starting with a grim 24% success probability, Conole demonstrates that simply correcting expense assumptions (removing a soon-to-be-paid-off mortgage and time-limiting travel spending) and deferring Social Security to age 70 raises success to 86%. Adding portfolio adjustments to front-load conservative assets for the Social Security gap years, then layering in Roth conversion tax strategy, brings the final probability to 85% with $1.6 million more projected wealth — all without working a single extra year.

## Key Takeaways

- Dialing in realistic retirement expenses — accounting for a mortgage payoff and time-limited travel — is the most important and often overlooked step before any other planning.
- Delaying Social Security to age 70 dramatically increases guaranteed lifetime income and can by itself raise plan success probability significantly.
- Early retirement years may require high portfolio withdrawal rates of 7-10%, which can be sustainable if Social Security and reduced travel spending reduce later withdrawals.
- Portfolio allocation should follow expense and Social Security decisions, not precede them; conservative assets are most needed during the Social Security gap years, not necessarily as a retiree ages.
- Roth conversions in low-income years between retirement and Social Security claiming (filling the 12% bracket) can save tens of thousands in lifetime taxes.
- Recalibrating the plan after all adjustments often reveals capacity for enhanced lifestyle spending that the original overly conservative plan missed entirely.

## Detailed Notes

### Jenny's Starting Position and Initial Plan Shortfall [00:01:02]

Jenny is 60, single, with $480k in a 401k, $76k in a Roth IRA, $146k in an individual account, a $765k home with $51k left on the mortgage ($1,000/month P&I), targeting $6,000/month in retirement at age 65. Social Security at 65 would provide $2,400/month. Without changes, assets run out at 85 and success probability is only 24%; working until 70 brings it to just 64%.

### Step 1 and 2: Dial In Expenses and Optimize Social Security [00:04:10]

The mortgage will be paid off in 4.5 years, dropping core expenses from $6,000 to $5,000 per month. Travel of $1,000/month is capped at 10 years (ages 65-75) rather than modeled through age 90. Delaying Social Security from 65 to 70 further increases guaranteed income. Together these two adjustments alone raise success probability from 24% to 86%.

### Step 3: Portfolio Allocation to Match Cash Flow Needs [00:08:18]

Delaying Social Security creates a high-withdrawal period in years 1-5 (6.8% to nearly 10% per year) before dropping to 4% and then 3% once Social Security begins and travel winds down. Jenny must hold conservative, stable assets sufficient to cover those early high-withdrawal years without relying on growth investments that could be depleted by a market downturn.

### Step 4: Tax Strategy — Roth Conversions [00:12:42]

Between retirement at 65 and Social Security at 70, Jenny has a low-income window. Converting traditional IRA funds to Roth just enough to fill the 12% tax bracket each year yields $52k more in tax-adjusted assets, $44k in lifetime tax savings, and $74k fewer withdrawals from tax-deferred accounts.

### Step 5: Recalibrate and Optimize Lifestyle [00:13:44]

With an 85% success probability and $1.6M more projected than the original plan, Jenny can recalibrate to improve quality of life — potentially doubling her travel budget to $2,000/month or exploring other lifestyle enhancements. A potential home sale at 85 when moving in with her daughter adds further financial cushion.
