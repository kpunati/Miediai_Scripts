# Here's How Early Retirement Will Impact Your Social Security Benefit

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2025-08-09
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=r2_QWrC8VAE
**Duration:** 17:14

## Summary

James Conole and Erin walk through the mechanics of Social Security benefit calculation, debunking the common belief that workers must stay employed until their 60s to maximize benefits. The formula uses average indexed monthly earnings from the highest 35 years, with inflation indexing ceasing at age 60. The hosts also explain the progressive bend-point structure that credits lower-income workers proportionally more, and carefully distinguish between when someone stops working and when they choose to claim — two entirely independent decisions with very different financial implications.

## Key Takeaways

- Social Security benefits depend on the 35 highest inflation-adjusted earning years, so retiring before 62 does not automatically reduce benefits if those 35 years are already complete.
- Earning even modest part-time income in semi-retirement can eliminate zero-earning years in the formula, increasing benefits more than most people expect through the high weighting of early bend-point dollars.
- The inflation indexing of past wages stops at age 60, meaning working years at 59 can actually carry more benefit weight than years at 61 in some scenarios.
- Claiming at 62 versus delaying to 70 can produce a $1,000/month difference in lifetime income floor, with the right answer depending on portfolio size, health, longevity expectations, and spousal considerations.
- The decision to stop working and the decision to claim Social Security are independent; someone could stop at 40 and still claim at 70.
- Checking personalized estimates at ssa.gov is a critical step that most pre-retirees skip, despite the website being simple to use.

## Detailed Notes

### How Social Security Is Actually Calculated [00:01:48]

Benefits are derived from average indexed monthly earnings (AIME) — the inflation-adjusted average of a worker's 35 highest-earning years. Early working years are adjusted upward for inflation, so low wages from decades ago do not necessarily drag down the benefit. Once someone has 35 years of maximum-taxed earnings, additional work provides no further benefit increase.

### Bend Points and the Progressive Benefit Structure [00:05:46]

The benefit formula applies decreasing marginal rates — approximately 90% of earnings up to the first bend point, then about 35%, then about 15% above the second bend point. This means lower earners receive Social Security as a higher fraction of their wages. In 2025, the earnings cap is $176,100; earning above this neither pays more tax nor builds additional benefit credit.

### The Impact of Working Fewer Than 35 Years [00:07:36]

If a person works only 30 years, Social Security fills the remaining 5 slots with zeros when calculating AIME, reducing the benefit. However, even a small amount of part-time income in semi-retirement can replace those zeros and improve the benefit, because the first dollars of AIME are weighted most heavily in the bend-point formula.

### Claiming Age: The Independent Decision [00:10:54]

Claiming at 62 delivers roughly 70% of the full-retirement-age (FRA) benefit; delaying to 70 delivers approximately 120%, illustrated at $1,400 versus $2,400 per month on a $2,000 FRA benefit. The decision hinges on portfolio sustainability during the delay period, health and longevity expectations, spousal and survivor benefit implications, and overall income floor preferences. The hosts stress there is no universally correct claiming age.
