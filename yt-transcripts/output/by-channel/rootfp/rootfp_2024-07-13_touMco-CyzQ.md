  Retirement is a significant milestone filled with new opportunities and challenges. Ensuring financial stability is crucial, often relying on financial advisors. However, even the best advisors can make costly mistakes. 

  One common error involves asset location, which means placing investments in accounts to minimize taxes. Though this strategy is sound, its execution often lacks long-term foresight.

# Mistakes Your Financial Advisor is Making with Your Portfolio

**Channel:** James Conole, CFP®
**Published:** 2024-07-13
**URL:** https://youtube.com/watch?v=touMco-CyzQ
**Duration:** 13:41

## Summary

James Conole uses a Vanguard blog post on asset location as a case study to demonstrate how a technically sound strategy can be harmful when applied without considering sequence of return risk in retirement. While asset location — placing bonds in tax-deferred accounts and stocks in taxable accounts — can improve after-tax returns by 0.5% to 3% during accumulation, in retirement the tax savings are much smaller and the strategy forces retirees to sell stocks at depressed prices to fund withdrawals. Conole argues that keeping some stable assets in the taxable account is more important than maximizing tax efficiency, and that Roth IRAs should be filled with high-growth equities to maximize tax-free compounding.

## Key Takeaways

- Asset location has legitimate merit during working years when income brackets are higher and bond allocations are often smaller.
- In retirement, asset location tax savings typically fall to roughly 0.1% per year because retirees are in lower tax brackets.
- Placing all stocks in a taxable brokerage account exposes retirees to forced selling at a loss during market downturns, negating years of tax savings in a single downturn.
- Roth IRAs should hold high-growth assets like equities, not bonds, because the tax-free compounding benefit of stocks far exceeds any short-term tax optimization from bonds.
- Good financial planning requires balancing competing priorities rather than optimizing any single variable, such as annual tax savings, at the expense of long-term portfolio resilience.

## Detailed Notes

### The Asset Location Concept and Vanguard's Recommendation [00:00:00]

Conole introduces asset location — the practice of placing investments in account types that minimize their tax drag — and uses a Vanguard blog post as an illustration. Vanguard recommends placing bonds in traditional IRAs and 401ks (shielding interest income from ordinary rates up to 37%), any excess bonds in Roth IRAs, and stocks in taxable accounts (benefiting from 0-20% long-term capital gains rates).

### Two Problems with the Standard Recommendation [00:04:11]

Conole identifies two flaws. First, placing bonds in a Roth IRA wastes its tax-free growth potential; stocks, which historically outperform bonds, should occupy Roth accounts to maximize tax-free compounding. Second, filling the taxable brokerage account entirely with stocks creates a dangerous withdrawal problem in retirement.

### The Hidden Cost in Retirement [00:07:18]

Using a JP Morgan chart of S&P 500 returns from 1950 onward, Conole shows that bear markets occur roughly once every five years with 20-25% declines. A retiree drawing 10% of an all-stock taxable portfolio during a 20-25% downturn is effectively liquidating assets at a severe loss. He argues this cost far outweighs the roughly 0.1% annual tax savings asset location provides in retirement.

### Practical Principles for Retirement [00:10:33]

Conole concludes with three principles: asset location works best during high-income working years; financial planning often involves balancing conflicting priorities rather than optimizing for one outcome; and a long-term view must always evaluate the full cost-benefit, including the reduction in Roth IRA growth from holding bonds there, not just the current-year tax savings.
